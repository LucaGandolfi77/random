
## Chapter 7: "The Party"

The bass thumped through the floorboards of the old Victorian house like a second heartbeat, reverberating in Chloe Sterling’s chest as she stepped onto the porch. Strings of fairy lights crisscrossed the backyard, casting a golden haze over the crowd. She adjusted the strap of her sequined clutch and smoothed her emerald dress—*not too revealing, but enough to remind everyone why she was the queen*. Her heels clicked against the wooden steps as she descended into the fray, her entourage trailing behind like satellites in orbit.

“Chloe! Over here!” Her best friend, Madison, waved from a cluster near the keg. The usual suspects were there—juniors and seniors who’d traded textbooks for red Solo cups, their laughter sharp with the kind of certainty that came from never having been on the outside. Chloe smiled, the one she’d perfected in middle school: lips curved, eyes glinting with mischief. It was a mask that had never failed her.

She took a swig from her cup, the cheap beer bitter on her tongue, and let her gaze drift across the yard. That’s when she saw him. Ethan Cole stood by the chain-link fence, his hands shoved into the pockets of his jacket, nodding at something a sophomore was saying. His hair was tousled from the wind, and there was a smudge of dirt on his cheek—probably from helping move tables earlier. Chloe’s stomach did that weird flutter again, the one that had been happening more often since their debate project. She scowled into her cup, as if the foam could explain why her pulse felt like it was trying to escape her ribs.

“You’re staring,” Madison said, sidling up beside her. 

“I’m not—” Chloe cut herself off, glancing away. Her cheeks burned. 

“Relax. I’m just saying, if you’re gonna make a move, don’t let him turn you into a puddle first.” Madison’s laugh was syrupy, but her eyes were calculating. “He’s not exactly… dynamic, you know?”

Chloe’s jaw tightened. She hated that word. *Boring*. *Soft*. The labels her friends tossed around like they were harmless. But when she looked at Ethan, she didn’t see dullness—she saw the way he’d defended her in class, the way he’d challenged her to find something beautiful in someone society deemed ugly. He made her think, and thinking was dangerous. Thinking led to questions she wasn’t ready to answer.

A commotion near the fence drew her attention. A freshman girl, tears streaking her mascara, was being consoled by someone. Chloe squinted. It was Ethan. He wasn’t saying much—just listening, his expression soft in a way that made her chest ache. The girl’s shoulders shook as she clutched a crumpled piece of paper. Ethan handed her a tissue from his pocket, his movements deliberate, gentle. 

*What is wrong with him?* Chloe thought, feeling something twist in her stomach. *Why does he even care?*

But then she saw the girl’s face. Not the smeared makeup or the red-rimmed eyes, but the relief in the way she leaned into his shoulder. Ethan didn’t flinch. He stayed there, a steady presence in the chaos of the party, until the girl’s sobs quieted into hiccups. When she finally walked away, wiping her eyes, Ethan didn’t watch her go. He just stood there, hands back in his pockets, like he hadn’t just performed some kind of quiet miracle.

Chloe’s grip on her cup tightened until her knuckles went white. 

“You okay?” Madison asked, noticing her silence. 

“Yeah. Just… tired.” Chloe excused herself, weaving through the crowd with more urgency than necessary. The music pulsed louder, but all she could hear was the echo of Ethan’s voice from their last conversation: *“I just don’t like seeing people treated like they’re invisible.”*

In the hallway, she pressed her palm against the wall, breathing hard. *What’s wrong with me?* she wondered. *He’s just… being nice. So what?* But it wasn’t *just* nice. It was different. Real. The kind of kindness that didn’t need an audience or a reward. The kind that made her want to believe in something bigger than herself, something that scared her because it felt like falling.

She wandered into the kitchen, where a group of kids were rummaging through the fridge. A boy she barely recognized grinned at her. “Hey, Sterling. You wanna dance?”

Chloe opened her mouth to reply, but her gaze drifted to the window. Outside, Ethan was lighting a cigarette, his silhouette framed by the fairy lights. He wasn’t even smoking it—he’d given it to someone else, a lanky junior who’d asked for one earlier. Chloe watched him laugh at something the kid said, that easy smile of his making the boy’s entire face light up. 

Her chest felt tight. Not jealousy—she wasn’t sure what it was. Longing? Recognition? She thought of her reflection that morning, the way she’d practiced her smirk in the mirror, the way she’d perfected her laugh to sound like wind chimes. But here was this boy who didn’t need any of that. Who didn’t need to be the center of attention because he’d already found something better: authenticity.

“Chloe?” The boy in the kitchen was still waiting. 

“I’m gonna head out,” she said, surprising herself with the steadiness of her voice. 

“Already? It’s barely midnight.”

“I’ve got an early morning.” She lied smoothly, grabbing her coat from the hook. 

Outside, the night air hit her like a slap. She walked without direction, her heels clicking against the sidewalk until she reached the park. The swings creaked in the wind, and she sat on one, kicking at the gravel. Her phone buzzed—a text from Madison: *“You ditched us??”* Chloe stared at it until her screen went dark, then shoved the phone into her pocket.

Why was she so messed up? He’d done nothing wrong. He’d done everything right, actually. And that was the problem. Because the more she watched him, the more she realized how little she knew about being good. Not performative niceness, but real goodness—the kind that chose to sit with a crying stranger instead of posting about it on social media. The kind that didn’t need applause.

She closed her eyes, remembering the girl’s face again, the way Ethan had looked at her like she mattered. When had Chloe stopped seeing people like that? When had she decided that cruelty was easier than empathy?

A car passed by, its headlights cutting through the darkness. Chloe stood, brushing off her dress. She didn’t have answers yet. But for the first time in years, she wanted to find them. 

As she walked home, the weight of the night settled on her shoulders—not the weight of shame, but of possibility. Ethan Cole had unknowingly lit a match, and Chloe Sterling was finally starting to see the shadows for what they were: illusions. 

She pulled out her phone and typed a single message: *“Can we talk tomorrow?”* 

She hit send before she could second-guess herself. Then she kept walking, her heart a little lighter, her mind a little clearer, her story just beginning to bend in a direction she’d never dared to imagine.
