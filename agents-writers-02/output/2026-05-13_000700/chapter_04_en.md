## Chapter 4 —The Eternal Feast  

The night air over the piazza of Via della Spiga smelled of jasmine and the faint, metallic tang of city rain that had not yet fallen. Aurelio Vianni stood on the marble steps of his palazzo, his silhouette a perfect cut against the amber glow of the lanterns. The façade, a palimpsest of gilded stucco and frescoed cornices, seemed to pulse with the same rhythm as his own heart—steady, confident, unassailable. He inhaled, feeling the cool breath of the evening settle in his lungs, and for a moment the world beyond his marble threshold dissolved into a distant hum.  

He was the god of this room, the axis upon which the salon of Milan spun. The invitation had been a simple piece of parchment, hand‑written in ink that shimmered like liquid gold, bearing only his name and the date. Yet the message was unmistakable: tonight, the universe would bow. He adjusted the cuff of his bespoke tuxedo, the silk brushing his skin with the softness of a lover’s whisper, and glanced at his reflection in the polished surface of the doorway. The man looking back was a study in symmetry—high cheekbones, a jawline that seemed chiseled by an unseen sculptor, eyes that held a spark of mischief and an unspoken promise of brilliance.  

The first guests arrived with the elegance of a ballet, each step measured, each smile rehearsed. There was Luca, his childhood friend, whose laughter still carried the reckless timbre of youth, and Giulia, a poet whose verses were whispered in cafés before being printed in the most prestigious literary journals. The artist Dario, his longtime confidant and the only man who had ever seen the cracks beneath Aurelio’s polished veneer, entered with a glass of vintage Barolo cradled in his hand, his eyes alight with admiration.  

“To the man who will never age,” Dario toasted, raising his glass so that the ruby liquid caught the light and fractured into a thousand tiny suns. The words hung in the air, a toast that resonated like a mantra. Aurelio felt a surge of warmth, a validation that his myth was still alive. He lifted his own glass, the crystal singing as it met the others, and replied, “To the beauty that outlives time.”  

Inside the grand salon, the room was a tableau of curated splendor. Crystal chandeliers cascaded light onto walls adorned with reproductions of Caravaggio and Modigliani, each piece selected to reflect Aurelio’s own aesthetic—high contrast, dramatic chiaroscuro, an emphasis on the sublime. The tables were laden with dishes that seemed to have been plucked from a Renaissance banquet: truffled risotto, lobster bisque infused with saffron, a dessert of pistachio gelato crowned with gold leaf. The scent of rosemary and rosemary mingled with the perfume of the women present—silk, amber, a whisper of sandalwood—creating an olfactory symphony that enveloped every guest.  

Aurelio moved through the crowd with the ease of someone who knew he was the center of gravity. He exchanged a few words with Giulia, his voice low and velvety, coaxing from her a confession about the way poetry could capture the fleeting nature of a moment. He smiled at Luca, recalling the nights they had spent in the back rooms of the Teatro alla Scala, dreaming of fame, of art, of a world that would remember them. Each interaction was a thread, weaving his personal legend into the fabric of the evening.  

He felt invincible. The notion of time as an adversary was a distant myth, a story told to the weary. He imagined himself as a statue carved from marble—permanent, immutable, admired for centuries. In his mind’s eye, the future stretched out like a polished hallway, each door opening onto more admiration, more influence, more beauty. The thought was intoxicating. He could almost hear the future whispering, “You are eternal.”  

Yet beneath the surface, a quiet current pulsed. The line near his eye that he would later notice in Chapter Five was already forming, a subtle reminder that the flesh was not impervious. He brushed the thought away as he would any inconvenience, focusing instead on the intoxicating glow of the party. He watched Dario set his brush down on a small easel that had been placed in a corner of the salon, as if he were preparing to capture the very moment on canvas. The painter’s eyes lingered on Aurelio, not merely seeing the surface but attempting to plumb the depths of his expression, the fleeting flicker of a smile that never quite reached his eyes.  

Aurelio’s inner monologue churned as he observed the room. *They all look to me, as if I were the sun itself. They admire my mind, my looks, my words. If I were to fade, would they still gather? Would the light dim?* He pushed these questions aside, replacing them with a mantra that had become his creed: **beauty is permanence**. He believed that intellect and aesthetic were inseparable, that his brilliance as a critic and essayist was proof that his essence was timeless. The essays he had penned—sharp, lyrical, filled with the kind of insight that made readers feel both enlightened and exhilarated—were his testament to an enduring legacy.  

The music swelled, a string quartet that seemed to echo the rhythm of his pulse. He took a sip of his Barolo, feeling the tannins coat his palate, the warmth spreading through his chest. In that moment, he imagined himself as a timeless figure, a myth that would be recounted in future generations. He could already see the headlines: *“Aurelio Vianni: The Immortal Sage of Milan.”* The thought made his heart race, a mixture of pride and a strange, almost childlike anticipation.  

Dario, ever the observer, caught Aurelio’s gaze and, with a subtle nod, raised his glass once more. “To the man who will never age,” he repeated, his voice low enough that only Aurelio could hear. The repetition was a ritual, a reinforcement of the belief that Aurelio was untouched by the inevitable. Aurelio smiled, feeling the weight of the words settle like a golden crown upon his head. He could almost feel the years compressing around him, the world slowing, as if time itself were holding its breath in reverence.  

As the night deepened, the party grew wilder. Laughter erupted, confessions spilled, and the clink of glasses became a percussive backdrop to the murmurs of admiration. A group of young architects, fresh from the university, gathered around Aurelio, eager to hear his thoughts on the future of urban design. He spoke with a fervor that made his words seem to ripple through the room, each sentence a brushstroke on the canvas of their imaginations. He spoke of harmony, of the marriage between function and beauty, of how a building could be a living poem. The young men and women listened, their eyes shining with the reflection of his brilliance.  

At one point, a sudden hush fell over the room as an elderly gentleman, a retired professor of philosophy, took the floor. His voice trembled, but his words carried the weight of decades spent in contemplation. “We are all travelers on the same road,” he said, “and the road is made of moments. The beauty of a moment is that it is fleeting, yet it is also eternal in memory.” The statement hung like a veil, and for a second Aurelio felt a flicker of unease, a whisper that perhaps his certainty was not absolute. He quickly covered it with a laugh, raising his glass again. “To the moments that never end,” he declared, and the room erupted in cheers.  

The evening stretched on, the candles burning low, their flames dancing in the drafts that slipped through the tall windows. Aurelio moved from group to group, his presence a magnetic force that drew people in, made them feel seen, heard, understood. He was the conductor of this symphony, each guest a note that resonated with his own. He felt a strange, almost ecstatic joy in being the center of such admiration, in knowing that his influence extended beyond the walls of his palazzo, into the very fabric of Milan’s cultural life.  

When the night finally began to wane, the guests started to leave, each carrying a piece of the evening’s magic with them. Some whispered promises to meet again, others lingered to exchange numbers, their eyes reflecting the lingering glow of the chandeliers. Aurelio stood at the doorway, watching them disappear into the night, feeling a peculiar mixture of triumph and melancholy. He knew that tomorrow, the world would continue its relentless march, that the sun would rise and the city would stir, but in this moment, he was the master of his own eternity.  

He turned back into the salon, the silence now more profound, the remnants of the feast scattered like fallen leaves. He walked to the grand staircase, his footsteps echoing softly on the marble. As he ascended, he caught his reflection in the polished banister once more. The man looking back was still impeccably groomed, his hair immaculate, his skin luminous. Yet a faint line—so subtle that it could have been a trick of the light—traced the corner of his eye, a whisper of time’s encroachment. He pressed a hand to his cheek, feeling the smoothness, and for an instant the world seemed to tilt.  

*What if*, he thought, *the toast was not a promise but a prayer?* The notion was intoxicating. He imagined himself, years hence, still standing at the center of a gathering, the same faces surrounding him, the same admiration flowing like a river. He saw himself older, perhaps, but still radiant, still the god of the room. He allowed himself to bask in this vision, letting it fill his chest with a warm, almost holy light.  

The final toast of the night was made by Dario, who, with a half‑smile, lifted his glass once more. “To the man who will never age,” he said, his voice carrying the weight of years of friendship. Aurelio felt the words settle into his bones, a pledge that seemed to echo beyond the walls of the palazzo, into the very essence of his being. He clinked his glass against Dario’s, feeling the crystal resonate like a bell tolling a promise.  

As the last guests departed, the palace fell into a hushed stillness. The chandeliers cast long shadows across the polished floors, and the scent of perfume lingered like an afterglow. Aurelio stood alone in the center of the room, the echo of laughter still ringing in his ears. He inhaled deeply, feeling the night air fill his lungs, and for a brief instant, he sensed the presence of something larger than himself—a timeless current that ran through the city, through art, through love, through loss.  

He walked to the large windows that overlooked the piazza, the city lights shimmering like a field of stars. The cool breeze brushed his face, and he let his eyes wander over the silhouettes of the buildings, the distant hum of traffic, the occasional flicker of a passing car’s headlights. In that vastness, he felt both infinitesimally small and enormously powerful. He was a part of something eternal, yet he also possessed the ability to shape it, to leave an imprint that would outlive his own fleeting years.  

*I am the god of this room*, he whispered to himself, the words barely audible over the soft sigh of the wind. *And gods do not age.* The conviction steadied him, gave him a sense of invincibility that was both intoxicating and comforting. He imagined himself as a statue—cold, unmoving, admired for centuries. He could almost feel the marble’s smoothness against his skin, the weight of eternity pressing down upon his shoulders, not as a burden but as a mantle of honor.  

He turned back toward the interior, his steps light, his posture regal. The party had been a celebration of his perceived immortality, a tableau that reinforced his belief that beauty and intellect were his birthright. He felt a surge of gratitude toward his friends, toward the admirers who had gathered to witness his brilliance, toward the world that had crowned him as its shining star.  

In the quiet that followed, Aurelio’s mind drifted to the future he had envisioned. He saw himself writing a new essay, his words still sharp, his prose still elegant, his influence still felt in the corridors of power and the salons of the elite. He saw himself traveling to distant cities, meeting other intellectuals, sharing his vision of beauty that transcended time. He imagined a life where each day was a continuation of the feast, where the celebration never truly ended, where the toast could be repeated ad infinitum.  

And yet, beneath that grand vision, a subtle, almost imperceptible tremor began to stir. It was not fear—at least not yet—but a whisper of curiosity, a question that would later grow louder: **What if the feast could never last?** The thought was a seed, planted gently in the fertile soil of his confidence, waiting for the right conditions to sprout.  

He paused at the threshold of the main hall, his silhouette framed by the doorway, the night beyond a dark canvas. He lifted his glass one final time, not to toast, but to drink the last of the wine, feeling its warmth spread through his chest, a reminder that even the most exquisite moments are fleeting. He set the glass down, the soft clink echoing like a punctuation mark at the end of a sentence.  

In that instant, Aurelio Vianni, the god of the room, the eternal feast, felt a flicker of something new—a recognition that his brilliance, his beauty, his charm, were not merely external trappings but part of a larger, ever‑changing tapestry. He was, after all, a man, subject to the same currents that moved the city, the same tides that shaped the art he so revered.  

And as the night deepened, the palazzo seemed to exhale, the candles flickering lower, the music fading into a soft, lingering chord. Aurelio stood alone, the echo of the toast resonating in his ears, the image of his own reflection lingering behind his eyes. He smiled, a genuine, unguarded smile that reached his eyes, and for the first time that evening, he allowed himself to feel not just the triumph of being admired, but the quiet, profound joy of simply being present in the moment—**the eternal feast** that, for now, was his world.
