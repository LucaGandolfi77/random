## Chapter 8 – The Museum of Lost Youth

The day began, as all of Aurelio’s days did, with an overelaborate ritual that felt more like a ceremony than a morning routine. He had the habit of lighting a single candle, the one with the pale blue wax that caught the light like a small star, and then pouring his coffee with a flourish that made the drink itself seem to applaud. But today he abandoned the ritual. The candle lay untouched in the corner of his study, its flame flickering once, then dying, as if it had been promised to be snuffed long before the sun rose.

He dressed as he always did—slim, tailored, with a silk collar, but without the extra preening that usually marked the preparation of his public self. He walked to the windows, where the light of the morning was not yet sharp enough to cut the shadows from the old oak floors. The city was still a hushed, gray whisper, the kind of morning that made him think of his own breath, heavy and deliberate. He felt, as if he could hear it, the faint pulse of the city, like a heartbeat that was not his own.

The purpose of his day was simple: to visit Professor Ferrante. Aurelio had known the professor from a much earlier time, when they were both young intellectuals, each a budding star in their own right. Ferrante was the one who had first named Aurelio’s essays “the purest form of shining stone,” and, perhaps more importantly, the one who had first drawn him into the world of critical analysis and aesthetics. Now, the professor was a frail man, with a crumbling facade that looked as if it had once been marble but was now bleached and cracked.

Aurelio’s heart beat with a strange mixture of anticipation and dread. He had been so careful to keep his life as a museum, a curation of flawless pieces, that the idea of confronting a piece that had already degraded felt like a breach of the sanctuary he had built. He imagined, in the back of his mind, Ferrante’s once-sharp eyes, his lines of text, his once-boisterous laughter, all now reduced to a mere echo.

The nursing home was a building that sat on the edge of an old Milan suburb, a place of quiet that had always seemed far too far from the glittering world his life had become. The garden that surrounded it was a testament to careful neglect—flowers that had once been the subject of his most vibrant essays, now wilting as if to remind him that even the most beautiful things succumb to time. He walked up the path, his gaze following the same stones he had once described in a long, celebrated essay on the fleeting nature of architecture.

Inside, the corridors smelled of antiseptic and tired hope. He was welcomed by a young nurse with a name tag that read "Lara." She was the keeper of the day, the one who took the elevator, the one who whispered a quiet “Buongiorno, signor Vianni” with the gentility of a librarian. He followed her down into the common room, where several elderly people sat, watching the television or clutching the armrests of their tables with a steadiness that betrayed a lifetime of anticipation.

The first thing that struck him was the silence that had settled over the room; a silence that was almost palpable, as if the walls were holding their breath. He found his way to the corner where Professor Ferrante was sitting, his eyes closed, a small smile playing on his lips, as if he were remembering a joyous memory. The old man had a white beard, the kind that was as unruly as the way he used to argue with his students about the nuances of modernist painting. His hair had all but vanished, and the skin on his cheek was the paper-thin, translucent memory of a woman’s soul, and yet his eyes glimmered with the same intensity he had shown in his early decades.

Aurelio approached him with a hesitation that startled him. He did not want to be seen as a voyeur, but he could not deny that a part of him was craving the confrontation. His throat tightened as he opened his mouth to speak.

"Signor Ferrante," he began, his voice dropping to a tone that was more reverent than he had ever used in front of anyone. "I…I have come because I thought…"

The professor's eyes flicked open, a soft, knowing glint that made Aurelio's heart pound. "Vianni, my boy," he said, his voice low and rough as gravel. "You always returned to my rooms like a pilgrimage."

Aurelio's mind flickered to the days when they had been together, arguing about the penetration of the human spirit in art. He remembered the way the professor would laugh, a booming sound that seemed to spill out of the walls, and feel the way the world was not only beautiful but also cruelly indifferent. He thought of the times Aurelio had sat in the back of the lecture hall, his pen scratching like a small rebellion.

"You have grown," Ferrante said, gesturing to an empty chair across from him. "And so have I, in a different dimension."

Aurelio took the chair, his hands trembling as they sank into the armrests, the memory of the weight of his own skin on his fingers as if he were looking at a mirror but with a new, more distant piece of glass. He looked at Ferrante again—his eyes, now a little younger, but lined with the kind of experience that could not be replicated in any other life. The face of the man he had idolized in his youth was now dressed in the same clothes of consequence that his own face wore: sorrow, patience, and an indulgence that came from a deep and honest love of beauty.

The professor turned his head slowly, as if to scan the room for a hidden observer. He began speaking with a voice that sounded almost like a prayer.

"You made me think," he said, as he spoke. "You made the world a place of lights, a place of dangerous beauty, and you taught me that those lights can become a curse, a threat to all of us in our own way. And now…now it is for me to look at you, and to see what I had once promised to be—to be the only one who sees beauty in your eyes, and yet I am no longer interested…"

Aurelio was lost. He had always thought that he had seen beauty in Ferrante’s eyes, but now, to him, it felt like an old illusion he had forgotten to remember. He looked at the old man in front of him, feeling a sudden sense that his own life had become a museum of lost youth.

Ferrante smiled, a small, almost sarcastic smile that cut through it all. "I had always known that the thing we try to hold onto is the thing that is slipping away," he said, as if he had read the sentence in a book that Aurelio had written. "And yet we are still at the same time that we have stuck to the idea."

The conversation turned to a topic that seemed almost inevitable, an all-encompassing theme that was both a necessary moment and a very painful one: the concept of mortality. Ferrante leaned forward and spoke about the inevitability of time, the hollowness of the young man who was determined to stay alive, and the loss of meaning that reality forces at times. He voiced the idea that there was no way to avoid death, no way to hold on to beauty. He made Aurelio feel as if he were a part of a conversation that his mind had never intended to be part of. He had always assumed that art was a way to escape death, but now he saw it as a mechanism that our children use to realize that the world is brittle and fragile.

When the conversation ended, Aurelio left the nursing home in a daze. He had been pushed by an indescribable sense of sadness, which was almost a sorcery that had happened to him in a sudden and bizarre way. He had never imagined that his muse could be a nurse or a scientist. He had thought that it was only the possibility that he had to remember that he was still very young, that his life was still a new beginning, that the world was still a place where he could continue to grow. But it was now, after his conversation with Ferrante, that he understood that he had to accept the fact that his life was an ending, that he had to accept himself and his own reality, and that he had to find a way to live, even if it was with a different perspective.

He was no longer able to see his life as a museum. He saw the world as he would see a museum that was in the process of deteriorating. He had to accept the reality that the world was in a state of flux, that there was no way to get rid of the fact that time would not take his beauty. He had learned that the only way to continue to live was to accept the fact that his life was not a museum, that his life was something else. He had to accept the fact that he can no longer live, and that he must look for his own path, even if it was a different path.

Aurelio walked home with the idea that he had to find a new life, perhaps a new meaning, because it was a very different sense of life. He had to realize that the world had a new time, that he had to accept this new reality.
