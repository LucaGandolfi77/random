## Chapter 9 — The Pharmacist's Cabinet

The first vial arrived on a Tuesday, in a box the size of a rosary case, wrapped in black tissue that smelled of nothing—not perfume, not the antique shop mustiness Aurelio had expected, but the clinical absence of scent. He held it under the kitchen light like a jeweler examining a flawed stone and read the label twice: *HGH – Sottocontrollo, uso estetico sperimentale*. Human growth hormone. Experimental cosmetic use.

His hands did not tremble. That was the first wrong thing. He had expected his hands to tremble, or at least to betray some flicker of the guilt he had been promised would arrive hand-in-hand with vanity. But there was only curiosity—a bright, clean curiosity, the same one that had made him fall in love with a single paragraph of Proust at fifteen, the one about the madeleine dissolving on the tongue. He uncapped it. Drew the syringe. Injected himself in the hip, the way Dr. Marenghi had demonstrated, with the practiced ease of a man who had done this many times before—not to himself, not yet, but to others. Others who were paying.

Dr. Marenghi's office was not an office. It was a narrow room behind a pharmacy in the Isola district, between a shuttered wine bar and a shop selling nativity figurines, and it smelled of rubbing alcohol and bergamot. The doctor himself was a slim man in his forties with hands like a pianist's—long-fingered, precise, unsettlingly calm—and he never sat down during their consultations. He moved around Aurelio the way a portrait painter circles the model, cataloguing imperfections with an eye that was simultaneously clinical and aesthetic.

"You see this," Marenghi said, pointing to the faint line near Aurelio's left eye, the one that had appeared three weeks ago and that Aurelio now checked in every reflective surface he passed—the darkened window of a café, the polished steel of an elevator door, the screen of his own phone, which he would unlock only to stare at his own face as though it belonged to someone he was trying to remember. "This is the first sentence of a longer text. And once that text begins, it does not stop."

"What text?"

"The text of what you are not willing to become."

Aurelio laughed. It was a good laugh—controlled, warm, the kind that made waiters bring another glass of wine without being asked. But something behind his sternum went cold, a small door closing in a room he didn't know he had.

He began coming every two weeks. Then every week. Then twice a week.

The regimen accumulated. Peptides he injected into the hollows of his temples. A topical retinoid that made his skin peel for days, raw and translucent as onion skin, before revealing something beneath that was almost—*almost*—what it had been. A course of laser treatments that left his face feeling sunburned and strangely euphoric, as though his skin had been forcibly reminded of its own vitality. Injectable fillers along the nasolabial folds that Marenghi administered with a needle so fine it barely left a pinprick, and for a few days Aurelio would look in the mirror and see himself again—not the wasted version, not the one with the crepe-thin eyelids and the jaw that had begun to soften—but the original. The masterpiece. The twenty-six-year-old who had made a room fall silent simply by entering it.

He knew what he was doing. He was not stupid. He was, in fact, the kind of intelligent that made his stupidity more terrifying, because it wore the mask of reason. He told himself the treatments were maintenance, the way one maintained a painting or a body of work. He told himself he was simply refusing to let talent curdle. He told himself, with the serene dishonesty of a man who has begun lying to himself in earnest, that this was not vanity. This was *rigor*.

Dr. Marenghi, for his part, said nothing that could be construed as a warning. He nodded. He adjusted dosages. He kept meticulous records in a leather-bound notebook. And when Aurelio mentioned, one evening, that the fillers seemed to be lasting less time than before—that his face was metabolizing the work faster, swallowing it, *hungry* for it—the doctor simply tilted his head and said, "Good. It means your body is alive. We want it alive."

The cost was not a problem. Not yet. It was, in fact, barely visible—a line item buried in his account statements between the restaurant at Via della Spiga and the subscription to the literary quarterly he had stopped reading but hadn't canceled. He told himself the spending was proportional to the return. Youth was the only currency that compounded. Beauty invested in beauty. The interest was inevitable.

But there were smaller costs that Marenghi didn't itemize. The supplements that arrived in plain envelopes—NMN capsules, alpha-lipoic acid, a powdered collagen from deep-sea fish that tasted like chalk and regret. The cryotherapy sessions, twenty minutes in a chamber so cold it made his eyes water and his thoughts stop, and for those twenty minutes he was nothing—no wit, no elegance, no curated self—just a body shivering in a white room, and the silence of that was so total it frightened him more than anything else.

He began canceling dinners. Not all at once. First Luca. Then the evening with Giulia and her husband, which he rescheduled three times until Giulia sent a single message—*Where are you?*—and he didn't answer. Then Dario, who called once, twice, a third time, and on the fourth Aurelio picked up and said, "I'm fine," in a voice so carefully modulated that Dario hung up without saying goodbye.

He told himself he was conserving energy. That beauty required solitude the way a flame requires air—intermittently, and on its own terms. He told himself the solitude was deliberate, architectural, a choice. But the truth was simpler and uglier: he couldn't bear to be seen while he was in the middle of being undone.

Because that was the thing he wouldn't say, even to himself. The treatments weren't working. Or rather—they were working in the way a fever works. They produced heat, flushed the skin, made the eyes look bright for a few days, and then the body settled back into whatever temperature it had decided was its own. The line near his eye was still there. His jaw still softened in the late afternoon. His hair, which he now photographed every morning from four angles, was beginning to thin at the temples in a way that no supplement or serum could reverse, because it was not a cosmetic problem. It was a *temporal* one. It was time arriving, with luggage, at his door.

He found himself standing in the pharmacy at midnight, alone, holding a jar of testosterone cream that Marenghi had recommended with the casualness of a man recommending a good wine. The pharmacist—a woman with reading glasses on a chain—looked at him and, for one unguarded moment, looked *through* him. Not at the face. At the man behind it. Aurelio felt that look like a needle between the ribs, and he paid without counting the change and walked home through streets that were empty and beautiful and indifferent to him, and in the dark of his bedroom he stood before the mirror and saw, for the first time, not a man losing his face, but a man losing his grip on the story he had told himself about who he was.

He pressed his palms against the glass. The glass did not press back.

*How much longer*, he thought, and the question was not about the treatments, or the supplements, or the fillers. It was about the whole elaborate architecture of *him*—the salon, the prose, the face, the name that opened doors—and whether any of it was load-bearing, or whether it had always been scaffolding, and he was now standing in an empty room pretending the walls were still there.

Dr. Marenghi would have a protocol for this. Marenghi always had a protocol.

Aurelio picked up the phone.
