## Chapter 10: The Erosion of the Circle

The silence of the palazzo had become a physical presence, a heavy, velvet curtain that muffled the sounds of the Milanese streets outside. For years, this house had been a sanctuary of noise—the rhythmic clinking of crystal, the melodic overlap of three different conversations, the laughter that acted as a soundtrack to Aurelio’s perceived divinity. Now, the echoes felt mocking.

Aurelio stood before the triptych of mirrors in his dressing room, the lighting calibrated to a surgical precision that left no shadow unexamined. He was not looking at himself so much as he was auditing a failing asset. He leaned in, his breath fogging the glass, tracing the perimeter of his jawline. Was it sagging? Or was it merely the light? He spent forty minutes applying a serum that cost more than a laborer’s monthly wage, massaging it into his skin with a desperation that bordered on the violent. 

*It is a war,* he told himself, his inner voice a frantic whisper. *A war of attrition. Every second is a skirmish, every hour a lost territory. I cannot surrender a single millimeter of my face to the gravity of the common man.*

The doorbell rang, a sharp, intrusive sound that made him flinch. He didn't move for several minutes, paralyzed by the sudden anxiety of being perceived. To be seen was to be judged; to be judged was to be found wanting. 

When he finally opened the door, Luca stood there. Luca, who had been his shadow since their university days, whose loyalty was as steady as a heartbeat. He was carrying a bottle of vintage Barolo and wearing a look of profound, quiet concern that Aurelio found utterly repulsive.

"You’ve missed the last three Thursdays, Aurelio," Luca said, stepping inside without waiting for an invitation. He looked around the foyer, noting the dimmed lights and the thin layer of dust on the console table. "The group is starting to ask. Not out of curiosity, but out of worry. You’ve become a ghost in your own house."

Aurelio closed the door with a click that sounded like a gavel. He didn't embrace Luca; he stepped back, instinctively creating a distance that allowed him to scan Luca’s face. He saw it immediately—the deepening of the nasolabial folds, the slight thinning of the hair at the temples. Luca was aging. It was a slow, inevitable collapse, and seeing it in his best friend felt like watching a mirror crack in real-time.

"I’ve been working," Aurelio lied, his voice smooth but brittle. "The new series of essays requires a certain... monastic isolation. I cannot be the center of a salon when my mind is occupied by the architecture of the Baroque."

Luca sighed, a sound of exhaustion that grated on Aurelio’s nerves. "You aren't working, Aurelio. You’re hiding. I saw you last week at the gallery opening—or rather, I saw you for five minutes before you vanished into the restroom to stare at yourself in the mirror. You looked... frantic. Not the usual vanity, but something else. Something hungry."

Aurelio’s expression hardened. The word *hungry* felt too accurate. He was starving for a version of himself that no longer existed, or perhaps one that had only ever existed in his imagination.

"You imagine things, Luca. Your own nostalgia is coloring your perception," Aurelio snapped, turning away to pour two glasses of water, avoiding the wine. He had read in a dubious medical journal that tannins accelerated oxidative stress. 

"Stop it," Luca said, his voice rising. "Stop this performance. We are your friends. We love you—not the image you curate, not the 'God of the Room,' but *you*. But you’ve started treating us like witnesses to a crime. Every time we speak, you look at us as if we are reminders of a disease you’re trying to cure."

Aurelio froze, the glass halfway to his lips. The accusation hit the center of his obsession. He did see them that way. He saw his circle of friends not as companions, but as a countdown clock. Every wrinkle on Luca’s forehead was a warning; every grey hair on Dario’s head was a prophecy. To love them was to accept the fate they shared, and Aurelio found that prospect intolerable.

"Perhaps," Aurelio whispered, his voice trembling with a sudden, cold rage, "I simply have higher standards for my surroundings than you do. I refuse to slide into a comfortable decay. I refuse to be 'distinguished.' Distinguished is just a polite word for old."

"And what is the alternative?" Luca asked softly. "To spend your life in a pharmacy? To treat your skin like a canvas to be bleached? You’re erasing yourself, Aurelio. If you scrub away everything that shows time has passed, there will be nothing left of you but a blank, porcelain mask."

Aurelio lashed out. It wasn't a physical blow, but a verbal one, delivered with the surgical precision of the critic he was. "And look at you, Luca. You’ve accepted it. You’ve surrendered. You wear your mediocrity like a badge of honor, pretending that 'wisdom' compensates for the loss of beauty. It doesn't. In this world, the only wisdom that matters is the power to remain desired."

The silence that followed was different from the silence of the house. It was a vacuum, sucking the air out of the room. Luca looked at him—not with anger, but with a devastating kind of pity.

"I used to admire your ambition," Luca said, setting the wine bottle on the table. "But this isn't ambition. It's a pathology. You’re so afraid of the sunset that you’re closing the curtains on everyone who actually cares about you."

Luca left without another word. The sound of the door closing was final, a punctuation mark at the end of a long, decaying sentence.

Aurelio stood alone in the center of the room. He felt a surge of triumph—he had defended his position, he had remained superior—but it was immediately followed by a hollow, aching void. He walked back to the mirror.

He began to analyze the skin around his eyes again. He imagined the cells dying, the collagen snapping like old rubber bands. He thought of Dr. Marenghi’s promise: *a new protocol, a deeper infusion, a way to freeze the clock.*

He realized then that the salon gatherings had stopped because he had stopped summoning them. He had become the architect of his own isolation. He had traded the warmth of human connection for the cold sterility of a preserved image. 

*They don't understand,* he told himself, leaning closer until his nose touched the glass. *They want me to fall with them. They want the comfort of shared ruin.*

But as he stared into his own pupils, he saw a flicker of something that terrified him. It wasn't a wrinkle or a spot. It was a look of profound loneliness. For a fleeting second, the mask slipped, and he saw a man who was not a god, but a prisoner—a prisoner in a palace of mirrors, guarded by the ghost of his own youth.

He quickly blinked the emotion away. He reached for the phone to call Dr. Marenghi. He needed a stronger dose. He needed something that would numb the fear and sharpen the facade. 

As he dialed, he caught a glimpse of the portrait Dario was painting in the other room—the one he had criticized so harshly. In the dim light, the painting looked honest. It looked human. With a sudden, violent impulse, Aurelio threw a silk cloth over the canvas, hiding it from view.

He could not bear to see the truth. He preferred the beautiful lie, even if he had to inhabit it alone.

The erosion of the circle was complete. The satellites had drifted away, leaving the star to burn in a vacuum, blindingly bright and utterly cold. He lay down on his bed, the expensive linens feeling like a shroud, and waited for the chemicals to take hold, dreaming of a world where time was merely a suggestion and beauty was the only law.
