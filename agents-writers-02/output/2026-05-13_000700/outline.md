# Outline — *Il Ritratto della Follia Dorata*

---

**Chapter 1 — The God of the Room**
Aurelio Vianni, a strikingly handsome and brilliant young man in his late twenties, holds court at a lavish Milan salon. His wit, charm, and beauty make him the gravitational center of an elite circle of friends, artists, and intellectuals who hang on his every word.

**Chapter 2 — A Gallery of Mirrors**
Aurelio surrounds himself with admirers who reflect his magnificence back to him. We see his daily rituals—skincare, exercise, curated conversations—all designed to maintain the pristine image the world has of him and that he has of himself.

**Chapter 3 — The Architecture of Genius**
Aurelio's professional brilliance is revealed: he is a celebrated art critic and essayist whose prose dazzles the literary world. His success feeds his belief that beauty and intellect are intertwined, and that both are his birthright.

**Chapter 4 — The Eternal Feast**
A grand party at Aurelio's palazzo serves as the apex of his social life. Every detail is exquisite, every guest enraptured. Aurelio feels invincible, convinced that time has no claim on him. His closest friend, the painter Dario, raises a toast to "the man who will never age."

**Chapter 5 — The First Line**
One morning, Aurelio notices a faint line near his eye that wasn't there before. He dismisses it, but the seed of dread is planted. He examines his reflection with new, hostile attention.

**Chapter 6 — The Clock in the Café**
During a lunch with his oldest friends, Aurelio realizes that they all look subtly older—thinner hairlines, deeper crow's feet, slower laughter. For the first time, he sees mortality not as an abstraction but as a thief approaching his door.

**Chapter 7 — The Portrait Session**
Dario begins a new portrait of Aurelio. As the painter works, Aurelio cannot stop critiquing every brushstroke that captures a feature he feels is already betraying him. The session ends in a quiet, unprecedented argument between the two friends.

**Chapter 8 — The Museum of Lost Youth**
Aurelio visits an old mentor, Professor Ferrante, now frail and forgotten in a nursing home. The encounter shakes him deeply—this genius, once as admired as Aurelio, is now invisible. Aurelio vows never to let that happen to him.

**Chapter 9 — The Pharmacist's Cabinet**
Aurelio begins a secret regimen of experimental treatments, supplements, and cosmetic procedures. He befriends a dubious dermatologist, Dr. Marenghi, who feeds his obsession with youth. His spending becomes reckless.

**Chapter 10 — The Erosion of the Circle**
Aurelio's friends begin to notice his withdrawal. His best friend, Luca, grows concerned as Aurelio cancels plans, obsesses over his appearance, and lashes out at anyone who mentions age. The salon gatherings grow sparse without him.

**Chapter 11 — The Woman in the Mirror's Edge**
Aurelio meets Celeste, a strikingly unconventional woman who works as a restorer of old paintings. She is fascinated by decay and imperfection, and she unsettles him by telling him that cracks are where the light enters. He is both repelled and magnetized.

**Chapter 12 — The Right Dose of Madness**
Aurelio, increasingly desperate, begins experimenting with stimulants and erratic behavior—staying up for days, wandering the city at night, seeking sensation as proof he is still alive. He starts writing feverish, brilliant, unhinged essays that attract a new, younger audience.

**Chapter 13 — The Burning Salon**
Aurelio hosts a wild, chaotic gathering in his palazzo that devolves into an orgy of excess—drink, arguments, confessions. Old friends are horrified; new admirers are enthralled. It is both the peak of his mania and the moment his old world begins to shatter.

**Chapter 14 — The Fall of the Idol**
A tabloid publishes unflattering photos of Aurelio looking haggard at a late-night event. The public mocks him. His publisher distances himself. Dario, the painter, cuts ties entirely. Aurelio's constructed world collapses in a single, devastating week.

**Chapter 15 — The Cellar of the Self**
Alone in his palazzo, Aurelio descends into a depressive spiral. He stops answering the door, stops looking in mirrors, lets his appearance deteriorate. In the dark, he confronts the void he has always feared—the possibility that his worth was never real.

**Chapter 16 — The Confession on the Rooftop**
Dario, worried despite his anger, finds Aurelio on the rooftop of the palazzo in a state of near-breakdown. They have a raw, brutal conversation where Aurelio admits his terror of irrelevance and his hatred for the man he has become. Dario offers no comfort—only truth.

**Chapter 17 — Celeste's Atelier**
Drawn by the wreckage of his public persona, Celeste finds Aurelio and invites him to her studio. Surrounded by half-restored paintings—beautiful things rescued from damage—Aurelio begins to see imperfection not as death but as transformation.

**Chapter 18 — The Madness That Heals**
Aurelio, still volatile but now channeling his chaos creatively, begins collaborating with Celeste on a radical project: a series of essays and paintings about beauty in decay. His mania softens into purpose. He starts to laugh again—not performatively, but genuinely.

**Chapter 19 — The Ugly, Beautiful Truth**
In a moment of vulnerability, Aurelio confesses to Celeste that he has always been terrified of being loved only for his surface. Celeste reveals her own brokenness—a past marked by loss and self-destruction—and they recognize in each other a shared refusal to be discarded by time. They kiss, not as conquest, but as confession.

**Chapter 20 — The Portrait Completed**
Dario finishes the portrait he started years ago: Aurelio, older, lined, imperfect, and luminous. At the unveiling, Aurelio stands beside Celeste, no longer the god of the room but something more enduring—a man who has passed through vanity, madness, and despair to find something real. The last line mirrors the toast from Chapter Four, now reframed with devastating irony: *"To the man who aged—and lived."*
