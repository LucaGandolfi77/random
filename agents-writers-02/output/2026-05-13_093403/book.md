## Chapter 1: The Curated Canvas

The morning light filtered through the linen curtains in precise, geometric patterns that would have made her high school geometry teacher proud. Lila Chen adjusted the angle of her phone on the tripod, ensuring that the natural illumination caught the gold hardware on her vintage Chanel blazer just so—the way the light pooled in the creases of the fabric like liquid honey, highlighting the meticulous artistry of decades past. 

Her Instagram feed was a museum of moments, each photograph carefully composed to convey lives she'd only glimpsed in fashion magazines and poetry collections. Today's post featured her standing beside the floor-to-ceiling windows of her apartment, the city skyline serving as a perfect backdrop to her carefully selected outfit: a cream silk blouse tucked into wide-leg trousers, accessorized with a single strand of pearls that had belonged to her grandmother. The caption read: "Morning verses and metropolitan dreams ✨ #aesthetic #fashionpoetry #citylights"

She smiled at her reflection in the phone screen, the same practiced smile she'd perfected over two years of building her brand as a fashion blogger-poet hybrid. The likes would begin trickling in within minutes—her followers had grown accustomed to her early morning posts, the ones that suggested she spent her days drifting through galleries and composing sonnets in café corners, rather than eating cereal for dinner while scrolling through dating apps.

But this was the magic of curation, wasn't it? The ability to transform ordinary Tuesday mornings into something extraordinary.

Lila set down her phone and moved to her desk, where a leather-bound journal waited alongside her laptop. The journal was her secret indulgence—a place where she wrote poems that would never see the light of social media, verses that spoke of loneliness disguised as independence, of conversations that existed only in her imagination. Her online persona was all about confidence and creativity, but here, in the privacy of her thoughts, she could admit the truth: sometimes the silence in her apartment felt so loud that she found herself talking to her plants just to hear a voice.

She opened to a fresh page and began to write:

*Silk and shadow, perfectly paired,*
*Every moment designed, prepared,*
*But what lies beneath the curated frame?*
*A heart that whispers another name—*
*Not loneliness, but longing bright,*
*For connection that feels just right.*

The words flowed easily, as they always did when she let her guard down. Poetry had been her solace since college, when she'd first discovered that arranging words in specific patterns could make the chaos of emotions somehow manageable. Fashion had followed naturally—both were forms of self-expression, both allowed her to present versions of herself to the world. Together, they created a language all her own.

Her phone buzzed with notifications. Fifty new likes, twelve comments, three direct messages from brands interested in collaborations. Lila's stomach fluttered with familiar excitement. This was what success felt like, wasn't it? Recognition, validation, proof that her aesthetic sensibilities resonated with others.

Yet as she scrolled through the comments—"obsessed with this look!" "your style is everything goals" "where is that blouse from?"—she couldn't shake the feeling that she was performing rather than living. Each compliment felt like applause for a character she'd created, not recognition of who she truly was.

The real Lila was the one who still got excited about finding vintage pieces at thrift stores, who wrote poems about subway rides and coffee shop conversations, who desperately wanted to share her mayo-stained sandwich with someone who wouldn't judge her for it. The Lila who existed in her posts was polished, put-together, effortlessly chic—everything that the real Lila aspired to be but wasn't quite sure she could sustain.

She closed her journal and pulled up her blog dashboard. Her latest post, a meditation on the relationship between color theory and emotional states, had garnered significant attention from both the fashion and literary communities. Comments ranged from philosophical discussions about synesthesia to requests for outfit details. It was exactly the kind of cross-pollination she'd hoped to achieve when she'd started combining fashion photography with original poetry.

But tonight, standing in front of her closet full of carefully selected pieces, Lila felt the familiar pang of isolation that came with living inside a perfectly composed photograph. Her phone buzzed again—a text from her friend Sarah asking if she wanted to grab drinks after work. Lila stared at the message, then at her reflection in the dark window.

*What would I wear?* she thought, running her fingers along the hangers. *Something that says I'm fun but not desperate, interesting but not intimidating, available but not needy.*

The irony wasn't lost on her. Here she was, surrounded by clothes that cost more than some people's monthly rent, paralyzed by the same insecurities that had plagued her in high school. Except now, instead of worrying about whether boys would notice her, she worried about whether her curated persona would attract the right kind of attention—attention that might lead to something real.

She thought about the poetry readings she'd attended, the indie concerts where she'd scribbled verses in the margins of her notebook, the gallery openings where she'd felt simultaneously invisible and hyper-visible. In those spaces, she'd catch glimpses of herself through other people's eyes—someone who looked like she belonged, who moved through creative circles with the confidence of someone who'd always known her place in the world.

But belonging wasn't the same as connection.

Lila had learned early on that vulnerability was a liability in the digital age. Better to present a life that looked like it belonged in a magazine than to risk appearing too human, too flawed, too desperate for love. So she'd built walls of silk and carefully chosen words, creating an aesthetic fortress that kept the world at bay while making her look approachable.

Her phone buzzed with another notification—a DM from a boutique asking about a collaboration. The timing was perfect; she could feature their pieces in tomorrow's post, style them with her signature blend of vintage and contemporary, write a poem about the intersection of commerce and creativity. It was work that felt meaningful, that allowed her to explore themes she cared about while building her platform.

And yet.

She found herself thinking about mayonnaise—specifically, the way it felt when it got on her fingers during lunch, the creamy texture that somehow made everything taste better. It was such a mundane thing, but lately, mundane had begun to feel revolutionary. In a world where everyone was trying to be extraordinary, maybe being ordinary was the most radical act of all.

Lila opened her journal again, searching for the right words to capture this feeling. How do you write about the desire to be seen when you've spent so long perfecting the art of hiding? How do you explain that sometimes the most authentic version of yourself is the one covered in condiment stains and uncertainty?

She wrote:

*In every perfectly styled frame,*
*There's a girl who forgot to claim*
*Her right to messy, to unsure,*
*To wanting something more.*

The words felt truer than anything she'd posted online in months. Here was the real Lila—not the one in the photographs, but the one who existed between them, who lived in the spaces between likes and comments, who dreamed of sharing sandwiches and secrets with someone who wouldn't flinch at the sight of mayonnaise on her fingers.

Her phone buzzed incessantly now, demanding her attention, reminding her that the world outside her apartment expected content, expected perfection, expected the curated version of herself that had become so successful. But as she sat there with her journal and her thoughts, Lila realized that the most important story she needed to tell wasn't the one playing out on her social media feeds—it was the one happening right here, in the quiet moments when she allowed herself to be imperfect, to be real, to be hopeful.

Tomorrow, she would put on her favorite outfit and pose by the window again, capturing the perfect light and the perfect angle. She would write a poem about architecture and urban beauty, something that would make her followers feel sophisticated and inspired. She would continue to build the life she showed the world, because that life was beautiful and meaningful in its own way.

But tonight, she would write about mayonnaise and vulnerability, about the courage it took to want something as simple and complicated as love. Because somewhere between the perfectly curated canvas and the messy reality of being human, Lila was learning that the most authentic version of herself might be the one she'd been afraid to show.

She closed her journal and picked up her phone, not to post or plan, but to simply sit with herself for a moment longer. Outside, the city continued its rhythm of lights and sounds, full of people living their own curated lives, each carrying their own secret hopes and fears. In her apartment, surrounded by beautiful things and beautiful illusions, Lila felt something shift—a recognition that perhaps the path to connection didn't lie in becoming more perfect, but in becoming more honest about the parts of herself she'd been hiding.

The likes would come tomorrow. Tonight, she had poetry to write and truths to tell, beginning with the simple admission that she was tired of being lonely in a room full of beautiful things.

## "The First Note"

The neon lights of the indie concert flickered like a heartbeat in the dimly lit room. Lila sat on an upturned chair, her fingers tapping rhythmically against her leather-bound journal. She sketched lyrics, her thoughts tumbling out in messy cursive. 

As she filled the pages with her poetic musings, a commotion erupted around her – the crowd was swirling with energy, the music echoing through the space. Lila leaned in closer to capture the raw emotion of the stage, her hazel eyes shining with anticipation.

That was when it happened. As the guitarist plucked the final strumming chord of the song, Lila's elbow bumped into her handbag. The heavy weight shifted, and a cascade of mayonnaise sprayed from the bag, splattering across the pages of her journal.

The room fell silent, the audience's murmurs morphing into a hushed gasp. Lila's cheeks burned, her heart sinking into a pit of despair. She looked up, her eyes searching for an explanation - but there was none.

The artist on stage caught the commotion, and he missed a note, his usual swagger replaced by confusion. Lila's heart ached with humiliation, a feeling so heavy she could barely breathe. She glanced down at her mayonnaise-stained sleeves, the evidence of her stumble, and felt an overwhelming sense of embarrassment.

As the crowd began to disperse, Lila stood up, her legs trembling as she tried to hide her shame. She tugged on her designer jacket, trying to hide the mayo-soaked cuffs, but the gesture only seemed to make matters worse.

"Hey, it's me!" a boy called out, his voice slicing through the chaos. He approached her, his dark hair falling loosely around his face. "I'm Marco, I saw your journal. You're the girl in the video, right? You're like, super talented and everything."

Lila blinked, surprised by his enthusiasm. She hesitated, then managed a small smile. "Thanks. I just love creating poetry, you know?"

"Yeah, I get what you mean." Marco gestured towards her journal, now smudged with mayonnaise. "Walking on eggshells is one way to describe it, honestly."

Lila chuckled, the tension in her shoulders easing. "Maybe I should've kept my elbows up, huh?"

Marco laughed, and in that moment, Lila felt a surge of relief. She looked down at her journal, the contents now a little muddier, but not entirely ruined. She poked at the mayonnaise-drenched page, and a strange sense of liberation washed over her.

"It was an accident, after all," she mused. "Not everything has to be perfect."

Marco nodded, his eyes filled with understanding. "Exactly, Lila. Sometimes, the messes are what make things interesting."

They stood there, exchanging conversation, until the final chord of the concert reverberated through the room, signaling the end of the night's festivities. Lila and Marco exchanged contact information, promising to stay in touch and keep creating.

As Lila made her way out of the venue, she felt a sense of relief wash over her. The spilled mayonnaise on her journal had been a catalyst for change, a turning point in her journey towards self-acceptance and creativity.

She glanced down at her sleeves, remembering Marco's words - "Everything has to be perfect?" She shook her head, her smile returning as she realized that embracing the messiness of life was the true key to finding love.

And perhaps, just perhaps, she had already found it.

## Chapter 3: Fashion as a Second Language

The invitation had arrived as a digital slip of minimalist elegance: cream-colored background, charcoal serif font, and a vague promise of "transgressive aesthetics." For Lila, a gallery opening was not merely a social engagement; it was a performance. In the ecosystem of her curated life, where every Instagram square was a calculated brushstroke of aspiration, an art opening was the ultimate stage. It was here that the boundary between the observer and the observed blurred, and Lila intended to be the latter.

She spent three hours preparing. Her ritual was a form of liturgy—the precise application of a wing of eyeliner that could cut glass, the careful layering of scents that smelled of sandalwood and old libraries, and the selection of an outfit that functioned as a manifesto. She chose a structural, avant-garde piece: a sculptural white blazer with exaggerated shoulders and an asymmetrical hem that defied gravity, paired with sheer stockings and architectural heels that made her feel like a sketch come to life. 

It was a bold choice. It was a loud choice. It was a choice designed to communicate a specific vocabulary: *I am sophisticated, I am daring, and I am entirely in control of my image.*

Yet, as she stepped out of her apartment and into the humid evening air, a flicker of anxiety stirred in her chest. It was a phantom sensation, a residue from the night of the indie concert. She looked down at her sleeves. She had scrubbed the fabric of her favorite blazer from that night, but the memory of the mayonnaise spill remained—a ghostly stain on her psyche. For the first time in her life, the perfection of her attire felt like a costume rather than a skin.

*Am I hiding behind the clothes?* she wondered, her reflection in the hallway mirror staring back with a precision that felt almost clinical. *Or am I finally speaking a language that people understand?*

The gallery was located in a converted warehouse in the arts district, a space of cavernous concrete floors and blinding white walls that seemed to swallow sound. As Lila entered, she was immediately enveloped by the low hum of curated conversation—the rhythmic cadence of "interesting," "provocative," and "subversive." The air tasted of expensive champagne and the metallic tang of fresh paint.

Lila moved through the crowd with a practiced grace, her head held high, her gaze drifting across the abstract canvases. She felt the familiar thrill of being seen. She could feel the eyes of the other attendees scanning her silhouette, measuring the lines of her blazer against the current trends. In this environment, fashion was the primary dialect. To wear the right silhouette was to signal membership in an invisible club; to miscalculate was to be exiled to the periphery.

She navigated the room like a ghost in a gallery of mirrors, nodding politely to acquaintances whose names she barely remembered and smiling at strangers who looked at her as if she were another piece of installation art. For a while, the facade held. She felt the intoxicating power of her curated persona—the "Fashion Poet," the girl who lived in the intersection of verse and velvet.

But as the evening progressed, the silence beneath the noise began to grow.

Lila found herself standing before a massive, monochromatic canvas—a study in greys and blacks that felt like a visual representation of a sigh. Beside her stood a group of peers: fashion students and aspiring curators she had encountered in the digital spheres of her blog. They were dressed in the same "uniform" of intellectual detachment—oversized black linens, chunky silver jewelry, and expressions of curated boredom.

"The negative space is almost aggressive, don't you think?" one of them remarked, a girl named Clara whose aesthetic was as sharp as her tone.

Lila shifted her weight, her heels clicking against the concrete. "I think it reflects a certain kind of loneliness," she replied, her voice soft but steady. "The way the grey bleeds into the black... it feels like a memory fading in real-time."

The group paused. For a heartbeat, Lila felt a spark of genuine connection. She had stepped out of the aesthetic and into the emotional. She had offered a piece of her interior world—the raw, uncurated poetry that lived in the margins of her notebooks.

Then, Clara’s gaze drifted downward.

Lila had forgotten, or perhaps had subconsciously ignored, the detail. In her haste to be bold, she had worn a vintage silk scarf draped artfully over her shoulder, a piece she had salvaged from a thrift store. But on the edge of the fabric, barely visible unless one was looking for a flaw, was a small, yellowish smudge—a remnant of her recent flirtation with "messiness," a tiny, dried speck of mayonnaise that had transferred from her fingers during a frantic midnight snack.

It was a microscopic imperfection. A glitch in the matrix of her perfection.

Clara’s eyebrow arched. A small, knowing smile played on her lips—not a smile of kinship, but of discovery.

"Oh, Lila," Clara murmured, her voice dripping with a faux-concern that felt like a needle. "I love the structural integrity of the blazer. Truly. But... is that a stain on your scarf? It looks almost... organic. Like a condiment?"

The silence that followed was deafening. The other members of the group leaned in, their eyes descending like vultures upon the small, pale spot of fabric. In an instant, the narrative shifted. Lila was no longer the "Avant-Garde Muse"; she was the girl with the condiment on her couture.

Lila felt a hot flush of embarrassment creep up her neck, clashing violently with the cool ivory of her blazer. The structural shoulders that had felt like armor moments ago now felt like a cage. The boldness of her outfit, which she had intended as a signal of strength, suddenly felt like a joke—a punchline that everyone else was in on.

*I am invisible,* she realized with a sudden, stabbing clarity. *They aren't seeing me. They are seeing the smudge.*

The irony was suffocating. She had spent hours crafting a visual identity to ensure she was noticed, yet the only thing that truly drew their attention was the one thing she hadn't intended to show. The "mess" she had begun to embrace in the privacy of her poetry was now an external mark, a brand of failure in a world that worshipped the sterile.

"It's just a bit of... something," Lila stammered, her confidence evaporating. "I didn't notice."

"Of course," Clara replied, though her eyes remained fixed on the stain. "It’s a very... *brave* choice. Very 'deconstructed.' Is it a commentary on the consumption of the domestic within the gallery space?"

The group chuckled—a light, brittle sound. They weren't praising her; they were mocking her under the guise of artistic analysis. They were speaking the language of the gallery, but they were using it to erase her.

Lila stepped back, the space around her suddenly feeling too tight. She looked around the room and saw a sea of perfectly tailored silhouettes, a parade of people who had scrubbed every impurity from their lives. They were like the paintings on the walls: beautiful, static, and entirely hollow.

She felt a sudden, visceral longing for the chaos of the indie concert. She missed the smell of sweat and cheap beer, the vibration of the bass in her marrow, and the honest, unplanned disaster of a mayonnaise-stained notebook. In that moment, the "perfect" version of herself felt like a lie she was tired of telling.

She retreated toward the edge of the room, finding a quiet corner near a ventilation duct. She leaned against the cold wall, her chest heaving. She looked at the small stain on her scarf. For the first time, she didn't feel the urge to hide it.

*Why am I so afraid of a smudge?* she asked herself. *Why does a drop of mayonnaise hold more power over my identity than a thousand lines of poetry?*

She closed her eyes and imagined her life as a canvas. For years, she had used a fine-liner pen, drawing precise, thin lines, ensuring that nothing bled over the edges. She had curated her fashion, her feed, and her friendships to fit a specific aesthetic of "longing and luxury." But the beauty of art—real art—was in the mistake. It was in the drip of paint that ruined the composition but gave it soul. It was in the smudge that proved a human hand had touched the work.

As she stood there, a stranger walked past—a man in a worn leather jacket that looked like it had seen a decade of rain and road trips. He didn't look like he belonged in the gallery; he looked like he had wandered in from a different century. He stopped for a moment, glancing at the art, and then his eyes caught Lila’s.

He didn't look at her blazer. He didn't look at her shoes. And when his gaze swept over the stain on her scarf, he didn't smirk. He simply nodded, a small, enigmatic gesture of acknowledgment, as if he recognized a fellow traveler in the land of the imperfect.

He didn't speak, and he didn't stay. He simply moved on, disappearing back into the crowd of monochrome linen.

Lila stayed in the corner for a long time, watching the party unfold. She realized that her fashion had become a second language, but it was one she was using to keep people at a distance. She was speaking in "couture" to avoid speaking in "truth." She was using the blazer as a shield, hoping that if she looked perfect enough, no one would notice the loneliness that clung to her like a second skin.

When she finally left the gallery, she didn't call a car. She walked home through the city, the architectural heels clicking a rhythmic beat against the pavement. The night air felt cooler now, refreshing.

Once inside her apartment, she didn't immediately strip off the outfit. She stood before the mirror and looked at the stain. It was small, absurd, and entirely out of place. It was a piece of the mundane intruding upon the magnificent.

Slowly, Lila reached for her notebook. She didn't write a poem about the art or the atmosphere of the gallery. Instead, she wrote a single line:

*The most honest thing I wore tonight was a stain.*

She smiled, a genuine, uncurated expression that didn't reach for the camera. She realized that the invisibility she had felt at the gallery wasn't because she wasn't enough, but because she was trying to be something that didn't exist. She was trying to be a painting when she was actually a poem—messy, rhythmic, and full of contradictions.

As she unbuttoned the sculptural blazer, she felt a strange sense of liberation. The "Fashion Poet" was still there, but the "Girl who Likes Mayo" was starting to demand a seat at the table. And for the first time, Lila found herself wondering what would happen if she stopped translating herself for the world and simply started speaking.

Lila si ritrovò davanti a un'enorme tela monocromatica — uno studio di grigi e neri che sembrava la rappresentazione visiva di un sospiro. Accanto a lei stava un gruppo di coetanei: studenti di moda e aspiranti curatori che aveva incontrato nelle sfere digitali del suo blog. Erano vestiti con la stessa "uniforme" di distacco intellettuale — lini neri oversize, gioielli d'argento massicci ed espressioni di noia studiata.

"Lo spazio negativo è quasi aggressivo, non trovate?" osservò una di loro, una ragazza di nome Clara, la cui estetica era affilata quanto il suo tono.

Lila spostò il peso, i tacchi che cliccavano sul cemento. "Penso che rifletta un certo tipo di solitudine," rispose, la voce bassa ma ferma. "Il modo in cui il grigio sfuma nel nero... sembra un ricordo che svanisce in tempo reale."

Il gruppo tacque. Per un battito di ciglia, Lila sentì una scintilla di connessione genuina. Era uscita dall'estetica per entrare nell'emozione. Aveva offerto un pezzo del suo mondo interiore — la poesia cruda e non curata che viveva nei margini dei suoi quaderni.

Poi, lo sguardo di Clara scivolò verso il basso.

Lila aveva dimenticato, o forse aveva inconsciamente ignorato, un dettaglio. Nella fretta di essere audace, aveva indossato una sciarpa di seta vintage drappeggiata artisticamente sulla spalla, un pezzo recuperato in un negozio dell'usato. Ma sul bordo del tessuto, appena visibile a meno che non si cercasse un difetto, c'era una piccola macchia giallastra — un residuo della sua recente flirtazione con la "disordinatezza", una minuscola goccia di maionese trasferitasi dalle dita durante uno spuntino notturno frenetico.

Era un'imperfezione microscopica. Un glitch nella matrice della sua perfezione.

L'arcata sopraccigliare di Clara si sollevò. Un piccolo sorriso consapevole giocò sulle sue labbra — non un sorriso di complicità, ma di scoperta.

"Oh, Lila," mormorò Clara, la voce che gocciolava di una finta preoccupazione che sembrava un ago. "Amo l'integrità strutturale del blazer. Davvero. Ma... quella è una macchia sulla tua sciarpa? Sembra quasi... organica. Come un condimento?"

Il silenzio che seguì fu assordante. Gli altri membri del gruppo si sporsero, i loro occhi che discendevano come avvoltoi sulla piccola, pallida macchia di tessuto. In un istante, la narrazione cambiò. Lila non era più la "Musa d'Avanguardia"; era la ragazza con il condimento sulla couture.

Lila sentì un vampata di imbarazzo salirle lungo il collo, contrastando violentemente con l'avorio freddo del suo blazer. Le spalle strutturate che pochi istanti prima sembravano un'armatura ora sembravano una gabbia. L'audacia del suo outfit, che aveva inteso come segnale di forza, improvvisamente sembrava una barzelletta — una battuta di cui tutti gli altri conoscevano il finale.

*Sono invisibile,* realizzò con una chiarezza improvvisa e lancinante. *Non stanno vedendo me. Stanno vedendo la macchia.*

L'ironia era soffocante. Aveva passato ore a costruire un'identità visiva per assicurarsi di essere notata, eppure l'unica cosa che aveva davvero attirato l'attenzione era l'unica cosa che non aveva intenzione di mostrare. Il "disordine" che aveva iniziato ad abbracciare nella privacy della sua poesia era ora un marchio esterno, un segno di fallimento in un mondo che adorava il sterile.

## Chapter 4: The Poet’s Dilemma

The "Velvet Bean" was the kind of café that smelled perpetually of burnt cinnamon and desperation. It was a sanctuary for the misunderstood, or at least for those who wanted to be seen as misunderstood. The lighting was a dim, amber haze, filtered through hanging ferns and the steam of a dozen espresso machines. For Lila, it was the perfect stage.

She had spent two hours preparing for the open mic night. Her outfit was a calculated study in "effortless intellectualism": a high-waisted charcoal skirt that swept the floor, a cream silk blouse with a pussy-bow tie, and a pair of vintage loafers that pinched her toes but looked exquisite in a photograph. She looked like a Pre-Raphaelite painting reimagined for a modern urban landscape. 

But beneath the silk and the structure, Lila was vibrating. 

In her hand, she clutched a small leather-bound notebook. Its edges were frayed, and if one looked closely—very closely—there was a faint, yellowish smudge on the corner of page twelve. A ghost of the mayonnaise incident from the concert. To anyone else, it was a stain; to Lila, it had become a secret talisman. It was the only thing in her current ensemble that wasn't curated. It was a puncture wound in her perfect facade, and it terrified her.

*Am I too much?* she wondered, glancing at the small crowd gathered around the makeshift stage—a wooden pallet covered in a faded Persian rug. *Or am I not enough?*

She had spent weeks polishing the poem she intended to read. It was a piece about the architecture of longing, using metaphors of scaffolding and glass cathedrals to describe the feeling of waiting for someone who might never arrive. It was lyrical, dense, and meticulously crafted. She had scrubbed every "clumsy" word, replacing them with adjectives that tasted of old libraries and rain-slicked cobblestones. She wanted the audience to feel the weight of her loneliness, but she wanted them to feel it through a filter of elegance.

"Next up," the host announced, a man with a beard that seemed to have its own zip code and a voice like gravel in a blender, "we have Lila."

Lila stepped onto the pallet. The shift in elevation felt like ascending a mountain. The spotlight—a single, flickering bulb—hit her, casting long, dramatic shadows across her face. She felt the sudden, visceral urge to hide. She wanted to shrink into the silk of her blouse, to disappear into the curated image she had built.

She cleared her throat, the sound echoing in the sudden silence of the room.

"This is called 'The Geometry of Absence,'" she began.

Her voice was steady, but her heart was a frantic bird hitting the walls of a cage. She read with a rhythmic precision, her cadence mirroring the heartbeat of a clock. She spoke of *crystalline silences* and *the obsidian weight of an empty chair*. She used words like *evanescent* and *labyrinthine*. She poured her soul into the verses, but she did so through a veil of linguistic ornamentation. She wasn't just telling them she was lonely; she was presenting her loneliness as a piece of fine art, framed in gold leaf and polished to a mirror shine.

As she reached the final stanza—a crescendo of metaphors about the intersection of grief and grace—she looked up. 

She saw a young man in the front row, wearing a black turtleneck and thick-rimmed glasses. He wasn't nodding. He wasn't moved. He was frowning, his head tilted slightly to the side, as if he were trying to solve a particularly irritating math problem.

Lila finished the poem. The silence that followed wasn't the heavy, respectful silence of a captivated audience. It was the awkward, hollow silence of people who weren't quite sure how to react.

A smattering of polite applause broke out, but it felt thin. 

"Thanks, Lila," the host said, stepping back onto the rug. "Anyone have any thoughts? We’re doing a feedback loop tonight."

Usually, people just said "Great job" or "Very moving." But the man in the turtleneck raised his hand.

"I have a question," he said, his voice clinical and devoid of warmth. "Lila, I felt the technical skill in the piece. The vocabulary is impressive. But I have to ask... why the scaffolding? Why the glass cathedrals?"

Lila blinked. "I... I was using them as metaphors for the structure of longing."

The man sighed, a small, condescending sound. "That's the problem. It felt like you were hiding behind the words. You’re overcomplicating the emotion. Loneliness isn't a 'labyrinthine obsidian weight.' Loneliness is just... wanting someone to hold your hand while you eat a sandwich. By dressing the emotion up in such expensive language, you’ve stripped away the honesty. It felt curated. Like a brochure for a feeling, rather than the feeling itself."

The room shifted. A few people nodded. A girl in the back whispered something to her friend, and Lila caught the word "pretentious."

The world seemed to tilt. The amber light of the café suddenly felt oppressive, the smell of cinnamon turning cloying and suffocating. Lila felt a hot flush creep up her neck, clashing violently with the cream color of her blouse. 

She didn't know how to respond. Her instinct was to defend her art, to explain that the complexity was the point—that the beauty of the language was the only way she knew how to make the pain bearable. But as she looked at the man, she realized he had seen right through her. He hadn't seen the "Poet"; he had seen the girl trying too hard to be a poet.

She stepped off the pallet, her loafers clicking sharply against the floor. She didn't look at the host. She didn't look at the audience. She hurried to the corner of the room, sinking into a velvet armchair that smelled of old tobacco.

*Curated.*

The word echoed in her mind, a rhythmic drumming. She looked down at her notebook, at the smudge of mayonnaise on page twelve. For the first time, she didn't see it as a mistake or a secret. She saw it as the only honest thing she had brought into the room. 

The mayonnaise stain was messy. It was accidental. It was absurd. It was, in the words of the man in the turtleneck, "just a sandwich."

She felt a wave of profound vertigo. For years, she had believed that the only way to be loved—or even noticed—was to be an idealized version of herself. She had built a fortress of designer clothes, lyrical poetry, and a polished online persona, believing that the fortress would protect her from the judgment of others. But the fortress had become a prison. She had polished the walls so brightly that no one could see the person living inside.

She watched the rest of the open mic with a detached sense of misery. Another girl got up and read a poem about her dog dying. It was simple. It didn't use words like *evanescent*. It used words like *sad* and *empty* and *quiet*. The audience leaned in. They wept. They connected.

Lila felt a sharp, stabbing jealousy, followed by a crushing realization. She had been treating her emotions like a fashion collection—something to be edited, tailored, and presented for approval. She had been so afraid of being "too much" or "not enough" that she had settled for being a performance.

*Is my art just a costume?* she asked herself.

She opened her notebook to a blank page. She tried to write a line about the feeling of failure, but her hand instinctively reached for a complex metaphor. She wrote: *My heart is a shattered vase of Ming porcelain, the shards reflecting a fragmented sky.*

She stopped. She stared at the sentence. It was beautiful. It was elegant. And it was completely lying.

She took a pen and violently crossed the line out, the ink bleeding through the paper. 

She thought about the mayonnaise. She thought about the way it had looked on her sleeve—clumsy, white, and utterly out of place. It was the most honest moment of her last few months. It was a moment where the curation had failed, and for a split second, she had been visible.

As she left the café, the cool night air hitting her face, Lila felt a strange mixture of devastation and liberation. The man in the turtleneck had stripped her of her armor, leaving her raw and exposed in the middle of a crowded room. She felt naked despite the silk blouse.

She walked toward the subway, her mind racing. She didn't want to go home and post a photo of her "poetic evening" on her blog. She didn't want to find the right filter to make the Velvet Bean look more atmospheric. 

She wanted to know how to be honest. 

But honesty was terrifying. Honesty meant admitting that she was a girl who liked expensive shoes and cheap condiments. It meant admitting that she was desperately lonely, not in a "crystalline silence" kind of way, but in a "crying in the shower because the apartment is too quiet" kind of way.

She stopped at a street vendor and bought a cheap hot dog, the kind that came in a soggy paper tray. She asked for extra mayonnaise. 

As she stood on the sidewalk, the neon lights of the city blurring around her, she watched a dollop of the creamy white sauce drip onto her charcoal skirt. A permanent stain. A flaw in the curation.

Lila looked at the stain and didn't move to wipe it away. She felt a small, tentative spark of something new—not the polished glow of an Instagram filter, but the flickering, uncertain light of a real emotion.

She was a poet who didn't know how to speak. She was a fashionista who was starting to hate her clothes. And she was a lonely girl who realized that the only thing standing between her and the world was the very image she had spent her whole life perfecting.

The dilemma was no longer about whether her poems were too complex. The dilemma was whether she had the courage to be simple.

## Chapter 5 – Mayo as a Muse  

The rain had turned the city’s sidewalks into ribbons of silver, each puddle catching the neon glow of boutique signs and the occasional flash of a passing car. Lila pulled her wool‑blend trench coat tighter around her shoulders, the collar brushing the nape of her neck where a few stray strands of hair had escaped the neat bun she always favored. She walked past the thrift‑store window that displayed a mannequin in a 1970s‑inspired silk dress, its sleeves flaring like the wings of a moth. The image made her smile—a reminder of how she could slip between eras as easily as she slipped between hashtags.

She was late, again. The deadline for her next blog post loomed in her mind like a metronome, ticking louder with each step. “Five more minutes,” she whispered to herself, though the tiny, stubborn voice in her head knew she was already past that limit. She turned the corner onto Rue de la Liberté, where the smell of fresh coffee mingled with the faint, nostalgic scent of old paper that seemed to seep from the cracked façade of a tiny shop she had never noticed before.

The shop’s window was cluttered with an odd assortment: a brass telescope, a stack of vintage postcards, a porcelain teacup with a chipped rim, and, in the very centre, a battered leather‑bound book whose title was barely legible: *Le Recettes de la Maïonnaise d’Antan*. Her breath hitched. She had walked past this shop a hundred times, but never had the curiosity to step inside. Now, the rain had driven her toward its warm interior, and the book called to her as if it held the answer to a question she hadn’t yet formed.

She pushed the door open, a tiny bell chiming above her head. The shop was a sanctuary of forgotten things. Shelves sagged under the weight of yellowed newspapers, tin boxes of buttons, and rows of glass jars filled with dried herbs that whispered of kitchens long closed. An elderly woman with silver hair pulled into a loose bun stood behind a counter, her eyes twinkling behind round spectacles.

“Bonjour, ma chère,” the woman said, her voice soft like the rustle of pages. “Looking for anything in particular?”

Lila glanced at the book again, her fingers itching to touch the worn cover. “I… I saw the mayonnaise book in the window. I’m… curious about it.”

The shopkeeper smiled, a crease forming between her brows. “Ah, the old mayo book. It’s been here longer than most of the other things. It belonged to my great‑grandmother, a chef who believed that a perfect mayonnaise could change a life.”

Lila felt a strange flutter in her chest, as if the notion of a simple condiment could indeed pivot the axis of her existence. “May I see it?”

The woman reached beneath the counter and produced a small wooden box, its surface smoothed by years of handling. She lifted the lid, revealing the leather tome nestled among a scattering of wilted lavender buds. Lila’s fingertips brushed the cover, and a faint, buttery scent rose—briny, tangy, and oddly comforting.

She opened the book to the first page, where a handwritten note in elegant cursive read: *“Pour la vie, pour l’amour, pour l’art.”* Below it, a list of ingredients stretched across the page in ink that had faded but not disappeared: egg yolks, Dijon mustard, white wine vinegar, olive oil, a pinch of salt, and—most intriguingly—a splash of lemon zest.

Lila’s mind raced. She could almost hear the soft pop of a bottle being uncorked, the hiss of oil meeting yolk, the gentle stir of a whisk. She imagined the creaminess spreading across a slice of sourdough, the way the tang of the lemon would cut through the richness, leaving a clean finish. She thought of her own life—layers of polished images and carefully chosen words, each one a glossy veneer that hid the mess beneath. Could this simple sauce be a metaphor for something more? Could she, for once, let the mess in?

She turned the page. The next entry was a poem, ink smudged by years of handling: 

*In the bowl of silence,  
Eggs whisper to the wind;  
Mustard’s fire ignites the night,  
Oil drifts like a lover’s sigh.  
Lemon’s kiss—sharp, bright—  
A promise kept, a heart alight.*

Lila’s eyes widened. The poem resonated with a part of her she rarely let surface. The imagery of the oil drifting like a lover’s sigh echoed the way she drifted through cafés, spilling her thoughts onto napkins, hoping someone would catch the drift. The lemon’s kiss—sharp, bright—felt like the flash of a camera lens, the momentary sting of a compliment that left her both exhilarated and wary.

She closed the book gently, feeling the weight of its stories settle in her hands. The shopkeeper watched her, an amused glint in her eye. “Your grandmother used to say that mayonnaise was the poetry of the kitchen. It takes patience, balance, and a willingness to let the ingredients surrender to each other.”

Lila laughed, a sound that startled her. “I’m a poet, but I’ve never thought of a sauce as a poem.”

The woman’s smile deepened. “Then perhaps it’s time you write a poem about mayonnaise.”

Outside, the rain had softened to a mist, the city’s hum now a muted backdrop. Lila slipped the book under her arm, thanked the shopkeeper, and stepped back onto the street. The world seemed a little brighter, as if the clouds had been parting just enough to let a sliver of sunlight touch the cobblestones.

She trudged home, the book’s pages rustling against her thigh. The apartment she shared with herself—walls plastered with mood boards, a writer’s desk cluttered with sketchbooks, a small balcony overlooking a graffiti‑sprayed alley—was a sanctuary of curated chaos. She dropped the book onto the coffee table, its weight echoing through the quiet space. The rain’s patter against the windows was now a soothing rhythm, matching the beat of her heart.

She set a pot of water to boil on the stove, the metallic clink of the kettle a metronome for her thoughts. She pulled a bowl from the cupboard, placed a whisk beside it, and opened the mayonnaise book to the first recipe. The ingredients were simple, yet the instructions were written with a reverence that made her feel as if she were about to perform a ritual.

She cracked two eggs, their yolks bright as sunrise, and set them in the bowl. She added a spoonful of Dijon, the mustard’s amber hue swirling like a sunrise over a foggy city. A splash of white wine vinegar followed, the acidic tang punching through the richness, reminding her of the first time she’d felt a genuine connection—a fleeting moment of true intimacy at a concert, when the vocalist’s voice cut through the thrum of the crowd and made her feel seen.

She began to whisk, slow at first, the motion steady and deliberate. The mixture thickened, the yolk and mustard binding together, like the first lines of a poem finding their rhythm. She drizzled in olive oil, a thin stream at first, then a steady cascade, the oil folding into the mixture, each droplet a note in a silent symphony. The bowl turned from a chaotic swirl of colors to a smooth, ivory emulsion, the texture glossy and inviting.

She added a pinch of salt, the grainy crystals reminiscent of the glitter that sometimes fell from the ceiling of a club when the DJ dropped a bass drop—a reminder of the places where she felt alive yet alone. Finally, she squeezed a wedge of lemon, the zest releasing a bright perfume that cut through the richness, a sudden flash of clarity in a sea of monotony.

She tasted a spoonful, the creamy tang exploding on her tongue. The flavor was complex yet harmonious, each element distinct yet inseparable. She laughed again, this time softer, more genuine. The sauce was perfect, an embodiment of balance—exactly what she had been striving for in her life, but had never found in her carefully curated feeds.

She set the bowl aside and opened a fresh page in her notebook, the one that had been splattered with mayonnaise during a previous concert. The paper was still damp, the ink a little smeared, but it felt right. She let the pen glide, the ink flowing like the oil she’d just whisked.

*In the kitchen’s hush, I stir—*  
*Eggs and mustard, a union pure.*  
*Oil drifts, a lover’s sigh,*  
*Lemon’s kiss, a bright surprise.*  

*I taste the tang, the sweet surrender,*  
*Finding balance, the heart’s true mender.*  

She paused, the words hanging between the lines, and thought of the countless times she’d tried to balance her public persona with her private yearning. She’d built walls of sequins and silk, each layer a defense against the world’s gaze. Yet here, in the quiet of her kitchen, she felt a vulnerability that was not weakness but openness—a willingness to let flavors mix, to let feelings merge, to accept the messiness that came with authenticity.

She wrote on, the poem evolving into something larger, something that stretched beyond the kitchen:

*The world is a runway, lights ablaze,*  
*Models strut, their faces a glassy glaze.*  
*But beneath the gloss, a secret spreads,*  
*A sauce of truth, where fear is shed.*  

*Mayonnaise, humble, unadorned, yet grand,*  
*It binds the broken, the stray, the bland.*  
*And in its swirl, I find my voice,*  
*A chorus of the quiet, a daring choice.*  

She read the lines aloud, the words reverberating off the bare walls, filling the space with a resonance that made the apartment feel less like a stage and more like a sanctuary. She imagined the poem being posted on her blog, the caption a simple photograph of a jar of mayonnaise beside a handwritten note. She imagined the comments: “So poetic!” “Never thought of mayo like that!” “You’re a genius!” And perhaps, she thought, someone would read between the lines and see the woman behind the glossy images, the one who loved concerts, poetry, and the messy beauty of a perfect sauce.

She placed the finished mayonnaise in a glass jar, sealing it with a ribbon of twine. The jar glistened under the soft glow of the lamp, its contents a creamy, golden promise. She set it on the windowsill, where the rain had ceased, leaving a fresh, clean scent in the air.

Later that evening, she sat at her desk, the laptop open to her blog’s drafting page. She typed the title: *Mayo as a Muse: Finding Poetry in the Ordinary*. She added a photo of the jar, the label handwritten in cursive: *Le Recettes de la Maïonnaise d’Antan*. She wrote a short introduction, explaining how a rainy stroll led her to a vintage recipe book, how the act of making mayonnaise became a meditation, and how the process mirrored her own search for balance.

She paused, hands hovering over the keyboard. Part of her wanted to keep this moment private, a secret tucked away in the pages of a journal. Yet another part—a louder, braver voice—urged her to share it, to let the world see the raw, unfiltered version of herself that had been hidden behind curated posts and filtered images.

She hit “Publish” and watched the page go live. A notification pinged almost immediately: a comment from a user named *Luna_Starlight*: *“Your mayo poem made me cry. I’ve always felt like a mess, but reading this… it’s beautiful. Thank you for sharing your truth.”* Lila felt a tremor in her chest, a mixture of relief and exhilaration. She replied, *“Thank you, Luna. May we all find the courage to whisk our own truths into something delicious.”*

She scrolled through the comments, each one a small affirmation, a connection forged through the simple act of sharing a recipe and a poem. In the back of her mind, she imagined a future where the lines between fashion, poetry, and food blurred, where she could wear a dress inspired by the swirl of mayonnaise, where she could read her verses at a concert while the crowd cheered for the rhythm of her voice, not the beat of the bass.

She leaned back, eyes flicking to the jar of mayo sitting beside her laptop. The liquid gold seemed to pulse with a quiet confidence. She realized that the absurdity she had once tried to hide—her love for mayonnaise, her obsession with the tactile mess of a spoonful of sauce—was not a flaw but a facet of herself that made her uniquely vibrant.

The night deepened, the city lights flickering like distant constellations. Lila stood, stretching her arms above her head, feeling the ache in her muscles from the evening’s emotional work. She walked to the balcony, the cool night air brushing her cheeks. The rain had left the streets glistening, each puddle reflecting the neon signs in a kaleidoscope of colors.

She thought of the next concert she would attend, the next poem she would write, the next outfit she would design. She imagined a dress with a pattern reminiscent of the creamy swirls of mayonnaise, a runway that smelled faintly of lemon zest and olive oil, a crowd that cheered not for the flash of couture but for the authenticity of a woman unafraid to embrace her quirks.

She took a deep breath, inhaling the scent of the city mixed with the lingering aroma of her kitchen experiment. In that moment, Lila felt a quiet certainty settle into her bones: the world could be as messy and beautiful as a jar of mayo, and she was ready to dive in, spoon in hand, heart open, ready to stir the next chapter of her life.

She turned back to her apartment, the lights inside glowing warmly, inviting her in. The notebook sat on the desk, its pages waiting to be filled. She smiled, feeling the first genuine, unfiltered smile in a long time. The quiet hum of the city outside seemed to echo the rhythm of her own heartbeat, a steady, hopeful cadence that promised that, perhaps, love—whether it came in the form of a partner, a poem, or a perfect sauce—was just around the corner, waiting to be discovered in the most unexpected of places.

## Chapter 6: "The Date That Fizzled"  

The first time Lila saw Marco, she thought he might be a character from a fashion editorial—petite, impeccably dressed in a charcoal pinstripe blazer that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it, his hair styled into a sleek undercut that betrayed no trace of effort. She’d matched his energy with a cobalt-blue dress she’d borrowed from a friend’s closet, its sequined hem catching the café’s pendant lights like scattered stars. The date was supposed to be effortless. A trial run of her curated universe, a voice in her head whispered, as she adjusted her pearl earrings and forced a smile.  

But effortless was a word she’d learned to distrust.  

The café, *Bistrot Lumen*, was a speakeasy of minimalist Scandinavian design, its walls clad in bleached oak and its menu printed on parchment. Lila had chosen it because it felt “Instagrammable,” a place where her aesthetic could shine. Marco had agreed without hesitation, his laugh sharp and warm as he gestured to an empty table. “You’re the one with the blue dress, right?” he’d said, his eyes lingering on the fabric’s iridescence. “It’s… bold.”  

Bold was a compliment she’d worn like a second skin since childhood. At 24, Lila had mastered the art of appearing effortlessly chic, a skill honed through years of curating her Instagram feed and poetry blog. Every outfit, every poem, every public appearance was a calculated statement: *I am polished, I am intentional, I am desirable.*  

But Marco’s boldness was not the same as her own.  

They talked at first about avant-garde designers, about the way tailoring could mimic the human form’s imperfections. Lila lit up, recounting a story about a failed photoshoot where her model’s dress had snagged on a door handle, leaving a threadbare tear that became the centerpiece of a viral post. Marco nodded, intrigued. “You turn accidents into art,” he said. “That’s rare.”  

She leaned in, her pulse steady. This was it. This was the validation she’d been craving since the poetry café in Chapter 4, since the gallery opening in Chapter 3. For once, someone seemed to see her not through the lens of her online persona but as a person.  

Until he didn’t.  

It happened over mayo.  

The conversation had drifted to sandwiches—Lila had mentioned her favorite, a homemade mayo-laced avocado on sourdough, a recipe she’d scribbled in her journal after discovering a vintage mayonnaise book in an antique shop (Chapter 5). Marco raised an eyebrow. “Mayo? Really?”  

She froze. “It’s not just mayo. It’s… complex. The way it transforms ordinary bread into something almost sacred.”  

He laughed, but it was a sharp sound, like a blade. “Sacred? You’re making me hungry.” He tilted his head, studying her. “You’re kinda weird, Lila.”  

The words hung in the air, cooler than the room. Lila’s fingers twitched against her lap. *Weird*. That was the word that had haunted her since childhood, a label she’d learned to cloak in sequins and sonnets. She’d spent years curating a version of herself that repelled the word, but here it was, thrown back at her like a boomerang.  

“I’m not weird,” she said too quickly, her voice higher than intended.  

Marco didn’t respond. Instead, he pushed a sandwich plate toward her, its contents a plain turkey and provolone. “Here. Eat.”  

She stared at the mayo-stained napkin she’d brought, the one she’d used to wipe her hands after scribbling lyrics at the indie concert in Chapter 2. The mayonnaise had stained it golden, a badge of her messiness. She’d kept it as a talisman. Now, it felt like a curse.  

“I—I don’t think you understand,” she said, her voice trembling. “It’s not just mayo. It’s part of who I am.”  

Marco shrugged. “Maybe it is. But you’re here to date me, not my lunch special.”  

The insult wasn’t literal, but it stung. Lila’s mind raced. *Who am I if not my quirks?* She’d built her identity around curation—fashion, poetry, even the way she filtered her Instagram posts to exclude anything “messy.” Here, in this moment, her defenses had crumbled.  

She wondered if Marco had ever been told she was “too much.” If he’d grown up in a world where uniqueness was a liability. Or if he’d simply never encountered someone who carried their oddness like a shield and a sword.  

The café’s silence pressed in. Lila’s throat burned. She wanted to lash out, to explain that her mayo wasn’t a flaw but a language—a way she connected to the world, to memories of her grandmother’s salads, to the way the tangy spread made her feel alive. But the words died on her lips.  

Instead, she reached for her phone.  

Her fingers hovered over the screen, tempted to text Marco a photo of her mayo journal (a habit she’d started in Chapter 9, though she hadn’t shared it yet). But she didn’t. She couldn’t. The act of showing him would have been a surrender, and surrender felt like admitting defeat.  

When she finally looked up, Marco was staring at her, his expression unreadable. “You okay?”  

She nodded, though her heart was a staccato of panic. “Yes.”  

The date ended that night with Lila walking home through the rain, her blue dress now a soggy shroud. She didn’t text Marco. She didn’t post about it. She didn’t even write about it in her journal—not yet. Instead, she sat on her fire escape, the mayo-stained napkin tucked into her bag, and let the storm mirror her internal tempest.  

The next morning, she opened her poetry blog.  

Under the hashtag #FashionForward, she published a new poem:  

*“I wore my dress like a second skin,*  
*its sequins catching light I didn’t need.*  
*But when he said “mayo,” I crumbled—*  
*a girl who feared the word “weird” too much to speak.*  
*Now I carry it like a shield, a secret,*  
*a jar of tang in a world that hates the fret.*  
*But maybe next time, I’ll bring the jar.*”*  

The post went viral.  

Not because of the poem’s brilliance, but because readers saw themselves in it. A teenager who hid their anxiety under a perfectly tied bow. A man who obsessed over vintage record players but was terrified of being judged for it. Lila didn’t expect this. She’d written the poem as a catharsis, not a manifesto. But in the chaos of the date, she’d discovered something vital: her quirks weren’t flaws. They were the fissures through which light could seep.  

That night, she dreamt of mayonnaise.  

It was a dream of abundance— jars overflowing, the spread dripping down her fingers as she shared it with strangers. In the dream, no one dismissed her. They asked for recipes. They laughed at her jokes. She woke up with a smile, the taste of mayo lingering on her tongue.  

The next day, she posted a new photo to Instagram: herself in her blue dress, holding a jar of homemade mayo, her face half-lit by the sun. The caption read simply: *“Some loves start with a jar.”*  

And for the first time in months, Lila didn’t delete it.

## Chapter 7: Concert Chaos

The festival was a living thing, a beast of sound and color that inhaled the dusk and exhaled a neon glow. Lila stood at its pulsating heart, a curated pixel in a swirling digital dreamscape. Her dress, a relic of 1960s optimism in buttercup yellow, was a conscious choice—a sunbeam against the gathering indigo, a direct challenge to the encroaching night. It was a statement, like everything she wore, a sentence in the silent language of fabric and form. Tonight, she was not just attending; she was performing her own attendance, for an audience of followers who would see only the glossy, filtered result.

Her hands, however, told a different story. They were faintly, stubbornly tacky. Earlier, in a moment of nervous ritual, she had dipped her fingers into the tiny jar of artisanal mayonnaise she carried like a talisman. The cool, viscous cream was a grounding shock, a sensory anchor. Now, the evidence of that private sacrament was drying on her skin, a faint, opalescent sheen that caught the festival lights like some strange, unidentifiable sap.

The band was a wall of reverb and earnest yelps. Lila swayed, eyes closed, trying to dissolve into the music, to let the poetry of the chords overwrite the anxious poetry of her own thoughts. She was composing a new verse in her head, something about distortion and desire, when the world tilted.

A sharp yank at her hem. A sound like tearing taffeta, sharp and impolite in the roar of the crowd. Her dress, her sunbeam, had caught on the jagged edge of a folding chair left askew by a departing group. For a heart-stopping second, she was rooted, a botanical specimen pinned to a corkboard. Then the fabric gave, a long, wicked rip from just above the knee to the very seam of her confidence.

A hot wave of shame, hotter than the press of bodies, flooded her. She stumbled, her hands flying out to catch herself—those tacky, betraying hands—and made contact with the cool, damp grass. The laughter started as a murmur, then crystallized.

“Nice look,” a voice sneered, close enough to smell the beer and cheap glitter on its breath. A girl with hair like a shaved kaleidoscope and a leather vest covered in questionable patches loomed over her. “Did you fall into a jar of… is that mayo? Oh my god, it *is* mayo. You’re, like, *literally* a walking condiment.”

The word *literally* hung in the air, a gavel strike. The group clustered around the girl—a flock of sharp angles and louder laughs—echoed the sentiment, their cackles weaving into the guitar feedback. Lila looked down. Her hands were smudged with grass and the unmistakable, ghostly residue of her muse. Her dress was torn, a gaping mouth of white lining against the yellow silk. She was not a sunbeam. She was a spill. A stain. A joke with a condiment packet.

The music seemed to fade to a distant, underwater throb. The vibrant festival was now a courtroom, and she was on trial for being too much, or not enough, or simply *wrong*. The curated canvas of her life—the perfect Instagram squares, the poetic Instagram captions, the meticulously planned outfits—shattered in the mud and mayonnaise. This was the uncurated self, raw and ridiculous and publicly humiliated.

She scrambled up, ignoring the offered hand from a kind-eyed stranger (a hand she couldn’t bear to take, not with these hands), and fled. She pushed through the swaying crowd, a ghost in a torn dress, her satchel thumping against her hip. She didn’t stop until she was outside the main floodlights, leaning against the rough bark of a tree, gasping as if she’d run for miles.

The isolation was instantaneous and absolute. It was a physical force, a vacuum where the music and laughter used to be. She was alone, truly alone, in a field of thousands. The tear in her dress felt like a wound, a literal opening through which her carefully guarded loneliness was now spilling, visible for anyone to see. She had wanted to be seen—for her poetry, for her style, for the intricate, beautiful soul she was certain lived beneath the surface. But this was not the seeing she had craved. This was being *exposed*. They hadn’t seen the soul; they’d seen the mess. The mayo. The tear. The frantic, failing attempt at grace.

Her phone was a dead weight in her bag. No posts from here. No poetic caption about the transcendent power of live music. What could she say? “Current vibe: public humiliation with a side of condiment.” The thought of crafting that into something palatable, something *on-brand*, made her stomach twist. Her blog, her sanctuary, her second language, suddenly felt like a museum of lies. How could she write about aesthetic harmony when her own seams were splitting?

She thought of the poetry open mic, where they’d called her emotions “overcomplicated.” They were right, she realized with a fresh wave of despair. Her feelings were a tangled knot—love and shame, pride and vulnerability, all mixed up like oil and egg yolk and vinegar. She wanted to be loved for the whole complicated recipe, but what if the world only ever wanted the glossy, emulsified final product? What if the moment they saw the separate ingredients—the mayo-stained fingers, the ripped dress, the desperate need—they turned away?

A tear finally escaped, tracing a hot path down her cheek. It had nothing to do with the torn fabric and everything to do with the tearing inside. She was back in Chapter 1, in the curated canvas, but now she could see the cracks in the paint, the frayed edges of the canvas itself. The persona was a fragile shell, and the concert chaos had cracked it wide open, leaving her exposed not just to the mocking groupie, but to her own terrifying, uncurated truth: that she was lonely, and that her quirks—the mayo, the poetry, the relentless curation—were not bridges to connection, but walls she’d built so high she’d forgotten how to let anyone climb over.

The music from the stage swelled to a crescendo, a triumphant, communal sound. Lila pressed her forehead against the tree’s rough bark, closing her eyes. In the darkness behind her lids, she saw not the stage lights, but the blank, white page of her journal. The secret one. The one where she wrote poems about mayonnaise and the quiet dignity of a perfectly balanced vinaigrette. That journal didn’t judge. It didn’t laugh. It just held the mess, and in doing so, made it feel sacred.

She stayed there until the set ended, until the crowd’s roar moved on to the next stage, leaving her in a sudden, echoing quiet. The first stars were coming out, sharp and indifferent. She wiped her cheeks with the back of her hand, leaving a faint, greasy streak. She was a mess. But she was her mess.

Slowly, she pushed off the tree. The walk back to the main path was a gauntlet of imagined glances and stifled giggles. But she held her head high, or as high as she could with a torn dress and tacky hands. She was not performing for them anymore. She was just Lila, alone in the chaos, and for the first time all night, that felt like a beginning, not an ending. The dress could be mended. The hands could be washed. But the crack in the curated facade? That, she sensed, was the first true thing she’d felt all evening.

## Chapter 8 – The Unspoken Rule  

The city hummed beneath Lila’s feet like a low‑frequency synth, a steady pulse that matched the rhythm of her own restless heart. She walked the familiar stretch of Via della Moda, the storefronts flashing neon gloss and vintage charm in equal measure, each window a curated tableau of the lives she both admired and tried to emulate. The air was scented with espresso, fresh rain on cobblestones, and—almost imperceptibly—by the time she passed the small boutique that sold artisanal condiments, a faint tang of oil and lemon zest that reminded her of the half‑finished mayo recipe she’d been tinkering with the night before.

Lila’s thoughts were a tangled skein of fabric and verse, each strand pulling at the other. She’d just left a brunch with her friend Giulia, who had introduced her to a new boutique that sold hand‑dyed silk scarves. Giulia had been animated, laughing as she described how the scarves “spoke in colors that no one else could hear.” Lila smiled politely, but inside a small, stubborn knot tightened—another reminder that every time she tried to let someone see the messier parts of herself, the world seemed to tilt toward a curated version of her.

She paused outside the doorway of *Cucina di Seta*, a cramped bistro that doubled as an impromptu gallery for up‑and‑coming artists. The chalkboard outside announced a poetry night at 8 p.m., and a tiny stack of flyers lay on the counter, each one hand‑drawn with ink splatters that looked suspiciously like the mayo stains that had become her inadvertent signature. Lila’s gaze lingered on a particular flyer: a sketch of a woman in a sleek black dress, a single white glove, and a jar of mayonnaise perched like a crown on her head. The caption read, *“When the Dress Meets the Dip.”* She felt a flicker of amusement, then a pang of embarrassment. The joke was still fresh in the minds of the people she’d met at the festival, the groupie who had sneered at her sticky fingertips, the date who’d dismissed her “obsession” as a gimmick. Each memory was a thread pulling her back into the same pattern—showing the polished self, then retreating when the other side saw the stains.

A sudden rustle of a newspaper caught her attention. A headline blared: *“The New Rules of Dating: Why Authenticity Is Overrated.”* Lila snorted, half‑amused, half‑despairing. The article argued that in an age of endless choice, people lean toward partners who fit an idealized image—a seamless blend of looks, hobbies, and social media aesthetics. The piece quoted a psychologist who said, *“We subconsciously filter out anyone who threatens the narrative we’ve built around ourselves.”* Lila felt the words hammering against the glass pane of her own self‑construction. She had built a narrative—designer shoes, perfectly styled Instagram grids, poetry that glittered with metaphors but never bled. She had filtered out anyone who didn’t match that story, anyone who might see the mayo‑stained sleeves, the clumsy laughter, the raw, unpolished verses that lived in her notebook.

She slipped the flyer into her bag and continued down the street, the city’s rhythm pulling her forward. The sidewalk café at the corner was full of people hunched over laptops, their screens awash with filtered images of brunches and travel. Lila imagined herself among them, a figure composed of crisp lines and glossy surfaces, yet she could not shake the feeling that something essential was missing—a crack in the veneer where real life seeped through.

She took a seat at a small table by the window, ordered a cappuccino, and opened her notebook. The pages were a mosaic of half‑finished poems, doodles of dresses, and the occasional splatter of mayonnaise that had somehow survived the cleaning attempts of the previous day. She inhaled the smell of coffee, letting it warm the cold corners of her mind. Her pen hovered above the blank space, waiting for the words to come.

*“I dress in silk, I sing in May,
My heart a jar, my love—display…*” she scribbled, the rhyme forced but familiar. She paused, feeling the weight of each syllable, each image she painted for the world. The poem felt like a costume, something she wore to protect herself from the rawness inside. She crumpled the page and tossed it into the trash.

Her phone buzzed. A notification from Instagram: *“Lila, your latest post has hit 2,500 likes! 🎉”* She glanced at the photo—a black‑and‑white shot of her in a vintage coat, the collar turned up, a single jar of mayo perched on a marble countertop like a prop. The caption read, *“When life gets messy, dress it up.”* A flood of comments praised the aesthetic, complimented her style, and asked about the mayo—*“Love the vibe! Is that real mayo? 😍”* The digital applause was intoxicating, yet each compliment felt like a thin layer of frosting over a cake that was still half‑baked.

She stared at the screen, feeling the familiar tug of two selves: the Lila who curated her feed, the Lila who whispered verses into the night, the Lila who wanted to be seen without the gloss. A quiet voice, softer than the city’s roar, rose from the depths of her chest.

*“Why do I keep hiding the parts that make me… me?”*

The question echoed, reverberating against the walls of her mind. She remembered the first time she had realized the pattern—at the gallery opening, when the crowd’s eyes lingered on her mayo‑stained sleeves instead of the avant‑garde sculpture she’d been so excited to discuss. She remembered the barista at the café who’d rolled his eyes when she recited a poem about mustard and heartbreak. She remembered Marco’s initial curiosity, his smile when he heard about her mayo journal, and the way his eyes had dimmed when she showed him the full, unfiltered version of herself.

She realized then that the rule she had been playing by was not written in any dating manual; it was an unspoken injunction she had internalized: *“Only present the parts that fit the aesthetic. The rest is too messy, too ordinary, too vulnerable.”* It was a rule that protected her from rejection, but also from connection. It was a rule that kept her in a glass cage, beautiful to look at, but empty inside.

Lila closed her notebook and set the pen down with a decisive click. She pulled the flyer from her bag and unfolded it, the sketch of the mayo‑crowned woman staring back at her. In that moment, she felt a sudden, fierce resolve. The unspoken rule had been a lie—a self‑imposed barrier that turned love into a performance.

She opened a new page, the paper smooth and waiting. This time, she would write without the constraints of fashion, without the pressure of a perfect Instagram aesthetic, without the fear of being judged for loving mayonnaise. She would write the truth, the messy truth, and see if anyone could still love the woman behind the curated canvas.

*“I am not a dress, nor a jar,
But a storm that sings in a bar,
I love the spill, the stain, the slip,
The way my heart forgets to zip.
If you can taste the brine and sweet,
And hear the rhythm of my beat,
Then stay, for I’m a work in progress,
A canvas that refuses to be flawless.”*

She read the lines aloud, her voice trembling at first, then gaining strength. The words felt like a ripple through the glass walls she had built. The café’s ambient chatter faded, and for a moment, only the soft hiss of the espresso machine and her own breath filled the space.

She took a photo of the page—not the polished, edited version she would normally post, but the raw, handwritten sheet with ink smudges and a stray mayo droplet that had managed to land on the margin. She typed a caption that was simple, almost blunt: *“No filters. No façades. Just me, a pen, and a jar of mayo.”* She hesitated, fingers hovering over the “share” button. The internal alarm of potential judgment sounded, but this time she turned it into a drumbeat—steady, purposeful.

She pressed “share.”

The likes began to trickle in, the comments softer, more personal. *“Your honesty is refreshing.”* *“The mayo metaphor is perfect—life’s messes make us who we are.”* *“I’ve always felt the same about my own quirks.”* A few emojis, a few heart symbols, but more importantly, a feeling that something inside her was shifting.

She sat back, sipping her cappuccino, letting the warm foam linger on her lips. The city outside continued its symphony of horns, footsteps, distant music. Inside, Lila felt a subtle crack in the polished surface—a fissure where light could now enter.

She thought of Marco, of the brief spark they had shared, of the way he had listened when she finally let down a piece of her armor. She wondered if he could see this new version of her, the one that didn’t hide behind a mayo‑crowned illustration but embraced the whole jar, the spill, the scent, the taste.

A text buzzed on her phone: *“Hey Lila, just saw your post. It’s… real. I’d love to hear more about the mayo journal sometime. Coffee?”* It was from Marco. Her heart leapt, a mixture of hope and fear.

She typed back: *“Coffee sounds perfect. I have a new poem I’ve been working on.”* She pressed send, feeling the same flutter she always felt before a concert—anticipation mingled with terror.

The night fell, the streetlights casting amber halos on wet pavement. Lila closed her notebook, tucked it into her bag, and left the café. She walked toward the river, where the water reflected the city’s neon scars, and for the first time in months, she didn’t think about how she looked, what she wore, or how many likes her post would garner. She thought about the unspoken rule she had finally broken, about the risk of being seen in all her unscripted glory.

She imagined a future where her partner would not be a mirror of her curated image, but a co‑author of the messy, beautiful manuscript of her life. A future where the mayonnaise jar sat on the kitchen counter, not as a prop, but as a reminder that flavor comes from the unexpected blend of ingredients—some sweet, some sour, some inexplicably strange.

As she crossed the bridge, the wind tugged at her hair, and she felt a smile bloom, genuine and unfiltered. The city’s symphony swelled around her, a chorus of honks, distant laughter, and the faint echo of a guitar from a nearby street performer. In that moment, Lila understood that the rule she had been living by was not a law of love; it was a self‑imposed limitation. She had chosen to rewrite it, one honest line at a time.

She turned back toward the bustling streets, her steps light, her heart a little less guarded. The night was still young, the world still full of curated images, but somewhere beneath the glossy surface, a raw, unfiltered voice had finally found its echo. And perhaps, just perhaps, someone else would hear it, too.

## Chapter 9: "The Mayonnaise Journal"  

The first entry in the journal was written in a faint, looping script that seemed to tremble with the weight of her unspoken thoughts. Lila sat cross-legged on the floor of her cramped apartment, the pages of a weathered notebook splayed open before her. The scent of lavender from her diffuser mingled with the faint tang of something else—something she couldn’t quite place, but which felt oddly comforting. She had always been drawn to the tactile, the sensory, the way words could spill like ink from a pen, but now, the act of writing felt different. Not just a way to document her life, but a lifeline.  

The journal was a hybrid creation, a fusion of poetry and recipe, a practice she had begun after that chaotic concert in Chapter 2, where the mayonnaise had stained her notebook and, in some perverse way, freed her. She hadn’t realized it then, but that accident had been the first crack in the polished veneer she had meticulously curated. Now, the journal was her sanctuary, a place where the mayonnaise wasn’t a stain but a muse, where the messiness of her emotions could exist without judgment.  

She began with the date—*May 12th*—and wrote about the way the mayonnaise had clung to her fingers as she tried to scribble lyrics in the dark, the way the sticky substance had felt like a secret, a rebellion against her own perfectionism. She described the mayonnaise as a "tangy whisper," a "sour note in a symphony of chaos," and paired it with a recipe for a *Dill Pickle Mayo* that she had discovered in the vintage book she had found in the attic of her grandmother’s house. The recipe, with its precise measurements and archaic phrasing, felt like a relic from another time, a bridge between the old and the new, the messy and the refined.  

As she wrote, her mind wandered to the gallery opening in Chapter 3, where her mayo-stained sleeves had been the only thing anyone noticed. ## Chapter 10: "The Fashion Fiasco"  

The sun hung low over the city, casting a golden haze over the rooftop where the photoshoot was supposed to be a celebration of Lila’s curated chaos. The city skyline stretched behind her, a mix of glass and steel that mirrored the tension in her chest. She stood in front of the camera, her hands gripping the edges of a vintage dress that had once felt like a rebellion—now it was just another layer of armor. The fabric was a deep emerald, its lace trim catching the light like a secret she wasn’t sure she wanted to keep. Around her neck hung a necklace made of tiny, glass mayonnaise jars, their translucent surfaces reflecting the fading light. It was absurd, she knew, but in her mind, it was a statement. A declaration that even in her most polished moments, she carried the messy, tangy essence of her true self.  

“Lila, the light’s shifting,” the photographer called from behind the lens. “Adjust your pose—maybe a little more… *dramatic*.”  

She exhaled, her breath fogging the air as she stepped back, letting the wind catch the hem of her dress. The model co-stars—two women in their twenties, their laughter sharp and practiced—watched her from the corner of the room. They were all smiles, their eyes glinting with the kind of confidence she’d worked hard to mimic.  

“Perfect,” said the photographer. “Now, let’s try the *actual* shoot.”

The jars, which she’d hoped would add a touch of whimsy, now looked like a joke. A punchline. She forced a smile, her fingers brushing the fabric of her dress as if it could shield her from the judgment she felt creeping in.

“It’s… it’s *art*,” she said, her voice too loud.  

The photographer’s smile faltered. “Art?” He leaned closer, his breath warm against her ear. “You sure about that?”

The photographer’s smile returned, but it was thinner now. “Alright. Let’s try the *final* shot.”  

Lila stepped forward, the camera’s flash blinding her. The mayonnaise jars glinted, their surfaces catching the light like tiny, fragile stars. But as the shot was taken, she saw it—the way the model co-stars’ eyes lingered on her hands, the way the photographer’s gaze lingered on the stains. When the shoot ended, the model co-stars gathered around her, their laughter sharp but not unkind. “You’re *so* committed to the bit,” one of them said, her voice dripping with irony. “I think it’s cute. But maybe… *less* mayo next time?”

The city’s noise swelled around her, but she couldn’t hear it. All she could hear was the echo of the photographer’s question, the way the model co-stars had looked at her like she was a performance, not a person.

A reminder of how she’d tried to shape herself into a version of herself that felt safe, that felt *acceptable*. But the stains—on her sleeves, on her hands, on her dress—were the parts of her that refused to be curated.

There was one about “The Tang of Regret,” another about “Sandwiches as Solace.” She traced the words with her finger, her heart aching. She’d written them in the hope that someone, anyone, would see the beauty in the madness. But now, the thought of sharing them felt like a betrayal.  

The next morning, she opened her laptop, her blog’s homepage loaded. The post she’d planned—a reflection on fashion and art—felt hollow. She had written it with the hope of connecting with others, of finding a community that appreciated her contradictions. But now, the words felt like a lie.  

Instead, she typed a single sentence: *“Sometimes, the most beautiful things are the ones we’re too afraid to show.”*  

She hit “publish,” the screen flickering as the post went live. Then, she closed the laptop, her hands trembling. The mayonnaise jar remained on her desk, its lid sealed tight. For now, it was enough.  

But as the sun rose the next day, she couldn’t shake the feeling that the world was watching. Not just the photoshoot, but the way she’d hidden herself behind the lens. The way she’d tried to curate her life into something that felt safe, only to be laughed at.

The thought was both a comfort and a curse. She didn’t know if she was ready for someone to see the mess beneath the polish. But she also didn’t want to keep hiding.  

The mayonnaise jar sat on her desk, its label faded. It was a relic of a time when she’d thought she could control the narrative. Now, she wondered if the real story was in the stains, in the things she’d tried to scrub away.  

And maybe, just maybe, that was enough.

## Chapter 11: The Poetry Open Mic

The café buzzed with its usual Wednesday evening energy—a low hum of conversations punctuated by the clink of coffee cups and the occasional hiss of the espresso machine. Lila sat in her usual corner booth, fingers drumming against the worn wooden table as she stared at the notebook in front of her. The page was blank except for a single line, scrawled in her looping script: *“Mayo and melodies, tangled in the dark.”* 

She’d been trying to write the poem for weeks, ever since the music festival disaster. The words felt too big, too raw, too *much*. Like the rest of her life. But tonight, she’d promised herself she’d read it aloud. No more hiding behind the safety of her blog’s filtered photos or the carefully curated captions that made her seem like someone she wasn’t. Tonight, she’d try to be real.

The open mic had started twenty minutes ago, and already three poets had taken the small stage in the back of the café. Lila watched them from her seat, her knees bouncing nervously. She’d practiced the poem in the shower, in her car, even while stirring her morning coffee, but now, under the dim amber lights of the café, it felt like a confession she wasn’t ready to make. 

*What if they laugh? What if they think I’m ridiculous?* The questions looped in her mind, familiar and unwelcome. But then she thought of the journal she’d been keeping—the one filled with poems about mayonnaise and heartbreak, about the way the crowd at the festival had sneered at her stained hands. She thought of the way her date had recoiled when she’d offered him a mayo sandwich, his face twisting as if she’d suggested something obscene. 

No. Tonight, she’d let the words spill out, even if they were messy. Even if they were *her*.

The host, a lanky man with a beanie and a voice that could cut glass, called for the next reader. Lila’s stomach lurched. She’d been dreading this moment, but now that it was here, she felt a strange calm. Maybe it was the coffee, or maybe it was the memory of her grandmother’s voice, soft and steady, telling her, *“Child, the world needs your truth, even if it’s wrapped in mayo and metaphors.”*

She stood, her chair scraping against the floor, and walked toward the stage. The spotlight hit her like a wave, and for a moment, she thought she might faint. But then she opened her mouth and began to read.

---

*“Mayo and melodies, tangled in the dark,*  
*You’re the tang in my tea, the static in my heart.*  
*I spilled you on my notebook once, a accident of fate,*  
*But now I see you’re the ink that makes my words great.*  

*At the festival, they laughed at my hands,*  
*Called them ‘unclean,’ said I didn’t understand*  
*The rules of the game, the way things are done.*  
*But maybe the game’s the problem, not me and my fun.*  

*You’re the condiment of my chaos, the spread on my bread,*  
*The thing that sticks when everything else is dead.*  
*So here’s to the mess, the stains, the absurd,*  
*The things that make me, me, and no one else’s word.”*

---

When she finished, there was a beat of silence. Then, a smattering of applause. Not thunderous, but warm. A few people nodded, and one woman in the front row gave her a thumbs-up. Lila’s cheeks flushed as she stepped back from the mic, her heart still racing.

But then she noticed him.

He was sitting near the back, a lanky figure in a faded band T-shirt and a jacket that looked like it had seen better days. His hair was tousled, and he had a notebook open on his lap, scribbling something. But when he looked up and met her gaze, he smiled. Not the polite, performative smile of someone being kind, but a real one—wide and bright, like he’d just heard a secret only he and she understood.

Lila’s breath caught. She didn’t know why, but something about that smile made her chest tighten. She quickly looked away, pretending to adjust her scarf, but she could feel his eyes on her. 

The host called for another round of applause, and the crowd obliged. Lila returned to her seat, her hands trembling slightly as she pulled her notebook close. She told herself to focus on her coffee, to ignore the stranger and his annoying, intriguing smile. But her mind kept drifting back to the poem. To the way the words had felt leaving her lips, like she was finally speaking in her own voice instead of someone else’s.

A few minutes later, the open mic ended, and the café began to empty. Lila lingered, pretending to text on her phone, when she heard a voice behind her.

“That was… something else.”

She turned to find the stranger standing there, his notebook tucked under his arm. Up close, she could see the faint smudge of ink on his thumb and the way his eyes crinkled when he smiled.

“Thank you,” she said, her voice steadier than she felt. “I wasn’t sure how it would land.”

“Land?” He laughed, a soft, raspy sound. “It hit harder than most of the stuff I’ve heard here. That part about the festival… you really *spilled* mayo on your notebook?”

Lila blinked. “How did you—?”

“I’m Marco,” he said, extending his hand. “And I may or may not be obsessed with mayonnaise. It’s a long story.”

She stared at him, her brain scrambling to process this new piece of information. “Wait, you… you *like* mayo?”

“I *love* it. It’s like… the unsung hero of the condiment world. Versatile, complex, a little misunderstood. Kind of like poetry.” He paused, then added with a grin, “Or maybe I’m just weird.”

Lila laughed, and it felt like the first real laugh she’d had in months. “I’m Lila.”

“Lila,” he repeated, as if tasting the name. “So, are you going to tell me the story behind the poem? Or do I have to buy you a sandwich to get it out of you?”

She hesitated, then nodded toward the café’s exit. “There’s a diner down the street that makes a mean BLT. With extra mayo, of course.”

As they walked out into the cool night air, Lila felt something shift inside her. Maybe it was the weight of the poem finally being shared, or maybe it was the way Marco’s eyes lit up when she mentioned her journal. Whatever it was, for the first time in a long time, she didn’t feel like she was performing. She felt like she was simply… herself.

And maybe, just maybe, that was enough.

## Chapter 12: "The First Spark"  

The air in the poetry open mic room was thick with the scent of coffee and anxiety, a familiar cocktail that made Lila’s palms sweat. She stood at the edge of the stage, her fingers brushing the worn leather cover of her journal—a relic of her obsession with mayo and the messy, unfiltered truths she’d scribbled within its pages. The crowd was a mosaic of faces, some leaning into the rhythm of the mic, others scrolling on their phones, their attention as fleeting as the notes she’d practiced in the mirror.  

Her poem, *“The Condiment of Solitude,”* had been a risk. She’d written it the night before, after a date that had fizzled like a dying flame, and a concert where her hands had been smeared with mayo, a detail she’d later framed as a metaphor for the chaos she’d tried to suppress. But tonight, she’d chosen to perform it, her voice trembling as she began:  

*“I once spilled mayonnaise on a notebook, a crime against the pristine, / But the stain became a canvas, and I learned to paint with the mess.”*  

The words felt foreign when she said them aloud, but the crowd leaned in, their murmurs softening into something like understanding. A man in the front row, his hair dyed the color of burnt copper, raised his hand mid-verse. “That’s… that’s *exactly* what I needed to hear,” he said, his voice tinged with awe. Lila’s cheeks flushed, but she kept going.  

The poem ended with her confessing the journal, the one she’d hidden in her bag like a secret. “It’s not just about mayo,” she said, her voice gaining strength. “It’s about the things we’re told to hide, the parts of ourselves that feel too much, too weird, too… *too much*.” The room fell silent. Then, a ripple of applause spread, slow and deliberate, like the first notes of a song that had been waiting to be played.  

Lila’s heart pounded. She had expected indifference, maybe even laughter, but this—this was different. The man in the front row was still looking at her, his eyes wide, as if she’d just revealed a truth he’d been too afraid to voice. Before she could think, she turned and walked off the stage, her heels clicking against the wooden floor.  

The crowd parted for her, and she moved quickly, her mind racing. *Who was he? Where did he come from?* She had no idea, but the way he’d watched her, the way his voice had cracked with sincerity, felt like a spark in the dark.  

She didn’t notice the man trailing behind her until he stepped into her path, his presence a quiet challenge. He was taller than she expected, his dark hair tousled, his eyes the color of storm clouds. “You’re not what I expected,” he said, his tone not unkind.  

Lila froze. “And what did you expect?”  

He smiled, a slow, knowing thing. “A girl who liked mayo. Who wrote poems about it. I thought you’d be… more guarded.”  

She blinked, surprised. “I’m not *that* guarded.”  

“Then why the journal?” he asked, his gaze flicking to the notebook peeking from her bag.  

Lila’s breath caught. She hadn’t meant to bring it, but it had been a habit, a crutch. Now, the weight of it felt heavier. “It’s… a work in progress,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper.  

He tilted his head, studying her. “Progress? Or a confession?”  

She laughed, the sound foreign in her own ears. “Both, maybe.”  

For a moment, the space between them felt charged, electric. Then he stepped closer, his presence no longer a shadow but a presence. “You’re not as alone as you think,” he said.  

Lila’s throat tightened. “I thought I was.”  

He reached into his pocket, pulling out a small, crumpled piece of paper. “I wrote a poem about you. Not the one you performed, but… the one I wrote *after* hearing you. It’s about the mayo, the concerts, the way you carry yourself like you’re both the artist and the audience.”  

She took the paper, her fingers brushing his. The ink was smudged, the words messy, but the emotion was raw. *“You’re a storm in a dress,”* he’d written, *“and I’ve been waiting for a thunderstorm to let me get soaked.”*  

Lila’s knees nearly gave out. She didn’t know if it was the poem, the proximity, or the way his eyes held hers, but something in her chest cracked open. For the first time in months, she didn’t feel like a performance. She felt seen.  

“I’d like that,” she said, the words slipping out before she could stop them.  

He smiled, and for the first time, she felt the weight of her curated image lift, like a dress she’d worn for too long. The spark had ignited, and now, it was up to her to decide whether to let it burn.

## The Dress and the Dip

The café sat on the corner of Birchwood and Fifth, wedged between a vintage record shop and a florist whose window displays changed with the seasons. Lila had passed it a hundred times on her way to more fashionable corners of the city, always promising herself she'd stop in someday, that someday that never came because somedays were reserved for curated moments, for outfits that photographed well, for the version of herself she was still learning to question.

Today, however, the promise had been kept. She sat in a corner booth with cracked red leather seats, her hands wrapped around a cup of chamomile tea that had long since gone lukewarm. The vintage dress she'd chosen—cream cotton with small blue cornflowers, a find from the Goodwill on Ninth Street she'd been saving for the right occasion—felt both like armor and like a confession. She had ironed it twice that morning, pressing the collar until it held the memory of intention, then deliberately rumpling it slightly at the sleeves. A small rebellion. A small truth.

*He might not even come*, she thought, the familiar voice of caution already gathering momentum. *He probably said that just to be polite. Stranger at an open mic, offering to meet for coffee—what kind of person does that? The awkward kind. The kind who regrets it by Tuesday.*

She glanced at her phone. Three minutes until the time he'd specified, if he was the kind of person who arrived on time, which she assumed he was. There had been something deliberate about him when he'd approached her after the performance—something patient and watchful that made her think he didn't say things he didn't mean. But then again, she'd been wrong about people before. She'd built an entire career on being wrong about people, on presenting them with a version of herself so polished and protected that they could only ever love the surface, never the strange and tender underneath.

The door chimed.

She looked up before she could stop herself, and there he was—Marco—shaking rain from a leather jacket that had seen better decades. His hair was darker than she'd remembered, curling slightly at the collar, and he carried a canvas bag stuffed with what looked like sheet music. He scanned the room with the distracted air of someone who wasn't entirely sure what he was looking for until he found it.

Their eyes met.

He smiled.

It wasn't a performance, that smile. It was small and slightly crooked and entirely unguarded, and Lila felt something shift in her chest—not the sharp pang of fear she'd grown accustomed to, but something softer. Something that felt, impossibly, like relief.

"Hey," he said, sliding into the booth across from her. "You came."

"You asked." She heard her own voice, surprised by how steady it sounded. "Did you think I wouldn't?"

"I thought you might have second thoughts." He shrugged, pulling off his jacket. "I would have understood. This is—I mean, it's a little unusual. Meeting someone you don't know for coffee."

"I don't usually do unusual," she said, then caught herself. "I mean—I do. I do unusual things. Just not... not where people can see."

He tilted his head, a gesture that reminded her of the way he’d listened during her poem—that night at the open mic, when she'd spilled her heart onto a stage and expected it to shatter. "Where people can see," he repeated. "That's an interesting way to put it."

The waiter arrived, saving her from having to explain. Marco ordered black coffee and a turkey sandwich, and when Lila hesitated over the menu, he said, "They have a good BLT here. If you're hungry."

"I'm always hungry," she admitted, then wondered if that was too much—too honest, too unfiltered. But he only nodded as if she'd said something perfectly reasonable.

"Food is good," he said. "I don't understand people who pretend they're not interested in eating."

The BLT arrived, and Lila discovered, to her quiet horror, that she had no idea how to eat a sandwich in front of a stranger while maintaining even the slightest shred of dignity. The bacon would drip. The lettuce would escape. There would be mayo—because there was always mayo—and she would have to navigate the social minefield of condiment management while trying to remember how to form coherent sentences.

*This is ridiculous*, she told herself. *You've done fashion shoots in the rain. You've performed poetry about condiments in front of a room full of strangers. You can eat a sandwich.*

But she couldn't, not really, because the sandwich was a test, wasn't it? A trial run for everything that came after. If she could eat this sandwich and still be seen—still be *liked*—then maybe there was something to this whole uncurated thing.

"You know," Marco said, watching her not-eat, "I looked up that poem you performed. The one about the mayo at the concert."

Her stomach dropped. "You did?"

"It was on your blog. The one with the vintage filter and the quotes from Baudelaire." He smiled again, that crooked smile. "I liked it. The part about the condiment as metaphor for the things we hide in plain sight. That's—that's good. That's really good."

"It was silly," she said, but the words came out softer than she intended. "I was just being absurd."

"Absurd is underrated." He leaned back, picking up his coffee. "Everyone tries so hard to be meaningful. No one tries to be weird anymore. There's no rebellion in being serious."

Lila stared at him. No one had ever called her weird like it was a compliment. No one had ever looked at the strange corners of her mind—the mayo fixation, the lyrical tangents, the way she found poetry in condiments—and seen something worth keeping.

"I have a journal," she said, before she could stop herself. "Full of poems. And recipes. For mayo. Different kinds."

Marco's eyebrows rose. "Different kinds?"

"There's the classic, obviously. But there's also garlic mayo, and chipotle mayo, and this one recipe from a 1950s cookbook that uses avocado oil and a little bit of honey. It's—" She stopped, suddenly aware that she was monologuing. "I'm sorry. I'm talking too much."

"No." He shook his head, and there was something in his expression that made her breath catch—something like recognition, like he'd found a piece of a puzzle he'd been missing. "Keep talking. I want to hear about the honey mayo."

So she told him. She told him about the vintage recipe book she'd found at the estate sale in Chapter 5, how the pages were stained with decades of butter and flour, how someone had scribbled notes in the margins about which brand of olive oil worked best. She told him about her grandmother, who had made her own mayo every Sunday, who had taught her that the best things in life required patience and a steady hand. She told him about the journal she'd started, the one that lived in a drawer by her bed, the one she was too afraid to show anyone because it was *too much*, too strange, too her.

Marco listened. He didn't check his phone or glance at the door or give her the polite nods that meant he was waiting for his turn to talk. He listened like her words mattered, like the strange small details of her inner world were worth holding onto.

"I'd like to read it sometime," he said, when she finally ran out of breath. "If you'd let me. The journal. I'd like to see it."

The sandwich sat between them, largely untouched. The lettuce had wilted slightly, and a thin line of mayo had begun to seep onto the plate. It was, objectively, a mess.

Lila picked it up and took a bite anyway.

The mayo was perfect—tangy and rich, with just a hint of sweetness from the tomato. The bacon was crisp, the lettuce crunchy, the bread soft. It was, in that moment, the best sandwich she had ever eaten, because she was eating it while being seen, and she was still here, still whole, still herself.

"It's good," she said, chewing. "The sandwich. It's really good."

Marco laughed—a real laugh, surprised out of him. "I'm glad. I was worried you'd hate it."

"Why would I hate it?"

"I don't know." He shrugged, looking almost shy. "You seem like the kind of person who has very specific opinions about things. The way you were looking at the menu—like it might personally offend you."

She grinned. "I was deciding whether to get the turkey club or the BLT. It was a genuine dilemma."

"A matter of life and death?"

"Obviously."

They both laughed then, and something in the air between them shifted—some invisible wall crumbling, some careful distance collapsing into something warmer. Lila felt it in her chest, that strange softening again, the feeling of setting down a weight she'd been carrying for so long she'd forgotten it was there.

"Can I tell you something?" she said, the words slipping out before she could vet them.

"Always."

"I was scared to come today. I almost canceled three times." She looked down at her sandwich, at the smear of mayo on her fingers. "I thought you'd see me and realize I was—I don't know. Too much. Too weird. Too obsessed with condiments."

Marco reached across the table. His hand hovered there for a moment, uncertain, then gently covered hers. The touch was light, tentative, but it sent sparks up her arm.

"I think," he said, "that I've been waiting to meet someone too much for a long time. And I finally found them, and I'm not going to be scared off by mayonnaise."

She looked up at him. His eyes were dark and earnest, and there was no performance in his face, no mask. Just a boy who liked weird poetry and good sandwiches and, somehow, inexplicably, her.

"I don't usually let people see this," she said softly. "The messy parts. The mayo parts."

"I know." He squeezed her hand. "That's what makes it special."

Outside, the rain had stopped. The sun was breaking through the clouds, casting golden light across the café floor. Lila looked at their hands together—her messy fingers, his steady ones—and felt something she hadn't felt in a very long time.

Hope.

Not the curated kind, not the filtered and perfect version she posted for her followers. The real kind. The messy, uncertain, terrifying kind.

"So," Marco said, pulling his hand back reluctantly to grab his coffee. "Same time next week?"

Lila smiled. "Only if they have the BLT."

"They better. Or I'm finding a new café."

She laughed, and the sound felt different in her mouth—lighter, freer. The dress felt different too, less like armor and more like a gift she'd given herself. The mayo on her fingers was still there, a small reminder of all the things she'd been hiding, and for once, she didn't want to wipe it away.

*This is it*, she thought, watching Marco steal a bite of her sandwich with a grin. *This is the beginning of something. The first page of a chapter I haven't written yet.*

She didn't know what came next. She didn't know if this would last, if he'd change his mind, if the fear would come creeping back in the dark. But for now, in this moment, she was here—messy and weird and entirely herself—and someone was sitting across from her, asking for more.

That was enough. That was more than enough.

That was everything.

## Chapter 14: "The Concert Proposal"

The text from Marco arrived at 11:47 p.m., glowing against the dark expanse of Lila's phone screen like a small, warm ember dropped into a room she had long kept sealed.

*There's a show Friday. Underground venue. No phones allowed inside. You in?*

No period. No emoji. Just the raw, unadorned question — *You in?* — as though the invitation were not an invitation at all but a dare, a threshold drawn in chalk across the threshold of something she was not yet brave enough to cross.

Lila read it once. Then again. Then a third time, because the words had begun to rearrange themselves in her mind, shifting from simple sentence to loaded proposition. She lay on her bed, the same bed where she had spent the better part of the last year composing poems about mayonnaise and loneliness in equal measure, and stared at the ceiling. The plaster had a hairline crack that traced a crooked path from the light fixture to the corner of the room, and she had never noticed it before. She noticed everything when she was nervous.

She typed a response — *yes*, just the word, clean and decisive — then deleted it. Typed *Friday works for me* — deleted that too. Finally, with the deliberateness of someone sealing a letter they know will change everything, she wrote: *Tell me more.*

His reply came within seconds, as though he had been waiting by his phone with the same restless urgency she had been nursing hers.

*It's in a basement, off Canal. Small room, no stage really — just a cleared space in the middle. The band is called Hollow Wire. They play like they're trying to forget something. You'd love it.*

Lila pressed the phone against her chest and breathed in the lavender fabric softener she had sprayed on her sheets that morning. She tried to imagine the scene: a basement, a crowd pressed close together, music loud enough to flatten the distance between strangers. She loved concerts. She had always loved them — the communion of sound, the way a room full of people could become a single organism, pulsing and swaying and gasping at the same moments. But underground concerts were different. Underground concerts required a kind of unselfconsciousness she had never quite mastered.

She thought of the gallery opening in Chapter Three, how she had stood in her bold emerald dress, feeling magnificent until she realized that no one was looking. She thought of the open mic in Chapter Eleven, how her voice had cracked on the second verse and she had seen pity in the eyes of the woman sitting in the front row. She thought of Marco, sitting across from her in the café on Saturday, his fingers wrapped around a paper cup of black coffee, his eyes doing that thing where they lingered just a moment longer than expected, as though he were reading a poem she had written inside her posture.

*He liked the journal,* she thought. *He liked the poem about the mayonnaise. He liked the dress.*

But liking the curated version of herself — the version she presented over coffee, in a carefully chosen vintage dress with her sleeves tucked neatly beneath a cardigan — was not the same as accepting the uncurated Lila. The Lila who carried a small jar of homemade aioli in her purse. The Lila who had, on more than one occasion, excused herself from a conversation to dab mayonnaise on a torn piece of bread and eat it with her fingers, as though it were the most natural thing in the world. The Lila whose journal smelled faintly of egg yolk and lemon juice, whose margins were stained with the pale ghosts of condiment fingerprints.

*What if he sees that?*

The thought arrived not as a whisper but as a scream, the kind that reverberates inside the skull at three in the morning when the world is quiet and there is nowhere for fear to hide. She pressed her palms against her eyes and saw, with terrible clarity, the scenario that would unfold: she would reach into her bag for her journal, and the jar would tip, and the mayonnaise — that thick, golden, impossible-to-explain substance — would spill across the pages, and Marco would look at her with that same expression the stranger at the fashion shoot had worn, the one that said *you are too much, and not in the way we want.*

She set the phone down on the bed beside her and pressed her face into the pillow. The fabric was cool against her cheek, and she inhaled the scent of herself — lavender, vanilla, and something underneath that she could never quite name, something that smelled like doubt.

---

Friday arrived with the particular weight of an afternoon that feels like a held breath.

Lila stood in front of her closet for forty minutes, which was thirty-seven minutes longer than she needed to choose an outfit on any normal day. The dress — the one Marco had admired — hung on its hanger like a promise, its fabric a deep burgundy that caught the light the way wine does when you tilt the glass just so. She had worn it on purpose that first time, she realized now. It was the dress she wore when she wanted to be seen. When she wanted to be believed.

But Friday was not about being believed. Friday was about being found out.

She tried on three outfits before settling on something she had never worn in public: a simple black turtleneck, dark jeans, and a pair of boots she had bought on sale and never had the courage to wear. The clothes were armor, but armor of a different kind — not the polished, curated kind she wore to gallery openings and dates that fizzled, but the kind that said *I am here, and I am not performing.*

She left the dress on its hanger.

Her purse, however, betrayed her. Inside it, nestled beside the journal with its stained and dog-eared pages, was a small glass jar of lemon-herb aioli she had made that morning. She had planned to eat it on toast, alone, in her kitchen, but some instinct — the same instinct that made her scribble poems in the margins of mayonnaise recipe books — had compelled her to bring it along. As though the aioli were a talisman. As though it were proof that she was, in fact, the person she feared Marco might not want her to be.

She caught her reflection in the hallway mirror before leaving. Black turtleneck. Dark jeans. Boots. Hair pulled back in a loose knot that was falling apart faster than she would have liked. She looked, she thought, like someone trying very hard to look like she wasn't trying at all.

*That's the hardest costume of all,* she told herself, and left before she could reconsider.

---

The address Marco had sent led her down a narrow alley behind a laundromat, past a rusted fire escape, and through a door marked only with a small, hand-painted arrow pointing downward. The stairs smelled of damp concrete and old cigarette smoke, and the fluorescent light above the entrance buzzed with the irregular rhythm of something dying. She descended, her boots echoing against the metal steps, and felt the bass before she heard it — a low, thrumming vibration that traveled up through the soles of her shoes and settled somewhere behind her sternum.

The basement was smaller than she had imagined. Roughly thirty people stood packed into a room with exposed brick walls and a concrete floor that had been covered in mismatched rugs. A single spotlight, jury-rigged from what appeared to be a construction lamp, illuminated the cleared space in the center where two figures were already setting up guitars and tuning strings with the casual precision of people who had done this a thousand times. There was no stage, no barrier, no separation between the performers and the audience — just a circle of bodies waiting to be filled with sound.

She scanned the room. A woman in combat boots and a leather jacket was rolling a cigarette by the door. Two men with paint-streaked overalls stood near the back, speaking in the low, conspiratorial tones of people who shared a secret. A couple swayed gently against the far wall, their foreheads touching. The air was thick with the mingled scents of beer, patchouli, and something metallic — the scent of electricity, perhaps, or anticipation.

And then she saw him.

Marco stood near the center of the room, his dark hair falling across his forehead in a way that made him look, improbably, like every concert photograph she had ever saved to her inspiration board. He was wearing a simple black shirt, sleeves rolled to his elbows, and when he saw her, his face broke into a smile that was not curated, not filtered, not composed for any audience. It was the kind of smile that arrived without permission and stayed without effort.

"You came," he said, as though the words surprised even him.

"You said *underground*," she replied, and was immediately mortified, because it sounded like a complaint, or a challenge, or something a woman in a movie would say while standing in the rain outside a jazz club in 1962.

He laughed — a real laugh, unguarded and warm — and took her arm. "It's better than it sounds. I promise."

They found a spot near the edge of the room, close enough to feel the vibration of the music but far enough to have a small pocket of space. Lila set her bag on the floor beside her and felt the jar inside it shift, a faint, liquid slide that sent a spike of anxiety through her chest. She pressed her hand against the bag as though she could hold the aioli in place through sheer force of will.

*Don't spill it. Don't open it. Don't, for the love of everything, pull out the journal.*

Hollow Wire took the stage — if two amps, a drum kit, and a microphone arranged in a loose semicircle could be called a stage — and the first chord hit like a fist against a wall. The sound was raw, unpolished, stripped of the production she was accustomed to hearing through her curated playlists. The singer's voice cracked on the high notes, and the guitarist's fingers slipped twice during the first song, and neither of them cared, because the music was not about precision. It was about presence. It was about the act of standing in a room and making noise together, of saying *I am here, and I feel things, and I am not ashamed.*

Lila felt something loosen in her chest. It was small at first — a crack in the plaster, like the one on her ceiling — and then it widened, and what poured through it was not fear but something closer to relief. She looked at Marco, who was watching the band with an expression of pure, unguarded absorption, and she thought: *This is what it looks like when someone is not performing.*

For a while, she let herself simply be. She stood in the dim light and let the music wash over her and did not think about how she looked, or whether her turtleneck was flattering, or whether the woman in the leather jacket was judging her shoes. She was just a body in a room, vibrating with sound, and it was enough.

But then — because there is always a *then* — the singer paused between songs and the room fell into a hush, and Lila's hand, seemingly of its own volition, moved to her bag. She unzipped it, felt the cool glass of the jar beneath her fingertips, and pulled out a small loaf of bread she had sliced that morning and wrapped in parchment paper.

"What are you doing?" Marco murmured, leaning closer so the band couldn't hear.

"Nothing," she whispered back, but her hands were already working — tearing the bread, unscrewing the lid, spreading a thick, generous layer of aioli across the soft interior with the practiced ease of someone who has done this alone, in secret, a hundred times.

She offered him a piece.

He looked at the bread. He looked at the jar. He looked at her, and in his eyes she saw something she had not expected — not judgment, not confusion, but a kind of recognition, as though he had been waiting for this moment all along.

"Is that — is this your thing?" he asked, and the question was gentle, genuinely curious, the way one might ask about a childhood memory or a recurring dream.

Lila's throat tightened. Every instinct she had — the ones honed by years of curating, filtering, and carefully selecting which parts of herself to show the world — told her to put the jar away. To wrap the bread and slide it back into her bag. To laugh it off and say *just kidding, I don't actually eat mayonnaise at concerts.*

But the music was playing, and the room was dark, and Marco was looking at her with his whole attention, and she was so tired of performing.

"It's lemon-herb," she said, and her voice was steadier than she expected. "I made it this morning. With fresh thyme and a little garlic. It's — it's the best thing I've ever put in my mouth, and I'm not even exaggerating. I know it's strange. I know people think it's —" She stopped, because the words were crowding in her throat like an audience waiting for the next verse. "I know what people think."

Marco took the piece of bread. He bit into it slowly, chewing with the same absorption he had given the band, and then he closed his eyes, and something shifted in his face — a softening, an opening, like a door she hadn't known was closed swinging gently inward.

"It's incredible," he said, and he meant it, and the way he meant it was the most beautiful thing she had ever been offered.

Lila laughed — not the polished, performative laugh she used at gallery openings, but a real laugh, startled and full and slightly too loud for the room. She covered her mouth with one hand, then lowered it, because she was done covering things.

Marco reached into his pocket and pulled out a folded piece of paper. She recognized it — he always carried things in his back pocket, small squares of paper with notes and phone numbers and fragments of lyrics. He unfolded it and placed it in front of her.

"Hollow Wire is playing again next month," he said. "A bigger venue. Not underground, but — I don't know. I thought you might want to come. If you wanted to."

She looked at the paper. His handwriting was angular and precise, but there was a smudge of ink near the edge, as though he had written it quickly, without thinking too much about how it looked.

"I want to come," she said.

Something passed between them then — not a declaration, not a confession, but something quieter and more durable, like the first thread in a fabric that would take years to complete. It was the look of two people standing on the edge of a version of themselves they had not yet dared to inhabit, deciding, together, to step forward.

Lila folded the paper and tucked it into her journal, between a poem about egg yolks and a sketch of a dress she would never buy. She picked up the jar of aioli, screwed the lid back on, and placed it carefully in her bag.

She did not hide it at the bottom.

She placed it right on top, where she could feel its weight against her hip, a small, cool weight that said: *This is who I am. This is all of it. The curated and the uncurated. The dress and the dip. The poem and the mayonnaise.*

And when the next song began — something slow and aching and full of the kind of longing that cannot be spoken, only felt — Lila closed her eyes and let the sound fill every hollow space inside her, and for the first time in as long as she could remember, she did not feel the need to fill them with anything else.

## Chapter 15: The Messy Confession

The venue was a basement beneath a laundromat, the air thick with the scent of stale beer, damp concrete, and the electric tang of anticipation. Lila pressed a hand to her stomach, the silk of her vintage emerald-green dress suddenly feeling like a shroud. Marco had chosen this place, a hidden cavern of sound, promising “real music, no curated playlists.” It was the antithesis of her carefully constructed world, and she had come, armed only with a small jar of mayonnaise in her clutch and a trembling resolve.

He was already there, a silhouette against the dim stage lights, his dark hair falling over his forehead as he nodded along to the opening band’s distorted guitars. He looked up, and his face broke into a smile that was both warm and, she would later realize, edged with a faint, unreadable caution. “You came,” he said, his voice carrying a note of genuine surprise that made her chest tighten.

“I said I would,” she replied, the words feeling stiff. She had spent an hour perfecting her “effortless” look: the dress, a pair of scuffed ankle boots, a single delicate silver necklace. No mayo stains in sight. A small victory.

They talked over the music, shouting about the band, about the city, about nothing of consequence. He bought her a cider, and she clutched the cold glass, its condensation mirroring the sweat on her palms. The conversation was easy, but it was a performance. She was still Lila-the-blogger, Lila-the-poet, curating her anecdotes, polishing her responses. The real her, the one who wrote odes to emulsified egg yolks and found solace in the rhythmic *shhh-clunk* of a washing machine, was locked away, whispering from a cell.

During a break between sets, the basement lights dimmed further, leaving only the soft glow of exit signs and the neon beer advertisement behind the bar. Marco leaned in, his shoulder brushing hers. “I’ve been thinking about your poem,” he said, his breath warm against her ear. “The one about the concert and the mayo. It was… startlingly honest.”

A blush crept up her neck. “It’s silly, really.”
“It’s not silly,” he insisted, turning to face her. “It’s specific. That’s what makes it true. Most people write about love or death. You write about… tang and texture. It’s a whole other sensory universe.”

In that moment, the carefully constructed wall seemed to waver. Here was someone who not only didn’t mock her quirk, but who seemed to *see* it, to appreciate its odd architecture. The urge to share, to finally be known, became a physical pressure in her throat.

“There’s… more,” she said, the words tumbling out before she could cage them. “A lot more. I have this… journal.”

He tilted his head, curious. “A journal?”

“It’s not just poems. It’s recipes. Vintage mayonnaise recipes. And sketches of dresses. And lyrics that are terrible. And… it’s where I put everything that doesn’t fit on the blog. The messy parts.” She was babbling, she knew, but the music had started again, a pulsing, rhythmic thing that felt like her own racing heart.

“Can I see it?” he asked, his eyes reflecting the stage lights.

Panic, cold and sharp, lanced through her. To show it was to be fully seen. To expose the lonely girl behind the curated canvas. But the look on his face was not one of judgment, but of invitation. A dare.

“Okay,” she whispered.

They found a corner propped up by stacked speaker cases, a precarious nook away from the crowd. Lila’s hands shook as she unclasped her clutch. She pulled out the small, leather-bound notebook, its cover soft from handling. This was her sanctuary, her confessional, her most private self. She placed it in his hands as if offering a sacred text.

He opened it. The first page was a poem, “*Sonnet for a Sandwich, Condiment-Crusted*,” written in her looping script. He read silently, his brow furrowed in concentration. Then he smiled, a real, unselfconscious grin. “This is beautiful. ‘The tang of vinegar on the tongue of dawn’… that’s good.”

He turned another page. A recipe for “1920s Thousand Island Dressing” was scrawled next to a pencil sketch of a flapper’s beaded dress. He traced the sketch with his finger. “You draw, too?”
“Badly,” she said, but the relief was flooding her, warm and sweet. “It’s all bad. The poems, the drawings, the recipes… it’s all just… messy.”

“It’s not messy,” he said, looking up from the page. “It’s *you*. It’s all connected. The fashion, the words, the… mayo. It’s a language. I get it.”

Tears pricked her eyes, but they were tears of a catharsis so long denied she almost didn’t recognize them. For the first time, she was not performing. She was simply Lila, sitting in a dirty basement, sharing her weird, wonderful, messy heart with a boy who was reading her recipe for “Lobster Thermidor with a Hint of Hellmann’s” and nodding as if it were the most profound culinary insight.

“I’ve never shown this to anyone,” she admitted, her voice thick.
“Thank you,” he said, and the sincerity in his eyes was a balm. He closed the book gently and handed it back. “It’s a masterpiece of eclectic genius.”

For the rest of the concert, they existed in a bubble. He asked about the vintage recipe book she’d found, about the inspiration for a particular poem. She asked about his music, his own unpublished lyrics. He showed her a chord progression on his phone, something haunting and minor. She told him about the fashion blog, not as a curated portfolio, but as a “public facade, a beautiful lie.”

“Why a lie?” he asked during a quiet moment.
“Because the girl on the blog never spills mayo on her notebook. She never has a panic attack because her tights have a run. She doesn’t spend Friday nights home alone with a jar of the good stuff and a pen.” The confession slipped out, raw and unadorned.

He was quiet for a moment, then said, “I have a panic attack before every show. My hands shake so bad I can barely hold the guitar pick.”
“You?” The image of confident, easygoing Marco shattered and reformed into something more complex, more human.
“Yeah. The curated canvas,” he said, echoing her earlier phrase. “Mine’s just… a stage persona. The guy who’s not terrified.”

They walked out into the cool, late-night air when the final chord faded, both a little dazed. The street was quiet, the city’s roar a distant hum. He walked her to her bus stop, and they stood under the flickering fluorescent light, the world reduced to this small, illuminated circle.

“I had the best time,” he said, his voice softer now.
“Me too,” she breathed. “It was… real.”

He hesitated, then reached out and brushed a stray strand of hair from her forehead. The touch was electric. “You’re incredible, Lila. You’re so… fully yourself. It’s inspiring.”

She smiled, a true, unguarded smile that reached her eyes. “So are you.”

He leaned in, and for a second, she thought he might kiss her. But he didn’t. He pulled back, shoving his hands into his pockets. “I should… let you get home.”
“Yeah. Okay.”

The bus arrived with a hiss of brakes. As she stepped on, she turned back. “Marco?”
He looked up from the sidewalk. “Yeah?”
“Thank you. For not thinking I was crazy.”

He smiled, but it didn’t quite reach his eyes. “Never.”

She found a seat by the window and watched him recede into the night, a solitary figure under the streetlamp. The elation of the evening began to curdle, subtly, into something else. The high of being seen had crested, and now she was left with the quiet, familiar ache of uncertainty. *He has a stage persona too*, she thought. *We’re all just performing*. The difference was, she had just handed him the script to her backstage chaos.

The next morning, the silence was deafening. She waited for a text. Nothing. She told herself he was sleeping, that he was busy, that underground musicians had unconventional schedules. But a cold knot of dread formed in her stomach. By afternoon, she could bear it no longer. She sent a simple, breezy message: *Had a great time last night. Hope you’re not too hungover.*

Hours passed. No reply.

The following day, the same. On the third day, she saw him. He was playing a set at a different, more mainstream venue. She went, telling herself she was just a fan of the music now, not a hopeful girl. She stood at the back, watching him command the stage, a confident, smiling performer. He was brilliant. But when his eyes scanned the crowd, they slid right over her, as if she were part of the wallpaper. The curated canvas had slammed down, hard and final.

After the show, she lingered by the merch table, her heart pounding a frantic rhythm against her ribs. He was signing a vinyl for a girl in a trendy band t-shirt. When he finished, he looked up, and his gaze met Lila’s. For a fraction of a second, there was a flash of something—guilt? Panic?—before it was replaced by polite, distant recognition.

“Hey,” he said, his voice all business. “Thanks for coming.”
“I… your set was amazing,” she managed.
“Thanks.” He gave a small, tight smile. “Well, I should… pack up.”

And that was it. He turned his back and began stacking cables, leaving her standing there, the invisible girl once more. The rejection was not a shout; it was a quiet, devastating whisper. *You were too much. The mess was too big. The canvas cracked, and I couldn’t bear to look at the broken pieces underneath.*

She walked home in a daze, the vibrant green dress feeling like a costume she’d been foolish to wear. The journal in her bag felt like a lead weight, a testament to her own embarrassing vulnerability. She had confessed. She had been real. And the world, in the form of Marco, had flinched.

That night, she didn’t write a poem. She didn’t cook. She opened her blog, stared at the pristine interface, and felt a wave of revulsion. The curated canvas was a prison, but the uncurated self, it seemed, was a monster too terrifying for even a kindred spirit to love. She closed the laptop. The silence in her apartment was no longer peaceful; it was an accusation.

The messy confession had been made. And the only thing it had truly revealed was the depth of her own loneliness, now magnified by the echo of a connection that had, for one fleeting night, promised to fill it.

## Chapter 16: The Fashion Break

The blue light of the smartphone screen felt like a surgical lamp, exposing every pore, every imperfection, and every lie of Lila’s digital existence. For three years, her life had been a series of carefully composed frames: a latte placed at a precise forty-five-degree angle next to a leather-bound notebook; a shot of her silhouette against a brutalist concrete wall, wearing a dress that cost more than her monthly rent but felt like a straitjacket. 

She looked at the "Post" button on her latest draft—a photo of a minimalist cream-colored blazer paired with a stark white backdrop. The caption was a curated fragment of a poem about longing, polished until the raw emotion had been sanded down into something palatable, something *aesthetic*.

Then, she thought of Marco.

She thought of the way he had looked at her journal—the actual, physical object with its smeared edges, the grease stains from late-night sandwiches, and the frantic, ink-blotted scribbles of a woman trying to find herself in the tang of a condiment. He had called it "fascinating" in the moment, but the subsequent silence had been deafening. The distance that had grown between them over the last few weeks wasn't a wall; it was a fog, thick and suffocating, born from the realization that her eccentricity might have been a bridge too far even for him.

Lila’s thumb hovered over the screen. A sudden, visceral wave of nausea hit her. Not a physical sickness, but a spiritual one. She felt a profound disgust for the girl in the photo—the girl who curated her sadness to gain likes, the girl who treated her identity like a mood board.

With a sharp, decisive motion, she didn't hit "Post." Instead, she deleted the draft. Then, she logged out.

The silence that followed was immediate and terrifying. For the first time in years, Lila wasn't performing for an invisible audience. She wasn't thinking about how her current mood would translate into a story or how her outfit would be perceived by the fashion elite of her followers. She was simply a twenty-four-year-old woman sitting in a room that smelled faintly of garlic and vinegar, wearing an oversized t-shirt with a mysterious yellow stain on the hem.

*Who am I when no one is watching?* she wondered.

The question echoed in the hollow of her chest. For so long, the answer had been "whatever the algorithm wants." She had spent her youth constructing a museum of herself, but she had forgotten to actually live inside the building.

Over the next few days, the "Fashion Break" became a sanctuary. It started with the clothes. Lila walked to her wardrobe—a curated collection of avant-garde pieces, architectural silhouettes, and high-fashion staples—and felt a sudden aversion to them. They felt like costumes for a play she no longer wanted to act in. She pushed the blazers and the structured skirts to the back of the closet, burying them under a mountain of oversized sweaters and worn-out leggings.

She stopped checking her notifications. The phantom vibration of her phone in her pocket became a ghost she chose to ignore. The anxiety of "staying relevant" was replaced by a heavy, grounding solitude. It was a lonely kind of peace, but it was honest.

She retreated into the only two things that didn't ask her to be a version of herself: poetry and mayonnaise.

It sounds absurd, perhaps even pathetic, to the outside observer. But for Lila, the mayonnaise was more than a condiment; it was a metaphor for the uncurated life. It was creamy, pungent, messy, and utterly devoid of pretension. You couldn't "style" mayonnaise. You couldn't make it look minimalist. It was what it was: a chaotic emulsion of oil and egg, a tangy defiance of the sterile world.

She spent her afternoons in the kitchen, the space she had previously avoided because it was "too messy" for her brand. Now, she embraced the chaos. She experimented with the vintage recipes she had discovered, whisking by hand until her wrist ached, adding pinches of smoked paprika, drops of lemon juice, and unconventional herbs. 

She began to write again, but not for the blog. She wrote in the Mayonnaise Journal, the pages swelling with the weight of her unfiltered thoughts. 

*I am a smudge on a white canvas,* she wrote one Tuesday afternoon, her handwriting jagged and hurried. *I have spent my life trying to be the line—straight, clean, intentional. But the smudge is where the truth lives. The smudge is where the oil meets the paper. The smudge is where I finally breathe.*

Her poetry shifted. The lyrical, flowery verse that had once earned her critiques for "overcomplicating emotions" vanished. In its place came something raw, almost guttural. She wrote about the smell of old bookstores, the sound of a bass guitar vibrating in a ribcage, and the specific, heartbreaking loneliness of being loved for a mask you spent years perfecting.

She wrote about Marco.

She wrote about the way his distance felt like a slow leak in a tire—not a sudden crash, but a gradual descent until she was scraping the pavement. She poured her longing into the ink, describing the void he had left not as a hole, but as a presence. He was the ghost in her kitchen, the silence between the lines of her poems.

Si aprì a una pagina fresca e iniziò a scrivere:

*Silice e ombra, perfettamente abbiniate,*
*Ogni momento scelto con cura, preparato,*
*Ma cosa si nasconde nel quadro studiato?*
*Un cuore che dice un altro nome—*
*Non solitudine, ma l’aspirazione luminosa,*
*Per un legame che avverte la giusta intensità.**

La scrittura scorreva senza fatica, come sempre, quando lasciava cadere la sua protezione. La poesia era sempre stata la sua salvezza dalla facciata, da quando aveva scopierto che ordina le parole per rendere comprensibile l’irrequietezza emotiva.

La felicità era questa: ogni giorno la cui giornata era una creazione, non solo per i social.

## "La Prima Note"

I luci neon del concerto indie danzavano come un battito di cuore nella stanza oscurata. Lila sedeva su una sedia capovolta, i suoi dita danzavano sul suo diario in pelle, tracciando versetti con scritture a matita. Le sue pensieri calavano fuori, in cursive. 

Man mano che riempiva le pagine con i suoi pensieri poetici, si diffuse tra le file una commozione – l'audience scorreva con energia, la musica echeggiava nello spazio. Lila si buttò verso il palco per catturare l'emozione grezza del palco, gli occhi marroni brillavano di anticipazione. 

E poi accadde. Al momento dell'ultimo strum di chitarra, Lila inciampò con l'elbamo del suo zaino. Il peso si spostò e c'era una cascata di mayonnaise che si spruzza dal bagaglio, schizzare sulle pagine del suo diario.

La stanza si zuffò, i mormorii dell'audience si trasformarono in un sospiro mormorando. I volti di Lila bruciavano, il suo cuore si abbassò in una fossa di disperazione. Alzò gli occhi, cercando di trovare un'spiegazione – ma non c'era.

L'artista sul palco catturò l'agitazione, e per un momento, perde una nota, il suo solito abbaglio venne sostituito dall'anonimato. Il cuore di Lila si piegò, una sensazione così pesante che non riusciva più a respirare. Guardò giù sulle sue maniche macchiate di mayonnaise, la prova del suo colpo di scena, e si sentì un'ondata di imbarazzo.

Mentre l'audience cominciò ad allontanarsi, Lila si alzò, le sue gambe tremavano mentre cercava di nascondere la sua vergogna. Teneva il suo giaccone perfetto, cercando di nascondere le cuffie bagnate di mayonnaise, ma il gesto sembrava solo peggiorare le cose.

"Ciao, sono io!", gridò un ragazzo, la sua voce tagliò attraverso il caos. Si avvicinò a lei, i capelli neri caddero naturalmente sul suo viso. "Sono Marco, ho visto il tuo diario. Sei la ragazza in video, giusto? Saresti come super talentuosa e tutto."

Lila sbuffò, sorpresa dalla sua entusiasmazione. Si strinse, poi smise di sorridere. "Grazie. Amo creare poesia, sai?"

"Sì, capisco esattamente" Marco indicò il suo diario, ormai macchiato di mayonnaise. "Camminare su uova in guscio è la maniera migliore per descriverlo, onestamente."

Lila rise, la tensione nei suoi spalle si allentò. "Forse avrei dovuto sollevarmi le braccia, eh?"

Marco rise, e in quel momento, Lila si sentì un'ondata di sollievo. Guardò il suo diario, ora un po' più macchiato, ma non completamente roto. Poi il mayonnaise bagnò la pagina, e un senso di libertà le si avvolse.

"Era un incidente, dopo tutto", mormorò. "Non tutto deve essere perfetto."

Marco annuì, gli occhi pieni di comprensione. "Esattamente, Lila. A volte, i disordini sono ciò che rende le cose interessanti."

Si guardarono, parlando, finché il concerto si concluse con il battito finale, segnando la fine della notte di festa. Lila e Marco scambiarono i loro numeri, promettendo di rimanere in contatto e di continuare a creare. 

Quando Lila uscì dalla locanda, si sentì un'ondata di sollievo. La mayonnaise spaldata sul suo diario aveva fatto da catalizzatore al cambiamento, un punto di svolta nella sua viaggio verso la propria accettazione e creatività.

Guardò giù sulle maniche, ricordando le parole di Marco - "Tutto deve essere perfetto?" Scosse la testa, sorrise quando si rese conto che accettare la messaggine della vita era la chiave vera al trovare l'amore. 

E forse, e forse, era già stata trovata.

## Capitolo 3:La moda come seconda lingua  

L’invito era arrivato sotto forma di uno scivolo digitale di eleganza minimale: sfondo color crema, carattere serif antracite e la vaga promessa di “estetiche trasgressive”. Per Lila, l’inaugurazione di una galleria non era un semplice impegno sociale; era una performance. Nell’ecosistema della sua vita curata, dove ogni quadrato di Instagram era una pennellata calcolata di aspirazione, l’apertura di una mostra era il palco definitivo. Era lì che il confine tra osservatore e osservato si sfumava, e Lila aveva intenzione di essere la seconda.  

Passò tre ore a prepararsi. Il suo rituale era una forma di liturgia: l’applicazione precisa di un tratto di eyeliner che avrebbe potuto tagliare il vetro, la stratificazione accurata di profumi che sapevano di sandalo e vecchie biblioteche, e la scelta di un outfit che fungeva da manifesto. Scelse un pezzo strutturale e d’avanguardia: un blazer bianco scultoreo con spalle esagerate e un orlo asimmetrico che sfidava la gravità, abbinato a collant velati e tacchi architettonici che la fecero sentire come uno schizzo prendere vita.  

Fu una scelta audace. Fu una scelta rumorosa. Fu una scelta progettata per comunicare un vocabolario preciso: *Sono sofisticata, sono audace e ho il controllo totale della mia immagine.*  

Eppure, mentre usciva di casa nell’aria umida della sera, un brivido di ansia le agitò il petto. Era una sensazione fantasma, un residuo della notte del concerto indie. Guardò le maniche. Aveva strofinato il tessuto del suo blazer preferito di quella notte, ma il ricordo della macchia di maionese rimaneva — una macchia spettrale nella sua psiche. Per la prima volta nella sua vita, la perfezione del suo abbigliamento sembrava un costume anziché una pelle.  

*Mi sto nascondendo dietro i vestiti?* si chiese, mentre il suo riflesso nello specchio del corridoio la fissava con una precisione quasi clinica. *O sto finalmente parlando una lingua che la gente capisce?*  

La galleria si trovava in un magazzino riconvertito nel quartiere artistico, uno spazio di cavernosi pavimenti di cemento e pareti bianche accecanti che sembravano inghiottire il suono. Entrando, fu subito avvolta dal basso ronzio di conversazioni curate — la cadenza ritmica di “interessante”, “provocativo” e “sovversivo”. L’aria sapeva di champagne costoso e del sentore metallico della vernice fresca.  

Lila si diresse tra la folla con una grazia studiata, la testa alta, lo sguardo che vagava tra le tele astratte. Sentì l’ebbrezza familiare dell’essere vista. Poteva percepire gli occhi degli altri partecipanti che scandagliavano la sua silhouette, misurando le linee del suo blazer rispetto alle tendenze attuali. In quell’ambiente, la moda era il dialetto primario. Indossare la silhouette giusta significava segnalare l’appartenenza a un club invisibile; sbagliare calcolo significava essere esiliati alla periferia.  

Attraversò la sala come un fantasma in una galleria di specchi, annuendo cortesemente a conoscenti di cui ricordava a malapena i nomi e sorridendo a sconosciuti che la guardavano come se fosse un’altra opera di installazione. Per un po’, la facciata tenne. Sentì il potere inebriante della sua persona curata — la “Poetessa della Moda”, la ragazza che viveva all’intersezione tra versi e velluto.  Ma con il procedere della serata, il silenzio sotto il rumore iniziò a crescere.  

Lila si ritrovò davanti a una gigantesca tela monocromatica — uno studio di grigi e neri che sembrava la rappresentazione visiva di un sospiro. Accanto a lei c’era un gruppo di coetanei: studenti di moda e aspiranti curatori che aveva incontrato nelle sfere digitali del suo blog. Erano vestiti con l’uniforme di distacco intellettuale — lini neri oversize, gioielli d’argento massicci ed espressioni di noia studiata.  

“Lo spazio negativo è quasi aggressivo, non trovate?” osservò una di loro, una ragazza di nome Clara, la cui estetica era affilata quanto il suo tono.  

Lila spostò il peso, i tacchi che cliccavano sul cemento. “Penso che rifletta un certo tipo di solitudine,” rispose, la voce bassa ma ferma. “Il modo in cui il grigio sfuma nel nero… sembra un ricordo che svanisce in tempo reale.”  

Il gruppo tacque. Per un battito di ciglia, Lila sentì una scintilla di connessione genuina. Era uscita dall’estetica per entrare nell’emozione. Aveva offerto un pezzo del suo mondo interiore — la poesia cruda e non curata che viveva nei margini dei suoi quaderni.  

Poi, lo sguardo di Clara scivolò verso il basso.  

Lila aveva dimenticato, o forse aveva inconsciamente ignorato, un dettaglio. Nella fretta di essere audace, aveva indossato una sciarpa di seta vintage drappeggiata artisticamente sulla spalla, un pezzo recuperato in un negozio dell’usato. Ma sul bordo del tessuto, appena visibile a meno che non si cercasse un difetto, c’era una piccola macchia giallastra — un residuo della sua recente flirtazione con la “disordinatezza”, una minuscola goccia di maionese trasferitasi dalle dita durante uno spuntino notturno frenetico.  

Era un’imperfezione microscopica. Un glitch nella matrice della sua perfezione.  

L’arcata sopracciliare di Clara si sollevò. Un piccolo sorriso consapevole giocò sulle sue labbra — non un sorriso di complicità, ma di scoperta.  

“Oh, Lila,” mormorò Clara, la voce che gocciolava di una finta preoccupazione che sembrava un ago. “Amo l’integrità strutturale del blazer. Davvero. Ma… quella è una macchia sulla tua sciarpa? Sembra quasi… organica. Come un condimento?”  

Il silenzio che seguì fu assordante. Gli altri membri del gruppo si avvicinarono, gli occhi discendevano come avvoltoi sulla piccola, pallida macchia di tessuto. In un istante, la narrazione cambiò. Lila non era più la “Musa d’Avanguardia”; era la ragazza con il condimento sulla couture.  Lila sentì un vampore di imbarazzo le salire lungo il collo, contrastando violentemente con l’avorio freddo del suo blazer. Le spalle strutturate che pochi istanti prima sembravano un’armatura ora sembravano una gabbia. L’audacia del suo outfit, che aveva inteso come segnale di forza, improvvisamente sembrava una barzelletta — una battuta di cui tutti gli altri conoscevano il finale.  

*Sono invisibile,* realizzò con una chiarezza improvvisa e lancinante. *Non stanno vedendo me. Stanno vedendo la macchia.*  

L’ironia era soffocante. Aveva passato ore a costruire un’identità visiva per assicurarsi di essere notata, eppure l’unica cosa che aveva davvero attirato l’attenzione era l’unica cosa che non aveva intenzione di mostrare. Il “disordine” che aveva iniziato ad abbracciare nella privacy della sua poesia era ora un marchio esterno, un segno di fallimento in un mondo che adorava il sterile.  

“È solo un po’… qualcosa,” balbettò Lila, la sua sicurezza evaporando. “Non me ne ero accorta.”  

“Certo,” replicò Clara, sebbene i suoi occhi rimanessero fissi sulla macchia. “È una scelta molto… *coraggiosa*. Molto ‘decostruita’. È un commento sul consumo del domestico all’interno dello spazio espositivo?”  

Il gruppo scoppiò in un riso leggero e friabile. Non stavano elogiandola; stavano prendendosi gioco di lei sotto le spoglie di un’analisi artistica. Stavano parlando la lingua della galleria, ma la usavano per cancellarla.  Lila fece un passo indietro, la spazio intorno a lei improvvisamente apparire troppo stretto. Si rifugiò in un angolo silenzioso vicino a una ventilazione. Appoggiò la schiena al muro freddo, il petto ansante. Guardò la piccola macchia sulla sciarpa. Per la prima volta, non sentì l’urgenza di nasconderla.  

*Perché ho così paura di una macchia?* si chiese. *Perché una goccia di maionese ha più potere sulla mia identità di mille versi di poesia?*  

Chiuse gli occhi e immaginò la sua vita come una tela. Per anni aveva usato una penna a inchiostro fine, tracciando linee precise e sottili, assicurandosi che nulla oltrepassasse i bordi. Aveva curato la sua moda, il suo feed e le sue amicizie per aderire a un’estetica di “desiderio e lusso”. Ma la bellezza dell’arte — arte vera — è nella parola sbagliata. È nella goccia di vernice che rovina la composizione ma le dona anima. È nella macchia che prova che una mano umana ha toccato l’opera.  

Mentre stava lì, un estraneo passò — un uomo con una giacca di cuoio consumata che sembrava aver vissuto un decennio di pioggia e viaggi. Non sembrava appartenere alla galleria; sembrava essere arrivato da un’altra epoca. Si fermò un attimo, osservò l’arte e poi i suoi occhi si posarono su Lila.  

Non guardò il suo blazer. Non guardò le sue scarpe. E quando il suo sguardo scorse la macchia sulla sciarpa, non sorrise né derise. Semplicemente annuì, un piccolo gesto enigmatico di riconoscimento, come se avesse riconosciuto un compagno di viaggio nel regno dell’imperfetto.  

Non parlò, non rimase. Semplicemente si allontanò, sparendo di nuovo nella folla di lini monocolor.  

Lila rimase nell’angolo per molto tempo, osservando la festa svolgersi. Realizzò che la sua moda era diventata una seconda lingua, ma era una lingua che usava per tenere le persone a distanza. Parlava in “couture” per evitare di parlare in “verità”. Il blazer era la sua corazza, sperando che, se fosse stata abbastanza perfetta, nessuno avrebbe notato la solitudine che le attaccava alla pelle come una seconda pelle.  

Quando finalmente lasciò la galleria, non chiamò un'auto. Camminò verso casa attraverso la città, i tacchi architettonici che batterono un ritmo costante contro il selciato. L’aria notturna le sembrò più fresca, più vivificante.  

Una volta rientrata, non si tolse subito l’outfit. Si fermò davanti allo specchio e guardò la macchia. Era piccola, assurda e fuori luogo. Era un pezzo di mundano che intrudeva sulla magnificenza.  

Lentamente, Lila afferrò il suo quaderno. Non scrisse un poema sull’arte o sull’atmosfera della galleria. Scrisse una sola riga:  

*La cosa più onesta che ho indossato stasera è una macchia.*  

Sorrise, un’espressione genuina e non curata che non cercava la macchina fotografica. Si rese conto che l’invisibilità che aveva provato in galleria non dipendeva dal non essere abbastanza, ma dal tentativo di essere qualcosa che non esisteva. Stava cercando di essere un dipinto quando, in realtà, era una poesia — disordinata, ritmica e piena di contraddizioni.  

Mentre sfilò il blazer scultoreo, provò una strana sensazione di liberazione. La “Poetessa della Moda” era ancora lì, ma la “Ragazza che ama la Maionese” cominciava a reclamare il suo posto a tavola. E per la prima volta, Lila si chiese cosa accadrebbe se smettesse di tradursi per il mondo e cominciasse semplicemente a parlare.

## Capitolo 4: Il dilemma della poetessa

Il "Velvet Bean" era quel tipo di caffè che diffondeva costantemente l'aroma di cannella bruciata e disperazione. Era un rifugio per gli incompresi, o per quelli che aspiravano al titolo. La luce era una nebbia dolce e opaca, filtrata dai felci pendenti e dal vapore di una dozzina di macchine da caffè. Per Lila, era la perfetta cornice.

Aveva passato due ore a prepararsi per l'apertura di mic. La sua eleganza indossava l'"intellettuale senza sforzo": una gonna grigia a vita alta che sfiorava il suolo, una camicetta di seta crema con un fiocco al collo e un paio di mocassini vintage che la stringevano i piedi ma sembravano meraviglioso nelle foto. Era come un'opera preraffaellita reinventata per un panorama urbano moderno.

Ma tra la seta e la precisione, Lila vibrava.

Al suo cintura stringeva un piccolo taccuino rilegato in pelle. I bordi erano sfilacciati e, se si guardava con attenzione — una attenzione particolare — c'era una macchia giallastra nell'angolo di pagina dodici. Un fantasma dell'incidente di maionese al concerto. Per qualcuno era una macchia; per Lila, era diventato un talismano segreto. Era l'unica cosa nel suo attuale ensemble che non era stata curata. Era una ferita nel suo profilo perfetto, e questo la costava la pelle.

*Sono troppo?* si chiese, fissando la piccola folla raccolta attorno al palco improvviso — un palo di legno coperto da un tappeto persiano sbiadito. *O non abbastanza?*

Era passata settimane a perfezionare la poesia che intendeva leggere. Era un pezzo sulle strutture della nostalgia, che utilizzavano metafore di scaffali e cattedrali di vetro per descrivere la sensazione di aspettare qualcuno che forse non sarebbe mai arrivato. Era lirico, denso e pensato. Aveva scelto ogni parola con cura, sostituendole con aggettivi che risvegliavano la memoria di vecchie biblioteche e pietre bagnate dalla pioggia. Voleva che l'audience sentisse il peso della sua solitudine, ma voleva che lo sentisse attraverso un filtro d'eleganza.

"Avanti in fila," annunciò l'ospite, un uomo con una barba sparsi e una voce simile al grappolo di mais, "è Lila."

Lila si alzò sul palco. La sensazione di salire sul palco sembrava l'ascesa di una montagna. Il riflettore — un'unica lampadina tremolante — la faceva brillare, proiettando lunghe ombre drammatiche sul suo volto. Sentì il desiderio improvviso di nascondersi. Voleva dipingersi in quella camicetta di seta, scomparire nell'immagine che aveva costruito.

Schiarì la gola, il suono che risuonava per l'attimo di silenzio nella stanza.

"Si intitola 'La geometria dell'assenza'," esordì.

La sua voce era ferma, ma il cuore era un uccello frenetico che batteva contro le pareti di una gabbia. Lesse con ritmo preciso, il suo ritmo che sembrava il battito di un orologio. Parlò di *silenzi cristallini* e del *peso ossidiana di una sedia vuota*. Usò parole come *evanescente* e *labirintico*. Versò la sua anima nei versi, ma lo fece attraverso una maschera linguistica. Non stava semplicemente dicendo che era sola; stava presentando la sua solitudine come un'opera d'arte raffinata, incorniciata in foglia d'oro e lucidata per i riflettori.

Quando raggiunse l'ultima strofa — il culmine delle metafore sull'intersezione tra dolore e grazia — alzò lo sguardo.

Vide un giovane uomo in prima fila, che indossava un dolcevita nero e occhiali da lettura. Non stava annuendo. Non era commossa. Era imperioso, il capo inclinato leggermente da lato, come se stesse cercando di risolvere un problema di matematica particolarmente fastidioso.

Lila terminò la poesia. Il silenzio che seguì non era quello pesante e rispettoso di un pubblico affascinato. Era il silenzio imbarazzante e vuoto di persone che non conoscevano bene come reagire.

Si diffuse un applauso cortese, ma sembrava poco convinto.

"Grazie, Lila," disse l'ospite, tornando sul tappeto. "Qualcuno ha delle opinioni? E' un giro di feedback stasera."

Di solito, le persone dicevano solo "Bene fatto" o "Molto commovente". Ma l'uomo con il dolcevita alzò la mano.

"Ho una domanda," disse, la sua voce fredda e al disaggio. "Lila, ho sentito la tua abilità nel pezzo. Il vocabolario è impressionante. Ma devo saperlo... perché le scaffalature? Perché le cattedrali di vetro?"

Lila si strinse nelle spalle. "Io... usavo le scaffalature come metafore per la struttura della nostalgia."

L'uomo fece una smorfia, un gesto di impotenza. "Questo è il problema. Mi è sembrato che ti stessi nascondendo dietro le parole. Stai complicando eccessivamente l'emozione. La solitudine non è un 'peso ossidiana labirintico'. La solitudine è solo... voler tenere la mano di qualcuno mentre si mangia un sandwich. Indossando l'emozione con un linguaggio così costoso, hai svuotato l'onestà. Sembrava falsa. Come una brochure di un sentimento, piuttosto che il sentimento stesso."

La stanza cambiò. Alcune persone annuirono. Una ragazza in fondo sussurrò qualcosa alla sua amica, e Lila capì la parola "pregiata".

L'uomo sembrava inclinarsi. La luce ambrata del caffè divenne improvvisamente soffocante, l'odore di cannella diventò pesante e opulento. Lila sentì un calore raccogliere il suo collo, contrastante con il color crema della camicetta.

Non sapeva come rispondere. Le sue intenzioni erano difendere l'arte, spiegare che la complessità era il punto — che la bellezza del linguaggio era l'unico modo che conosceva per rendere il dolore sopportabile. Ma guardando l'uomo, capì che l'aveva vista attraverso. Non aveva visto la "Poetessa"; aveva visto la ragazza che si stava sforzando di essere una poetessa.

Si diresse verso la finestra, i mocassini che scivolavano leggermente sul pavimento. Non guardò l'ospite. Non guardò il pubblico. Si diresse verso l'angolo della stanza, cercando il comfort di una poltrona di velluto che nell'arredamento di vecchio tabacco.

*Curata.*

La parola continuava a riecheggiare nella sua mente, come un tamburo ritmico. Guardò il suo taccuino, la macchia di maionese a pagina dodici.

## Capitolo 5 – Mayo come musa  

La pioggia aveva trasformato i marciapiedi della città in nastri d’argento, ogni pozzanghera catturava il bagliore al neon delle insegne delle boutique e il lampo occasionale di un’auto che sfrecciava. Lila strinse il trench in lana‑mistura più attorno alle spalle, il colletto sfiorava la nuca dove qualche ciocca ribelle si era sfuggita dal raccolto ordinato che amava portare. Passò davanti alla vetrina del negozio dell’usato, dove un manichino indossava un vestito di seta ispirato agli anni ’70, le maniche spalancate come le ali di una falena. L’immagine le fece sorridere: le ricordava quanto fosse facile per lei scivolare tra epoche diverse proprio come tra hashtag.

Era di nuovo in ritardo. La scadenza del prossimo post sul blog le pulsava nella mente come un metronomo, sempre più forte ad ogni passo. “Ancora cinque minuti”, sussurrò a sé stessa, benché la piccola voce ostinata nella testa sapeva che quel limite fosse già superato. Girò l’angolo su Rue de la Liberté, dove l’odore di caffè appena fatto si mescolava al profumo nostalgico di carta vecchia che sembrava trasudare dalla facciata screpolata di un minuscolo negozio che non aveva mai notato prima.

La vetrina del negozio era stipata di oggetti disparati: un telescopio di ottone, una pila di cartoline d’epoca, una tazzina di porcellana con il bordo scheggiato e, al centro, un libro rilegato in pelle logoro dal titolo quasi illeggibile: *Le Recettes de la Maïonnaise d’Antan*. Il respiro le si fermò per un attimo. L’aveva superato cento volte, ma non aveva mai provato la curiosità di entrare. Ora la pioggia l’aveva spinta verso quel caldo interno, e il libro le parlava come se contenesse la risposta a una domanda che non aveva ancora formulato.

Spinse la porta, e una campanellina tintinnò sopra la sua testa. Il negozio era un santuario di cose dimenticate. Gli scaffali cedevano sotto il peso di giornali ingialliti, scatole di latta piene di bottoni e file di vasetti di vetro carichi di erbe secche che sussurravano di cucine ormai chiuse. Dietro il banco, una donna anziana con i capelli d’argento raccolti in un morbido nodo osservava, gli occhi scintillanti dietro occhiali rotondi.

«Bonjour, ma chère», disse la donna, la voce dolce come lo sfogliare di una pagina. «Cerchi qualcosa in particolare?»

Lila di nuovo occhi sul libro, le dita tremanti per toccarne la copertina consumata. «Io… ho visto quel libro sulla maionese in vetrina. Sono… curiosa.»

La proprietaria sorrise, una ruga formandosi tra le sopracciglia. «Ah, il vecchio libro di mayo. È qui più a lungo di molte altre cose. Apparteneva alla mia bisnonna, una chef che credeva che una maionese perfetta potesse cambiare una vita.»

Un brivido strano le attraversò il petto, come se l’idea di un semplice condimento potesse davvero spostare l’asse della sua esistenza. «Posso vederlo?»

La donna afferrò sotto il banco una piccola scatola di legno, levigata dagli anni di manipolazione. Sollevò il coperchio, rivelando il tomo di pelle annidato tra alcuni boccioli di lavanda appassiti. Le dita di Lila sfiorarono la copertina, e un lieve profumo burroso si sollevò—salato, pungente e stranamente confortante.

Aprì il libro alla prima pagina, dove una nota scritta a mano in elegante corsivo recitava: *«Pour la vie, pour l’amour, pour l’art.»* Sotto, un elenco di ingredienti si stendeva sulla pagina con inchiostro sbiadito ma ancora leggibile: tuorli d’uovo, senape di Digione, aceto di vino bianco, olio d’oliva, un pizzico di sale e—soprattutto—un tocco di scorza di limone.

La mente di Lila corse. Sentiva quasi lo scoppio di una bottiglia che si sventola, il sibilo dell’olio che incontra il tuorlo, il dolce movimento di una frusta. Immaginava la cremosità spalmata su una fetta di pane a lievitazione naturale, il limone che trafiggeva la ricchezza con una freschezza pulita. Pensò alla sua vita—strati di immagini lucide e parole scelte con cura, ogni strato una patina lucida che nascondeva il caos sottostante. Quella semplice salsa poteva essere una metafora di qualcosa di più? Poteva, per una volta, lasciar entrare il disordine?

*Nella ciotola del silenzio,  
le uova sussurrano al vento;  
il fuoco della senape accende la notte,  
l’olio scivola come il sospiro di un amante.  
Il bacio del limone—tagliente, luminoso—

Gli occhi di Lila si spalancarono. Il verso risuonava con una parte di sé che raramente lasciava emergere. L’immagine dell’olio che fluttua come un sospiro d’amante riecheggiava il suo andare fluttuante tra i caffè, spargendo pensieri su tovagliolini, sperando che qualcuno cogliesse il senso. Chiuse delicatamente il libro, avvertendo il peso delle sue storie nelle mani. Richiede pazienza, equilibrio e la volontà di far sì che gli ingredienti si arrendano l’uno all’altro.»

Lila rise, un suono che la sorprese. «Sono una poetessa, ma non ho mai pensato a una salsa come a una poesia.»

Il sorriso della donna si allargò. «Allora forse è il momento di scrivere una poesia sulla maionese.»

Fuori, la pioggia si era trasformata in una nebbia leggera, il brusio della città ormai un sottofondo ovattato. Lila infilò il libro sotto il braccio, ringraziò la commessa e tornò in strada. Il mondo sembrava un po’ più luminoso, come se le nuvole si fossero aprite appena abbastanza da far filtrare un raggio di sole sui ciottoli.

Camminò verso casa, le pagine del libro fruscianti contro la coscia. L’appartamento che condivideva con sé stessa—pareti tappezzate di moodboard, una scrivania ingombra di taccuini, un piccolo balcone affacciato su un vicolo ricoperto di graffiti—era un santuario di caos curato. Depose il libro sul tavolino da caffè, il suo peso riecheggiò nello spazio silenzioso. Il ticchettio della pioggia contro i vetri era ora un ritmo rilassante, in sintonia con il battito del suo cuore.

Mise sul fuoco una pentola d’acqua, il tintinnio metallico del bollitore diventò il metronomo dei suoi pensieri. Tirò fuori una ciotola dal mobile, posò accanto una frusta e aprì il libro di maionese alla prima ricetta. Gli ingredienti erano semplici, ma le istruzioni scritte con una reverenza tale da farle sentire di stare per compiere un rito.

Rompé due uova, i loro tuorli gialli come l’alba, e li versò nella ciotola. Aggiunse un cucchiaio di Senape di Digione, il colore ambrato che si mescolava come un’alba su una città avvolta nella nebbia. Iniziò a frustare, lentamente all’inizio, il movimento costante e deliberato. La miscela si addensava, il tuorlo e la senape si legavano come i primi versi di una poesia che trovano ritmo. La salsa era perfetta, incarnazione di equilibrio—esattamente ciò che cercava nella vita, ma non aveva mai trovato nei suoi feed curati.

Posò la ciotola da parte e aprì una pagina bianca nel suo taccuino, quello che una volta si era macchiato di maionese durante un concerto. La carta era ancora umida, l’inchiostro un po’ sbavato, ma sembrava giusta. Lasciò la penna scivolare, l’inchiostro fluendo come l’olio appena frustato.

*Nel silenzio della cucina, mescolo—*  
*Uova e senape, un’unione pura.*  
*L’olio scivola, sospiro d’amante,*  
*Il bacio del limone, sorpresa luminosa.*

*Assaporo l’acidità, la dolce resa,*  
*Trovo equilibrio, il vero guaritore del cuore.*

Si fermò, le parole sospese tra le righe, e pensò a quante volte avesse cercato di bilanciare la sua immagine pubblica con il desiderio privato. Eppure lì, nella quiete della sua cucina, avvertiva una vulnerabilità che non era debolezza ma apertura—la volontà di far mescolare i sapori, di far fondere i sentimenti, di accettare il disordine che accompagna l’autenticità.

Continuò a scrivere, il poema si trasformava in qualcosa di più ampio, che superava le mura della cucina:

*Il mondo è una passerella, luci accese,*  
*Modelle sfilano, i volti lucidi come vetro.*  
*Ma sotto il luccichio, un segreto si diffonde,*  
*Una salsa di verità, dove il timore si scioglie.*

*La maionese, umile, spoglia, ma maestosa,*  
*Unisce i frammenti, gli erranti, i banali.*  
*E nel suo vortice trovo la mia voce,*

Lesse ad alta voce, le parole rimbombavano tra le pareti spoglie, riempiendo lo spazio di una risonanza che rendeva l’appartamento meno un palcoscenico e più un rifugio. Immaginò i commenti: «Così poetico!», «Mai pensato alla mayo così!», «Sei un genio!». Redasse una breve introduzione, spiegando come una passeggiata sotto la pioggia l’aveva condotta a un vecchio ricettario, come il gesto di preparare la maionese fosse diventato meditazione, e come il processo rispecchiasse la sua ricerca di equilibrio.

Si fermò, le mani sospese sopra la tastiera. Una parte di lei voleva tenere quel momento privato, un segreto custodito in un diario. Un’altra, una voce più alta e più coraggiosa, la incitava a condividere, a far vedere al mondo la versione grezza e non filtrata di sé stessa, nascosta dietro post curati e immagini ritoccate.

Mi sono sempre sentita un pasticcio, ma leggendo questo… è bellissimo. Grazie per aver condiviso la tua verità.» Lila sentì un brivido al petto, una mescolanza di sollievo ed eccitazione. Rispose: «Grazie, Luna. Che tutti noi troviamo il coraggio di frustare le nostre verità in qualcosa di delizioso.»

Scorse i commenti, ognuno un piccolo riconoscimento, un legame stretto attraverso il semplice atto di condividere una ricetta e una poesia. In fondo alla mente, immaginò un futuro in cui le linee tra moda, poesia e cibo si fossero sfumate, dove potesse indossare un vestito ispirato al vortice della maionese, dove potesse recitare i suoi versi a un concerto mentre la folla applaudiva il ritmo della sua voce, non il battito del basso.

Si appoggiò allo schienale, gli occhi che si posavano sul barattolo di mayo accanto al laptop. Il liquido dorato sembrava pulsare di una tranquilla sicurezza. Realizzò che l’assurdità che un tempo aveva cercato di nascondere—l’amore per la maionese, l’ossessione per il disordine tattile di un cucchiaio di salsa—non era un difetto ma una sfaccettatura di sé che la rendeva singularmente vibrante.

La notte si fece più profonda, le luci della città scintillavano come costellazioni lontane. La pioggia aveva lasciato le strade lucenti, ogni pozzanghera rifletteva i neon in un caleidoscopio di colori.

Pensò al prossimo concerto a cui avrebbe assistito, al prossimo poema che avrebbe scritto, al prossimo outfit che avrebbe progettato. Immaginò un vestito con un motivo che richiamasse i vortici cremosi della maionese, una passerella che profumasse appena di scorza di limone e olio d’oliva, una folla che applaudisse non per l’effimero flash della couture ma per l’autenticità di una donna che non ha paura di abbracciare le proprie stranezze.

Prese un respiro profondo, inspirando il profumo della città mescolato a quello residuo della sua esperienza in cucina. In quel momento Lila sentì una certa quieta certezza insinuarsi nelle ossa: il mondo poteva essere disordinato e bello come un barattolo di maionese, e lei era pronta a tuffarsene, cucchiaio in mano, cuore aperto, pronta a mescolare il prossimo capitolo della sua vita.

Ritornò al suo appartamento, le luci interne brillavano accoglienti, invitandola dentro. Il taccuino rimaneva sul tavolo, le pagine pronte a essere riempite. Sorrise, sentendo il primo vero, non filtrato sorriso da tempo. Il brusio quieto della città fuori sembrava echeggiare il ritmo del suo stesso battito, una cadenza costante e speranzosa che prometteva che, forse, l’amore—che fosse sotto forma di partner, di poema o di una salsa perfetta—fosse dietro l’angolo, pronto a essere scoperto nei luoghi più inaspettati.

##Capitolo 6: “L'Appuntamento che Scadde”

La prima volta che Lila vide Marco, le fu quasi come osservare un personaggio di una moda editoriale: minuta, impeccabilmente vestita con un blazer a righe di antracite che sembrava assorbire la luce invece di rifletterla, i capelli pettinati in un taglio netto che tradiva sforzo alcuno. Lei lo aveva allineato al suo ritmo con un vestito blu cobalto preso in prestito dall’armadio di una amica, il suo lembo scintillante che catturava le luci sospese del bar come stelle sparse.

L’appuntamento doveva essere senza sforzo. Una prova del suo universo curato, un sussurro interno le diceva di sistemare gli orecchini a perla e di forzare un sorriso.  

Ma “senza sforzo” era una parola che aveva imparato a temere.  

Il locale, *Bistrot Lumen*, era una speakeasy di design minimalista scandinavo, con le pareti rivestite di quercia sbiancata e il menù stampato su pergamena. Lila l’aveva scelto perché le sembrava “instagrammabile”, un posto dove il suo gusto poteva brillare. Marco aveva annuito senza esitare, il suo riso era tagliente e caldo mentre indicava un tavolo libero. “Sei quella con l’abito blu, vero?” aveva detto, i suoi occhi fissi sull’iridescenza del tessuto. “È… audace.”  *Audace* era un complimento che Lila indossava come una seconda pelle da sempre. A 24 anni aveva dominato l’arte di apparire impeccabilmente chic, un’abilità affiatata negli anni di curazione del suo feed Instagram e del suo blog di poesia. Ogni abito, ogni poema, ogni apparizione pubblica era una dichiarazione calcolata: *Sono curata, sono intenzionale, sono desiderabile.*  

Ma l’audacia di Marco non era la stessa della sua.  

All’inizio parlarono di designer avant‑garde, di come un taglio impeccabile potesse imitare le imperfezioni del corpo umano. Lila si illuminò, raccontando di un servizio fotografico fallito in cui il vestito di una modella si era impigliato nella maniglia di una porta, lasciando una spaccatura che divenne il fulcro di un post virale. Marco annuì, incuriosito. “Transformi gli incidenti in arte,” aveva detto. “È raro.”  

Si avvicinò, il suo battito regolare. Questa era la convalida che aveva desiderato da quando aveva recitato al caffè di poesia del Capitolo 4, quando aveva aperto una mostra al Capitolo 3. Per una volta qualcuno sembrava vedere sé stessa non attraverso il filtro della sua presenza online, ma come persona vera.  

Finché non lo fece.  

Accadde con la maionese.  

La conversazione era scivolata verso i panini—Lila aveva menzionato il suo preferito, un avocado con maionese fatta in casa su pane di segale, una ricetta che aveva annotato su un taccuino dopo aver scoperto un libro vintage di tecniche di maionese in una libreria d’antiquariato (Capitolo 5). Marco alzò un sopracciglio. “Maionese? Davvero?”  

Lei rimase congelata. “Non è solo maionese. È… complessa. Il modo in cui trasforma il pane ordinario in qualcosa di quasi sacro.”  

Lui rise, ma il suono fu tagliente, come una lama. “Sacro? Mi stai facendo venire fame.” Inclinandosi, la fissò. “Sei un po’ strana, Lila.”  

Le parole rimasero sospese, più fresche della stanza. Le dita di Lila si mossero nervosamente sul grembo. *Strana*. Era la parola che l’aveva perseguitata da bambina, un’etichetta che aveva cercato di nascondere dietro paillettes e sonetti. Aveva trascorso anni a costruire una versione di sé che respingesse quel termine, ma ora le veniva lanciata indietro come un boomerang.  

“Non sono strana,” rispose troppo rapidamente, la voce più alta del previsto.  

Marco non rispose. Invece, spintonò un piatto di panini verso di lei, il suo contenuto un semplice tacchino e provola. “Mangia.”  Stariò fissando il tovagliolo macchiato di maionese che aveva portato con sé, quello usato per pulirsi le mani dopo aver annotato versi al concerto indie del Capitolo 2. La maionese aveva lasciato una macchia dorata, un sigillo della sua imperfezione. Ora le sembrava una condanna.  

“I—I non penso che tu capisca,” balbettò, la voce tremante. “È parte di chi sono.”  

Marco fece un gesto noncurante. “Forse lo è. Ma tu sei qui per uscire con me, non per il mio panino speciale.”  

L’insulto non fu letterale, ma fece male. La mente di Lila corse: *Chi sono se non per le mie stravaganze?* Aveva costruito la sua identità intorno alla curatela—moda, poesia, persino il modo in cui filtrava le foto su Instagram per escludere tutto ciò che era “disordinato”. In quel momento, le sue difese crollarono.  

Si domandò se Marco fosse mai stato chiamato “troppo strano”. Se fosse cresciuto in un mondo dove l’unicità era un fardello. O se semplicemente non avesse mai incontrato qualcuno che portasse la propria eccentricità come uno scudo e una spada.  

Il silenzio del bar si fece pressante. La gola di Lila bruciò. Voleva reagire, spiegare che la sua maionese non era un difetto ma una lingua—un modo di collegarsi al mondo, ai ricordi delle insalate della nonna, al modo in cui quella leggera acidità la faceva sentire viva. Ma le parole morirono sulle labbra.  

Invece raggiunse il telefono.  Le dita si fermarono sopra lo schermo, tentate di inviare a Marco una foto del suo quaderno di maionese (un’abitudine iniziata al Capitolo 9, ma ancora non condivisa). Ma non lo fece. Non poteva. Mostrarglielo sarebbe stato un arrendersi, e arrendersi significava ammettere la sconfitta.  

Quando alzò lo sguardo, Marco la fissava, l’espressione impenetrabile. “Stai bene?”  

Lei annuì, anche se il cuore batteva a ritmo di staccato. “Sì.”  La serata finì con Lila che camminava a casa sotto la pioggia, il suo vestito blu ormai un mantello fradicio. Non mandò messaggi a Marco. Non ne parlò su Instagram. Non lo annotò nemmeno sul suo diario—non ancora. Si sedette sul balcone di ferro, infilando il tovagliolo macchiato nella borsa, e lasciò che la tempesta riflettesse il suo interno turbolento.  La mattina dopo aprì il suo blog di poesia.

*“Ho indossato il mio vestito come una seconda pelle,*  
*le sue perline catturavano luce che non mi serviva.*  
*Ma quando lui ha detto “maionese”, sono crollata—*  
*una ragazza che temeva la parola “strana” troppo per parlare.*  *Ora la porto come uno scudo, un segreto,*  
*una bottiglia di acidità in un mondo che odia le frecce.*  
*Ma forse la prossima volta porterò la bottiglia.”*  

Il post divenne virale.  

Non per la sua genialità poetica, ma perché i lettori si riconobbero in esso. Una teen-ager che nascondeva l’ansia dietro un nodo perfetto. Un uomo ossessionato dai vecchi giradischi ma terrorizzato dal giudizio per questo. Lila non si aspettava quel clamore. Aveva scritto il poema come catarsi, non come manifesto. Ma nella caotica serata, aveva scoperto qualcosa di vitale: le sue stravaganze non erano difetti, erano crepe attraverso cui la luce poteva entrare.  

Quella notte sognò la maionese.  

Fu un sogno di abbondanza— barattoli che traboccavano, la crema colante colava dalle dita mentre la condivideva con sconosciuti. Nel sogno nessuno la derise; chiedevano ricette, ridevano delle sue battute. Sorrise svegliandosi, il sapore di maionese ancora sulla lingua.

**Capitolo 7: Il Caos del Concerto**

Il festival era una creatura vivente, un mostro di suono e colori che inalmi il tramonto e ne espirò una luce neon. Lila si mise al suo cuore pulsante, un pixel curato in una palda digitale. Il suo abito, un ricordo di ottimismo degli anni '60 in giallo di zucca, era una scelta consapevole: un raggio di sole contro il profondo indaco, un desafio diretto alla notte che si avvicinava. Era una dichiarazione, come ogni cosa che indossava, una parola scritta nella lingua silenziosa del tessuto e della forma. Quella notte, non era solo una spettatrice; stava eseguendo la sua presenza, davanti a un pubblico di follower che vedrebbero solo il risultato lucido e filtrato.

Ma le sue mani raccontavano una storia diversa. Leggera, tenaci, un po’ tacquissima. Avait appena immerso le dita in quella piccola fiala di mayonnaise artigianale, custodendola come un talismano. La crema fredda e viscoso fu un colpo di terra, un ancoraggio sensoriale. Ora la prova di quella sacramento privato si stava asciugando sulla sua pelle, un leggero sfarza opalescente che rifletteva le luci del festival come se qualcosa di strano e indefinibile si aggirava lì.

La band era un muro di reverb e sguardi feraci. Lila oscillava, occhi chiusi, cercando di fondersi con la musica e far dominare da quella poesia dei suoni l'ansìa dei suoi pensieri. Stava composendo una nuova strofa nella sua mente, qualcosa su distorsione e desiderio, quando il mondo si inclinò.

Una pinzata acuta sul suo abito. Un suono come il strappo di una taffette, tagliente e sprezzante nel rombo della folla. Il suo abito, il suo raggio di sole, era caduto su un angolo di sedile piegato, lasciato in disordine da un gruppo in partenza. Per un istante assordante, era radicata, una pianta botanica pinalata su un cartellino. Poi il tessuto cedette, una lenta, vile lacerazione da un leggero incastro al confine della sua sicurezza.

Un calore dimenticato le sorrise. Lo sputacchio fu caldo, più intenso della pressione dei corpi, e ricadde sulle sue guae. Descendere fu un momento di shock e vergogna. Le sue mani, quelle tenaci, si sollevarono per riprendersi, sfiorando la grama fresca e umida. Il riso cominciò come un sussurro, poi si solidificò.

“Guarda bene,” sussurrò una voce, vicina abbastanza da sentire l’aroma di birra e glitter barato. Una ragazza con capelli come un kaleidoscopato e uno scaquille in pelle si alzò sopra di lei. “Sei caduta in un… in una bottiglia di mayonnaise? Oh Dio, è *una* mayonnaise. Sei, come se fossi *literalmente* un condimento.”

La parola *literalmente* pesò nell’aria come un colpo di pedina. L’insieme si radunò intorno a lei, un branco di angoli affilati e risa rumorosa, che intrecciò i loro piangere con il feedback dei chitarri. Lila guardò il pavimento. Le sue mani erano impacchetti di lana e il residuo spettrale del suo ispirazione. Il suo abito era strappato, un’apertura bianca che si muoveva contro la seta gialla. Non era un raggio di sole. Era un fluido.

La musica si fece un lento squillio sott'acqua. Il festival vibrante era scomparso, un’audibilità, e lei era assunta per un giudice. Quella facciata calcolata, i post Instagram perfetti, le righe poetiche, i set colorati, si frantumarono nel fango e nella mayonnaise. Questa era l’autentico sé, crude e ridicola, esposta al pubblico.

Si rialzò, ignorando la mano gentile di un estraneo a occhi pieni di compassione (una mano a cui non poteva guardare), e fuggì. ## Capitolo 8 – La Regola Silenziosa

Percorse l'abitual tratto di Via della Moda, i negozi di fronte brillavano di vernice neon e charme vintage in misura eguale, ogni vetrata una tavolozza curata delle vite che ammirava e che cercava di emularsi. *“Indossatore de seta, Cantante di May,

Chiusse il diario e posò il pennellino con un intendito clic. Prese il volantino dalla borsa, e lo sviluppò, il disegno della donna con la cima di mayonnaise che la guardava indietro. In quel momento, si sentì una risoluzione improvvisa e ferma. La regola silenziosa era stata un inganno—a barriera autoproietta che faceva di l'amore un'opera da presentare.

Si aprì una nuova pagina, il cartone liscio e ansiosa. Ma una tempesta che canta in bar,

Solo me, una penna e una jar di mayonnaise.”* Si appoggiò, i dita che inciampavano sul pulsante “condividere”. L'allarme interno per la possibile giudicata scoppiò, ma quest'epoca la trasformò in un battito—costante, intenzionale.

Premette “condividere.”

## Capítulo 9: "Il Giornale della Maionese"

## Capitolo 10: *Il Fiasco della Moda*

Il sole pendeva basso sulla città, avvolgendo il tetto in un'aura dorata. Lo shooting doveva essere una celebrazione del caos curato di Lila, ma l'aria era tesa come una corda di violino. Lo skyline si stendeva alle sue spalle — un mosaico di vetro e acciaio che rispecchiava la contrazione nel suo petto. Si mise davanti alla macchina fotografica, le mani strette ai bordi di un abito vintage che un tempo le era sembrato un atto di ribellione. Ora era solo un'altra strato di armatura. Il tessuto era verde smeraldo profondo, e il pizzo che lo orlava catturava la luce come un segreto che non era sicura di voler custodire. Al collo portava una collana fatta di piccoli barattoli di vetro pieni di maionese, le superfici traslucide che riflettevano la luce morente. Era assurdo, lo sapeva. Ma nella sua testa, era una dichiarazione. Un proclama che, anche nei momenti più patinati, portava con sé l'essenza scomposta e piccante della sua vera natura.

«Lila, la luce sta cambiando», la voce del fotografo arrivò da dietro l'obiettivo. «Aggiusta la posa — un po' più… *teatrale*.»

Sospirò. Il suo respiro si condensò nell'aria mentre indietreggiava, lasciando che il vento catturasse l'orlo del vestito. Le colleghe modelle — due ragazze sulla ventina, risate taglienti e ben studiate — la osservavano dall'angolo della stanza. Sorridevano tutte, gli occhi lucenti di quella sicurezza che lei aveva faticato tanto per imitare.

«Perfetto», disse il fotografo. «Ora proviamo lo *shooting vero e proprio*.»

Lo stomaco di Lila si contorse. Lo shooting doveva parlare della sua *eleganza*, della sua *arte*, ma nel momento in cui la luce colpì i barattoli di maionese, tutto prese una piega diversa. I barattoli, che lei speravano aggiungessero un tocco di malizia infantile, ora sembravano una barzelletta. La battuta finale. Forzò un sorriso, le dita che sfioravano il tessuto come se potessero proteggerla dal giudizio che sentiva strisciare dentro.

«Lila, le mani sono… *macchiate* di nuovo», disse il fotografo, con una punta di divertimento nella voce.

Si irrigidì. La maionese — ancora sulle sue dita dallo shooting precedente — si era trasferita sull'abito, una macchia bianca contro il verde smeraldo. Le colleghe modelle trattennero risatine, gli occhi che schizzavano tra lei e la macchina fotografica.

«È… è *arte*», disse, con una voce troppo alta.

Il sorriso del fotografo vacillò. «Arte?» Si avvicinò, il fiato caldo contro il suo orecchio. «Ne sei sicura?»

La domanda restò sospesa nell'aria, e per un istante Lila pensò a mentire. Ma le parole *arte* e *disordine* erano aggrovigliate nella sua mente, e non riusciva a districarle. Invece, annuì, con la gola secca.

Il sorriso del fotografo tornò, ma era più sottile ora. «Va bene. Proviamo l'*ultimo* scatto.»

Lila si fece avanti. Il flash della macchina fotografica la accecò. I barattoli di maionese luccicarono, le superfici che catturavano la luce come piccole stelle fragili. Ma quando lo scatto fu preso, lo vide — il modo in cui gli occhi delle colleghe modelle si soffermavano sulle sue mani, il modo in cui lo sguardo del fotografo restava sulle macchie. Non si trattava solo del disordine; era l'*idea* di lei. Il modo in cui aveva cercato di imbottigliare le sue eccentricità in un'affermazione di moda, solo per vederle derise.

Quando lo shooting finì, le colleghe si radunarono attorno a lei, le risate taglienti ma non cattive. «Sei *davvero* impegnata nel ruolo», disse una di loro, con una voce satura di ironia. «È carino. Ma magari… *meno* maionese la prossima volta?»

Le guance di Lila bruciavano. Aprì bocca per rispondere, ma le parole restarono impigliate. Invece, si voltò sui tacchi, i suoi click rimbombare sul cemento del tetto. Il brusio della città cresceva intorno a lei, ma non lo sentiva. Tutto ciò che poteva udire era l'eco della domanda del fotografo, il modo in cui le colleghe l'avevano guardata come se fosse un numero, non una persona.

Quella notte, distesa nel suo appartamento, il barattolo di maionese sul comodino che catturava la luce della luna, lo fissò. Era un simbolo, si rese conto. Un promemoria di come aveva cercato di plasmar in una versione di sé che fosse sicura, *accettabile*. Ma le macchie — sui polsini, sulle mani, sull'abito — erano le parti di lei che si rifiutavano di essere domate.

Tirò fuori il suo diario, quello che teneva nascosto sotto il letto, le pagine piene di poesie e ricette. Le ricette a base di maionese erano le più assurde, le più intime. C'era *Il Pungolo del Rimpianto*, c'erano *Panini come Consolazione*. Sfogliai le parole con il dito, il cuore che le pulsava dolente. Le aveva scritte nella speranza che qualcuno, chiunque, vedesse la bellezza nella follia. Ma ora, il pensiero di condividerle le sembrava un tradimento.

La mattina seguente, aprì il portatile, la homepage del suo blog già caricata. Il post che aveva programmato — una riflessione sulla moda e sull'arte — le sembrò vuoto. Lo aveva scritto con la speranza di creare un legame con gli altri, di trovare una comunità che apprezzasse le sue contraddizioni. Ma ora le parole le sembravano una bugia.

Invece, digitò una sola frase: *«A volte, le cose più belle sono quelle che abbiamo troppa paura di mostrare.»*

Cliccò su «Pubblica», lo schermo lampeggiò mentre il post andava in onda. Poi chiuse il portatile, le mani tremanti. Il barattolo di maionese rimase sul comodino, il coperchio ben chiuso. Per il momento, era abbastanza.

Ma quando il sole sorse il giorno dopo, non riuscì a scrollarsi di dosso la sensazione che il mondo stesse guardando. Non solo lo shooting, ma il modo in cui si era nascosta dietro l'obiettivo. Il modo in cui aveva cercato di curare la propria vita in qualcosa che sembrasse sicuro, solo per essere derisa.

Pensò a Marco, lo sconosciuto del palco aperto, e si chiese se anche lui si fosse mai sentito così. Se anche lui avesse mai provato la sensazione di essere una performance, una versione di sé che non combaciava del tutto.

Il pensiero era insieme conforto e condanna. Non sapeva se fosse pronta a farsi vedere nel disordine sotto la patina. Ma non voleva neanche continuare a nascondersi.

Il barattolo di maionese giaceva sul comodino, l'eticheta sbiadita. Era una reliquia di un'epoca in cui aveva creduto di poter controllare la narrazione. Ora si chiedeva se la vera storia non fosse nelle macchie, in quelle cose che aveva cercato di cancellare.

E forse, chissà, era abbastanza.

## Capitolo 11: Laserata di poesia aperta

Il caffè ronzava con la sua solita energia di mercoledì sera — un brusio basso di conversazioni intercalato dal tintinnio di tazzine di caffè e dall’occasionale sibilo della macchina per l’espresso. Lila era seduta nel suo solito angolo, con le dita che tamburellavano sul tavolo di legno consumato mentre fissava il quaderno davanti a lei. La pagina era vuota, tranne per una singola riga, tracciata con la sua calligrafia fluida: *“Mayo and melodies, tangled in the dark.”*  

Aveva cercato di scrivere il poema per settimane, da quando il disastro del festival musicale era accaduto. Le parole le sembravano troppo grandi, troppo grezze, troppo *troppo*. Come il resto della sua vita. Ma quella sera si era promessa di leggerlo ad alta voce. Nessun nascondersi più dietro le foto filtrate del blog o le caption curate con cura che la facevano sembrare qualcun’altra. Quella sera avrebbe cercato di essere reale.

L’apertura del mic era iniziata da venti minuti e già tre poeti si erano esibiti sul piccolo palcoscenico sul retro del caffè. Lila li osservava dal suo tavolo, le ginocchia che ballerine di nervosismo. Aveva provato il poema nella doccia, in auto, anche mentre mescolava il caffè del mattino, ma ora, sotto le luci ambrate del locale, le parole le sembravano una confessione a cui non era ancora pronta.  

*E se ridono? E se pensano che sia ridicola?* Le domande giravano nella sua mente, familiari e fastidiose. Ma pensava al quaderno che teneva nascosto — riempito di poesie su maionese e cuore spezzato, sul modo in cui la folla al festival l’aveva derisa per le mani macchiate. Pensava al modo in cui il suo appuntamento aveva fatto una smorfia quando le aveva offerto un panino con la maionese, il suo viso contorcendosi come se le avesse proposto qualcosa di obsceno.  

No. Quella sera avrebbe lasciato che le parole travolgessero, anche se erano disordinate. Anche se erano *lei*.

Il conduttore, un tizio magro con un berretto e una voce che poteva tagliare il vetro, aveva chiamato il prossimo lettore. Lo stomaco di Lila balzò. Dava quasi fastidio a quel momento, ma ora che era arrivato, provò una strana calma. Forse era il caffè, o forse era il ricordo della voce di sua nonna, dolce e ferma, che le aveva detto: *“Bambina, il mondo ha bisogno della tua verità, anche se è avvolta nella maionese e nelle metafore.”*  

Si alzò, la sedia le scricchiolò sotto, e si avvicinò al microfono. Il riflettore la colpì come un’onda, e per un attimo pensò di poter svenire. Ma aprì la bocca e iniziò a parlare.

---

*“Mayo and melodies, tangled in the dark,*  
*You’re the tang in my tea, the static in my heart.*  
*I spilled you on my notebook once, a accident of fate,*  *But now I see you’re the ink that makes my words great.*  

*At the festival, they laughed at my hands,*  *Called them ‘unclean,’ said I didn’t understand*  
*The rules of the game, the way things are done.*  
*But maybe the game’s the problem, not me and my fun.*  

*You’re the condiment of my chaos, the spread on my bread,*  
*The thing that sticks when everything else is dead.*  
*So here’s to the mess, the stains, the absurd,*  
*The things that make me, me, and no one else’s word.”*

---

Quando terminò, ci fu un attimo di silenzio. Poi, un applauso sommesso ma caloroso. Qualcuno annuì, e una donna in prima fila le gave un pollice verso. Le guance di Lila sicolorarono mentre tornava al suo tavolo, le mani che tremavano leggermente mentre riacciuffava il quaderno. Si disse di concentrarsi sul caffè, di ignorare lo sconosciuto e il suo sorriso intrigante, ma la mente le tornava al poema. Alle parole che avevano lasciato le sue labbra, come se finalmente stesse parlando con la sua voce e non con quella di qualcun altro.

Qualche minuto dopo, la serata di poesia finì e il caffè cominciò a svuotarsi. Lila rimase impigliata in un’azione di pretesto, fingendo di mandare un messaggio sul telefono, quando udì una voce alle sue spalle.

“È stato… qualcosa di diverso.”

Si voltò e trovò lo sconosciuto in piedi lì, un ragazzo alto con una maglietta di un gruppo musicale consunto e una giacca che sembrava aver vissuto melhor giorni. I suoi capelli erano scompigliati e aveva un quaderno aperto in grembo, su cui stava annotando qualcosa. Ma quando alzò lo sguardo e incrociò i suoi occhi, le regalò un sorriso. Non era quel sorriso cortese e forzato di chi vuole apparire gentile, ma un sorriso vero — ampio e luminoso, come se avesse appena ascoltato un segreto che solo lui e lei avevano capito.

Il respiro di Lila si bloccò. Non sapeva bene il motivo, ma quel sorriso le strette il petto. Guardò distrattamente il suo scialle, ma sentiva ancora i suoi occhi addosso.

Qualche minuto dopo, la serata di poesia terminò e il caffè cominciò a svuotarsi. Lila rimase immobile, fingendo di guardare il telefono, quando udì una voce alle sue spalle.

“Quello è stato… qualcosa di diverso.”

Si girò e trovò lo sconosciuto in piedi davanti a lei, con il quaderno infilato sotto il braccio. Da vicino notò la leggera macchia d’inchiostro sul suo pollice e il modo in cui i suoi occhi si incurvavano quando sorrideva.

“Grazie,” disse, la voce più ferma di quanto si sentisse. “Non sapevo se sarebbe stata accolta.”

“Accolta?” rise lui, una risata soffice e graffiante. “Mi ha colpito più di molte cose che ho sentito qui. Quella parte sul festival… davvero *hai spargi* la maionese sul quaderno?”

Lila blincolò. “Come l’hai saputo?”

“Io sono Marco,” disse, tendendo la mano. “E potrei essere ossessionato dalla maionese. È una storia lunga.”

La fissò, il cervello che lottava per elaborare questa nuova informazione. “Aspetta,… tu… ti piace la maionese?”

“La adoro. È come… l’eroe non celebrato dei condimenti. Versatile, complesso, un po’ incompreso. Un po’ come la poesia,” rispose con un sorriso, aggiungendo, “o forse sono semplicemente strano.”

Lila rise, e fu la prima risata autentica che aveva fatto in mesi. “Io sono Lila.”

“Lila,” ripeté lui, come se assaporasse il nome. “Allora, mi racconti la storia dietro il poema? O devo comprarti un panino per ottenerlo?”

Esitò, poi indicò verso l’uscita del caffè. “C’è un diner giù per strada che fa un ottimo BLT. Con maionese extra, ovviamente.”

Mentre uscivano nella fresca aria notturna, Lila percepì un cambiamento dentro di sé. Forse era il peso del poema finalmente condiviso, o forse era il modo in cui gli occhi di Marco brillavano quando menzionò il suo journal. Qualunque cosa fosse, per la prima volta da molto tempo non si sentiva più in performance. Si sentiva semplicemente… sé stessa.

E forse, solo forse, era già abbastanza.

La stanza dello spazio poetico era avvolta da un’atmosfera carica di caffè e tensione, un’ambientazione che avvolgeva il lettore in un’intensità palpabile. Lì, Lila tenace, le dita sfioravano il copertino in pelle consumato di un diario segreto, cederete alla memoria di un tempo in cui la mayonnaise era un lusso esclusivo, e le verità non filtrate degli schizzi appostati sui suoi appunti. La folla, un mosaico di volti: alcuni danzavano al ritmo del microfono, altri scrollavano smartphone, la loro attenzione fugace come i note che aveva plasmato nella sua mente.  

La sua poesia, *“Il Condimento dell’Isolamento”*, era stata una sfida. Scritta la notte prima, dopo un appuntamento esaurito e un concerto dove le sue mani erano segnate di mayo, quella stessa mania di nascondere i frammenti dell’incertezza. Oggi, invece, si era fatta partecipa, la voce tremante mentre recitava:  
*“Era stato un disastro di condimenti… ma la macchia diventa un canvass, imparare a dipingere con il disordine.”*  
Le parole sembravano estranee ad alta voce, ma la folla si fermò, un respiro condiviso. Un uomo in avanti, con capelli che sembravano fuoco ardotato, alzò la mano al suono: “Esattamente ciò che servivo,” disse, la voce intonata di meraviglia. Le sue labbra tremarono, ma proseguì.  

L’epilogo finì con la confessione del diario, custodito nascosto come un segreto. “Non si tratta solo di mayo,” rivelò lei, la voce avvolta da una forza nuova. “Tratta di ciò che ci insegnano a nascondere, di ciò che noi stessi temereò troppo di più.” Il silenzio calò, poi un brusio di applausi, lento e assoluto, come un inizio improvviso.  

Lila clencò le labbra. L’uomo aveva osservato, aveva sussurrato con serietà, la sua voce non un’offesa, ma un invito. Si girò, un momento di sfida nel vuoto. Non si notò il suo seguente, ma notò il modo in cui i suoi occhi lo fissavano, il momento in cui un raggio di luce lo attraversò. Là, una verità si ruppe: lei non era mai stata sola.  

L’incertezza colò toccò il suo stomaco. Aveva immaginato indifferenza, forse risate, ma questa era diversa. Non era più un omaggio, ma un segreto che le bruciava. Per la prima volta da mesi, non era più un’attore, ma un essere interamente condiviso.  

L’incertezza gli riprese il fiato. Non era *la* vulnerabilità, ma quel lato che si era sempre rifiutato di abbracciare.

## Il Vestito e l'Accente  

Il caffè era seduto sul angolo tra Birchwood e Fifth, incastonato tra una libreria vintage e una florista la cui vetrina cambiava con le stagioni. Lila lo aveva toccato centinaia di volte sul suo percorso verso nuove anime della città, promettendosi di fermarsi, sognando un giorno quel luogo. Ma il tempo, le sue promesse prive di realtà, le avevano tolto: era riservato solo a momenti curati, a look solo, a versione di sé incastonata, non ancora superata.  

Oggi però, la promessa era giusta. Si sedette in un bancone di tappeti rossi consumati, il tè chamomile che il calore aveva abbandonato. Il vestito che aveva scelto—cotone crema con corni di grano blu, un indiggapato da Goodwill—a sembrava un'armatura e un confidante. L'aveva lavata due volte che mattina, premendo il colletto per fissare il ricordo di un'intenzione, poi quasi scuotendo leggermente le falange. Una ribellione silenziosa. Un vero fatto.  

*Potrebbe non arrivare nemmeno lui*, pensò, l'urgenza familiare che si alzava. *Forse disse solo per apparire cortese*. Stranger a un locale improvvisato, offrendosi per caffè. L'equivalente? Un tipo straniero. Qualcuno che si svaluta in silenzio. Ma forse era vero: aveva imparato a confondere percepire.  

Lo sguardo sul telefono. Cinque minuti prima che l'orario lo annunciasse, se lui fosse puntuale… lo ipotizzava. C'era stato qualcosa nella sua espressione, pazienza, attenzione che faceva dubitare. Eppure, non era così: aveva tradito la fiducia con esseri comuni.  

Un colpo di scena: *Forse non arriverà*, sussurrò. *Forse non è mai arrivato*. Restò in silenzio, fino a quando non fissò il mozzicone sull'angolo.  

Lì entrò Marco, con il vento che sferzava le capelli più scuri, una giacca di pelle che raccontava storie di decenni. Il suo sorriso non era un fragile complimento, ma un’onestà. Un movimento che fece vibrare il tono dell’interesse. La serata non era solo nostalgia: era un confronto.  

Un amico, forse. Dei momenti in cui il condimento era un tesoro e la condivisione un’altra lingua. Ma qui, l’acqua di fondo non era mai stata lo stesso.  

*Perché?* Si chiese. Marco prese il suo posto con delicatezza, come adattarsi a un ritmo diverso. Mentre parlava, lei notò come il suo sguardo non si addensasse con la distrazione, ma si intrecciasse con lei. Un legame sottile, quasi intangibile, che sembrava stattamente stabile.  

Un suono, un’ombra di riconoscimento. *Forse,* mormorò. *C’è qualcosa di familiare, nonostante l’incompletezza.*  

*Lo confessò*—sulla spalla, senza esitazioni—*segnò di non dire, ma prometteva. Un accordo silenzioso.  

Un pizzico di frustrazione. *Basta*, sussurrò il cuore. Non era un errore. Era un’opportunità.  

L’insistenza lo fece sì. Con voce calma, gli chiese: *Vuoi che legga l’archivio?* La pagina di un libro dimenticato, il ricordo di olio d’oliva, note scritte a pagina. Marco condivideva storie, come lui con i condimenti. *L’aspetto perfetto. Ma c’è anche…*  

La tazzina di pane, il brevetto dell’odore di miele, il sapore del tempo perso—tutto si rivelava. *C’è un errore*, pensò, ma non poteva distoglihrsi. Per ora. Per sempre.  

Lui lo fissò, presente, e nel silenzio che seguì, ogni lacuna si compenseva. *Lo conosco*, disse, *ma non è gentile con chi è lui. Eppure…*  

Ecco. Un inizio che non si fermava. Un finale incerto, ma vivo. Fino a quel momento.

## Capitolo 14: "La Proposta del Concerto"

Il messaggio di Marco arrivò alle 23:47, scintillante sullo schermo del telefono di Lila come una piccola brace rovente caduta in una stanza che da tempo aveva chiuso a chiave.

*Ce n’è una venerdì. Locale underground. Nessun cellulare ammesso dentro. Ti ci trovi?*  

Nessun punto. Nessun emoji. Solo la domanda nuda — *ci sei?* — come se l’invito non fosse un invito vero e proprio, ma una sfida, una soglia tracciata a gesso sul bordo di qualcosa che ancora non aveva il coraggio di attraversare.

Lila lo lessò una volta. Poi ancora. Poi una terza, perché le parole cominciarono a riordinarsi nella sua mente, trasformandosi da semplice frase a proposta carica di significato. Si sdraiò sul letto, lo stesso su cui aveva passato l’ultimo anno a scrivere poesie su maionese e solitudine in egual misura, e fissò il soffitto. La stuccatura aveva una crepa sottile che tracciava un percorso irregolare dalla lampada al angolo della stanza, e fino a quel momento non l’aveva mai notata. Notava tutto quando era nervosa.

Scrisse una risposta — *sì*, solo quella parola, pulita e decisa — poi la cancellò. Scrisse *Venerdì va bene per me* — anche quella finì nel cestino. Infine, con la deliberata lentezza di chi chiude una lettera sapendo che cambierà tutto, scrisse: *Dimmi di più.*

La sua risposta arrivò in un lampo, come se Marco avesse tenuto il telefono in mano con la stessa urgenza irrequieta che aveva nutrito lei.

*È in un seminterrato, fuori dal Canal. Piccola stanza, senza palco vero — solo uno spazio vuoto al centro. La band si chiama Hollow Wire. Suonano come se stessero cercando di dimenticare qualcosa. Ti sarebbe piaciuto.*

Lila premé il telefono al petto e inspirò il profumo di ammorbidente alla lavanda che aveva spruzzato sui cuscini quella mattina. Provo a immaginare la scena: un seminterrato, una folla stretta l’una accanto all’altra, musica così alta da annullare la distanza tra gli estranei. Amava i concerti. Li amava sempre — la comunione del suono, il modo in cui una stanza piena di persone può diventare un unico organismo, pulsare e dondolare e sospirar al ritmo delle stesse cose. Ma i concerti underground erano diversi. Richiedevano una sorta di spensieratezza che non era mai riuscita a padroneggiare.

Pensò all’inaugurazione della galleria del Capitolo 3, dove indossò l’abito verde smeraldo e si sentì magnifica finché non capì che nessuno la stava guardando. Pensò al suo open‑mic del Capitolo 11, dove la sua voce si incrinò al secondo verso e vide pietà negli occhi della donna al primo posto. Pensò a Marco, seduto di fronte a lei al tavolo del caffè di sabato, le dita avvolte attorno a una tazza di carta con caffè nero, gli occhi che facevano quella cosa strana: fissavano un attimo più a lungo del previsto, come se leggesse un poema scritto nella sua postura.

*Lui ha apprezzato il diario,* pensò. *Ha apprezzato il poema sulla maionese. Ha apprezzato l’abito.*

Ma apprezzare la versione curata di sé — quella che mostrava al caffè, vestita con un abito vintage scelto con cura e con le maniche infilate sotto un cardigan — non era la stessa cosa che accettare l’Lila non curata. Quella che portava una piccola bottiglia di maionese fatta in casa nella borsa. Quella che, più di una volta, si era ritirata da una conversazione per spalmare maionese su un pezzo di pane strappato e mangiarlo con le dita, come se fosse la cosa più naturale del mondo. Quella il cui diario profumava di tuorlo d’uovo e succo di limone, le cui pagine erano macchiate da fantasmi pallidi di impronte di condimenti.

*E se lo vedesse?*

Il pensiero arrivò non come un sussurro, ma come un urlo, il tipo di urlo che riecheggia dentro il cranio a mezzogiorno quando il mondo è silenzioso e non c’è posto dove nascondere la paura. Premette i palmi contro gli occhi e vide, con terribile chiarezza, la scena che si sarebbe svolta: avrebbe estratto il diario dalla borsa, la bottiglia si sarebbe ribaltata, e la maionese — quella densa, dorata, impossibile da spiegare — si sarebbe versata sulle pagine, e Marco l’avrebbe guardata con quell’espressione che lo sconosciuto dello shooting aveva mostrato, quella che diceva *sei troppo, e non in modo positivo*.

## Capitolo 16: La pausa dalla moda

La luce blu dello schermo dello smartphone le sembrava un lampo chirurgico, che metteva a nudo ogni poro, ogni imperfezione e ogni bugia dell'esistenza digitale di Lila. Da tre anni la sua vita era stata una sequenza di inquadrature studiate con cura: un latte posato a un angolo esatto di quarantacinque gradi accanto a un quaderno coperto di pelle; una foto della sua sagoma contro un muro di beton armé, con un vestito che costava più dell'affitto mensile ma che le stringeva il corpo come una camicia di forza.

Guardò il pulsante "Pubblica" sul suo ultimo bozzetto — una foto di un blazer minimalista color crema appoggiato su sfondo bianco, netto e spoglio. La didascalia era un frammento curato di un verso su un sentimento di mancanza, levigato fino a renderlo digeribile, qualcosa di *estetico*.

Pensò al modo in cui lui aveva guardato il suo diario — l'oggetto vero e proprio, fisico, con i bordi sfatti, le macchie di grasso dei panini consumati di notte, le annotazioni febbrili e sbiadite dall'inchiostro di una donna che cercava se stessa tra le note di un condimento. ## Capitolo17: "La Sedia Vuota"

Il locale era un club sotterraneo dalle luci tenui, le pareti tappezzate di graffiti e un leggero ronzio di basso che pulsava come un battito cardiaco. Lila era arrivata un’ora prima, come sempre, i suoi stivali di pelle battevano un ritmo sul pavimento di cemento. Aveva scelto quel posto per il suo fascino discreto—nessun velluto, nessun’area VIP, solo un’energia grezza e incontaminata che le faceva male al petto in un modo che non riusciva a spiegare. Era qualcosa di più affilato, un vuoto che le pungeva i bordi delle costole.

Si era venuta qui per scrivere. Non per il suo blog, non per il suo diario di poesie, ma per sé. L’atto di sedersi in silenzio, circondata da quella musica che aveva il potere di squartare le mura della sua mente, era diventato un rituale. Estrasse il suo quaderno, le pagine ancora macchiate dei resti di maionese sulle dita, un promemoria della giornata assurda: un pranzo rovesciato, un maldestro tentativo di creatività e la silenziosa consapevolezza che aveva passato troppo tempo a far passare le sue stravaganze per dei difetti.

Il primo brano partì, una melodia lenta e avvolgente che la fece chiudere gli occhi. Immaginò il volto di Marco, il modo in cui la sua risata portava sempre un caldo che sembrava un segreto. *E se fosse qui?* si chiese, il cuore che faceva una goffa piroetta. Ma il pensiero svanì subito. Lui era stato al concerto della settimana scorsa, ma non l’aveva più vista. Magari si fosse già spostato. Forse aveva già trovato qualcun altro. *Forse non se n’è nemmeno accorta.*  

Le dita le scorrevano sul margine del quaderno, l’inchiostro ancora umido del poema che aveva scritto quella mattina. Non perché fosse timida—dopotutto aveva recitato un pezzo di spoken‑word a un slam di poesia—ma perché l’essere osservata la faceva sentire smarrita, come se la sua pelle fosse trasparente.

Si sistemò la posizione, incrociò le gambe e infilò il quaderno sotto il braccio. Il posto accanto a lei era vuoto, un promemoria netto della distanza tra lei e le persone con cui desiderava connettersi. Pensò alla serata che non era decollata, all’uomo che aveva liquidato la sua fissazione per la maionese come “da bambini”. *“Sei troppo,”* le aveva detto, e lei aveva annuito, le guance in fiamme. Ora, l’idea di essere “troppo” le pesava come una maledizione.

La musica si alzò, un’esplosione di tamburi e chitarre elettriche che la fece dondolare involuntariamente. Voleva ballare, lasciarsi travolgere dal ritmo, ma i suoi piedi rimanevano ancorati al pavimento. Invece fissava la folla, osservando come la gente si muovesse—alcuni ridendo, altri persi nella musica, altri semplicemente in piedi, disconnessi. *“Perché non posso essere così?*” pensò, mentre le dita accarezzavano distrattamente il motivo sulla manica. *“Perché sento sempre di osservare dal bordo?”*  

Un ricordo emerse: le mani di Marco, callose dalla chitarra, il modo in cui aveva posato una mano sulla sua spalla dopo il suo open mic. *“Non sei solo un’ombra,”* le aveva detto. *“Sei un’intero universo.”* Ma cosa voleva dire, esattamente? E se avesse interpretato quelle parole come una cosa che non comprendeva? E se avesse già deciso che lei era “troppo”?  

Il pensiero le stringeva il petto. Voleva credere che lui la vedesse, che fosse attratto dalle parti di lei che erano disordinate, le parti che aveva passato anni a nascondere. Ma la verità era che non lo sapeva. E l’incertezza era un peso ingombrante.

La canzone terminò e la folla esplose di nuovo, questa volta per un classico cover rock. Le dita di Lila scivolarono nella tasca, dove il suo telefono riposava. Tutto quello che voleva era sentirsi parte di qualcosa, essere vista non come una blogger di moda o una poetessa, ma come *Lila*.

La musica cambiò di nuovo, una melodia più dolce che fece impennare il suo respiro. *“È per questo che scrivo,”* pensò, le parole che le si formavano nella mente come un mantra. *“Per sentire qualcosa di reale, qualcosa che non debba essere curato.”* Estrasse il quaderno dalla borsa, le pagine ancora umide, e lo aprì a una pagina bianca. Fece una pausa, il respiro le si fece corto. Le parole erano una confessione, una vulnerabilità che non le aveva permesso di sentire da tempo. Sotto, aggiunse:

La musica si alzò di nuovo e Lila si alzò, le gambe tremolanti. Non sapeva cosa fare—forse uscire, forse restare, forse sedersi e lasciar scivolare il silenzio. Ma mentre si voltava per andare via, un lampo di movimento attirò la sua attenzione.

Una figura stava vicino al bar, la silhouette sfumata dalla luce tenida. Era lui. Marco. La schiena era rivolta, ma il modo in cui si muoveva—il modo in cui le spalle si piegavano mentre sistemava la giacca—era inequivocabile. Si fermò, il cuore le balzava.  Per un attimo furono due persone nella stessa stanza ma mondi diversi. Poi, senza una parola, lui si voltò e si allontanò, scomparendo nella folla. Il respiro di Lila si bloccò. Era un segnale? Un caso? O solo un altro promemoria di quanto fossero lontani?  Non lo sapeva. Ma mentre usciva nell’aria fresca della notte, la sedia vuota dentro il suo cuore sentiva un po’ meno peso. Forse il vuoto non era un fallimento. Forse era un inizio.  

Le luci della città si fusero intorno mentre camminava, il poema ancora ardente nella sua mente. Per la prima volta in molto tempo, non si sentiva come se stesse solo curando la sua vita. Stava vivendo, disordinata, imperfetta e bellamente umana.

# Capitolo 18:La Rivoluzione del Mayonnaise  

Nell’ silenzio della sua stanza, Lila sfiorava i bordi del suo quaderno preferito, quelle pagine intrise d’inchiostro e di ricordi. I suoi versi, delicati come petali, fluttuavano sopra il caos delle presentazioni di sé. Ma sotto le organizzazioni rigide, una ribellione più silenziosa covava—nata dalla dolce acidità della maionese su un quaderno.  

Ricordava il giorno in cui, durante una notte di scrittura, aveva rovesciato la crema sul suo quaderno. L’incidente fu sia una caduta che una rivelazione. Le pagine erano macchiate, l’inchiostro sbiadito, ma i suoi pensieri erano più chiari che mai. *“Sono la macchia sul quadro,”* mormorò, *“non un difetto, ma una firma.”*

L’uomo annuì, un lento sorriso gli illuminò il viso. “Mi piace.”  

Quel momento segnò l’inizio di una trasformazione silenziosa. Lila iniziò a sperimentare il suo stile, abbracciando i difetti che prima le apparivano debolezze. Provò vestiti che evidenziavano le sue curve, giocò con motivi che raccontavano la sua passione, scrisse poesie che intrecciavano il ricordo pungente della maionese e il ritmo della città. Il cammino, però, non fu privo di ostacoli. Confrontò momenti di dubbio, chiedendosi se la sua voce trovasse spazio in un mondo che ancora premiava la lucidità sulla passione. Si chiese se avrebbe mai incontrato qualcuno che la vedesse per quella che era—imperfetta, fragrante e piena di vita.  

Poi, una sera, si trovò a una piccola inaugurazione di galleria, circondata da opere che narravano storie non dette. Una donna si avvicinò, i suoi occhi si posarono sulle pagine del quaderno di Lila. “Le tue parole sono come un profumo,” disse, “qualcosa che non riesco a nominare.” Offrì di condividere la sua ultima poesia, un pezzo ispirato alla dolcezza della maionese e agli echi di un concerto dimenticato. Mentre la serata avanzava, Lila percepì una connessione che non aveva mai provato prima—radicata nelle quirks condivise e nella coraggiosa esposizione di sé. ### FINAL OUTPUT

Lila si guardò intorno, immersa nel silenzio che si dipinse tra le ombre del chiuso. Per la prima volta, non si chiese se stesse sembrando troppo o non abbastanza. Si sentì semplice, presente, lontana dalle aspettative altrui.

Marco era venuto a lei in un momento di verità, come se finalmente avesse trovato una voce. “L’ho letto,” disse, la mano che si posò sul suo sacchetto di formaggi. “Ma è bello, non seppellirti troppo.”

Fu lì, tra silenzi e battute di parole, che Lila capì che l’amore non era la ricerca di uno specchio perfetto, ma l’acquietamento di un’ombra che finalmente riusciva a vedere. E per la prima volta, non cercò di nasconderci.

# Capitolo 20: “Il Vestito, il Bacio e la Danza”

Dopo una settimana lunga, Lila si trovò sul bordo del teatro, i suoi dita sfioravano il bordo sfilacciato del suo vestito preferito—un ricordo delle giacche sartoriali che indossava di tanto in tanto. L’aria era impregnata dell’aroma della pioggia e del ronzio lontano di una città che non dorme mai. Teniva saldamente il suo diario, le pagine sussurravano segreti che solo lei poteva udire. Quella sera avrebbe ricordato: non solo le note, ma il tremito delle mani mentre scriveva, il battito del cuore che sembrava una tamburella sotto la pelle.

Credeva sempre nel potere della presentazione: come un vestito possa trasformare una persona, come una parola ben posizionata possa sollevare lo spirito. Ma quella notte era diventata un fantasma del proprio fare: lo sguardo vagava sul palco, le luci tremolavano come un battito nervoso. Un pensiero le colpì come un improvviso vento: *e se lasciassi andare?*

Le luci del palco si abbassarono e la folla cadde in silenzio. Lila fece un passo avanti, i suoi stivali urlavano sul cemento come un metronomo che conta i secondi verso la connessione. Aprì la bocca, ma le parole si fermarono. La sua voce, di solito un strumento levigato, cedette, sostituita da una confessione cruda e senza copertura. Parlò di maionese, della complessità acidula che avvertiva in ogni goccia, di come una volta fosse stata una fonte di imbarazzo, ora un’ispirazione.

La stanza urlò. Le grida di entusiasmo esplosero, ma per Lila era solo un sussurro. Osservava i volti, ognuno un specchio che rifletteva la sua stessa esitazione. Pensò a Marco, al modo in cui l’aveva ascoltato con uno sguardo gentile, al suono del suo riso che riecheggiava tra le pareti. Ricordò il concerto che l’aveva isolata, il modo in cui si era nascosta dietro un sé curato. Ma quella notte, qualcosa cambiò. Qualcosa fragile, come il primo fiore di primavera.

Con un respiro, riprese. Non era più un poema, ma una confessione. Le parole scolarono, non per un pubblico, ma per sé stessa. Parlò della pressione di essere perfetta, della paura di essere vista come difettosa, delle lotte silenziose che combatteva in silenzio. La folla si avvicinò, non per giudicare, ma per ascoltare. E per la prima volta, si sentì ascoltata.

Quando le ultime righe del suo poema svanirono, le luci si accesero di nuovo e la stanza esplose in applausi. Ma Lila non si voltò. Sentì qualcosa spostarsi dentro di sé—qualcosa dolce, come il primo sussurro della primavera. Si rese conto che l’amore non era una questione di perfezione. Era una questione di vulnerabilità, di condividere le parti più disordinate di sé.

Le luci si abbassarono di nuovo, e Lila tornò indietro, il torace tremolante. Non sapeva se troverebbe un compagno, ma una cosa era certa: non era più solo un’immagine curata. Era una donna, disordinata e meravigliosa, pronta a compiere il prossimo passo.

## Il Concluso di un Viaggio

I raggi degli applausi rimasero nella sua mente mentre si allontanava dal teatro, i suoi stivali scricchiolavano delicatamente sul sentiero di ghiaia. Le luci della città scintillavano intorno a lei, riflettendosi sull’acqua di un ruscello vicino. Aveva passato la serata a combattere con le parole, a lottare con il peso delle proprie aspettative. Ma mentre attraversava le strade silenziose, provava un cambiamento—una certezza calma che stava iniziando a capire se stessa.

Si fermò sul bordo di un parco, la brezza fresca sussurrava nei capelli. Il mondo intorno a lei era vivo, vibrante, pieno di possibilità. Per la prima volta dopo mesi, si permise di sognare senza la paura del giudizio. Pensò a Marco, allo sconosciuto che l’aveva sfidata, a colui che la vedeva non solo per le sue stranezze, ma per la sua anima. La sua presenza era stata una scintilla, un promemoria che forse l’amore non richiedeva un’apparenza perfetta.

Lila stringeva il diario al petto, i bordi consumati dall’uso continuo. Lo aprì su una pagina bianca, il silenzio premendo le orecchie come un sussurro gentile. Con un profondo respiro, iniziò a scrivere—non a riguardo della perfezione, ma della presenza. Dei piccoli miracoli di essere umana, della bellezza nella sua storia disordinata.

Mentre il sole calava, dipingendo il cielo di sfumature arancioni e rosa, Lila si trovò a sorridere. Non era un sorriso grandioso, ma uno che sembrava genuino, come la prima nota di una canzone che non sapeva di aver perso. Si rese conto che l’amore, pensò, non era adattarsi a un modello. Era trovare qualcuno che la vedesse per chi davvero era—difettosa, inaspettata e infinitamente degna.

Con uno sguardo finale all’orizzonte, Lila entrò nella notte, il cuore più leggero di quanto lo fosse in anni. Non sapeva cosa riservasse il futuro, ma era pronta a abbracciarlo, un passo alla volta.
