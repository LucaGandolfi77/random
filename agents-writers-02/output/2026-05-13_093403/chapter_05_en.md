## Chapter 5 – Mayo as a Muse  

The rain had turned the city’s sidewalks into ribbons of silver, each puddle catching the neon glow of boutique signs and the occasional flash of a passing car. Lila pulled her wool‑blend trench coat tighter around her shoulders, the collar brushing the nape of her neck where a few stray strands of hair had escaped the neat bun she always favored. She walked past the thrift‑store window that displayed a mannequin in a 1970s‑inspired silk dress, its sleeves flaring like the wings of a moth. The image made her smile—a reminder of how she could slip between eras as easily as she slipped between hashtags.

She was late, again. The deadline for her next blog post loomed in her mind like a metronome, ticking louder with each step. “Five more minutes,” she whispered to herself, though the tiny, stubborn voice in her head knew she was already past that limit. She turned the corner onto Rue de la Liberté, where the smell of fresh coffee mingled with the faint, nostalgic scent of old paper that seemed to seep from the cracked façade of a tiny shop she had never noticed before.

The shop’s window was cluttered with an odd assortment: a brass telescope, a stack of vintage postcards, a porcelain teacup with a chipped rim, and, in the very centre, a battered leather‑bound book whose title was barely legible: *Le Recettes de la Maïonnaise d’Antan*. Her breath hitched. She had walked past this shop a hundred times, but never had the curiosity to step inside. Now, the rain had driven her toward its warm interior, and the book called to her as if it held the answer to a question she hadn’t yet formed.

She pushed the door open, a tiny bell chiming above her head. The shop was a sanctuary of forgotten things. Shelves sagged under the weight of yellowed newspapers, tin boxes of buttons, and rows of glass jars filled with dried herbs that whispered of kitchens long closed. An elderly woman with silver hair pulled into a loose bun stood behind a counter, her eyes twinkling behind round spectacles.

“Bonjour, ma chère,” the woman said, her voice soft like the rustle of pages. “Looking for anything in particular?”

Lila glanced at the book again, her fingers itching to touch the worn cover. “I… I saw the mayonnaise book in the window. I’m… curious about it.”

The shopkeeper smiled, a crease forming between her brows. “Ah, the old mayo book. It’s been here longer than most of the other things. It belonged to my great‑grandmother, a chef who believed that a perfect mayonnaise could change a life.”

Lila felt a strange flutter in her chest, as if the notion of a simple condiment could indeed pivot the axis of her existence. “May I see it?”

The woman reached beneath the counter and produced a small wooden box, its surface smoothed by years of handling. She lifted the lid, revealing the leather tome nestled among a scattering of wilted lavender buds. Lila’s fingertips brushed the cover, and a faint, buttery scent rose—briny, tangy, and oddly comforting.

She opened the book to the first page, where a handwritten note in elegant cursive read: *“Pour la vie, pour l’amour, pour l’art.”* Below it, a list of ingredients stretched across the page in ink that had faded but not disappeared: egg yolks, Dijon mustard, white wine vinegar, olive oil, a pinch of salt, and—most intriguingly—a splash of lemon zest.

Lila’s mind raced. She could almost hear the soft pop of a bottle being uncorked, the hiss of oil meeting yolk, the gentle stir of a whisk. She imagined the creaminess spreading across a slice of sourdough, the way the tang of the lemon would cut through the richness, leaving a clean finish. She thought of her own life—layers of polished images and carefully chosen words, each one a glossy veneer that hid the mess beneath. Could this simple sauce be a metaphor for something more? Could she, for once, let the mess in?

She turned the page. The next entry was a poem, ink smudged by years of handling: 

*In the bowl of silence,  
Eggs whisper to the wind;  
Mustard’s fire ignites the night,  
Oil drifts like a lover’s sigh.  
Lemon’s kiss—sharp, bright—  
A promise kept, a heart alight.*

Lila’s eyes widened. The poem resonated with a part of her she rarely let surface. The imagery of the oil drifting like a lover’s sigh echoed the way she drifted through cafés, spilling her thoughts onto napkins, hoping someone would catch the drift. The lemon’s kiss—sharp, bright—felt like the flash of a camera lens, the momentary sting of a compliment that left her both exhilarated and wary.

She closed the book gently, feeling the weight of its stories settle in her hands. The shopkeeper watched her, an amused glint in her eye. “Your grandmother used to say that mayonnaise was the poetry of the kitchen. It takes patience, balance, and a willingness to let the ingredients surrender to each other.”

Lila laughed, a sound that startled her. “I’m a poet, but I’ve never thought of a sauce as a poem.”

The woman’s smile deepened. “Then perhaps it’s time you write a poem about mayonnaise.”

Outside, the rain had softened to a mist, the city’s hum now a muted backdrop. Lila slipped the book under her arm, thanked the shopkeeper, and stepped back onto the street. The world seemed a little brighter, as if the clouds had been parting just enough to let a sliver of sunlight touch the cobblestones.

She trudged home, the book’s pages rustling against her thigh. The apartment she shared with herself—walls plastered with mood boards, a writer’s desk cluttered with sketchbooks, a small balcony overlooking a graffiti‑sprayed alley—was a sanctuary of curated chaos. She dropped the book onto the coffee table, its weight echoing through the quiet space. The rain’s patter against the windows was now a soothing rhythm, matching the beat of her heart.

She set a pot of water to boil on the stove, the metallic clink of the kettle a metronome for her thoughts. She pulled a bowl from the cupboard, placed a whisk beside it, and opened the mayonnaise book to the first recipe. The ingredients were simple, yet the instructions were written with a reverence that made her feel as if she were about to perform a ritual.

She cracked two eggs, their yolks bright as sunrise, and set them in the bowl. She added a spoonful of Dijon, the mustard’s amber hue swirling like a sunrise over a foggy city. A splash of white wine vinegar followed, the acidic tang punching through the richness, reminding her of the first time she’d felt a genuine connection—a fleeting moment of true intimacy at a concert, when the vocalist’s voice cut through the thrum of the crowd and made her feel seen.

She began to whisk, slow at first, the motion steady and deliberate. The mixture thickened, the yolk and mustard binding together, like the first lines of a poem finding their rhythm. She drizzled in olive oil, a thin stream at first, then a steady cascade, the oil folding into the mixture, each droplet a note in a silent symphony. The bowl turned from a chaotic swirl of colors to a smooth, ivory emulsion, the texture glossy and inviting.

She added a pinch of salt, the grainy crystals reminiscent of the glitter that sometimes fell from the ceiling of a club when the DJ dropped a bass drop—a reminder of the places where she felt alive yet alone. Finally, she squeezed a wedge of lemon, the zest releasing a bright perfume that cut through the richness, a sudden flash of clarity in a sea of monotony.

She tasted a spoonful, the creamy tang exploding on her tongue. The flavor was complex yet harmonious, each element distinct yet inseparable. She laughed again, this time softer, more genuine. The sauce was perfect, an embodiment of balance—exactly what she had been striving for in her life, but had never found in her carefully curated feeds.

She set the bowl aside and opened a fresh page in her notebook, the one that had been splattered with mayonnaise during a previous concert. The paper was still damp, the ink a little smeared, but it felt right. She let the pen glide, the ink flowing like the oil she’d just whisked.

*In the kitchen’s hush, I stir—*  
*Eggs and mustard, a union pure.*  
*Oil drifts, a lover’s sigh,*  
*Lemon’s kiss, a bright surprise.*  

*I taste the tang, the sweet surrender,*  
*Finding balance, the heart’s true mender.*  

She paused, the words hanging between the lines, and thought of the countless times she’d tried to balance her public persona with her private yearning. She’d built walls of sequins and silk, each layer a defense against the world’s gaze. Yet here, in the quiet of her kitchen, she felt a vulnerability that was not weakness but openness—a willingness to let flavors mix, to let feelings merge, to accept the messiness that came with authenticity.

She wrote on, the poem evolving into something larger, something that stretched beyond the kitchen:

*The world is a runway, lights ablaze,*  
*Models strut, their faces a glassy glaze.*  
*But beneath the gloss, a secret spreads,*  
*A sauce of truth, where fear is shed.*  

*Mayonnaise, humble, unadorned, yet grand,*  
*It binds the broken, the stray, the bland.*  
*And in its swirl, I find my voice,*  
*A chorus of the quiet, a daring choice.*  

She read the lines aloud, the words reverberating off the bare walls, filling the space with a resonance that made the apartment feel less like a stage and more like a sanctuary. She imagined the poem being posted on her blog, the caption a simple photograph of a jar of mayonnaise beside a handwritten note. She imagined the comments: “So poetic!” “Never thought of mayo like that!” “You’re a genius!” And perhaps, she thought, someone would read between the lines and see the woman behind the glossy images, the one who loved concerts, poetry, and the messy beauty of a perfect sauce.

She placed the finished mayonnaise in a glass jar, sealing it with a ribbon of twine. The jar glistened under the soft glow of the lamp, its contents a creamy, golden promise. She set it on the windowsill, where the rain had ceased, leaving a fresh, clean scent in the air.

Later that evening, she sat at her desk, the laptop open to her blog’s drafting page. She typed the title: *Mayo as a Muse: Finding Poetry in the Ordinary*. She added a photo of the jar, the label handwritten in cursive: *Le Recettes de la Maïonnaise d’Antan*. She wrote a short introduction, explaining how a rainy stroll led her to a vintage recipe book, how the act of making mayonnaise became a meditation, and how the process mirrored her own search for balance.

She paused, hands hovering over the keyboard. Part of her wanted to keep this moment private, a secret tucked away in the pages of a journal. Yet another part—a louder, braver voice—urged her to share it, to let the world see the raw, unfiltered version of herself that had been hidden behind curated posts and filtered images.

She hit “Publish” and watched the page go live. A notification pinged almost immediately: a comment from a user named *Luna_Starlight*: *“Your mayo poem made me cry. I’ve always felt like a mess, but reading this… it’s beautiful. Thank you for sharing your truth.”* Lila felt a tremor in her chest, a mixture of relief and exhilaration. She replied, *“Thank you, Luna. May we all find the courage to whisk our own truths into something delicious.”*

She scrolled through the comments, each one a small affirmation, a connection forged through the simple act of sharing a recipe and a poem. In the back of her mind, she imagined a future where the lines between fashion, poetry, and food blurred, where she could wear a dress inspired by the swirl of mayonnaise, where she could read her verses at a concert while the crowd cheered for the rhythm of her voice, not the beat of the bass.

She leaned back, eyes flicking to the jar of mayo sitting beside her laptop. The liquid gold seemed to pulse with a quiet confidence. She realized that the absurdity she had once tried to hide—her love for mayonnaise, her obsession with the tactile mess of a spoonful of sauce—was not a flaw but a facet of herself that made her uniquely vibrant.

The night deepened, the city lights flickering like distant constellations. Lila stood, stretching her arms above her head, feeling the ache in her muscles from the evening’s emotional work. She walked to the balcony, the cool night air brushing her cheeks. The rain had left the streets glistening, each puddle reflecting the neon signs in a kaleidoscope of colors.

She thought of the next concert she would attend, the next poem she would write, the next outfit she would design. She imagined a dress with a pattern reminiscent of the creamy swirls of mayonnaise, a runway that smelled faintly of lemon zest and olive oil, a crowd that cheered not for the flash of couture but for the authenticity of a woman unafraid to embrace her quirks.

She took a deep breath, inhaling the scent of the city mixed with the lingering aroma of her kitchen experiment. In that moment, Lila felt a quiet certainty settle into her bones: the world could be as messy and beautiful as a jar of mayo, and she was ready to dive in, spoon in hand, heart open, ready to stir the next chapter of her life.

She turned back to her apartment, the lights inside glowing warmly, inviting her in. The notebook sat on the desk, its pages waiting to be filled. She smiled, feeling the first genuine, unfiltered smile in a long time. The quiet hum of the city outside seemed to echo the rhythm of her own heartbeat, a steady, hopeful cadence that promised that, perhaps, love—whether it came in the form of a partner, a poem, or a perfect sauce—was just around the corner, waiting to be discovered in the most unexpected of places.
