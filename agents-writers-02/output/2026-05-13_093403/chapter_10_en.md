## Chapter 10: "The Fashion Fiasco"  

The sun hung low over the city, casting a golden haze over the rooftop where the photoshoot was supposed to be a celebration of Lila’s curated chaos. The city skyline stretched behind her, a mix of glass and steel that mirrored the tension in her chest. She stood in front of the camera, her hands gripping the edges of a vintage dress that had once felt like a rebellion—now it was just another layer of armor. The fabric was a deep emerald, its lace trim catching the light like a secret she wasn’t sure she wanted to keep. Around her neck hung a necklace made of tiny, glass mayonnaise jars, their translucent surfaces reflecting the fading light. It was absurd, she knew, but in her mind, it was a statement. A declaration that even in her most polished moments, she carried the messy, tangy essence of her true self.  

“Lila, the light’s shifting,” the photographer called from behind the lens. “Adjust your pose—maybe a little more… *dramatic*.”  

She exhaled, her breath fogging the air as she stepped back, letting the wind catch the hem of her dress. The model co-stars—two women in their twenties, their laughter sharp and practiced—watched her from the corner of the room. They were all smiles, their eyes glinting with the kind of confidence she’d worked hard to mimic.  

“Perfect,” said the photographer. “Now, let’s try the *actual* shoot.”  

Lila’s stomach twisted. The shoot was supposed to be about her “elegance,” her “artistry,” but the moment the light hit the mayonnaise jars, it turned into something else. The jars, which she’d hoped would add a touch of whimsy, now looked like a joke. A punchline. She forced a smile, her fingers brushing the fabric of her dress as if it could shield her from the judgment she felt creeping in.  

“Lila, your hands are… *stained* again,” the photographer said, his voice laced with amusement.  

She froze. The mayonnaise—still on her fingertips from the previous shoot—had smudged into her dress, a smudge of white against the emerald. The model co-stars stifled giggles, their eyes darting between her and the camera.  

“It’s… it’s *art*,” she said, her voice too loud.  

The photographer’s smile faltered. “Art?” He leaned closer, his breath warm against her ear. “You sure about that?”  

The question hung in the air, and for a moment, Lila considered lying. But the words “art” and “messy” were tangled in her mind, and she couldn’t untangle them. Instead, she nodded, her throat dry.  

The photographer’s smile returned, but it was thinner now. “Alright. Let’s try the *final* shot.”  

Lila stepped forward, the camera’s flash blinding her. The mayonnaise jars glinted, their surfaces catching the light like tiny, fragile stars. But as the shot was taken, she saw it—the way the model co-stars’ eyes lingered on her hands, the way the photographer’s gaze lingered on the stains. It wasn’t just the mess; it was the *idea* of her. The way she’d tried to bottle her quirks into a fashion statement, only to have them mocked.  

When the shoot ended, the model co-stars gathered around her, their laughter sharp but not unkind. “You’re *so* committed to the bit,” one of them said, her voice dripping with irony. “I think it’s cute. But maybe… *less* mayo next time?”  

Lila’s cheeks burned. She opened her mouth to respond, but the words stuck. Instead, she turned on her heel, her heels clicking against the rooftop’s concrete. The city’s noise swelled around her, but she couldn’t hear it. All she could hear was the echo of the photographer’s question, the way the model co-stars had looked at her like she was a performance, not a person.  

That night, as she lay in her apartment, the mayonnaise jar on her desk catching the moonlight, she stared at it. It was a symbol, she realized. A reminder of how she’d tried to shape herself into a version of herself that felt safe, that felt *acceptable*. But the stains—on her sleeves, on her hands, on her dress—were the parts of her that refused to be curated.  

She pulled out her journal, the one she’d kept hidden under her bed, its pages filled with poetry and recipes. The mayo recipes were the most absurd, the most personal. There was one about “The Tang of Regret,” another about “Sandwiches as Solace.” She traced the words with her finger, her heart aching. She’d written them in the hope that someone, anyone, would see the beauty in the madness. But now, the thought of sharing them felt like a betrayal.  

The next morning, she opened her laptop, her blog’s homepage loaded. The post she’d planned—a reflection on fashion and art—felt hollow. She had written it with the hope of connecting with others, of finding a community that appreciated her contradictions. But now, the words felt like a lie.  

Instead, she typed a single sentence: *“Sometimes, the most beautiful things are the ones we’re too afraid to show.”*  

She hit “publish,” the screen flickering as the post went live. Then, she closed the laptop, her hands trembling. The mayonnaise jar remained on her desk, its lid sealed tight. For now, it was enough.  

But as the sun rose the next day, she couldn’t shake the feeling that the world was watching. Not just the photoshoot, but the way she’d hidden herself behind the lens. The way she’d tried to curate her life into something that felt safe, only to be laughed at.  

She thought of Marco, the stranger from the open mic, and wondered if he’d ever feel the same way. If he, too, had ever felt like a performance, a version of himself that didn’t quite fit.  

The thought was both a comfort and a curse. She didn’t know if she was ready for someone to see the mess beneath the polish. But she also didn’t want to keep hiding.  

The mayonnaise jar sat on her desk, its label faded. It was a relic of a time when she’d thought she could control the narrative. Now, she wondered if the real story was in the stains, in the things she’d tried to scrub away.  

And maybe, just maybe, that was enough.
