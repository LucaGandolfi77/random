## Chapter 12: "The First Spark"  

The air in the poetry open mic room was thick with the scent of coffee and anxiety, a familiar cocktail that made Lila’s palms sweat. She stood at the edge of the stage, her fingers brushing the worn leather cover of her journal—a relic of her obsession with mayo and the messy, unfiltered truths she’d scribbled within its pages. The crowd was a mosaic of faces, some leaning into the rhythm of the mic, others scrolling on their phones, their attention as fleeting as the notes she’d practiced in the mirror.  

Her poem, *“The Condiment of Solitude,”* had been a risk. She’d written it the night before, after a date that had fizzled like a dying flame, and a concert where her hands had been smeared with mayo, a detail she’d later framed as a metaphor for the chaos she’d tried to suppress. But tonight, she’d chosen to perform it, her voice trembling as she began:  

*“I once spilled mayonnaise on a notebook, a crime against the pristine, / But the stain became a canvas, and I learned to paint with the mess.”*  

The words felt foreign when she said them aloud, but the crowd leaned in, their murmurs softening into something like understanding. A man in the front row, his hair dyed the color of burnt copper, raised his hand mid-verse. “That’s… that’s *exactly* what I needed to hear,” he said, his voice tinged with awe. Lila’s cheeks flushed, but she kept going.  

The poem ended with her confessing the journal, the one she’d hidden in her bag like a secret. “It’s not just about mayo,” she said, her voice gaining strength. “It’s about the things we’re told to hide, the parts of ourselves that feel too much, too weird, too… *too much*.” The room fell silent. Then, a ripple of applause spread, slow and deliberate, like the first notes of a song that had been waiting to be played.  

Lila’s heart pounded. She had expected indifference, maybe even laughter, but this—this was different. The man in the front row was still looking at her, his eyes wide, as if she’d just revealed a truth he’d been too afraid to voice. Before she could think, she turned and walked off the stage, her heels clicking against the wooden floor.  

The crowd parted for her, and she moved quickly, her mind racing. *Who was he? Where did he come from?* She had no idea, but the way he’d watched her, the way his voice had cracked with sincerity, felt like a spark in the dark.  

She didn’t notice the man trailing behind her until he stepped into her path, his presence a quiet challenge. He was taller than she expected, his dark hair tousled, his eyes the color of storm clouds. “You’re not what I expected,” he said, his tone not unkind.  

Lila froze. “And what did you expect?”  

He smiled, a slow, knowing thing. “A girl who liked mayo. Who wrote poems about it. I thought you’d be… more guarded.”  

She blinked, surprised. “I’m not *that* guarded.”  

“Then why the journal?” he asked, his gaze flicking to the notebook peeking from her bag.  

Lila’s breath caught. She hadn’t meant to bring it, but it had been a habit, a crutch. Now, the weight of it felt heavier. “It’s… a work in progress,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper.  

He tilted his head, studying her. “Progress? Or a confession?”  

She laughed, the sound foreign in her own ears. “Both, maybe.”  

For a moment, the space between them felt charged, electric. Then he stepped closer, his presence no longer a shadow but a presence. “You’re not as alone as you think,” he said.  

Lila’s throat tightened. “I thought I was.”  

He reached into his pocket, pulling out a small, crumpled piece of paper. “I wrote a poem about you. Not the one you performed, but… the one I wrote *after* hearing you. It’s about the mayo, the concerts, the way you carry yourself like you’re both the artist and the audience.”  

She took the paper, her fingers brushing his. The ink was smudged, the words messy, but the emotion was raw. *“You’re a storm in a dress,”* he’d written, *“and I’ve been waiting for a thunderstorm to let me get soaked.”*  

Lila’s knees nearly gave out. She didn’t know if it was the poem, the proximity, or the way his eyes held hers, but something in her chest cracked open. For the first time in months, she didn’t feel like a performance. She felt seen.  

“I’d like that,” she said, the words slipping out before she could stop them.  

He smiled, and for the first time, she felt the weight of her curated image lift, like a dress she’d worn for too long. The spark had ignited, and now, it was up to her to decide whether to let it burn.
