## Chapter 14: "The Concert Proposal"

The text from Marco arrived at 11:47 p.m., glowing against the dark expanse of Lila's phone screen like a small, warm ember dropped into a room she had long kept sealed.

*There's a show Friday. Underground venue. No phones allowed inside. You in?*

No period. No emoji. Just the raw, unadorned question — *You in?* — as though the invitation were not an invitation at all but a dare, a threshold drawn in chalk across the threshold of something she was not yet brave enough to cross.

Lila read it once. Then again. Then a third time, because the words had begun to rearrange themselves in her mind, shifting from simple sentence to loaded proposition. She lay on her bed, the same bed where she had spent the better part of the last year composing poems about mayonnaise and loneliness in equal measure, and stared at the ceiling. The plaster had a hairline crack that traced a crooked path from the light fixture to the corner of the room, and she had never noticed it before. She noticed everything when she was nervous.

She typed a response — *yes*, just the word, clean and decisive — then deleted it. Typed *Friday works for me* — deleted that too. Finally, with the deliberateness of someone sealing a letter they know will change everything, she wrote: *Tell me more.*

His reply came within seconds, as though he had been waiting by his phone with the same restless urgency she had been nursing hers.

*It's in a basement, off Canal. Small room, no stage really — just a cleared space in the middle. The band is called Hollow Wire. They play like they're trying to forget something. You'd love it.*

Lila pressed the phone against her chest and breathed in the lavender fabric softener she had sprayed on her sheets that morning. She tried to imagine the scene: a basement, a crowd pressed close together, music loud enough to flatten the distance between strangers. She loved concerts. She had always loved them — the communion of sound, the way a room full of people could become a single organism, pulsing and swaying and gasping at the same moments. But underground concerts were different. Underground concerts required a kind of unselfconsciousness she had never quite mastered.

She thought of the gallery opening in Chapter Three, how she had stood in her bold emerald dress, feeling magnificent until she realized that no one was looking. She thought of the open mic in Chapter Eleven, how her voice had cracked on the second verse and she had seen pity in the eyes of the woman sitting in the front row. She thought of Marco, sitting across from her in the café on Saturday, his fingers wrapped around a paper cup of black coffee, his eyes doing that thing where they lingered just a moment longer than expected, as though he were reading a poem she had written inside her posture.

*He liked the journal,* she thought. *He liked the poem about the mayonnaise. He liked the dress.*

But liking the curated version of herself — the version she presented over coffee, in a carefully chosen vintage dress with her sleeves tucked neatly beneath a cardigan — was not the same as accepting the uncurated Lila. The Lila who carried a small jar of homemade aioli in her purse. The Lila who had, on more than one occasion, excused herself from a conversation to dab mayonnaise on a torn piece of bread and eat it with her fingers, as though it were the most natural thing in the world. The Lila whose journal smelled faintly of egg yolk and lemon juice, whose margins were stained with the pale ghosts of condiment fingerprints.

*What if he sees that?*

The thought arrived not as a whisper but as a scream, the kind that reverberates inside the skull at three in the morning when the world is quiet and there is nowhere for fear to hide. She pressed her palms against her eyes and saw, with terrible clarity, the scenario that would unfold: she would reach into her bag for her journal, and the jar would tip, and the mayonnaise — that thick, golden, impossible-to-explain substance — would spill across the pages, and Marco would look at her with that same expression the stranger at the fashion shoot had worn, the one that said *you are too much, and not in the way we want.*

She set the phone down on the bed beside her and pressed her face into the pillow. The fabric was cool against her cheek, and she inhaled the scent of herself — lavender, vanilla, and something underneath that she could never quite name, something that smelled like doubt.

---

Friday arrived with the particular weight of an afternoon that feels like a held breath.

Lila stood in front of her closet for forty minutes, which was thirty-seven minutes longer than she needed to choose an outfit on any normal day. The dress — the one Marco had admired — hung on its hanger like a promise, its fabric a deep burgundy that caught the light the way wine does when you tilt the glass just so. She had worn it on purpose that first time, she realized now. It was the dress she wore when she wanted to be seen. When she wanted to be believed.

But Friday was not about being believed. Friday was about being found out.

She tried on three outfits before settling on something she had never worn in public: a simple black turtleneck, dark jeans, and a pair of boots she had bought on sale and never had the courage to wear. The clothes were armor, but armor of a different kind — not the polished, curated kind she wore to gallery openings and dates that fizzled, but the kind that said *I am here, and I am not performing.*

She left the dress on its hanger.

Her purse, however, betrayed her. Inside it, nestled beside the journal with its stained and dog-eared pages, was a small glass jar of lemon-herb aioli she had made that morning. She had planned to eat it on toast, alone, in her kitchen, but some instinct — the same instinct that made her scribble poems in the margins of mayonnaise recipe books — had compelled her to bring it along. As though the aioli were a talisman. As though it were proof that she was, in fact, the person she feared Marco might not want her to be.

She caught her reflection in the hallway mirror before leaving. Black turtleneck. Dark jeans. Boots. Hair pulled back in a loose knot that was falling apart faster than she would have liked. She looked, she thought, like someone trying very hard to look like she wasn't trying at all.

*That's the hardest costume of all,* she told herself, and left before she could reconsider.

---

The address Marco had sent led her down a narrow alley behind a laundromat, past a rusted fire escape, and through a door marked only with a small, hand-painted arrow pointing downward. The stairs smelled of damp concrete and old cigarette smoke, and the fluorescent light above the entrance buzzed with the irregular rhythm of something dying. She descended, her boots echoing against the metal steps, and felt the bass before she heard it — a low, thrumming vibration that traveled up through the soles of her shoes and settled somewhere behind her sternum.

The basement was smaller than she had imagined. Roughly thirty people stood packed into a room with exposed brick walls and a concrete floor that had been covered in mismatched rugs. A single spotlight, jury-rigged from what appeared to be a construction lamp, illuminated the cleared space in the center where two figures were already setting up guitars and tuning strings with the casual precision of people who had done this a thousand times. There was no stage, no barrier, no separation between the performers and the audience — just a circle of bodies waiting to be filled with sound.

She scanned the room. A woman in combat boots and a leather jacket was rolling a cigarette by the door. Two men with paint-streaked overalls stood near the back, speaking in the low, conspiratorial tones of people who shared a secret. A couple swayed gently against the far wall, their foreheads touching. The air was thick with the mingled scents of beer, patchouli, and something metallic — the scent of electricity, perhaps, or anticipation.

And then she saw him.

Marco stood near the center of the room, his dark hair falling across his forehead in a way that made him look, improbably, like every concert photograph she had ever saved to her inspiration board. He was wearing a simple black shirt, sleeves rolled to his elbows, and when he saw her, his face broke into a smile that was not curated, not filtered, not composed for any audience. It was the kind of smile that arrived without permission and stayed without effort.

"You came," he said, as though the words surprised even him.

"You said *underground*," she replied, and was immediately mortified, because it sounded like a complaint, or a challenge, or something a woman in a movie would say while standing in the rain outside a jazz club in 1962.

He laughed — a real laugh, unguarded and warm — and took her arm. "It's better than it sounds. I promise."

They found a spot near the edge of the room, close enough to feel the vibration of the music but far enough to have a small pocket of space. Lila set her bag on the floor beside her and felt the jar inside it shift, a faint, liquid slide that sent a spike of anxiety through her chest. She pressed her hand against the bag as though she could hold the aioli in place through sheer force of will.

*Don't spill it. Don't open it. Don't, for the love of everything, pull out the journal.*

Hollow Wire took the stage — if two amps, a drum kit, and a microphone arranged in a loose semicircle could be called a stage — and the first chord hit like a fist against a wall. The sound was raw, unpolished, stripped of the production she was accustomed to hearing through her curated playlists. The singer's voice cracked on the high notes, and the guitarist's fingers slipped twice during the first song, and neither of them cared, because the music was not about precision. It was about presence. It was about the act of standing in a room and making noise together, of saying *I am here, and I feel things, and I am not ashamed.*

Lila felt something loosen in her chest. It was small at first — a crack in the plaster, like the one on her ceiling — and then it widened, and what poured through it was not fear but something closer to relief. She looked at Marco, who was watching the band with an expression of pure, unguarded absorption, and she thought: *This is what it looks like when someone is not performing.*

For a while, she let herself simply be. She stood in the dim light and let the music wash over her and did not think about how she looked, or whether her turtleneck was flattering, or whether the woman in the leather jacket was judging her shoes. She was just a body in a room, vibrating with sound, and it was enough.

But then — because there is always a *then* — the singer paused between songs and the room fell into a hush, and Lila's hand, seemingly of its own volition, moved to her bag. She unzipped it, felt the cool glass of the jar beneath her fingertips, and pulled out a small loaf of bread she had sliced that morning and wrapped in parchment paper.

"What are you doing?" Marco murmured, leaning closer so the band couldn't hear.

"Nothing," she whispered back, but her hands were already working — tearing the bread, unscrewing the lid, spreading a thick, generous layer of aioli across the soft interior with the practiced ease of someone who has done this alone, in secret, a hundred times.

She offered him a piece.

He looked at the bread. He looked at the jar. He looked at her, and in his eyes she saw something she had not expected — not judgment, not confusion, but a kind of recognition, as though he had been waiting for this moment all along.

"Is that — is this your thing?" he asked, and the question was gentle, genuinely curious, the way one might ask about a childhood memory or a recurring dream.

Lila's throat tightened. Every instinct she had — the ones honed by years of curating, filtering, and carefully selecting which parts of herself to show the world — told her to put the jar away. To wrap the bread and slide it back into her bag. To laugh it off and say *just kidding, I don't actually eat mayonnaise at concerts.*

But the music was playing, and the room was dark, and Marco was looking at her with his whole attention, and she was so tired of performing.

"It's lemon-herb," she said, and her voice was steadier than she expected. "I made it this morning. With fresh thyme and a little garlic. It's — it's the best thing I've ever put in my mouth, and I'm not even exaggerating. I know it's strange. I know people think it's —" She stopped, because the words were crowding in her throat like an audience waiting for the next verse. "I know what people think."

Marco took the piece of bread. He bit into it slowly, chewing with the same absorption he had given the band, and then he closed his eyes, and something shifted in his face — a softening, an opening, like a door she hadn't known was closed swinging gently inward.

"It's incredible," he said, and he meant it, and the way he meant it was the most beautiful thing she had ever been offered.

Lila laughed — not the polished, performative laugh she used at gallery openings, but a real laugh, startled and full and slightly too loud for the room. She covered her mouth with one hand, then lowered it, because she was done covering things.

Marco reached into his pocket and pulled out a folded piece of paper. She recognized it — he always carried things in his back pocket, small squares of paper with notes and phone numbers and fragments of lyrics. He unfolded it and placed it in front of her.

"Hollow Wire is playing again next month," he said. "A bigger venue. Not underground, but — I don't know. I thought you might want to come. If you wanted to."

She looked at the paper. His handwriting was angular and precise, but there was a smudge of ink near the edge, as though he had written it quickly, without thinking too much about how it looked.

"I want to come," she said.

Something passed between them then — not a declaration, not a confession, but something quieter and more durable, like the first thread in a fabric that would take years to complete. It was the look of two people standing on the edge of a version of themselves they had not yet dared to inhabit, deciding, together, to step forward.

Lila folded the paper and tucked it into her journal, between a poem about egg yolks and a sketch of a dress she would never buy. She picked up the jar of aioli, screwed the lid back on, and placed it carefully in her bag.

She did not hide it at the bottom.

She placed it right on top, where she could feel its weight against her hip, a small, cool weight that said: *This is who I am. This is all of it. The curated and the uncurated. The dress and the dip. The poem and the mayonnaise.*

And when the next song began — something slow and aching and full of the kind of longing that cannot be spoken, only felt — Lila closed her eyes and let the sound fill every hollow space inside her, and for the first time in as long as she could remember, she did not feel the need to fill them with anything else.
