
## Sogno in Galleria

The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II stretched before them like a cathedral of commerce, its glass vault mirrored impossibly skyward, creating the illusion that they stood beneath an enormous eye watching over Milan. Marco guided Elena forward, his steps careful on the black and white marble floor, the pattern beneath their feet shifting and rippling like water disturbed by an underground current. She wore a dress the color of midnight, and it moved with her as if she were already part of the architecture, another element in the gallery's grand design.

"Per chi non crede nella fortuna," she said, her voice barely above a whisper, "questa è la cosa più vicina che ho visto mai." She gestured with her hand, fingertips tracing the geometric lines that converged at the center of the space. "Qui sotto si tengono le mani. Si fa un desiderio."

Marco felt his breath catch somehow, as if she had plucked something delicate from within his chest and placed it briefly in the air between them. He had read about wishes in books as a child, these small superstitions that adults carried like secret reliquaries. His mother used to touch the base of the column when they passed through here, murmuring some indistinct prayer for good fortune. Now he looked at Elena and wondered if she believed in this sort of thing, in the power of designated spaces to amplify longing.

"I've never made a wish here," he admitted, because it was true. He had walked through this gallery dozens of times, sometimes stopping to purchase something trivial, sometimes simply standing still and letting the vastness of it press against his ribs like a hand trying to push its way out. But tonight felt different. Tonight the weight of the space seemed to bend toward them, to acknowledge their presence as something worthy of witness.

Elena turned to face him fully, and in that moment the other patrons blurred into shadows along the periphery of their vision. She raised her hand slightly, fingers spread against the cool glass ceiling, and Marco understood that she was offering him something without words. Not a wish exactly, but an invitation. A chance to step across some invisible threshold that separated ordinary moments from those charged with possibility.

"What would you wish for?" she asked.

He thought quickly, as if the answer might slip away if he hesitated too long. "For time to slow down in places like this. For seconds to stretch until they become minutes, minutes until they become hours." He glanced at her then, really looked at her for the first time since they had entered the gallery. "Is that too strange?"

She smiled then, that slow unfurling expression that revealed something tender and vulnerable beneath her features. "No," she said. "It's perfect."

They moved together toward the center of the space, where the flooring patterns intersected in a star shaped configuration. Here the lighting was different, softer somehow, casting their shadows not onto the ground but floating above it like translucent ghosts. Marco found himself uninspired by photography for the first time in years, overwhelmed by the sheer impossibility of capturing what they were experiencing in that instant. Some moments were too complete, too perfectly formed to be translated into static images.

Elena took his hand, her skin warm against his despite the chill that had settled into the evening air. They began to move, slow circling steps that required no music but felt musical nonetheless. The gallery seemed to pulse around them, its arched ceiling expanding to accommodate their motion, its mirrored surfaces multiplying them endlessly into infinity. Each step carried them forward and backward simultaneously, creating a rhythm that existed only for them.

"Do you remember our first meeting?" he asked suddenly, surprised by the question even as it formed in his throat.

She nodded against him, close enough now that he could feel the rise and fall of her breath. "I remember the coffee."

"The spill."

"Especially the spill." She laughed softly, a sound like wind chimes. "But I remember your eyes too. The way they held mine for just a second longer than necessary."

Marco closed his eyes then, letting her words wash over him while he focused on the sensation of her body pressed against his. In the silence between heartbeats, he realized that this was what he had been searching for all along—not perfection or completion, but presence. The simple act of being fully engaged with another person in a world that demanded constant distraction.

"What if we don't stop?" he asked, opening his eyes to find hers again.

"Then we won't."

They walked backward across the gallery then, still holding hands, still moving to whatever internal metronome guided their steps. Around them, couples paused to watch, their faces expressions of mild curiosity mixed with something approaching envy. Marco understood this reaction without needing explanation. Everyone carried within them the memory of a moment when everything had aligned, when desire and possibility had intersected with such precision that the world had seemed to tilt on its axis.

Elena rested her head against his shoulder, and he felt her tremble slightly. Not from cold, but from the intensity of feeling that made speech unnecessary. In situations like this, he had always reached for his camera, documenting rather than participating, observing rather than being observed. But tonight something else motivated him. Perhaps it was the recognition that some experiences transcended documentation, that their meaning resided not in preservation but in living.

"You're thinking about leaving tomorrow, aren't you?"

The question came from nowhere and everywhere simultaneously, emerging from the space between them like smoke given form. Marco sobered instantly, his mental gears shifting to accommodate this new information. "How did you—"

"I saw your messages. The ones about money, about deadlines." She turned to look at him fully now, her eyes searching his face for reassurance. "You think I don't know because you didn't tell me directly."

He wanted to lie, to suggest that she had misunderstood, but the truth felt too heavy to carry alone anymore. "No," he said finally. "You're right. I was going to leave. I was going to take the jobs in Madrid, in Barcelona. Places where the air would be different, where the buildings would cast different shadows."

Elena nodded slowly, as if this revelation confirmed something she had already suspected. "And now?"

"Now I'm not so sure leaving is what I need." He lifted her hand to press his lips to her knuckles. "Maybe staying is scarier."

The admission hung between them like incense smoke, curling and dissipating in the gallery's vast space. Around them, the city continued its nocturnal rhythm, but down here beneath the glass ceiling, time operated by different rules. Seconds stretched into minutes, minutes into something approaching eternity.

"You could stay," she said. "We could—"

"We could figure it out." He cupped her face then, thumb brushing away a stray curl that had escaped from her hair. "But that's not the point, is it? The point is that for the first time in a long time, I feel like I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be."

Elena's breath hitched, and when she speak her voice carried the weight of something precious being offered rather than taken. "Then don't leave."

The word fell from her lips like a key turning in a lock, and suddenly everything else—the deadlines, the debt, the familiar ache of professional uncertainty—seemed distant and manageable. Not solved, not eliminated, but contextualized within something larger than individual circumstance. Love had a way of doing that, of expanding the boundaries of possibility until previous limitations appeared negligible.

They stood there then, forehead to forehead, the gallery's architecture supporting them as much as their own connection. Around them, couples continued to drift through the space, but Marco and Elena existed in their own bubble of light and shadow, separated from the world by nothing more substantial than the choice to remain present with each other.

In the distance, he could hear the bassline of some unseen musician echoing through the corridor, a low frequency that seemed to emanate from the building itself. The sound wrapped around them like an embrace, and Marco realized that this was what he had been searching for all along—not completion or resolution, but this simple recognition that some people made the world brighter simply by standing in it.

Elena stepped back just enough to look at him properly, her expression honest in a way that surprised him. "Do you love me?"

The question landed like a stone dropped into still water, sending ripples across the surface of his consciousness. He had spent so long convincing himself that love was a luxury he couldn't afford, that attachment was weakness disguised as tenderness. But standing here, with her hand in his and the entire city spread out beneath them like a map waiting to be explored, he found that honesty required no translation.

"Yes," he said. "I think I do."

And in that moment, beneath the gallery's glass ceiling with the city sleeping around them and Elena's heart beating softly against his palm, Marco Ferraris understood that happiness wasn't something to be captured or preserved, but something to be lived entirely in the interest of sharing.
