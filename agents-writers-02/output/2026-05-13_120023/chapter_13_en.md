The room was dim, the only light coming from a single lamp that threw a pool of yellow onto the floor. Outside, the rain fell in a steady rhythm, the kind of drumming that seemed to echo the heartbeat of the entire city. She could feel the vibrations in her chest, a low hum that matched the thrum of the rain against the glass. The world outside had its own pulse, and inside, she had been trying to compose a new rhythm, a new pattern of colors that would somehow capture the remnants of Julian’s music.

It had been a month since the last time they had spoken face to face. She had walked away from their shared apartment, the old piano, and the melancholy that had followed them there, hoping that distance would unclench the knots that had wrapped themselves around their hearts. But now, the ghost of his presence sat across from her on the couch, his silhouette a faint outline against the light of the lamp.

She could feel his absence like a missing note in a chord, an incomplete harmony that hung heavy in the air. In the silence, she imagined him walking into the room, his boots thudding against the floorboards, the faint scent of his cologne lingering like a ghostly perfume. The thought made her heart pound with both dread and longing.

The door creaked open and Julian staggered in, his shoulders hunched, a tired smile forming on his lips that did not quite reach his eyes. He held a small canvas in his hands, the paint still wet, the colors a swirl of blues and grays. He looked at her, eyes wet, a shimmer of a smile at the corner of his mouth.

“Hey,” he whispered, as though she weren’t there. The sound of his words carried a quiet ache, a sound that made the hair on the back of her neck rise.

Elena swallowed. She had forgotten how to speak to him directly, how to look into his eyes and see beyond the wall he had built around his heart.

“Julian,” she said, her voice barely a breath, “I was just—”
