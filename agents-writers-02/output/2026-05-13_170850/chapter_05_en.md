## Chapter 5: Letters from the Past

The letter smelled of foxfire and old sugar.

Elena held it up to the afternoon light filtering through the attic window, turning the brittle edges so the ink could catch the warmth. The handwriting was small—almost anxious—and the paper had yellowed to the color of a field mouse's belly. She had read it once already, standing on the attic stairs with plaster dust still clinging to her sandals, her heart doing something she hadn't authorized. Now, sitting on the floor with her back against the eaves and a cup of tea going cold beside her, she read it again.

*My dearest Alessandro,*

*The fig tree has dropped its last fruit. I counted the days you promised you would come, and I counted them again, and still the number ends in silence. They say the family will not allow it. They say I am foolish. Perhaps they are right. But foolishness, dear heart, is the only honest country I have ever known.*

*If you find this, know that I did not leave because I stopped believing. I left because I would have stayed forever, and forever is a door with no handle.*

*Yours, in the only way that matters,*
*Lucia*

Elena set the letter down and pressed her palms against her eyes. The words had changed. She was certain of it. The first time she had read them they had seemed like a ghost story—elegant, distant, belonging to another century. Now they sat inside her chest like a splinter that had worked its way deeper.

She thought about the way Marco had stood in the vineyard that morning, his sleeves rolled to the elbow, turning a spade of earth with a precision that suggested he had done it ten thousand times. The way he had paused, looked at her—not through her, but at her—and said, *You're staring at that fresco like it owes you money.* And she had almost smiled. Almost.

Now she turned the letter over.

On the back, in different ink—a redder, more hurried hand—she found a name. *Alessandro Mancini.*

The breath left her in a slow, deliberate way, as though she were trying to hold on to it and failing.

Mancini. She knew that name. She knew it the way you know the shape of a room in the dark—not by sight, but by the way your hand finds the wall.

Marco Mancini.

She sat very still. Outside, the cypress trees along the ridge swayed in a wind that carried the green, metallic smell of the vineyards. Somewhere below, a bird hammered against a shutter and then fell silent, as though ashamed.

Elena unfolded the letter once more and read the final line. *If you find this, know that I did not leave because I stopped believing.* She thought of her sister. She thought of the hospital corridor, the antiseptic light, the way time had folded in on itself and become something unrecognizable. She thought of how people said *I'm here for you* and meant it, and how none of that was enough to fill the space where Chiara had been.

*Forever is a door with no handle.*

She whispered the words aloud, hearing how they sounded in the empty attic, and the sound of them was so exactly the thing she had been carrying that her throat closed around it.

---

That evening she did not go to the vineyard. She stayed in the villa with the windows open and the silence pressing against her like wet linen. She spread the letter on the kitchen table alongside a dictionary and a slim volume of Italian poetry she'd found in a drawer—Francesco Guccini, the spine cracked, the margins filled with a woman's handwriting that was not her mother's. The letter. The frescoes. The name. They were converging now, and she could feel the architecture of the story assembling itself beneath her hands, the way a mural reveals itself as you scrape away the surface.

She called the letter's author Lucia. Her great-aunt. The name appeared nowhere in her mother's stories, in the careful, edited version of family history that had been handed down like an heirloom—polished, purposeful, missing whatever it was missing. Her mother had left Tuscany when Elena was four. She had left her father. She had left, as far as Elena knew, everything.

But this letter suggested that leaving was a tradition in their family. A particular inheritance. One passed from woman to woman like a stain in the blood.

At nine o'clock, her phone buzzed.

*Marco: The east wall needs another pass. The plaster is softer than I thought. Come when you're ready.*

She stared at the message. She wanted to go. She wanted to refuse. She wanted to stand in this kitchen and hold this letter until it dissolved against her skin.

*I'm on my way,* she wrote, and then deleted it, and then wrote it again.

---

He was already in the courtyard when she arrived, his silhouette cut clean against the lantern light he'd strung between the lemon trees. He looked up when he heard her footsteps on the stone, and something in his expression shifted—not surprise, exactly, but recognition. As though he had been expecting her to come around to this particular truth.

"You found the name," he said.

It was not a question.

Elena stopped three paces from him. The air between them smelled of damp earth and woodsmoke and something faintly sweet she couldn't name. "Alessandro Mancini," she said. "My great-aunt wrote to him."

Marco nodded. He set down the trowel. "Alessandro was my great-grandfather."

The ground beneath her felt uncertain.

"I know what you're going to say," she started, but she didn't know what she was going to say. That was the problem. She had rehearsed this moment on the walk over—had built a fortress of composure out of silence and careful distance—and now it was dissolving in the warm night air, and she was standing in front of him with nothing but the truth and the letter and the terrifying openness of a woman who had not yet learned how to be known.

"Tell me," she said instead.

Marco was quiet for a long time. A bat moved through the lantern light in a frantic, looping arc. He leaned against the wall of the villa and looked up at the sky, as though the answer were written there in stars he could read.

"She was the most beautiful woman in the valley," he said finally. "And he was the only man who saw her."

Elena waited.

"They were young. It was 1946. The war had broken something in this place—more than the buildings, more than the roads. People. They were desperate for anything that felt like life. He brought her flowers from the vineyard. She painted his portrait in secret. They planned to marry."

He paused. His jaw tightened.

"Then his mother found the letters."

The silence that followed had weight. Elena felt it settle over the courtyard like a second night falling.

"What happened?" she asked.

Marco looked at her. In the lantern light, his eyes were the color of dark honey, and in them she saw something she recognized—something old and unspeakable and still, after all this time, aching.

"They made her leave," he said. "They said it was for her own good. They said the family would be ruined. She was sent to Florence, and she never came back. The letters were taken. The portrait was hidden. And my family agreed to forget."

He turned the trowel over in his hands. "We've been forgetting ever since."

Elena felt the letter in her coat pocket, its edges pressing against her hip like a secret she was carrying in her bones. She thought of the frescoes beneath the plaster. The hidden things. The way the walls of this villa held every version of the story except the one that mattered.

"You knew," she said. "When you offered to help. You already knew."

"Yes."

"And you didn't tell me."

He met her gaze. "I was hoping you'd find it yourself. I was hoping the walls would say what I couldn't."

Elena looked at him—really looked—and saw in his face the same expression she had seen in her own reflection that morning: the face of someone standing at the edge of something they could not undo.

"The letter says she didn't stop believing," Elena murmured.

Marco's breath caught. It was small, barely there, but she heard it the way you hear a door opening in an empty house.

"I know," he said.

They stood there in the lantern light, the ruined villa at their backs, the vineyards stretching dark and silent into the hills. Neither of them moved. Neither of them spoke. And in the space between them—fragile, unbearable, alive—something that had been locked for decades began, at last, to turn its handle.
