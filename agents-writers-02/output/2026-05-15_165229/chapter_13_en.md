## Chapter 13 – Between the Seasons

The tide had already begun its nightly lull, the surf whispering against the stone shore in a rhythm older than the town itself. Mira stood at the threshold of the shop, the glass of the portal a shallow pool of moonlight, a silver ring in the air where the sea met the world. She pressed her palm to the cool, wet glass and felt the pulse of the ocean underneath—a gentle thrum that seemed to echo the pulse of something far older, something that had been waiting for her.

She could have walked back to the library, to the shelves of familiar titles, to the comforting smell of paper and ink. But the pull was stronger than the pull of any bookmark. It was a tug that felt like a finger brushing her spine, urging her forward into the depths of the unseasons. She inhaled, and the salty air tasted of possibility.

When she first found the shop, it had been as if the world had turned on its heel. A key of tarnished brass, the shape of a crescent moon, had slipped from the tide's gentle grasp. The door, once hidden beneath the waves, had emerged like a pearl at dawn. Days had passed—weeks, months—where she had come back to the shop repeatedly in secret, each time feeling a little less in control.

Tonight, the shop seemed to breathe, as if it were a living thing, and its voice was not one of words but of weather itself. The first page she had read in the manuscript had looked like a snowfall, the very breath of an unseasoned winter. The second page had been a blue, cool drizzle. She had felt every sensation, each with a memory that had never been lived, yet known as if it were a dream.

She stepped inside, and the air was cool, damp, filled with the scent of seaweed and old paper. The shelves were stacked with tomes that rang like wind chimes in a storm, each spines etched with weather patterns: a line of black, broken white, a swirl of green. She could feel the pulse of the sea through the floorboards that were slick with moisture.

Her heart was hovering between the urge to rewrite the weather and the fear of the consequences. The town had been dying with a drought that had turned the fields into ash and the water supply into a memory of itself. The storm she had conjured a week earlier had returned life to the fields, but the people were now looking at her with a mix of gratitude and dread.

She walked, as if guided by a wind, to the back of the shop where the manuscript lay. The book she had found at the beginning of her journey, “Chronicles of Unseasons,” had been a collection of weather in language. She had learned to read it as a musician learns a new instrument—by listening to the vibrations of each note. Mira had spent countless nights in the shop listening to the pages read themselves: a gust of wind that would cause her lips to curl, a rain that would leave a cool drop of dew on her skin. Each reading had become a communion with the sea, a dialogue with the ocean’s memory.

She lifted the book, the weight of it a familiar weight. The pages were thick, almost translucent, and the ink seemed to shimmer like the surface of a tide. She flipped to a page that had a counter‑spelling—a series of words that, in the language of the sea, would silence the portal. She stared at the words, the letters forming a sentence that was, in a sense, a promise. It promised a choice: to lock the portal, to keep the unseasons a memory, or to open the door and rewrite the weather.

She could feel the weight of the shop, the walls whispering in the wind, the sea’s pulse beneath her feet. The words seemed to glow, and her mind raced, thinking of the fields that were now alive, of the children who slept in dreams, hearing the sea’s lullaby. But the words were also a warning—a warning that the sea had a memory, that it had lived seasons that were never lived. She felt a pang in her chest that was both grief and hunger.

The storm that she had conjured a week earlier had shown her that the town could be saved. But she had also seen that the town had lost something in doing so, something that was tied to the past. She thought of the old women with silver hair who sat by the windows, eyes closed as they remembered the time when the sea would rise high and the town would prepare for the winter. She thought of the children who had grown up with the scent of rain on their lips, of them now dreaming of the lost seasons.

Her hands trembled as she turned the page. She read the counter‑spelling aloud. The words formed a sentence that was, in a sense, a promise: “If the ocean’s memory is the key, the portal shall remain, but the sea shall hold its breath and the dream shall be preserved.”

The room shivered. The shop, the shelves, the books—everything seemed to breathe with her own pulse. She could see the sea’s surface above the glass, a dark water that was still, for a moment, as if it were listening. The wind that had been breezing through the shop’s open windows had stilled. The very earth, the floorboards, seemed to hold its breath in anticipation.

She closed her eyes and felt the sea flood the shop. The words she had spoken had a resonance in the room, a sound that was not just a sound but a vibration. The tremor she felt was not a quake but a gentle thrum that was the cosmos’s way of acknowledging a choice. She could feel a thread of air that ran through her palm, a thread that had once been the wind, now hushed, now holding a secret. She could feel the runes of the manuscript that vibrated with the presence of the sea.

When the tremor subsided, the shop was as it had been. The shelves were still, the books were still, the sea was still. Mira’s breath was a quiet exhale, a release of the tension that had been coiling within her. She felt the weight of the sea on her chest, and she felt that she had made a choice.

She thought of the storm she had conjured, of the light that now bathed the fields, of the hope that now glimmered. She thought of the old woman’s lullaby, of her whispered warnings. She thought of the children’s dreams, of the hunger for a memory.

She knew that she would have to explain the choice to a town that had found new hope. She would have to stand in front of them and tell them that the unseasons were a story, a memory, a gift to be preserved rather than a resource to be exploited. She would have to keep the portal still.

Mira closed the book and laid it gently on the glass. The words glowed faintly, as if breathing. The sea above her was dark, but the droplets that fell from the sky-lens of the shop were like jewels. She felt the wind, tinged with the scent of salt and seaweed. She listened to the sound of the sea as it moved.

She took one last breath, feeling the ocean’s breath mingle with her own. She turned away from the shop, feeling the sea’s pull still but not as strong as before. She walked back to the library, to the town. She had a story to tell, a tale that would be remembered, the story of a town that had chosen memory over change, that had chosen the weight of the past over the weight of the present.

She stepped onto the shore, the moon’s silver light dancing on the waves. The sea was still, the waves lapping at the shore in a rhythmic pattern that was a promise. She could almost hear the wind whispering, “Between the seasons,” as if the sea itself were reminding her that there was a balance between the two.

She smiled, a small, quiet smile that was a secret between her and the sea. The story that would live on in the memory of her town would be the one that preserved the unseasons, a story that would keep the memory alive, a story that would keep the seasons alive, a story that would keep the sea alive.

She knew that the future would come with its own storms, its own challenges. But she had the knowledge that the sea was not just a force of nature but a keeper of memory, and that the memory of the unseasons would live on in the hearts of the town’s people. She had chosen to preserve the unseasons, to preserve the memory, to preserve the story, to preserve the heart of the sea, and she was ready to face the next chapter.

— MIRA
