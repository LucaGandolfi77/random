## Chapter 18 – The Portal’s Lull

The sea had never been so quiet. The waves, once a restless choir of rolling tides, fell now like a single breath—soft, measured, almost reverent. Mira walked along the jetty, her bare feet sinking into the damp sand that had never known the sharp sting of a gull’s cry, the sharpness of a storm’s howl. The gulls had begun to return, their cries thin as paper, but the wind that followed them carried a scent of salt and something older, something that smelled like a lullaby that had slipped through the cracks of memory.

She paused at the end of the pier, where the water met the sky in a seamless, unbroken line. The horizon seemed to hold its breath, as though the ocean itself were listening to a hymn sung by the wind. It was in this hush that Mira felt the weight of the manuscript close to her chest, as if it were a living thing, its pages breathing with the pulse of the sea.

For a long moment she simply stared. The portal, once a crack in the world that had drawn her like a moth to a flame, was now a silent, patient stone. The venturi of weather that had once rumbled with stories of the unseasons had quieted, leaving only a faint, steady hum—an echo that resonated in the corners of her mind, a reminder that the wind had always carried secrets.

When she looked out over the water, the surface glimmered with shards of moonlight, each glittering fragment a memory of a season that had never been. In the distance, a lone gull dipped and rose, its wings slicing the air like a silver note. The gull, a simple creature, seemed to carry the weight of the universe on its back, a silent witness to the quiet that had settled over the sea.

Mira lifted her hand to touch the manuscript again. Her fingers were damp, the salt of the sea coating them, and the leather of the cover was cool against her skin. She could feel the words that had once danced like ash in the wind, the way they had whispered their power, their promise. Yet now, that whisper was gone, replaced by a stillness that was both a mercy and a warning. The storm that had once raged across the town’s fields had been quelled—though the soil still bore the scars of its aftermath, the way a cracked road holds the memory of a rain that fell too hard.

She thought of the town’s children, who had slept under blankets of damp cloth, their dreams haunted by the echo of the unseasons. The wind that had once carried the scent of unbloomed flowers now carried a quiet reverence for the forgotten. The children had grown, their laughter a little more subdued, their eyes a little more aware of the world’s fragile balance.

The slow, deliberate rhythm of the sea was a reminder that change had a price. The storm that had brought prosperity to the fields had also carved away the ghostly memories of the lost seasons, leaving behind a stark landscape that was both beautiful and barren. The town had tasted the sweetness of abundance, and felt the bitterness of loss. The elders had stood in silence, their faces etched with the sorrow and hope that came with the decision to preserve the unseasons, to let the sea’s lull be a testament to the cost of their choice.

Mira closed the manuscript gently, pressing it against her chest as though it were a heart. Its pages seemed to breathe, the ink shimmering as if a faint breeze had yet to stir them. She wondered whether the words had ever truly left the book or if they were still waiting, silent and patient, for another soul to read them. But for now, the words lay quiet, a testament to the moment when the wind had been hushed by the town’s new resolve.

The black air of the ship’s dock was cool against her skin. The scent of maritime pine mingled with the salty air, a scent that had been there since before the town’s first house had been built on the coast. She could almost hear the echo of the wind in the chimneys of the old houses, the wind that once carried the scent of unbloomed flowers and unfreezed rivers.

Mira turned back to the pier. The sky above was a canvas of indigo and gray, the clouds a soft, shivering mass that seemed to hold back the sun for a moment before it slipped behind the horizon. The sea, as if it understood the weight of the choice that had been made, seemed to sigh, a sound that was both a lull and a hymn, a lull that was also a promise.

She thought of the world beyond the tide’s reach, the world that had once been captured in the pages of a book that had, for a fleeting moment, allowed a town to rewrite its destiny. The world beyond the sea, with its own storms and its own seasons, had not yet known the gentle hush that fell over the town’s shore.

The quiet settled around Mira like a shroud. She could feel the sea’s breath against her face, a gentle reminder that nothing truly disappears; it simply changes. For the first time in a long while, she allowed herself to feel that calm, the calm that came from knowing that she had made a choice that was not only wise but compassionate.

The wind that now swept past her was soft, a whisper that carried the memory of the unseasons. It brushed through her hair, a gentle, metallic scent of salt and old paper. She could almost hear the murmur of the sea, the rustle of forgotten leaves in a breeze that had never had a chance to bloom. It was a lull, a quiet that was at once a promise of return and a reminder of what was lost.

She walked back to the small wooden house that she had called home for as long as she could remember. The house stood at the edge of the town, a quiet sentinel that had watched as the sea’s lull had settled over the town’s shoreline, a home that had guarded the memory of the unseasons. The house had always been a place where whispers of the sea were kept, where the waves inside the walls could only be heard in the quiet of the night.

She stepped inside, the wooden floor creaking under her weight as she made her way to the desk where the manuscript had first found her. Its pages were still near, the ink a subtle glow in the dim light of the room. She let her fingers trace the lines of ink, feeling the stories that had moved her heart. In the quiet of the night, she felt the presence of the town, the presence of the sea, and the presence of the unseasons. All of them lay in the same breath, one after another, singing a lull that was a testament to the town’s heart.

Mira sat at the desk, her back against the old oak wood. The wind outside was quiet, a lull that seemed to echo the quiet of the town’s heart. She closed her eyes, feeling the weight of the manuscript settle against her chest, as if it was a reassurance that her choice was the right one.

She heard the wind once more, a delicate, soft hum that seemed to warn her of what was to come. She knew that she would live in a world that had its own storms, its own seasons. She also knew that the town would remember the unseasons, the ones that had never bloomed and the ones that never froze, and that they would live on in the hearts of the people who had seen them through the pages of the book that she had found deep beneath the sea. In the quiet of the night, she felt the sea’s lull, the gentle hum that would carry the town’s story to the unseasons, to a place where the wind never stilled, where the seasons were always from the past and always alive, forever.
