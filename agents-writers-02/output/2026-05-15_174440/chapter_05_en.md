## Chapter 5 – Shadows of the Past

The night after the lullaby, the field hospital lay quiet under a sky that refused to hide the stars. Amina sat on the cold concrete of the infirmary’s back hall, her bare feet tingling against the chill. The walls were lined with corrugated metal sheets, each one scarred by the fire that had once eaten the village. She had barely slept in the last two days, her mind a whirl of duties, the scent of antiseptic, and the soft hum of distant artillery that pulsed like a heartbeat.

She stared at the faded photograph of her family that she had tucked into the small pocket of her scrubs. The picture showed a younger version of herself, with hair wild like the tresses of the great acacia trees, standing beside her mother, a woman who wore a faded shawl and a smile that seemed to hide secrets. The back of the photo was marked with a single, handwritten note in her father's scrawl: *“Remember me.”* It was a note she had never seen until that night, when the storm that raged outside had left the shutter open, exposing a slip of paper that had slipped from the envelope that had found its way into her pocket by happenstance.

Amina’s fingers hovered over the photo. She had thought it a relic, an old memento of a life before the war. But the rustle of the wind that carried the scent of burnt maize and wet earth made her feel as if the image had a pulse. She closed her eyes and let memories unfurl like the petals of a dried apricot tree.

---

### The Echoes of Her Father

Amina’s father, Kofi, had vanished when she was only four. The war had been a long, silent monsoon that swept through the valley, leaving villages in tatters and families broken. She had grown up with the warning that some stories are better left untold, but her mother had always whispered a rumor in the quiet of dawn. She’d say, *“Kofi went to the hills, to where the sky touches the earth, and he never returned.”* Those words had been a lullaby, a promise of a return that never came.

She could still hear his voice in the echoing corridors of the hospital, in the wind that swirled around the tents, carrying the distant cries of children. The phantom of his presence hovered precariously between hope and grief, a double-edged sword that had made her both wary and courageous.

She remembered the day she first picked up the letter that she had never seen before. It was a thin, weathered sheet of paper, ink faded by the rain, written in a hand that was unmistakably her father's. He had scrawled a single line in a field, on the day the war had ended in a final, harrowing ceasefire that had left the valley scarred but alive.

> *“This war will not take you away, for I am in the sky. If you ever feel lost, look up, and you will find me.”*

She tried to make sense of it. She found the line repeated in her thoughts, a mantra that tugged at her heart. She wondered if it was a riddle, a warning, or a love letter that had never been finished. The image of her father in the sky, wrapped in the dust of the past, became a shadow over her present. In that way, Kofi was not entirely absent; he was an open wound that had always been there, always waiting to be closed.

---

### The Mother's Secret

The next day, Amina found herself in the back of the hospital, where the nurses took a break. She heard her mother humming a song that had once been sung in the courtyard of their home. The song was a lullaby from her grandmother, a melody that had been passed down through generations and had become a family legacy.

Her mother had noticed the way Amina was staring at the photograph and had approached her with a small, worn notebook. Its leather cover had worn by the days of war, but the pages were filled with ink, hands that had carried the weight of a mother’s love and grief. Amina's mother handed her the notebook.

The pages were full of her mother's diary. It was a record of the war, the loss, and the small moments that still held a glimmer of light. It was written in a language that was more than words, a language that was the echo of a heart that had never stopped beating for her daughter.

*“Love is the only hope that can keep a world alive. I have written this to remind you that hope lives in us. I do not want to lose you, my child. My love for you is a bright star that will guide you through every battlefield.”* The last line was a promise that Amina had never known that her mother had made.

She read every line, and the tears that had been drying in her eyes were replaced with an unbreakable resolve. Amina realized that the war had not only taken her father’s presence but it had also given her a way to survive: the memories of the past. She understood that the war in the valley was not just a violent conflict but a battle that had been fought within her.

---

### The Present

Amina turned to the infirmary and saw Milo entering, his face drawn with exhaustion but his eyes still filled with curiosity. He had been staying in the makeshift barracks outside the hospital, his notebook tucked behind his ear, ready to capture the humanity behind the horrors.

He had been silent during the lullaby, but the song had left an indelible mark on him. He was a foreign journalist who had come to cover the conflict, but his eyes had revealed that he had found something deeper than a story to write. He saw the beauty in the pain and the strength in the suffering.

When Amina offered him a cup of tea, Milo accepted. He sat, and the two of them found themselves in a conversation that had the structure of a dance. They spoke of the war, the lullaby, the rumors that had circled in the village, the idea that some stories should remain untold. Milo revealed that his father had once been a photographer who captured the beauty of the African desert, and that the war had left him with a yearning to tell stories that were not only about the conflict but also about the people.

The conversation was a reminder that the war was not just about the fact that it was happening but about the people who lived in it. The war was a story that was not defined by the bullets but by the people who survived it.

---

### The Past, the Present, and the Future

Amina, once again, looked at the photograph. She thought of her father's line, of the promise his mother had made, and of the moments that had taught her that love was a powerful force. She realized that the war was not a story that only he could write but also a story that she could write, with the help of Milo.

She put the photograph on the back of the infirmary’s door and looked at the notes she had left in the notebook. The notes were a way to keep the conversation alive. She wrote in the same handwriting as her mother’s and added a line that was new, something that had not yet been written.

*“I write this to find a way to tell the story of the war and the people who survived it. I write this to honor the memory of my father. I write this to give you a chance, Milo, to be a part of the story.”*

Milo looked at the note with a quiet nod. He saw that Amina was not only a nurse but also a storyteller with a heart that had the courage to paint a picture that would bring the war’s humanity to the world.

---

### The Legacy

The night went on with a quiet devotion. The battlefield outside was a churning sea of fire that illuminated the sky and the weary faces of the soldiers and the civilians. The sound of distant artillery kept the calm, but the silence that shivered between the two of them allowed them a moment to breathe. They realized that the war was a story about more than the battles and the carnage; it was about people who had a will to survive.

The next day, Amina began to write. She grabbed her mother’s notebook, the one whose pages were filled with ink, and she began to write a story that would keep the memory of the war alive. She wrote about the lullaby and the war and the people who had survived it. She wrote about the love that had survived the war and how it carried her forward.

The story was a promise that she would never leave the memory of the war behind. She would write and document the war that would survive her and would bring the stories of the people to the world. She would find a way to keep the story alive.

---

The night the war took a respite, the village was alive in the memory of the photographs. The lullaby was a reminder that among the shroud of war, there existed a story that would carry the people forward. And in that story was a promise that the war would not be a story that was only told by the leaving side, but a story that would be shared by the people in the present, and that would be remembered by the future.

The war was a story that would become a reference point for the next generation; a legacy that would keep the people alive. Amina would carry that legacy across the valleys that had been carved by war and would write a story that would be remembered for as long as the world had a heart.
