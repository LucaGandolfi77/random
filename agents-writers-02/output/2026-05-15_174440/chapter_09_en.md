## Chapter 9 – The Siege of Sankoré  

The first crack of thunder rolled across the plains of Sankoré as the horizon turned a bruised crimson. Amina stood at the edge of the makeshift hospital, the thatched roof of the tents a fragile tapestry against the sky. The air smelled of sweat, antiseptic, and the faint, acrid tang of gunpowder carried by the wind. She could feel it in her bones, the metallic taste of fear that had settled in her mouth since the war’s first roar.

Behind her, the field ambulance—our only lifeline—was a small, battered convoy of plastic and prayer. Milo, who had been her confidante and accidental lover, leaned against the hood of a truck, his eyes scanning the horizon with a vigilance that seemed almost superstitious. He had a pen tucked behind his ear, ready to capture another headline, another story that might change the world. Yet now, the world seemed to tilt on an axis she could not see.

Amina’s hands trembled as she checked the first aid kit over her shoulder, feeling the weight of each bandage, each vial of antibiotics. Her mind flitted back to the morning when the artillery began to roar, the dust devils that turned the sky into a canvas of black and gold, and the faint lullaby that had floated from the village, a thread of humanity in a broken place. She had clung to that lullaby like a life raft, a reminder that even in war, music could still find a way to heal.

Milo’s voice cut through the silence. “We’re on the brink, Amina. The front has pushed three kilometers past the old railway. The soldiers are going to cross the river tonight. We can’t keep the hospital open if the town falls. We have to decide now.”

He turned to her, eyes sharp with urgency. “I have a story. The enemy is taking the town. I can’t let the world know what’s happening in the streets. I need to go deeper. It could be dangerous, but I’ll make it safer.”

Amina swallowed. Her throat tightened. “And what about the wounded? The children? The men who have no choice but to stay?”

Milo’s shoulders slumped. “There’s no one to rescue them, Amina. The border is a wall of military trucks, the roads are blocked. We either leave… or we stay and become a beacon for the enemy. Either way, we’re in a powder keg.”

She felt the tremor of the ground vibrate through the soles of her bare feet. It was a hiss, a whisper of the conflict that had already begun to devour everything she had known. Amina thought of Lila, her sister, who had been swept away by the chaos when the first shell had ripped through her village. She thought of the boy who had been buried in the dirt beside one of the tents, a silent testament to the futility of war, and she wondered whether Milo would ever see him in the same way.

She lifted her gaze to the horizon, where the first light of dawn began to break. The dust flared, a dry, golden flame that danced in the wind. Beneath the dusty sky, the old stone walls of Sankoré stood proud, their walls a relic of a once-great empire. The war had turned them into a battlefield. Amina’s heart pounded. Her love for Milo surged, a flame that could burn the earth or safeguard the world.

“Please,” she whispered, her voice a fragile vow. “I’ll stay. I’ll keep the hospital running. I can’t leave you behind. I can’t leave the wounded. I can’t... I can’t let anyone else die while I stay in one corner.” Her eyes flickered to Milo’s face, a mirror of the same fear that made her blood run cold.

Milo swallowed, the sound sharp in the stillness of the morning. He could not ignore the truth of her words. He also knew the truth of his own conscience, that of a man who had carried letters from strangers, whose fingers could write a headline that could change the world. He could not carry that weight alone.

“We’ll do this together,” he said, the word heavy with the weight of their decision. “I’ll stay with you. I’ll stay with the wounded.”

Amina’s breath hitched. The war could not stop them. They had already faced so many demons. When the first shell cracked, the first mortar, the first bullet that had grazed her father's shoulder. They had faced the darkness together, feeding on each other’s courage. They were a pair of soldiers who had found an unlikely haven in each other.

The day stretched to a gray sky. The sun grew low, casting an amber glow across the battlefield. The artillery went off again, a low, guttural rumble that seemed to shake the earth. Amina paced the perimeter of the tents, feeling the sweat pour down her forehead, the distension of her chest. Her mind was a whirl of frantic thoughts—what to do to keep the wounded safe, how to keep the town from falling, and most of all, how to hold Milo against the tide of fear that threatened to pull him away.

Milo did not go back out to the front lines. He had a reputation as a journalist, an unrelenting pursuit for truth. Yet he knew that the truth was not what he had been taught to value. The truth that mattered to him was the story of a woman who was a beacon for the wounded, a woman who had been standing against death with her hands and her heart.

The night was thick with dust, the sky covering the sounds in an eerie stillness. Milo’s voice came from the corner of the hospital where a small tent had been set up for his lodging. He sat cross-legged, his hands wrapped around a battered notebook, the pages filled with a bold, hurried script. He said, “Amina, I can’t… I have to go. I can’t… I have to tell the world what we are doing. I’m sorry.”

He stumbled on the edges of his words, his voice laced with all the fear that had been growing within him. “It’s not safe. The enemy will find us. They will take you. And then, I will never see you again.”

Amina’s heart shattered. She reached out, grasping his chest, feeling the pulse in his veins. The world had become one of uncertainty, but she still felt a spark of hope—that he would stay. She could not bear the thought of losing him. She turned to the window and saw the confluence of the blood that had been spilled on the ground, a river that flowed in stunted, hopeless, clotted shapes of humanity.

She swallowed again, as if filling her lungs with air that would be safe. “Milo,” she began, her voice trembling. “I… I can’t let the world be the only witness. The stories are alive, even if the world can’t feel the sorrow of the soldiers. The war is here. The wounded are here.”

He looked at her, his eyes a storm of fear. “I will stay with you,” Milo said, his voice a murmur.

Her fingers moved with the precision of a surgeon, her hands steady even as the world seemed to crumble. The little boy was a son of the soil, a boy who had never known a world beyond the dusty streets.
