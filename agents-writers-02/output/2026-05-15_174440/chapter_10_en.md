## Chapter 10 – Letters in the Fray

The night had settled over the makeshift hospital like a shroud of gray smoke, the ceaseless thrum of artillery a distant drumbeat that never quite faded. Amina crouched beside the battered tin bed of a young soldier, his eyes fluttering open and closed like the wings of a moth in search of the nearest light. The thin sheets fluttered in the draft that slipped through the broken windows, carrying with them the scent of antiseptic mingled with distant smoke and the faint, unsettling perfume of muskets.

She tucked a single red ribbon—Milo’s locket—into the care of a handkerchief, pressing it against the wound with a trembling finger. The soldier’s blood was a cold, spreading river beneath her palm, and she could feel the weight of the world pressed against her chest. The world, she realized, was not just the sound of distant gunfire or the soft keening of patients; it was the fragile thread of hope that still clung to her heart, the one she had found in Milo’s eyes two weeks prior, now fraying at the edges.

Milo’s voice, soft and urgent, cracked through the chaos. “I’m coming for you.” It was a simple statement, but it carried a promise that felt larger than the war itself. She had never known anyone who could conjure that kind of breathless certainty in the thickest of smoke, and now she was drawn to it as if it were a magnet pulling her from the desolation around her.

She closed her eyes, breathing in the scent of blood and tin, and pressed the locket against her throat. “I’ll be waiting,” she whispered, her voice raw. “I’ll be writing to you.”

The kettle whistles, a small, clear sound that seemed to cut through the din. She looked up to see Milo, his dark hair disheveled, his face pale from heat and exhaustion, his jacket soaked through. He rushed forward, a brief, frantic hand on the soldier’s shoulder.

“Get him back,” he hissed. “We’ve got to keep moving.”

When the soldier’s pulse steadied, Milo turned to her, his eyes reflecting a storm of unspoken thoughts. He pressed the paper against the edge of the makeshift desk. It was a letter, penned in a hurried hand, the ink smeared from a tear.

*Amina,* he began, the words trembling in his voice, *I am writing to you because I cannot imagine a day without your light. The war has taken so much from us—our homes, our dreams, our peace. I have seen so much blood, I have seen so many faces that will never be remembered. But you are the only thing that makes the darkness bearable. I am writing this because I am scared—scared that I might never see you again. I am scared that I might die and that you will be left behind, alone. I am scared that I am leaving behind a future that we both dared to dream.*

He paused, looking at the space between the words, as if searching for a missing piece of himself. *I have a promise I must keep,* he continued, *a promise I made to my mother before I left for the front. I must tell you that I will return to you. No matter where I am, no matter what I do, I will fight for us, for the love that we have born. I will write you. I will give you a piece of me.*

His voice broke. *I love you, Amina.*

The letter was simple, but it was a mirror of his soul: raw, honest, unfiltered. She read it again, the words ringing in her ears. She saw the fear in his eyes, the conviction of his promise, and the love that had found its way into his heart.

She folded the letter carefully, putting it into the envelope that still smelled of the soldier’s blood. The hospital’s hum intensified as a new wave of wounded arrived, the bright white of the sheets streaked with bruises and searing pain.

She knew that she could not send the letter. Milo was out there somewhere, in a different, possibly dead world. She could not know where he was, and if he was alive, if he would ever see the letter and read her words. Yet, the act of writing it, of hearing his voice, was enough to soothe the jagged edges of the war’s noise. She felt a small fragment of peace, a fragile hope, like a small spark of flame in the darkness.

She folded the paper again, and this time she slipped it into a small hollow in the back of a wooden box. The box was small, wooden, and weathered. It was a box that Milo had given her, saying, “Hold onto my words, and you’ll always have me with you.” It was a promise that she would keep, no matter what.

When Milo was gone, the war would come to take him in some other form, but his words would remain in her heart, a lantern that would guide her through the darkness.

She could hear the soldier’s pale breathing, the clack of the tin on the floor, the wind rustling the leaves outside, the distant gunfire that rattled her bones. She could hear the echo of Milo in the wind, the light that he had brought to her life. She could feel the weight of her love, the connection that bound her to a man she had never known, but that she would change her life forever.
