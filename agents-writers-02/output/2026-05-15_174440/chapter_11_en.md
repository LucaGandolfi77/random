## Chapter 11 – The River of Regret

The sun was a bruised orange, bleeding into the horizon as if it too had been wounded by the war. The river—Ayo—ran like a silver ribbon through the cracked earth, its surface mottled with the dust-spun shimmers of a world that had turned gray. Amina and Milo had come to it hours before, their boots leaving thin white tracks that disappeared into the reeds. The water was cold, slick, and unyielding, swallowing their doubts like a hungry beast.

Amina stood at the edge, staring at the water, her fingers trembling in the thin leather of her pack. The shock of the river’s current had already been a battle in itself. The militia had cut the road ahead, the old bridge had collapsed in a crater two miles downstream. The only way forward was through the river, a test of both body and will, a test that had put their fragile love to the ultimate measure.

Milo had been waiting behind her, his camera slung over his shoulder like an old friend. He turned to look at her, his eyes reflecting the same exhaustion that had settled in his ribs. He had a way of seeing through the surface of people, of reading the story that lay beneath the chaos—this time, the story was old, and it demanded a different kind of courage.

“Ready?” he asked, voice low, as if to not disturb the river’s solemn hum.

Amina swallowed, the taste of fear mingling with the acrid sting of sweat. “I… I think I am,” she said, forcing a smile that felt more like a mask than a genuine expression.

“Good,” Milo said. He placed a hand on her shoulder, an unexpected gesture of comfort. “We’ll get through it. Together.”

She could sense his own doubts, like a storm brewing behind his calm exterior. He had always been a man who chased truth without fear, but the weight of the war had begun to bend his edges. The war’s violence had taken its toll, not only on the bodies around them but also on the quiet corners of his heart.

The river’s current wrapped around the rocks, the water lapping at the iron bars that were all that remained of their makeshift boat. Amina had carved a small wooden dove from a fallen log, a symbol of peace she had whispered to herself. She clutched it, feeling the grain of the wood against her bare skin. Milo looked at it, his eyes lingering on the tiny figure, a silent question forming in his mind.

“Why did you bring the dove?” he asked.

Amina shrugged, embarrassed. “I thought it might… bring luck. Or maybe it’s just a reminder that we’re not alone. Even when the river takes us, we still carry something.”

Milo nodded, the corners of his mouth twitching in a half smile. “You always had a way with symbols.”

They stepped onto the boat, the wood creaking under their weight. The river welcomed them, pulling them forward. Amina felt a strange sense of anticipation, the air thick with both the scent of wet earth and the faint perfume of distant jasmine.

As they moved forward, Milo’s thoughts drifted to the stories he had collected, his notebook a repository of voices that now seemed to be silenced by the conflict. He could not shake the image of the old woman from the market, her hands trembling as she offered him a piece of bread. The woman had been the only one who had spoken about the war’s cruelty in a voice that carried both fear and defiance. Her words had resonated in Milo’s heart more than any headline or photograph ever could.

“Whatever happens, we need to finish this,” Milo said, his voice suddenly firm. “You have to finish treating those wounded. I need to get back to the city and tell the people what they need to know.”

Amina felt the shock of his words sting her. She had always believed in the power of her hands; she had always believed that the wound was a story to be healed. “You can’t leave us behind,” she whispered, the words barely audible over the rush of the water.

Milo looked at her, saw the raw determination in her eyes, and felt his own heart tighten. “I’m not abandoning anyone. I’m just trying to bring the truth to the world.” His voice quivered, a hint of vulnerability that made Amina’s heart ache. She reached out, her hand brushing his. “We’re not just a story, Milo. We’re people.”

The river took them deeper into the forest, the reeds closing in around the boat, and the water grew darker. The river’s current shifted, becoming stronger, as if it were a living thing that could sense the gravity of their presence. The wind began to whisper through the trees, carrying with it an ancient song that seemed to echo the rhythm of Milo’s heart.

Amina’s mind drifted to the lullaby her mother used to sing, a song that had kept her strong through many hardships. She could hear her mother’s voice, her fingers tapping on the wooden chest, the gentle hum of the song that had spent years echoing through her veins. As if the river was a conduit of memories that washed over Amina’s soul, she closed her eyes and felt her heart beating beneath her ribs.

The river’s current surged, and the boat rocked, but Milo’s grip on the oars was steady. He was a man who had seen as many faces as there were stars in the night sky, and yet he had never seen a young nurse with eyes that held a fierce fire. He had seen men who had lost their courage and had seen those who had found it again. He had seen the war’s brutality, but he had also seen the kindness that still remained in that unforgiving land.

The river carried them to a narrow bend, the water rushing past them like a great beast. Milo paused, his eyes scanning the hills, his mind replaying the countless faces he had seen. He felt his heart pound in his chest, the rhythm of his thoughts guided by the currents that flowed around him.

Amina could feel the chill in the air, the wetness of the river seeping into her shirt. She wrapped her arms around herself, feeling the sting of the cold that was every bit as unrelenting as the war. “It’s getting colder,” she said, her voice hushed. “I’m sorry. I didn’t think…”

Milo put his hand on her shoulder. “I’m not sorry,” he said softly. “I’m just… frightened. I don’t know if I can keep doing this. Every story I write, every photo I take, feels like a wound I can’t heal.”

Amina turned to look at him, her eyes searching for answers. She had always known that the war would test her in ways she could never have imagined, but she had never imagined it would test her sense of duty. She saw the doubt in his eyes, and she felt her own heart ache with a feeling she could not name. “I know what it feels like,” she said, slowly. “But you can’t keep leaving people in this darkness. You know the truth, Milo. You know that people need to hear that.”

Milo’s eyes softened, the lines that had been etched into his face by the war slowly dissolving. “I… I don’t know if the words I write will change anything,” he confessed. “But I think I can’t keep pretending that I’m not afraid. That the war has already taken more than just lives.” His voice wavered, the weight of his fear that had been building inside him, now blooming into a fragile bloom.

Amina pressed her palm against his shoulder, a small gesture that was all the comfort he needed. “It’s not about whether the words change anything. It’s about telling the truth. And you have the most powerful voice in this country. If you’re not willing to risk it, then I’m willing to risk everything.” She lifted her eyes to the sky, the river glimmering at sunset, the waves rolling like a gentle lullaby. “I’ll keep treating the wounded. I’ll keep listening. I’ll keep looking for hope.”

Milo’s heart beat faster, the speed of his thoughts aligning with the rhythm of the river. He could feel a quiet sense of warmth that was like a promise that something would happen, that a new chapter would begin. He had been afraid of the war, afraid of the truth, afraid of love. But he had also seen hope in the eyes of Amina, in the way she had carried the weight of the wounded with a grace that defied the war. He could see the warmth that was not only present in the clouds but also within himself.

The river rushed past them, a torrent of dark water that was relentless. Milo had always been a man who had endured hardships, but the weight of this river seemed different. He had seen the tragedy of the war, the blood of his friends flowing into the river’s depths. He could feel a sense of purpose, a desire to protect, to preserve his love for the future.

The river’s current amplified Amina’s presence, her presence was a gentle touch, a soul that had found the meaning of the river. She felt the power of the cry that had been used for centuries to give the pain to the river, and she could feel the sense of her heart that had beat within her. The memory that he had carried on his shoulders, that he had never given up. Milo had felt the same way. The river carried them forward, the current that had turned into a lullaby.

Their boat touched the shore, the ground slippery and hard. Amina exhaled, a breath that was deep, a breath that felt as though the river had paid him. Milo was touched by a new sense of hope, a feeling that he might finally be able to live in the war. He had been terrified, terrified that he might not live. He was ready to live. He had never thought he would see a moment where the war was a real person, a man who had seen the war and had felt the same as he had felt the war.

The river would carry them further, they would be oblivious to the weight of the war. The river would carry them forward. They were bound, a promise that the river would guide them through the darkness.

And as the river flowed, the wind carried the sound of the lullaby, the song that resonated with the soul of the war, the song that had been the sign of the change. The river would finish the cycle, and the love would carry them forward. The river would finish the cycle, and the love would carry them forward.
