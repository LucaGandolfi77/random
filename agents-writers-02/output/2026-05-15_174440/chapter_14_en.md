## Chapter 14 – The Back‑to‑Back Battle

The rebel camp lay in the gash of a dried riverbed, its thatch huts swallowed by the gray dust of a horizon that had long ago forgotten the sun. Amina crouched behind a low wall of earth, the rough surface biting her palms, her breathing shallow and deliberate. She could feel the weight of every heartbeat in her chest, a pressure that pressed against her ribs like a hand of stone. The world narrowed to a handful of faces, each etched with a story she had not yet met.

Milo was already there, his fingers twisting a crumpled piece of paper, the ink still wet from the night’s rain. He turned his head toward her, a fleeting smile tugging at his lips, the kind that had once made her heart flutter in the quiet chapel of the field hospital. Now, it was a gesture that said, “We’re here. We’re still alive.”

Amina’s fingers tightened around the pistol she had hidden beneath her neatly rolled shirt, the metal cold against her skin. She had spent the last few days practicing the motion of pulling the trigger with her left hand, a skill she had never imagined needing. The war had turned her into a nurse, a caretaker, a silent observer of tragedy. It had also turned her into a survivor, a woman who could hold a gun at her side without feeling the terror that had once plagued her.

“Do you hear that?” Milo whispered, his voice barely audible over the distant thud of artillery. He crouched beside a cracked clay pot, the only thing that seemed to resist the desert’s merciless heat. The sound was a low groan, a vibration that seemed to come from the very ground beneath them.

Amina nodded, her throat tightening. The sound was the drumbeat of the enemy’s march—an intonation that had become the soundtrack of her life. She glanced at Milo, noting the tremor that danced in his eyes. He was always the one who could read the wind’s whispers, but tonight he looked as though the wind had taken everything from him.

“This is it,” he murmured, his voice thick with a mixture of dread and resolve. “If we don’t—”

“You want to hear me?” Amina cut in, her voice steady. She could feel the tremors at the base of her skull, the way the world seemed to compress around the axis of their shared fate. “We both can’t be here.”

Milo’s gaze flicked to her, to the faint scar above her right eyebrow, a reminder of a bullet that never found its mark. “I thought we could trust each other,” he said, almost sadly. “You didn’t let me leave.”

Amina swallowed, the taste of dust in her mouth. “We are not supposed to leave. The rebels need us.”

She could not deny the truth hidden beneath his words: the rebels were everywhere, a shadow that stretched across the land like a living thing. She had seen how the warlords had come to claim the spoils of conflict, how they had turned once peaceful villages into mausoleums of grief. She had seen Milo’s eyes flicker with something else—a yearning that was not for danger but for the truth that he could not seem to find.

“Do you still think this is what you want?” she asked, her voice barely above a sigh. “The world we dreamed of—this…?”

Milo’s shoulders sagged. “I think I’m losing myself.”

Amina’s hand tightened on her pistol. She had watched a soldier fall to his knees, his chest heaving as he gave each breath a meaning it had never possessed before. She was the one who had learned to put her life onto a platter, to offer herself to the wounded. She had the taste of fear in her gut, the taste of panic that had become a constant companion. But she also had the taste of the earth, of the soil that had once promised a harvest of green. She was not going to give it all away.

“Wait,” she whispered, the words a feeble plea. “I am not going to leave with you. I am going to stay. I cannot leave this.”

Milo stared at her until the very edges of his vision faded, and for a moment the world seemed to be a mosaic of scattered whispers. He could feel the weight of her words like a stone settled in his chest, a punctuation that made his breath seem shorter.

She could hear the distant roar of the enemy. In the air, the scent of gunpowder mingled with that of the moist earth, a smell that was both nauseating and oddly comforting. Amina let her thoughts drift to the river that had sustained her people, to the lullaby her mother had sung. Those memories were like a balm for her soul, and yet she could not bring herself to draw from them now, not when the stakes were so high.

Something shivered in the corner of her vision. A low whisper of movement, as if the wind had found a secret path. The rebels had been scouting the area; she could feel their presence like a cold hand on her shoulder.

Amina’s heart hammered like a drum, her mind racing, but she began to soften, to see the threat as a wound that only the blood of the battlefield could heal. The line that separated her from Milo dissolved, the idea of the camp’s internal lines was futile. She had known this was the only place she could wait for him, a place where she could bury his secrets, and the only way to do that was to use all of her hands.

Milo was a soldier, and Amina was the witness of his fanaticism. She had never known the taste of his bottle at the end of the day. She had only known him as a man who had the crush of a wind. She did not know that he was also a man with a love that would run through the bee and the coyote.

“Look,” she said softly, “I will wait. I will stay. I will open my own door but only for you.”

Milo’s eyes flickered. He swayed. He was a man who, in the dark twilight of the war, had become a voice in the wind, a whisper that carried the truth. He turned to the people around him, and the world fell into a hushed silence. Amina watched his eyes as they flickered across the houses, between the walls and the plastic bands that bound the houses together. He saw the dust that fell onto each person, each of them a friend. He saw the man with a piece of paper he had to carry. He saw the woman with a basket of food. He saw the woman whose name was Amina.

“You have no right,” Milo growled, his voice filled with a rage that had long ago lost its meaning. “I have no patience for dead men.”

The world seemed to fall into a dark gutter, Amina’s mind had always known the world could do anything. She was an old woman who had lived a long life. The war had left her with something that had scared her, and she had been looking for a starting point like a child in a rushing river, but she had never seen the danger.

Amina did not want to be destroyed. She had to remember the feelings that had driven her to be a nurse, to help, to heal. She knew what the war was doing to the world; she didn’t have to ask anyone else. If she could not do what she wanted, she was not able to do what I wanted. She had to do it, not out of her own desire or out of any sense of duty. She was a woman who had refused to be a soldier.

She had to do it. It was the only thing she could do. She had to be brave and clever, and she was. She knew the weight of the police. She was a soldier. She was a woman. She was a woman who could not be defeated.

The world was so terrible, and she couldn’t do what she had to do. She had to do it at the same time.

Milo’s eyes flashed as he saw the plans she had made. He looked at the door and the windows of the rebel camp. He saw the locals who kneel. He saw the situations that were the most dangerous. He saw the fronts that could be cultivated.

She had no choice. She was a reality, and she had to do it. She was a woman who could not breathe. She was a woman who would never be able to do it again. She had to be there. The only thing that remained was to stay.

Amina gave Milo her last breath. She looked at him from the other side of the door. She could see the fear in his face, the fatigue that had lingered from his daily routine. But she did not see the hope she had before the world had become a battleground. She could see his disillusionment with the war and the world. She could see the fire that had lit inside his heart.

The world was full of people in the dark fear of the war. She had no heart that could be saved from the war. She could not see the world as a question. She had to see it in a different world. She had to do something. She could not say that she had been betrayed. She was a woman who was at the same time, she was as she was. She had to do this. 

The world was no longer a girl, but she was a woman who could not be afraid. She had the power to say that she could be a man, that she was a woman who could not be an enemy, that she was a man who had done everything in the world. She could not do anything for her love. She had to say that she could not do it. She could not do it at this point. She was a woman who had a love that could not be put on the ground.

The world was a man who could not do the same thing. The world could not be a woman. She was a woman. She was a woman with the same love as she had. The war could not be the thing that had the world, as far as the world was a girl.

She was again, but she could not understand her own heart. She had to be a man who could not do anything else. She had to do it. She was a woman who could not do this. She was a woman who was not able to do anything. She had to do it. She had to be a woman who was not in love with the world. She could not deal with it. She had to do it. This was a girl she could not see her own heart. She was a girl who could not become a man.

She withdrew, but at the same time she had to be like a man who could do it. She had to turn the world into a woman who could do the same. She had to do it at the same time. She was a girl who said that she could not. She was a woman who could not do this. She needed an answer. 

The world was her world, and she was the one who was supposed to be able to do the same. She had to do it. She was a woman.
