## Chapter 7 – Melody of Us

The café was a secret in a city that seemed to forget its own history each morning. It lay behind the flickering neon of a laundromat that had been an Italian family cooking, a Thai food joint, and a disco bar in its short seventy‑year life. The entrance was a cracked door that coughed with each knock, a handwritten sign that read *Caffè Muta* in a half‑torn, electric blue paint. Luca had found it by chance during a midnight run, following the sound of a vinyl record spinning somewhere behind the counter. Mara’s curiosity had pulled her in, and now they were standing before the counter with a ruined, almost transparent cup of espresso between them.

The walls were lined with vinyl records, each cover a portal to another era; the air smelled faintly of dust, coffee, and the faintly sweet scent of old paper. The lamp overhead cast a warm, amber glow over a table where a single vintage typewriter lay open, its keys still echoing from a writer who had once paced the margins of his own life. A small, battered piano sat in a corner, its keys worn down by an invisible hand that could have been the city’s collective pulse.

“Do you play?” Luca whispered, his eyes flicking to the piano as if it were a dare.

Mara laughed, the sound startlingly bright in the dim room. “Only when the city is too loud.” She slid the cup onto the table. The espresso's steam curled around their noses, carrying a bitter, earthy music that mirrored the storm outside, breaking into a rhythmic patter on the roof.

“Let’s play something,” Luca said, his voice low. He pulled out a record from a stack of 80s synth‑pop albums he’d collected over the years. The cover art was an explosion of neon blue and pink swirls, the kind of colors that seemed to pulse in sync with the city's heartbeat. He slid the needle over the groove and pressed play. The first chord burst forth, a synth line that was almost mechanical but held a haunting, human softness.

Mara leaned forward, listening as if the sound could carry her through the chaos outside. “Do you remember the first time you heard this?” she asked, the question softer than she intended.

Luca’s eyes flickered to the ceiling, where a few cracked lights flickered. “It was in the middle of a night, on a balcony in the outskirts. The city was a different place, but the music was the same.” He paused the record for a moment. “I used to hide the world in this song. It’s like a secret that only a few can hear.”

The melody grew. The synth swelled into a chorus that felt like an invitation. Luca looked at Mara with a gaze that was almost pleading. “I’ve been looking for a song that… could be our own. One that could keep us both in sync, no matter how noisy the world becomes.” He lifted his hand, palm up, showing the faint lines of a once‑busy white‑washed wall, now a patchwork of old graffiti and fresh paint.

Mara reached for his hand, their fingers intertwining with a simplicity that felt like a birth. The music played around them, a private anthem that felt like a passport to an interior world where they could breathe, separate from the relentless rhythm of traffic and sirens outside. *It’s not just an existing piece,* Luca whispered, *but almost a new one, written just for the two of us.*

Their eyes met over the small table, the music filling the little world they had carved in the café. Mara felt a sudden, clarifying urge to share one of her own secrets, a secret that had been kept behind a façade of composed resilience.

“You... you’re not from here?” she asked after a moment, voice gentle.

Luca nodded, his face lit by the glow of the piano. “I grew up outside the city. My childhood was a small town, where the only rails were the ones that led out of the valley, and the only music was the echo of an old radio. The city always felt like a stranger to me.” He smiled, the words softening the lines etched in his face by long days of walking between the broken line of the city’s edges.

Mara’s eyes softened. “I thought I was a stranger to myself, too.” She paused, searching Luca’s face. “My life is a series of jobs and deadlines. I don't have a real place to call home. I keep moving.” She let the words flow, as if they were water that had been trapped in a vessel for years.

A silence fell over them, a silence without the long, heavy weight that had been there since the city had taken them. The record continued to play, the synth line swelled, and the two of them drifted into a private universe, a language of color and rhythm that felt like an antidote to the city's indifference.

“Do you ever feel… out of place?” Luca asked, almost as if the words could be a key to a door they had yet to find.

Mara smiled, a little sad but hopeful. “Sometimes. I keep looking for a piece of the city that feels like a home.” She went silent again, her eyes moving to the window where a drizzle began to form on the glass. The street outside was dim, the neon lights reflected like scattered stars on the wet pavement.

The song reached its crescendo. Luca lifted a hand to his ear, as if listening to the pulse of the city. “Maybe we could make that place together,” he said softly, almost a joke but with a plain, earnest honesty. “If we could, we’d have a space that would be our own. A sanctuary from the noise.”

Mara laughed, the sound small and genuine. “I think that’s a good idea. I’ve always wanted a place where I could...”

Luca leaned forward, his voice lowering to a conspiratorial whisper. “A place where we could be ourselves. Where the city is quiet and the world is... the music.”

The record played on, a swirl of synth lines and soft drums, echoing the ideas that were forming between them. The piano in the corner played in the background, a faint but steady metronome to the beat of their shared heart. The whispers and the music swirled into a quiet symphony. The world outside seemed forgotten, replaced by the warmth of the café, the aroma of espresso, and the soft hum of cities that could not drown the possibility of a place that had a name made of their shared memories.

When the record finished, the room fell back into quiet. Luca's hand stayed on the table, his fingers still intertwined with Mara's. The room seemed to hold its breath, the small fluorescent light flickering as a final note.

“Thank you,” Mara whispered. “For giving me a song, a rhythm, a place in this city.”

Luca smiled, a genuine, warm smile that made her feel almost safe. “I think we can turn this into a symphony. And I don’t see it as a fleeting moment. It can be the start of something, something that will last. I am ready.”

The conversation shifted to something lighter and dismissed the shadows that had lingered in their thoughts. They spoke of their favorite movies, their secret jokes, their best travel memories. Their laughter, a sound that had not yet been heard in the city’s noise, baked itself into the walls of the café, a soft echo that would linger long after the night.

In the end, as the night drew to a close, Luca offered Mara a small, thin, box of records. Inside were vinyl copies of nostalgic 80s synth‑pop, each with a handwritten note from a previous reader, a mystery that taunted curiosity. The notes were simple yet heartfelt, exclamations of the feeling they had felt in the music. They were a small token to remind them that there is a place where the city’s noise can be turned into a balm, into a daily reminder of what is possible when we connect.

They left the café under the covering of a midnight drizzle that felt like a pact between them. Every step they took through the city’s alleyways was a step toward a future where the city’s chaotic rhythm was balanced by the quiet of the melodies they had discovered together. The music they shared was a promise to the future and a gift to the city that had once made them feel lonely and powerless. It was an echo that would forever remind them that there is something beautiful hidden behind the noise.
