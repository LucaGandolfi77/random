## Chapter 10 – Rain‑kissed Confessions  

The night had been a merciless percussion, a drumbeat of thunder that seemed to crack the city’s concrete heart. Luca and Mara slipped into the narrow, shadowed underpass beneath the overpass that shepherded the river’s meandering course. Their breaths, ragged and hot, fogged in the cold air as they hurried past abandoned mailboxes and rusted bicycles.

“Better than the bridge,” Luca muttered, pushing Mara forward.

She glanced back to see the jagged silhouette of the old bridge, its girders twisted like a spider’s web. Rain hammered the steel, splattering the puddles on the cracked asphalt. The sound was a low roar, almost a hiss, like the city lungs were beating too fast.

The bridge loomed ahead, a skeletal structure that seemed to absorb the rain. The light from the streetlamps dimmed behind the clouds, leaving a blue-grey glow that swallowed Mara’s face. She shivered, her fingers still trembling from the fall earlier, from the collision that had sparked this evening’s chain of events.

“Stop,” Luca whispered, grabbing her wrist. “Let’s not waste time. There’s a small shop right there, a mailbox with a brass lock. Inside, there’s a small garden. It’s a safe spot.”

Mara’s eyes widened. She had heard about the secret garden behind the old post office, where the city’s noise fell silent. It was a rumor, a whispered legend among those who walked the streets after dark. If they were to find a place to shelter, it had to suffice.

They ran, darting between dumpsters, their shoes splashing through the rain-soaked puddles. The city’s hum shrank, replaced by the muffled hum of the roof tiles clicking against the storm. They stumbled into a narrow hallway, the air heavy with the smell of damp concrete and earth.

The small shop was an old leather-bound gate, a door that creaked when they pushed it open. Inside, a tableau of vines saturated in emerald, green leaves that shimmered under the weak light of a single bulb. The rain slanted through the rafters, filtering in a pale, watery glow. In the center of the room, a small pot of rosemary and a bouquet of wildflowers in a chipped porcelain vase. A wooden table sat under the lone bulb, a single chair opposite it, and a single framed photograph of a man and a woman laughing in the rain, their faces blurred by the storm.

The silence was thick, reverent. The only sound was the distant echo of a siren, a low, mournful howl that seemed to mourn the city’s cries. Luca let his eyes wander over the walls, each one covered in flyers and posters for local events, a testament to the community’s resilience.

Mara’s fingers curled around the warm mug they’d been given, a chipped ceramic with a chipped handle. They sat opposite each other, a table between them, a small garden outside. The world outside the window was a blur of rain and steel.

“Do you think it’s safe?” Mara asked, her voice barely a whisper.

“The city... it’s a labyrinth,” Luca replied, staring into the pot of rosemary. “But I’ve walked this road a long time. It’s a labyrinth, but there’s a way out.”

He placed his hand over her own on the table, a gesture that felt both sudden and natural. She looked at his fingers, tiny eddies of warmth. The gentle rhythm of the rain, the quiet hum of dandelions, the scent of rosemary made the moment feel almost… sacred.

Her heart fluttered, hesitant, as she looked at his eyes. The storm outside raged, but inside, the tension was calm. The world seemed to tilt, the smell of rain, the sound of thunder, the hum of the city – they were all part of a tapestry that was beyond her control, but also part of the fabric of their lives.

“Do you ever…?” Mara began, her voice trembling.

“But what?” Luca asked, turning his gaze to her.

Mara’s eyes were bright, filled with a mixture of nervousness and curiosity. “Do you ever think about… about where you belong? Where you’re going? To be a part of something that’s real, that’s… that matters?”

Luca paused, the question resonating with his own hidden fear. He had always been an outsider, a nomad on a city that never slept, wandering through the streets, searching for meaning. He had been a wanderer, searching, and his past had been a series of memories, a collection of postcards that had landed in his pockets, seeds of something that may have yet to bloom.

She reached out, placed his hand in hers, palms brushing together like a bridge. “I’ve always felt that way too,” she whispered, her voice trembling. “I’ve always dreamed of a place where I truly belong. I always felt like I was… like I was a part of something that’s real. But when I hear the city’s sound, it feels like I’m just a ghost. I want something real.”
