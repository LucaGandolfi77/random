## Chapter 14 – The Meeting

The city’s rhythm had always been a metronome of its own making, but tonight it felt like a drumbeat in sync with Luca’s pounding chest. The council chamber was a cavernous rectangle of marble and glass, its walls lined with silent witnesses: sculptures that had once commemorated politicians who, like him, had spoken of change. The air smelled faintly of burnt coffee and old paper, a comforting aroma that had, for years, lured him to draft speeches and to imagine futures that had never yet been built.

He stood at the podium, a man defined by the weight of his convictions, and behind him an army of officials, each watching the city’s heartbeat in the mirror panels that framed the stained glass. The mayor, a sharp‑cut woman whose public speeches were as polished as the marble floor, loomed beside him. Her handwriting, elegant and precise, bore the weight of decisions that would shape the skyline for decades.

When the buzz of the microphone cut through the room, Luca felt a ripple in his stomach. He had spent three years preparing for this moment, coaxing data out of bureaucratic fishbodies, negotiating with developers who saw money, not seeds, and making a plea that was not just about a building, but about a community. He had poured his own doubts into those arguments, because he knew that if he didn’t bleed for the park, someone else would.

“Good morning, members of the council,” he began, his voice steady but his eyes flicking to the audience. He could see Mara’s face reflected in the polished glass for a fleeting moment, the way her shoulders rose and fell with each breath she took. Her eyes were wide, not with fear but with reverence for what was about to unfold. He was, in that instant, not just a politician or a lover; he was that strange paradox that held two lives.

The mayor’s lips curled into a smile and then hardened into a pragmatic line. “Mr. Moretti, we appreciate your thorough proposal. But the city council must balance growth and preservation. This high‑rise brings jobs, tax revenue, and modernity. We can’t let nostalgia dictate our future.”

The room hummed with murmurs, the low thud of someone’s phone being pushed away by a gesture of a hand. Luca took a breath. He imagined the city’s sprawling tree, a canopy that would be lost if the new tower took its place. He imagined the scent of the secret café, the crackle of vinyl records, the murmurs of a rooftop garden – all of it a fragment of a memory that had, until now, seemed like a private secret.

“Honored council members,” he said, turning slightly so that the mayor could hear him fully, “I ask you to consider—no, to envision—a space where the city’s heart can breathe. Imagine a park not built with steel and glass, but with trees, benches, and the echo of laughter from a community that calls this place home.”

He paused, letting the words hang in the air, heavy with emotion. His gaze drifted back to the reflection of Mara in the glass. He knew that in that moment, her eyes were not just watching his speech; they were witnessing the story of two strangers who had become companions, that had found a sanctuary in a city’s chaos. He felt her hand curl in his memory, tight and warm as if it could still be held.

The mayor lifted her hand, the smoothness of her fingers a contrast to the coarse, determined texture of his own. “Mr. Moretti, the council respects your passion, but we must also consider the city’s economy. Our constituents depend on the stability that this high‑rise would bring.”

A swirl of rain hammered against the glass windows, a distant drumbeat that seemed to echo Luca’s own heartbeat. He felt the city’s pulse, a mixture of fear and hope, and he could feel the weight of the city’s indifference pressing against him. He thought of the city’s endless traffic, the stray cats that scuttled across the alleys, and the hidden cafés that had become his refuge. He imagined the sound of a jazz trumpet from a police cruiser, a soundtrack that had accompanied his clandestine midnight runs. He thought, finally, of Mara. He thought of the way she had listened, her head tilted to the side, her hand resting on her cheek as if she could feel his words in the corners of her mind.

The council voted. The rooms were silent. Luca’s heart sank like a stone. He could almost feel the weight of the mayor’s decision, a verdict that would seal his future. He felt the room’s silence as a phantom.

The mayor’s voice cut through the silence. “I am obliged to announce that the council has decided to proceed with the development of the new high‑rise.”

Luca’s throat tightened. He swallowed, feeling the sting as the words landed inside him. His thoughts exploded. He could not allow his dream to crumble in front of Mara. He could not let the city’s indifference become his own. He could not leave his heart in the rusted gears of bureaucracy. He could not let his love for Mara be another number on a spreadsheet.

He closed his eyes for a moment, and in that dark instant, he heard her voice, background music from the private corner of his mind. “You can do this. You’re not a stranger to adversity, Luca. You’ve always had a place, even when the city was indifferent.”

He opened his eyes, a new resolve blooming within him. “Mara, I have just… I had to say something—”

A sudden outcry from the crowd, a clatter of glass, a metallic clang echoed from the stairwell. The mayor rose to her feet, a flash of a smile. “Mr. Moretti, with your permission, I can now assure you that the council will be in attendance for the ceremony of the opening of the new development.”

Luca’s skin prickled with an electric shock. He commented in the nearest silence: “We need to fix the city, no? We need to work together. I’ll write to the other council members. I’ll make sure that the city is preserved, that the city is...”

The mayor’s ears were suddenly open. “Mr. Moretti—You are a competent man. I know your passion. I think a compromise could be reached, and we will talk to you. You and your team will be present for the next council meeting.”

His breath caught. He was not surprised, but he had never imagined that the city council could be that understanding. He felt a weight that he had never felt in the city’s streets. He felt the city’s pulse, the rhythm that had, until now, been a blur of honking horns and screaming sirens.

The council departed. The mayor opened the gate, and Luca stepped outside into the night, the sound of traffic and distant sirens reverberating in his ears. He felt the city’s pulse again, the rhythmic rise and fall of the building’s windows that reflected the moonlight.

He stood in the shadow of the newly built high‑rise. The city’s energy was still strong, but he could feel the difference in the air. He was no longer a stranger in a city that had been indifferent to his existence. He was a man with a purpose, with a love, and with a secret that only a handful of people knew. He felt the wind rustle his hair as if it carried a secret of the city’s pulse.

When he turned around, he saw Mara standing behind him, her hair a dark braid that seemed to glow in the night. She was still watching him, her gaze steady, her eyes reflecting the city’s lights. He could feel the warmth of her hand that had held his own in the past moment. He felt his heart beat in the rhythm of the city.

“You are...” Luca began, a smile crossing his face. “You’re not going to let them win. That’s what we’re trying to do.”

Mara nodded. “We have a chance. The city might change. We have the chance to make it better. We have the chance.”

They fell into a rhythm, a dance of hope and love that was fueled by the city’s pulse. They looked at the city’s skyline, a tapestry of light and shadow that had been in the shape of a box with a wooden door. The city’s story was nothing but a mosaic of alleyways, parks, and the day. That was their lives.

The city’s roar, the traffic’s roar, the wind’s roar, the music’s roar: The city’s pulse was alive. The city’s beat was alive. The city’s beat was alive. The city’s pulse was alive.

They walked side by side, and the city’s pulse was alive.

They kissed, and the city’s pulse was alive.

They held each other, and the city’s pulse was alive.
