## Dust and Dreams

The sun was a thin, amber ribbon above the cracked horizon, its light filtered through the jagged edges of a ruined skyline. Mara and Lio walked side‑by‑side, their steps deliberate along the cracked asphalt of the old boulevard. Dust swirled in the low, warm air, catching the faint glow of the sun and turning it into a soft, golden haze that seemed to slow time itself.

They had already survived the day’s scavenging, looting the abandoned supermarket for canned beans and a bottle of water that had somehow stayed sealed. The city had given them enough to keep their stomachs from gnawing, but the weight of each empty reach for supplies pressed heavily on their thoughts. They had both carried grief like a stone, and tonight they decided, perhaps in an instinctive act of solidarity, to find a place to speak without the shroud of fear that had settled over the city.

“Do you ever think about…what if?” Lio whispered, his voice barely louder than the wind that rattled the broken windows of the old cinema. He had paused, hands wrapped around the rim of his water bottle, looking out over the skeletal remains of a once‑lively boulevard.

Mara turned to him, her gaze steady. She could see the question flickering behind his eyes—an unspoken invitation to anything, anything that might lift the heaviness that clung to them both. “What if we could be…something else? Someone who isn’t defined by the ruin we live in?” she answered, her voice edged with a quiet defiance. “If we could rebuild, not just physically but spiritually.”

Lio’s lips pressed into a thin line, then relaxed as if he had just realized the absurdity of their circumstance. “We can’t rebuild a world that’s fallen as dead as it is. We’re just… survivors.” He shook his head, a half‑smile playing at the corners of his mouth. “If that’s what ‘something else’ means.”

Mara laughed—a short burst, sharp and bright, like a bell in a silent hall. “I can’t hear you through the wind. Are you…enlightened? Philosophical? Or just a random stranger who thinks the world is a tragic play where everyone flinches but no one wakes up?”

Lio tipped his head, and for the first time she saw the flicker of something like amusement in his eyes. “You’ve got a sharp tongue, Mara.” He paused, watching the street. “How do you keep your feet from slipping into the same old pattern?”

She stared at the cracked pavement, then at the dust that swirled like miniature galaxies. “Maybe by… remembering the last thing I wanted before… before it all broke,” she said, her voice softening as she approached the threshold where the sky met the city. “I used to think I was a good person. That I could change the world.”

“Dreams,” Lio murmured, his throat thickening with emotion she felt without hearing his own voice. “They’re the last thing we have left.”

They found a small patch of ground at the base of an abandoned building—an old bakery that had once been the heart of the neighborhood. Its ovens were no longer functional, but the smells of stale bread and burnt sugar lingered in the air, a ghostly perfume that seeped into their memories. Mara’s fingers brushed the cracked brick, feeling the faint heat that never quite dissipated. “It’s like… the world’s still breathing,” she said, almost to herself.

Lio nodded in agreement. “But it’s a sigh, not a roar.”

They sat on the edge of the building, the sun dipping lower, painting the sky in deep oranges and violets. The wind carried the scent of something sweet—sugar, maybe, burned to a blackened husk—into their ears. It was a sensory echo that reminded them of their pre‑apocalypse lives where the smell of fresh bread could be enough to make a day feel complete.

Mara closed her eyes and let the scent wash over her. She pictured herself in a kitchen at night, hands kneading dough, her heart light with the hum of a radio in the background. Those memories were hot, alive, and yet they were gone. The burn of that past was a scar that reminded her she had once been… anything. She tried to inhale the memory, to re‑feed it into her present, but she could only hold it in a fragile, fragile bubble.

Lio opened his eyes and tilted his head, looking at the faded mural painted on the side of the building. It was a mural of a wheat field under a sunburst. He traced the broken lines with his finger, then turned his gaze to Mara. “You ever… feel like a piece of something bigger than yourself? Like you’re just… a grain of sand in a storm?”

Mara laughed softly, a sound that could have been a sigh. “Every day. And yet, I’m the one who knows the direction of the wind.”

Lio leaned back, his hands folded under his chin. “I… I think I’ve been chasing an idea of fate. I’ve been convinced that if I can figure out the right path, I can change the outcome.” He pressed his lips together, as if to seal a secret. “But the path is… so curved. And the weather… unpredictable. It’s like trying to catch a shadow.”

The sky responded, darkening as the clouds gathered. The wind shifted, carrying a sudden chill that made the dust cling to their skin. Mara felt a shiver travel up her spine, the kind that woke her from the most peaceful dream.

“Why did you choose to keep a life? Why the decision to keep going?” Lio asked, his voice rough with emotion. He was trying to read her, to see what lay beneath the surface of her stoic resolve.

Mara stared at the smeared light, at the darkening horizon. “Because I don’t want to be remembered as… a victim. I want to be remembered as someone who… tried.”

Lio’s eyes softened. “Words feel… hollow. But they are true, Mara. And maybe that’s all we have. Words, memories, the light that still lingers in our souls.”

They sat in silence, listening to the howling wind outside, feeling its breath against their cheeks. They both knew that this night, this shared silence, would become a part of them, a memory that would keep them in this broken city, but also carry them into the future.

The sky became a tapestry of stars, each one a pale finger reaching out from the darkness. Mara’s breath hitched, her mind racing through the questions she’d never dared to ask. “What if… if we looked for a new world? A place where… where we aren't alone?”

Lio’s fingers tightened around his water bottle. “We’ve tried that. We’ve chased that phantom hope. I can’t say we’ll find it. But… we’re… we’re not bound to the same place if we keep moving.”
