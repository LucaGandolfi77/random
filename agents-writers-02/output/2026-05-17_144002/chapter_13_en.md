## Chapter 13 – The Protest

The city never slept, but that night the streets felt as though they had finally found a rhythm of their own. A steady drumbeat, not of sirens or traffic but of voices—low, rising, breaking apart and rejoining in a chorus that cut through the usual din. Mara stood there, shoulders hunched against the chill that seeped through the concrete, her hands wrapped tight around a cardboard cup that had seen better days.

“You’re not going to get banned, right?” Theo’s voice crackled over the radio in his pocket, the low hum of the underground transit system muffling the distant clamor. He was standing a few meters away, eyes fixed on a white poster that seemed to glow under the harsh fluorescence: **“The City’s Voice – Unite for Change.”** The ink was stained with rain, the words a bleak, urgent promise.

Mara glanced at the crowd. Strangers, some with protest signs, others with faces hardened by the relentless grind of the metropolis. A vendor’s cart jostled, a group of teenagers laughed loudly, a woman with a stroller fought to keep her child from getting swallowed by the crowd. The city’s chaotic pulse was unmistakable, but there was something else bubbling beneath it—the collective hum of indignation, of people daring to speak in a city that thrived on anonymity.

Theo had come earlier, in a hurry that had made him feel like a runner in a marathon while everyone else was on a treadmill. He had known it was going to be a long night, but the thought of Mara standing on the sidelines, mentally buffering her protest with the same cautious optimism she applied to her freelance pieces, made his pulse thud against his ribs. He had promised herself that tonight would be a turning point, a moment for both of them to step into a space they’d only spoken of in whispers while humming old jazz tunes in hidden gardens.

It was in the way the city’s overarching sounds softened, in the way a young man’s voice, cut short by a sudden shout from a police officer, left a visceral ache in her chest.

She turned to Theo. His face was lit with a strange hope, and his eyes held a quiet conviction that she hadn’t seen before. The man who had once been so careful with his words now spoke like a preacher on a podium. “We’re not just shouting into the void,” he whispered. “We’re telling the city that we exist.”

Mara closed her eyes for a moment, listening to the city’s heartbeat—a rhythm that pulsed with buildings that spiraled towards the clouds and with the relentless rhythm of traffic that never surrendered. She felt a shiver that she couldn’t shake. The city was a beast that devoured patience, yet in that moment she felt it, almost—like a friend who had been beckoning her from the shadows.

The line between the protestors and the police blurred, each step to the north, each movement in the same rhythm. The city’s heart—its relentless pulse—pulsed faster as the tension rose. The air became heavy with the smell of fear and hope, a strange mixture that made Mara’s chest tighten.

Their hand was a small island amid the infinite space of the protest. The shared warmth was a quiet testament to the fact that, even in the heart of the city’s chaos, they had found a way to stay close.
