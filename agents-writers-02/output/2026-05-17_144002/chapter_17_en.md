## Chapter 17 – Virtual Horizon  

The screen glowed like a dying ember in the midst of the city’s neon haze. Mara stared at it, the dim apartment lights reflecting off the glass and turning the room into a quiet laboratory of longing. On the other side of a twenty‑minute flight, Theo’s city skyline flickered through his own window—a lattice of lights that seemed both familiar and foreign. Between them, the distance was measured in pixelated waves, in the cadence of typed words that tried, sometimes failing, to bridge the gulf that time and geography had carved.

It was a Tuesday, but for them it was any day of the week—Mondays were the most unbearable. Mara had had a rough morning at the design studio, her head crammed with the new client’s demands and her own creative drought. Theo, meanwhile, had just landed in Brooklyn after a whirlwind pitch that had left him exhausted and exhilarated. The two had carved out a routine out of necessity: a late‑night video call at 11:45 p.m. when the city fell into a hushed lull, and a text forum that buzzed with jokes and apologies for missed calls.

The first thing that struck them in the morning was the silence. It had always been a paradox: their conversations were a riot of sound, full of laughter, sharp remarks, and the kind of intimacy that only close friends share. Now, the silence that followed the last line of text made them both feel like strangers in a room with the lights off.

“Did you sleep?” Mara typed, her fingers hovering over the keyboard.

Theo’s reply appeared three seconds later, a simple “Yeah… oddly.” He had never been very good with describing his own emotions, preferring the sharpness of words to the blur of feelings that seemed to creep in when he was alone.

Mara stared at the message for a long time, the words drifting across her screen like a train passing a window. She could almost hear the rumble of the city that had surrounded them on the day that first met, the way the subway car had been packed, the way the rain had drummed on the windows. It was a small echo of the past, now replaced by the quiet hum of her apartment's air conditioner.

She could see Theo in the video call—his hair a little messier than usual, his eyes a little tired. She could see the city skyline behind him, a familiar silhouette that had once seemed so far away. The distance had become an invisible line that they both tried to cross.

“Hey,” she said, finally. The video call pinged in her headphones, and Theo’s face appeared on the screen. His smile was a small, crooked thing, but it was enough to make her heart skip a beat.

“You look… different.” His voice carried a faint French accent that she had always found charming. “What’s that on your face?”

She laughed. “I… I just had a coffee. It’s… weird.” She lowered her eyes. “I was just thinking about… us.”

His expression changed to something softer, something that made the rest of her feel like it was not that far away. “Same thing,” he said. “I was looking at the skyline and thinking about the way you see everything so differently. Like you’re looking at a city.”

A pause stretched between them, a thin line of time that connected the two of them across an ocean. Mara could feel the weight of the silence, the way it pressed against her chest like a stone. She wanted to speak, to make her voice a part of the conversation, to set the rhythm of their communication back into the familiar cadence they had once shared.

She thought of the city, the way it was always full of sound. The rain, the cars, the way the neon lights turned the streets into a living canvas. She thought of the moment she had first met Theo, on that delayed train, the way they had shared a cramped carriage and the electric connection that had sparked like a lightning bolt. Those memories were always a part of her, a raw and unfiltered part of her life. She was scared of losing those memories, of letting her heart sink into the void of distance.

She finally spoke, her voice steady, her tone deliberate. “Do you remember the night we were at the speakeasy?”

Theo’s eyes lit up, a flicker of nostalgia in his gaze. “Yeah. The vinyl, the songs, the way the music seemed to come alive with our laughter. I didn’t realize how much I miss that.”

“Me too.” Her voice trembled slightly. “I miss us. I miss the way we would talk for hours in a coffee shop, and let the city go by. The city was always in the background, like it wasn’t the main thing. Now, the city is the only thing we have.”

Theo's hands trembled, his fingers tightening on the phone. “I’ve been thinking about that a lot. The city, the noise, the endless possibility. I don’t want to lose that. I also want to be with you. I don’t know what the future holds, but I want to find a way.” He paused for a moment. “What do you think?”

She stared at his words, trying to think in the right direction. The city was alive, chaotic, a place where people were always moving, always chasing something. She couldn’t let herself fall into a trap where she would lose herself in the city. She had to think about her future, her career, and her love for Theo. She had to think about the possibility of living in a different city, a different culture. She had to think about her love.

She thought of the way she had grown in the city. The city had taught her how to love, how to breathe, how to find hope. She had a lot more to do, so as a writer, that was the best she could do.

She took a deep breath and looked at the window. The sky was a dark blue, a canvas of stars. The city beyond was still alive, the lights and the noise and the letters. She thought of how the city had always had a way of putting her back in her own place. She thought of the way she was holding onto something that was true, something that was worth fighting for.

She typed with a steady hand. “We can work this out. We can find a way to stay together. I know it’s hard, but we’re in this together.”

Theo’s reaction was immediate. He laughed, a sound that could have been a cry. “I love you,” he said. “I love you."

“Same,” she replied. “I love you. I love how you’re here, your voice, your stories. I love your sense of humor, your passion. I love how you’re there for the little things. That’s what makes me smile.”

They both laughed, the sound of their laughter laced with a bittersweet quality. The city was full of people. There were countless stories unfolding around them, countless moments of humanity. They found themselves lost in their own, drifting into a dreamlike place. The world around them fell away, the city became a distant memory, and all that remained was the love they shared.

The evening was unusually quiet that night. The city had fallen into a calm hush, the only sound the distant hum of traffic. The city’s light was still there, but the city was a place that was holding its breath. They were in the middle of a quiet day, and they were holding onto something.

The conversation flowed naturally. They talked about small things, about the days that had passed, about the city. The words were like a language they had always shared, a language that was little more than a language. They had both been searching for something, and they had found it. They had found a place where their hearts were in the right place.

They both felt the city as a place that was alive, a place that was a part of them. They had the knowledge that they would be living together in that city. They had a common sense of where their hearts were, a common understanding of the nature of the city: it was a city that was always on the move, always in motion, and they were the ones who would help it move forward.

As they said goodnight, the city had given them a promise— a hope that there were countless possibilities and that the city was an ever-changing landscape that would only add to their love in the future. The city was not a barrier, but a place that would be part of their love, a place that they would never leave.

They left the image behind, knowing that they would soon see each other again. The distance was only a line drawn between them, a line that would be erased one day. They had a promise to keep, a promise that would guide them and support them through the journey. The city was their background, but it was also a place that would bring them together. The city was not a barrier, but a part of their love. The city was a place that they would never leave. The city was a place where they would grow and find a way to keep their love.
