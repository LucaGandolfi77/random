## Chapter 3 – *The Poet’s Arrival*

The bell had barely begun to toll when the doors of the abandoned church swung inward, the hinges protesting in a weary groan that seemed to echo the hollow rhythms of the city outside. The chill that had settled into the stone nave was punctuated by a sudden warmth, a breath of air that carried the scent of wet parchment and old ink. Luca stepped across the threshold, his shoulders hunched as if bearing an invisible weight, and his gaze fell upon Elena, who was alone in her own world, the violin cradled like a secret.

She had been tracing the line of the rosary beads with a fingertip, her eyes closed, when the faintest tilt of her head brought a frame of a man into sight. He was tall, with a lean frame and hair that stubbornly refused to stay in place, streaked with the gray of early dawn. His shoes left faint imprints on the stone floor, as if he had been walking for hours over some invisible path. Luca’s voice, when he spoke, was not a sudden shout but a question, small and held between the ribs of his chest.

“Do you… do you play?” he asked, barely audible, as though it were a prayer.

Elena opened her eyes, and the suddenness of his presence shocked the quiet she had cultivated. Her fingers, already poised over the strings, trembled slightly, a reflex of the shared tremor of a nervous heart. She looked up at him, taking in the intensity of his gaze—an intensity that mirrored her own, a kind of yearning that seemed to be born of the same place.

“Only when I can find the right note,” she replied, her voice trembling just as her hands had.

Luca’s gaze lingered on the instrument that seemed to join his presence as if they were two halves of a single whole. The violin was a thing of beauty, its varnish aged, its wood resonant; it seemed to hold a story, a conversation waiting to be spoken.

He moved closer, the soles of his shoes clicking softly against the stone. The way the light fell on him, the way it gave him a halo, made him seem almost divine. He was a paradoxical blend of light and darkness, like a poem itself, a composition of contradictions, an artist that existed for the sake of themes that could be understood from the point of view of a man who had already seen too many things.

“Your bow… it sings,” Luca said. “I thought the violin would ask for a singer, but it is you who might be that singer.”

Elena felt a pang that was not sorrow but a strange comfort. The violin had been a place of solace for her, a way to silence the thought that had taken over her previous days. The man’s words were a small comfort, a small hope, a small sense of trust in a world that was too wide for her to hold. For a moment she could see the way his eyes flickered across the walls, the way the ghostly figures of the past had been present in the old church, like a ghost that had fallen apart.

“Did you come here because this place is haunted?” Elena asked, her voice hushed and full of reverence.

Luca’s lips twitched into a smile that was a little too sharp, almost a gentle, almost an awful. “I know the place was an old church that was never finished. And I know the place where I see my own fears in the shadows. I could not help but come here to find a space where I could be myself.”

Elena was left with a silence that was as fragile and as thick as a bubble. She could have gone away from Luca, or, in a small, small way, found a way to continue what she had been doing.

“I would like to hear your music,” Luca said. “I have written a poem that could fit the rhythm of your violin. Maybe we can create a new story, a new memory.”

Elena nodded, her heart racing, but she had never considered her music as a part of a poem. She had known only the violin as a cultural heritage, as a lifeline that connects to the past. Luca’s words made her feel as if she were stepping into a doorway that led to a new opportunity. She smiled, keeping her voice steady, “That would be a great idea.”
