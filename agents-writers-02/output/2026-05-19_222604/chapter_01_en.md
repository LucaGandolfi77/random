
## Chapter 1: The Apartment Agreement

The apartment was a masterpiece of Milanese inefficiency. Perched on the fourth floor of a *palazzo* that had forgotten to install an elevator in the age of the *portineria*, it was a sun-drenched, soul-crushing 35 square meters. The living room was a sofa that had seen better centuries, a bookshelf sagging under art books, and a window that framed a spectacular view of a graffiti-covered wall. The kitchen was a narrow, tiled afterthought, a corridor with a stove, a sink, and approximately 18 inches of counter space on a good day.

This was the battleground.

Luca, a fashion intern whose hands were usually buried in silk and chiffon, stood in the doorway, his linen shirt feeling absurdly crisp. He had been lured here by the gravitational pull of guilt, wielded by their mutual friend, Giulia. “It’s just once a week,” she’d said, her eyes wide with a manipulative innocence that was her superpower. “You’re both so… *talented*. And my place is *così piccola*. We need a sanctuary. A salon. A place for *cibo e conversazione*.” She had neglected to mention the sanctuary was a closet with a hotplate.

He ran a hand through his already-perfectly-tousled dark hair and surveyed the enemy territory. The air smelled faintly of old cabbage and despair.

From the depths of the kitchen, a percussive symphony of cupboard banging and muttered curses announced the arrival of the other half of this unholy alliance. A moment later, a man emerged, wiping his hands on a dish towel slung over his shoulder. He was… a force. Broad-shouldered, with forearms dusted with faint floury scars and a faded tattoo of a fish skeleton peeking from beneath a faded black t-shirt sleeve. His dark curls were a mess, and his eyes, a startling, assessing grey, held the weary intensity of a sous-chef at the end of a double shift.

“You’re the fashion guy,” he said, not a question. His voice was a low, gravelly baritone that seemed to vibrate with the hum of the refrigerator.

“And you’re the chef,” Luca replied, forcing a smile that felt brittle. “Sous-chef, sorry. At *Trattoria della Scala*?”

“Assistant to the *sottocuoco*. Same thing, less pay.” He extended a hand, still faintly greasy. “Riccardo.”

They shook. Luca noted the callouses, the practical strength. Riccardo noted the soft, uncalloused fingers, the manicured nails. A silent, mutual assessment was made, and both found the other profoundly inadequate for the task at hand.

“Giulia says you can cook,” Luca ventured, trying to claim the high ground of the single, pathetic counter.

“Giulia says a lot of things,” Riccardo grumbled, already moving to claim the space. “I can cook. But this…” He gestured at the two-burner stove, the miniature fridge, the single, sad saucepan. “This is not cooking. This is a war crime.”

Luca felt a spark of indignation. “It’s a kitchen. We have knives. We have heat. We have… each other.” He winced internally at the last part. It sounded like the beginning of a terrible reality show.

“*Oddio*,” Riccardo groaned, opening the cupboard to reveal a motley collection of chipped bowls and a single, warped non-stick skillet. “We have a death trap. What’s your specialty, *gastronomo*? Deconstructed carbonara? Edible flowers on a bed of foam?”

“I make a very good *risotto alla milanese*,” Luca said coolly, his Milanese pride stung. “And I know the difference between a *soffritto* and a *brunoise*.”

Riccardo paused, a chipped plate in his hand. “*Risotto*?” A slow, skeptical smile spread across his face. “In this pot? With that heat? You’d need the patience of a saint and the luck of the devil. You’re a romantic.”

“And you’re a cynic,” Luca shot back, the first true smile of the evening touching his lips despite himself. “What’s *your* philosophy, Chef? Throw everything in a pot and hope for the best?”

“My philosophy,” Riccardo said, turning to face him, leaning against the counter and crossing his arms, “is that food should be honest. It should taste of what it is. No foam. No flowers you can’t eat. Just good broth, good rice, good olive oil. And it should be *eaten*, not photographed.”

Luca felt a traitorous flutter in his chest. The man was infuriating, but he spoke of food with a reverence that matched Luca’s own. “So you’re a minimalist.”

“I’m a *realist*.”

“And I’m a *dreamer*,” Luca finished, the words hanging in the steamy, cabbage-scented air.

They stared at each other. The absurdity of the situation crashed over them. Two strangers, bound by a friend’s well-meaning blackmail, about to embark on a weekly culinary suicide pact in a kitchen the size of a broom closet. Luca saw the exhaustion in Riccardo’s eyes, the deep lines of stress around them. He saw the faint tremor in his hands—not from nerves, but from the ghost of a thousand kitchen services. Riccardo saw the careful poise in Luca’s posture, the way his eyes darted around the room, assessing, planning, already trying to impose order on the chaos.

A laugh bubbled up in Luca’s throat, a release of pure, unadulterated panic. It came out as a choked snort.

Riccardo’s skeptical look melted into a grin. “What?”

“We’re going to burn this place down,” Luca said, the laughter finally breaking free, genuine and a little hysterical. “Giulia’s going to come home to a pile of ash and our charred ambitions.”

“At least the *polizia* will know we died for a good cause,” Riccardo chuckled, the sound warm and unexpected. “Fighting over counter space. It’s a noble end.”

The laughter between them was the first true thing in the room. It cut through the tension, the smell of old cabbage, the looming specter of failure. For a moment, they weren’t a fashion intern and a sous-chef. They were just two people in a stupid, small kitchen, about to do something stupid and small together.

“Okay,” Riccardo said, wiping his eyes. “New plan. No *risotto*. No foam. We start with the basics. *Aglio, olio e peperoncino*.”

“*Spaghetti*? In this pot?”

“We break them.”

Luca looked at the single, small pot. “We’ll need water.”

“We’ll boil it in shifts.”

“You’re serious.”

“I’m a *realist*, Luca. I deal in what’s possible. Right now, what’s possible is not setting off the smoke alarm before the guests arrive.” Riccardo opened the tiny fridge, revealing a single, sad lemon, a half-empty bottle of cheap white wine, and a withered chili pepper. “We have the *peperoncino*. We have… hope.”

Luca felt a strange, warm solidarity settle over him. This wasn’t about perfect food. It was about showing up. About trying, together, in a space that was too small and a situation that was too ridiculous. It was, he realized with a start, the most alive he’d felt in months.

“Alright, *realist*,” Luca said, rolling up his sleeves. “You handle the water. I’ll handle the *aglio*. But if we scorch the garlic, I’m blaming your *soffritto*.”

Riccardo grabbed the only knife—a dull, heavy thing—and began to clumsily peel the garlic. “Deal. But if the pasta sticks together, it’s because you’re *sognatore*—you’re dreaming of silk, not stirring.”

They fell into a rhythm, clumsy and awkward. Luca minced the garlic with careful, precise chops, the scent sharp and green. Riccardo wrestled with the pot, filling it from the sink, the water splashing onto the floor. They bumped elbows constantly, muttered “*scusa*” and “*permesso*” with every turn. The kitchen was a pressure cooker of frustration and proximity.

As the water finally began to boil, a loud, cheerful *bussare* came from the front door. Giulia’s voice, bright and carrying, called out, “*Aprire! È arrivato il cibo!*”

Luca and Riccardo froze, looking at the meager, half-prepped ingredients on the counter, then at each other, flour from the bag Riccardo had knocked over dusting their shoulders like a bizarre, culinary snowfall.

The first guest had arrived.

Riccardo met Luca’s eyes, a wild, panicked light in his own. Luca felt a grin tug at his lips again. He gave a slight, determined nod.

“*Iniziamo*,” he said. Let’s begin.

And in the tiny, ill-equipped kitchen in Milan, with the smell of garlic and cheap wine and possibility filling the air, two chaotic cooks took their first, stumbling step into a story that was already becoming something neither of them could have predicted. The disaster was about to begin, and for the first time, Luca found himself hoping it would be a magnificent one.
