
## Chapter 2: The Pasta Incident

The kitchen, if it could be called that, was a narrow galley barely wider than Luca’s shoulders. One wall held a two-burner hot plate, a miniature fridge humming with the effort of cooling a single bottle of wine, and a sink that dribbled brown water for exactly thirty seconds before sighing into silence. The other wall was a precarious shelf of mismatched dishes, a single chipped pasta bowl, and a collection of thrift-store mugs. Counter space was a theoretical concept, represented by a stained wooden block barely large enough for a cutting board. This was the arena.

Chiara stood before the hot plate, her back rigid, a wooden spoon clutched like a scepter. She wore a faded black t-shirt and jeans still dusted with flour from the morning’s bakery shift. Her dark hair was pulled into a messy ponytail, a few rebellious strands clinging to her neck. She had already declared, with the authority of someone who had once made a passable risotto by the age of eight, that they would begin with *aglio e olio*—a simple, elegant test of fundamentals.

Luca, in a crisp, if slightly rumpled, button-down shirt from his fashion internship, hovered by the sink, meticulously washing a single tomato. “We should really start with the mise en place,” he said, his voice tight. “Everything organized. It’s the foundation of any professional kitchen.”

“It’s pasta with garlic and oil,” Chiara retorted, not turning. “The foundation is boiling water. Watch.” She spun, grabbed the largest pot—a warped, aluminum thing that listed to one side—and filled it from the sink. Water sloshed onto the linoleum. “See? Foundation laid.”

Luca watched the water cascade onto the floor, then carefully placed the washed tomato on a towel. “You’re getting water everywhere. And the pot’s not centered on the burner. It’ll heat unevenly.”

“It’s a hot plate, not a Vulcan range. It heats ‘unevenly’ by definition.” She slammed the pot onto the burner. It teetered. “There. Stable.”

He flinched. “You can’t just… slam it. You’ll damage the coil.”

“It’s fine.” She turned to the counter, scanning their sparse supplies. “Where’s the good olive oil? The one your aunt brought?”

“In the cupboard. The dark green bottle. But we should use the regular one for cooking, save the good one for finishing. It’s a waste to heat extra virgin.”

“*Sciocchezze*,” she muttered, pulling out the prized bottle. “Garlic needs good oil. It infuses it. You don’t cook with the cheap stuff, you boil pasta in it.” She poured a generous three-count into the pan, the green-gold liquid pooling in the bottom.

Luca opened his mouth to argue about smoke points, but she was already turning back to the fridge, pulling out a head of garlic. With a decisive thwack of her palm, she separated the cloves, then began peeling them with a swift, efficient motion, the papery skins flying into the sink.

“You’re making a mess,” he observed, wiping a stray skin from the counter with a flick of his wrist.

“It’s organic matter. It’ll compost.” She grabbed a knife—a dull, serrated thing more suited to slicing bread—and began hacking at the cloves, reducing them to uneven, jagged chunks. “You want them finely minced? For *aglio e olio*? The slices should be thin, like little golden boats. These,” she gestured with the knife, “are more like lifeboats after a shipwreck.”

“Uniformity is key for even cooking,” Luca said, taking the knife from her hand. His fingers brushed hers; hers were cool and slightly calloused, his were long and smooth. A tiny, static shock of awareness passed between them, instantly drowned out by the clatter of the knife on the board. “Let me.”

He began slicing, his movements precise, each clove yielding perfect, translucent slivers. The rhythm was meditative, a small island of control in the chaotic kitchen.

Chiara watched his hands for a second too long, then shook her head. “You’re so… *methodical*. It’s just pasta.” She turned to the pot. “Is that water boiling yet? It’s been, like, four minutes.”

“It takes time to reach a rolling boil. Especially on this appliance. Patience.”

“We don’t have patience! The friends will be here in two hours. We have to make a good impression.” She jabbed a finger at the pot. “That water is not boiling. It’s having a mild anxiety attack.”

He was about to explain the difference between a simmer and a boil when she reached past him, turned the hot plate to its highest setting, and cranked the knob on the other burner to ‘high’ as well, placing the garlic pan on it. “There. Double the firepower.”

“You’ll scorch the garlic!”

“I’ll watch it.” She planted herself in front of the pan, wooden spoon at the ready.

Luca stared at the pot, willing it to boil faster. The kitchen was already growing warm, the air thick with the scent of damp linoleum and old grease. He could feel the panic, cold and slick, beginning to coil in his stomach. This was a disaster. They were going to serve burnt garlic and soggy pasta to their friends, who would laugh and never let him live it down, who would see how utterly incapable he was outside the curated world of fashion showrooms and sample sales.

A sharp, sweet smell cut through the mustiness—the garlic, hitting the hot oil. Then, a second later, a sharper, acrid scent. “Chiara, it’s browning too fast!”

“I know, I know!” She stirred frantically, but the garlic had already begun its swift descent from golden to dark brown to black. Wisps of blue smoke began to curl from the pan.

“It’s smoking!”

“I see that!” She lifted the pan off the heat, but the damage was done. The garlic was a bitter, charred mess. “Merda.”

Luca, without thinking, grabbed the pan from her hand. “Give it here.” He set it back on the burner, turned the heat down to low, and swiftly chopped another clove. “You need to start over. Oil, fresh garlic, low heat. Let it infuse slowly.” His voice was calm, instructive. He poured fresh oil, added the new slices, and stirred gently. The kitchen filled with the correct, nutty aroma.

Chiara stared at his back, at the set of his shoulders as he took charge. A traitorous warmth, unrelated to the stove, flickered in her chest. “You’re good at that,” she said, her voice softer.

He glanced over his shoulder. “At fixing your mistakes?”

“At knowing what to do.” She leaned against the counter, watching him. The frantic energy in the room had shifted, focused now on this single, salvageable task.

The pot chose that moment to finally erupt, a vigorous, rolling boil. Luca grabbed the bag of pasta—a cheap, supermarket spaghetti—and began to break the strands in half over the pot. “We need to salt the water. Generously. Like the sea.”

“The sea is really salty,” she said, watching him pour in a small mountain of coarse salt. “You’re not making a saline solution, you know.”

“It’s the only chance the pasta has to be seasoned from within.” He stirred the pasta in, the water momentarily calming before surging back to life.

They worked in a new, silent proximity. Chiara, chastised, fetched the colander from the dish rack. Luca, with a newfound sense of purpose, monitored the garlic, now perfectly golden and fragrant. He added a pinch of red pepper flakes, a grind of black pepper. The simple sauce was coming together.

“Okay,” he said, turning off the burner under the pasta. “Drain it.”

Chiara positioned the colander in the sink. Luca, holding the heavy pot, began to pour. But the warped pot, still unsteady, caught the edge of the colander. A torrent of starchy, boiling water missed the colander entirely and cascaded onto the counter, flooding the space between them, splashing onto Chiara’s jeans and the bag of flour she’d left out.

“*Ahi!*” She jumped back, cursing.

“I’m so sorry!” Luca nearly dropped the pot, lunging to set it down. “The pot is defective! It’s not my fault!”

“It’s not my fault the counter is a disaster zone!” she shot back, but she was laughing, a short, startled burst of sound. She looked down at her soaked jeans, then at the small lake forming around their feet. “Great. Now I’m wearing soup.”

Luca stared at the mess, the ruined sauce, the flooded counter, the charred pan from the first attempt. The panic returned, full force. His eyes met hers, wide with shared horror. This was it. The final, humiliating proof of their incompetence.

And then, something shifted. The sheer, absurd magnitude of the disaster broke over them. The tension, the clashing, the desperate need to impress—it all dissolved into the ridiculous reality of two adults who had flooded a kitchen with pasta water.

A snort escaped Chiara. Then a giggle. Then Luca, to his own astonishment, let out a short, sharp laugh. It was a brittle sound, but it was real.

“We,” Chiara gasped, wiping water from her eyes, “are the worst hosts in Milan.”

“We’re not hosts,” Luca corrected, a grin tugging at his lips despite himself. “We’re culinary terrorists.”

She laughed again, a fuller, brighter sound that filled the cramped kitchen. “The garlic is perfect, though,” she said, nodding toward the pan.

He looked. She was right. The second batch was a masterpiece of simplicity, fragrant and golden. He turned off the burner. The only sounds were the drip of water into the sink and their own unsteady breathing.

“We should probably clean up,” Luca said, his voice quieter now.

“Yeah.” She grabbed a couple of dish towels from a drawer, tossing one to him. They began mopping the floor in a clumsy, synchronized silence. The anger was gone, replaced by a buzzing, breathless awareness. Every time their hands brushed, picking up the same cloth, a tiny jolt passed between them.

The front door of the apartment burst open. “We’re here! And we brought wine!” Marco, their mutual friend and the architect of this whole arrangement, strode in, followed by Sofia and Paolo. They stopped dead in the entryway, taking in the scene: the flooded floor, the two sopping wet cooks, the charred pan, the perfect golden garlic oil sitting serenely on the stove.

Luca and Chiara froze, towels in hand, looking like deer in the headlights of their friends’ scrutiny.

Marco’s eyes went from the mess to their flushed, guilty faces. A slow smile spread across his. “Wow,” he said, setting down the wine. “You guys weren’t kidding about the ‘culinary chaos’ clause in the agreement.”

Sofia giggled. “It smells amazing in here. Like… garlic and defeat?”

Chiara looked at Luca. He looked back. And in that shared glance, over the wreckage of their first attempt, something crystallized. The frustration, the clashing—it hadn’t been just frustration. It had been friction, the dangerous, sparking kind. And in the aftermath of the flood, covered in pasta water and laughing at their own catastrophe, they had both felt it: the first, fragile thread of a connection that had nothing to do with the friends watching and everything to do with the strange, electric understanding between them.

“It’s a work in progress,” Luca said, finally, his voice steady.

Chiara smiled, a real, unguarded smile that reached her eyes. “A very, very messy work in progress.”

And as their friends stepped into the chaotic, aromatic kitchen, the spark, though unacknowledged, was no longer a secret. It was there, in the space between them, as tangible as the steam still rising from the ruined pot, cutting through the frustration and the flour dust with the sharp, sweet promise of something new.
