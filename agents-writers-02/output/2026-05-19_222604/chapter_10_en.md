
## Chapter 10: The Pressure Cooker

The recipe was written in a spidery hand on a brittle, tea-stained card, the ink faded to the colour of a sigh. *Risotto alla Certosina*, it was called. A dish their grandmother had supposedly perfected in the kitchens of a now-vanished monastery on the outskirts of Pavia. To Luca, it was legend. To Chiara, it was a ghost.

They had both stared at it when she’d unearthed it from a box of her nonna’s things during a rare moment of shared quiet. “It’s impossible,” she’d whispered, tracing the list of ingredients: three kinds of wild mushrooms, foraged not bought; a fumet of river eel and perch; a final mantecatura with a precious, aged gorgonzola dolce. “She only made it for feast days. It’s… it’s a prayer.”

Luca, of course, had seen it as a challenge. A culinary Everest. “We can do it,” he’d said, his eyes already calculating reductions and timings. “We’ll forage the mushrooms in the park. We’ll make the fumet from scratch. It’ll be our masterpiece.”

Now, standing in the tiny kitchen at 6 p.m. with the first guests due in two hours, the recipe card mocked them from its perch on the microwave. The air was thick with the smell of something acrid and burnt—the fumet, which had boiled over violently, leaving a caramelised scar on the stovetop and a cloud of bitter smoke that refused to dissipate. The three kinds of mushrooms—porcini, ovuli, and chiodini—lay in a wilted, muddy heap in a colander, having been insufficiently cleaned. The river eel, purchased from a dubious fishmonger at the crack of dawn, stared up from a board with a single, accusing milky eye.

“I told you we should have started yesterday,” Chiara muttered, not for the first time, as she poked at the glutinous mass that was supposed to be a *soffritto* of onion and celery. It had seized into a sticky, golden-brown knot.

“And I told you the fumet needed a longer reduction,” Luca shot back, waving a towel at the smoke detector, which had begun to chirp its low-battery warning in a syncopated rhythm with Chiara’s rising heartbeat. “But you were worried about the mushrooms getting soggy.”

“They *are* soggy! And now they smell like dirt and regret.” She slammed a wooden spoon onto the counter, the sound swallowed by the general chaos. “This whole thing is a mistake. We should have just made another risotto. A normal one.”

“Normal is boring,” Luca retorted, but his bravado was a thin veneer. He could feel the structure of his plan collapsing—the precise timeline he’d drawn up in his head now a crumpled ball of anxiety in his pocket. The guests—Marco, Sofia, Paolo, Giulia, and the new girl, Elisa—were expecting a spectacle. They were *known* for their spectacles. Now they would get a train wreck.

The first guest arrived an hour later. There was a frantic, whispered conference by the front door.

“Just… distract them,” Luca hissed, shoving a bottle of wine into Chiara’s hand. “I’ll salvage what I can.”

Chiara plastered on a smile, her face feeling stiff. “Ciao, tutti! So glad you’re here!” Her voice was an octave too high. She took their coats, her movements jerky. “Wine? We have… we have a special fumet… it’s, um, *caramellato*.”

In the kitchen, Luca was waging a desperate war. He strained the eel-fumet, now a murky, over-reduced syrup. He threw the sodden mushrooms into a hot pan with a prayer. They squeaked in protest. He turned to the rice, measuring it with a shaking hand. *Arborio*, not the *Carnaroli* he’d wanted. *Too late now.* He toasted it in the pan, the grains turning translucent at the edges. He added the fumet, a glug at a time. It absorbed too quickly, the rice already threatening to stick.

“Chiara!” he bellowed, not turning. “The gorgonzola! Where did you put the gorgonzola?”

“I didn’t touch it!” she called back, her voice tight. “It’s on the second shelf! Behind the… the thing!”

He found it, a pale, creamy wedge. He crumbled it, his fingers working it into a soft paste. The rice was *all’onda*, too liquid. He needed to *mantecare*, to whip in the cold butter and cheese off the heat to create that final, creamy wave. But the heat was too high. The moment he took the pan off, the rice would seize.

“We have to plate,” he said, more to himself. “Now.”

Chiara appeared in the doorway, a tray of empty plates in her hands. Her eyes took in the scene: the smoking pan, the desperate man, the rice that looked more like porridge. The laughter from the living room was a distant, cruel soundtrack.

“It’s not ready,” she stated, the fight draining from her, leaving behind a hollow dread.

“It has to be.” He began to spoon the soupy rice onto the plates, a messy, inelegant portion. “The gorgonzola… sprinkle it on top. Now.”

She did as she was told, the crumbled cheese a pathetic white snowfall on the beige sludge. They carried the plates out in a silent, grim procession, placing them before their friends with averted eyes.

The first bite was a study in quiet horror. Marco chewed slowly, his brow furrowed. Sofia’s smile didn’t reach her eyes. Paolo, ever the blunt one, put his fork down. “It’s… salty,” he said. “And the mushrooms are… gritty.”

“It’s an acquired taste,” Luca said, his voice brittle. He took a bite himself and nearly gagged. It was salty, yes, and the rice was mushy, and the gorgonzola had clumped into greasy pockets. It tasted, he thought with a surge of shame, like failure.

“An acquired taste,” Chiara repeated, a hysterical giggle bubbling in her throat. She clapped a hand over her mouth. “It’s… it’s a relic. A prayer to blandness.”

That was the spark. Luca’s face flushed. “It’s not *bland*,” he snapped, his voice low and sharp. “It’s complex. It’s *authentic*. You’re the one who didn’t clean the mushrooms properly, who let the fumet burn, who—”

“Who let you talk me into this madness?” Chiara shot back, standing up. Her chair scraped the floor. “You and your grand, stupid visions! ‘We’ll forage in the park!’ The mushrooms were full of sand! ‘We’ll make a perfect fumet!’ You reduced it to glue!”

“At least I have a vision!” he hissed, stepping closer. “You just want to play it safe! Another pasta, another risotto! Boring, predictable, *safe*!”

“Safe is better than inedible!” she cried, her voice breaking. “At least people don’t have to pretend to like it! At least we don’t have to stand here while our friends politely choke down your ego!”

The room had gone utterly silent. All eyes were on them. The intimate, warm space of their dinners had vanished, replaced by a courtroom. Luca saw the pity on Sofia’s face, the discomfort on Marco’s. He saw Giulia looking at her plate, embarrassed.

Something in his chest cracked. The anger evaporated, leaving behind a cold, hollow shame. He looked at Chiara, her eyes bright with unshed tears, her hands clenched into fists at her sides.

“You’re right,” he said, the fight gone from his voice. It was a whisper, but in the silence, it was deafening. “It’s terrible. I’m sorry.”

He didn’t say *I’m sorry I yelled*. He didn’t say *I’m sorry I got carried away*. He just said *I’m sorry*, and the inadequacy of it hung in the air.

Chiara stared at him, the fight draining from her as well, replaced by a vast, empty exhaustion. The silence that followed wasn’t just the absence of sound; it was a physical thing, a cold wall rising between them. The dinner was over. Not just this dish, but the easy, joyful rhythm they’d built. The dance was over, and they were left standing on a silent, shattered floor.

“I’ll clean,” she said finally, her voice flat. “You… you take care of the guests.”

She turned and walked back into the kitchen, leaving him alone with the wreckage and the stares. The chapter of their perfect, chaotic partnership had closed with a slammed door and a silent, accusatory table. The pressure cooker had finally blown its lid, and nothing would ever be the same.
