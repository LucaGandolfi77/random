
## Chapter 2: Gathering Ingredients

The morning sun filtered through the lace curtains of Jamie’s apartment, casting a honeyed glow over the cluttered kitchen table. Alex stood at the window, sipping coffee from a chipped mug, while Jamie rifled through a handwritten list. The previous night’s impulsive decision to host a dinner party still buzzed between them, a live wire of excitement and anxiety.  

“You’re overthinking it,” Alex said, setting down their mug. Their voice carried the easy confidence of someone who’d grown up in kitchens, where improvisation was as essential as salt. “We’ve got this.”  

Jamie glanced up from the list, their pen tapping against the paper. “Easy for you to say. You’re the one who suggested we cook for twelve people in my shoebox of an apartment.”  

“Relax. It’ll be fun.” Alex grinned, but there was a flicker of nerves behind their eyes. They’d never been one for planning, preferring the thrill of last-minute decisions, but Jamie’s methodical nature had a way of grounding even their wildest ideas.  

The local market was a riot of color and sound by the time they arrived, stalls overflowing with produce that seemed to pulse with life. Jamie moved with purpose, basket in hand, while Alex lingered behind, distracted by a display of heirloom tomatoes.  

“Basil,” Jamie announced, holding up a sprig. “Fresh, not the dried stuff in the back of your pantry.”  

Alex raised an eyebrow. “Since when do you care about herbs?”  

“Since I decided to make a proper pesto. You know, the kind that doesn’t come from a jar?”  

“Ah, so now you’re a purist.” Alex plucked a tomato, rolling it between their fingers. “I prefer to let the ingredients speak for themselves, not smother them in… whatever that green paste is.”  

Jamie’s laugh was sharp, but not unkind. “It’s called *pesto*, and it’s going to be the star of the appetizer. You’ll thank me later.”  

“Or I’ll poison everyone and we’ll never speak again.”  

“That’s the spirit.”  

They wandered deeper into the market, their bickering softening into a rhythm. At the olive oil stall, Alex paused, studying the labels. “This one’s from Tuscany. Notes of almond and pepper.”  

Jamie peered over their shoulder. “It’s also twice as expensive as the stuff I usually buy.”  

“Quality matters.” Alex’s tone was firm, but there was a vulnerability in the way they held the bottle, as if it were something precious. “You can’t rush good food.”  

“I’m not rushing. I just don’t see the point in—”  

“The point is flavor.” Alex turned to face them, their expression earnest. “If we’re doing this, let’s do it right.”  

For a moment, Jamie said nothing. They’d never thought of cooking as an act of care, not until Alex’s hands had brushed theirs the night before, steady and sure as they’d plated the first course. Now, standing here in the middle of the market, they wondered if that was what this was—a gesture, however small, of wanting to get things right.  

“Fine,” Jamie said finally. “But if this olive oil is terrible, I’m blaming you.”  

Alex’s smile was bright enough to rival the sunlit tomatoes.  

At the cheese counter, their debates grew more animated. Jamie favored a sharp pecorino, while Alex insisted on a creamy goat cheese for the salad.  

“You’re going to overpower the greens,” Jamie argued.  

“And you’re going to bore everyone to tears.”  

“Is that so?”  

“Mm-hmm.” Alex leaned closer, their voice dropping to a whisper. “But I suppose I could be convinced to compromise… if you’re nice to me.”  

Jamie’s cheeks warmed, but they kept their tone light. “I’ll be *very* nice.”  

The vendor, an elderly man with flour dusting his apron, watched their exchange with a knowing smile. “Young love,” he said, sliding a wedge of cheese across the counter. “It’s the secret ingredient in every great dish.”  

Alex laughed, but Jamie felt their stomach flutter.  

By the time they reached the wine section, their baskets were overflowing. Jamie had insisted on a robust cabernet for the main course, while Alex had selected a crisp sauvignon blanc to pair with the scallops.  

“You’re going to regret that,” Jamie said, eyeing the bottle.  

“Why? Because I have better taste than you?”  

“Because you always think you know best.”  

“And you always play it safe.”  

It was a familiar argument, one they’d had a dozen times in the kitchen, but here, surrounded by the chaos of the market, it felt less like a fight and more like a dance.  

“I’m not playing it safe,” Jamie said, their voice softer now. “I just… I want everything to be perfect.”  

Alex’s expression shifted, something almost tender flickering in their gaze. “It already is.”  

They walked in comfortable silence for a while, the weight of the morning settling between them. Jamie found themself stealing glances at Alex, noting the way their hair caught the sunlight, the way their laugh lingered in the air like the scent of fresh bread.  

At the checkout, their hands brushed as they unloaded the basket, and Jamie felt a spark of something—anticipation, maybe, or the quiet thrill of knowing that whatever happened next, they’d face it together.  

Outside, the city buzzed with the promise of afternoon. Alex hoisted their bag onto their shoulder, their earlier nerves replaced by something steadier.  

“So,” they said, falling into step beside Jamie. “Ready to cook?”  

Jamie nodded, their heart lighter than it had been in weeks. “Yeah. I think I am.”  

As they walked home, the ingredients cradled like treasures between them, the kitchen felt less like a challenge and more like a beginning.
