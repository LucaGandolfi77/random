
## Chapter 5: Tasting Notes

The sauce simmered in its shallow pan like liquid amber, releasing curls of steam that carried the perfume of reduced white wine and shallots. Jamie lifted the wooden spoon and let a drop fall back into the pan, watching it thicken mid-air before disappearing into the gentle bubble. The sound was hypnotic—soft, rhythmic, like the whisper of secrets being kept.

“You’re overthinking it,” Alex said, leaning against the counter beside them. Their voice carried that particular lilt it got when they were trying not to seem too interested, when they wanted to appear casual but every word betrayed them.

Jamie’s shoulder brushed against Alex’s arm as they both leaned over the stove. The contact sent an unexpected shiver through them—not from the heat, but from something else entirely. They’d been working side by side for hours now, and Jamie had lost count of how many times their skin had accidentally touched. Each brush seemed to linger longer than necessary.

“It needs something,” Jamie murmured, more to themselves than to Alex. They dipped the spoon back into the sauce and lifted it to their lips. The flavor bloomed across their tongue—bright acidity from the wine, the sweet foundation of caramelized shallots, the subtle earthiness of thyme. But something was missing. Something that would tie it all together.

Alex watched Jamie’s face as they tasted, the slight concentration between their brows, the way their lips parted slightly as they considered the flavor. There was something unbearably intimate about watching someone taste something you’d made, about seeing their honest reaction play across their features.

“Try a pinch of sugar,” Alex suggested suddenly.

Jamie looked up, surprised. “Sugar? In a savory sauce?”

“Trust me.” Alex reached for the small bowl of granulated sugar on the counter. Their fingers closed around it briefly before passing it over. Jamie’s hand covered Alex’s for a moment, and neither moved. The sugar slipped between their fingers, granules scattering like tiny diamonds across the worn wooden surface.

“Sorry,” Jamie breathed, but they didn’t move their hand either.

Alex’s pulse seemed to quicken. Jamie could feel it in the slight tremor that ran through Alex’s fingers where they remained trapped beneath their own. “It’s okay,” Alex whispered. “I mean, it’s just sugar.”

But it wasn’t just sugar. It was the warmth of skin against skin, the electric current that seemed to arc between them whenever they touched. Jamie finally pulled their hand back, but slowly, reluctantly, as if moving through honey.

They added the sugar anyway—a tiny pinch, barely a teaspoon—stirring it into the sauce with slow, deliberate movements. The transformation was immediate. The sharp edges of the wine softened, the shallots’ sweetness deepened, and somehow the entire composition found its missing piece.

“How did you know?” Jamie asked, genuinely curious.

Alex shrugged, but Jamie could see the small smile tugging at the corner of their mouth. “My grandmother always said that a pinch of sweetness could fix almost anything. Even when you don’t think you need it.”

Jamie tasted again. The sauce was perfect now—not too sweet, just enough to balance the acidity and bring out all the other flavors. It was like Alex themselves, Jamie realized with a jolt. Sharp and bright when they needed to be, but with this unexpected sweetness underneath that made everything better.

“This is incredible,” Jamie said, genuinely impressed. “You have an amazing palate.”

Alex preened slightly, but there was something vulnerable in their expression too. “I’ve had years of practice. My mom was terrible at cooking, so someone had to learn.”

The casual admission made Jamie’s heart ache in a way they hadn’t expected. They wanted to ask more—about Alex’s childhood, about their family, about everything that had shaped them into the person who could taste a sauce and know exactly what it needed. But the words stuck somewhere between their tongue and their teeth.

Instead, they gestured toward the sauce with their spoon. “Taste it. Tell me what you think.”

Alex stepped closer, close enough that Jamie could smell the faint scent of their cologne mixed with the kitchen’s usual aromas. Close enough that Jamie’s breath hitched slightly. Close enough that when Alex leaned over to taste the sauce from the same spoon Jamie was holding, their faces were mere inches apart.

Time seemed to slow. Jamie watched Alex’s eyes close as they tasted, saw the slight nod of approval, the way their lips curled around the wooden spoon. Every nerve ending in Jamie’s body felt heightened, aware of Alex’s presence in a way that was almost painful.

When Alex opened their eyes, they were looking directly at Jamie. The connection between them was so palpable that Jamie felt like they might drown in it. Alex’s gaze dropped to Jamie’s mouth for a fraction of a second before meeting their eyes again.

“This is perfect,” Alex said softly. Their voice was rougher than usual, and Jamie’s stomach flip-flopped.

“It needs something else,” Jamie heard themselves saying, even though they knew it was perfect. They just wanted an excuse to keep Alex there, close like this, forever.

Alex tilted their head slightly. “What does it need?”

Jamie didn’t answer. Instead, they found themselves leaning forward, drawn by something stronger than gravity, something that had been building since they’d first started planning this dinner party. Alex’s breath hitched, and for a moment Jamie was sure they were going to kiss them—that they both wanted it desperately.

But then the sound of keys jingling in the lock echoed from the apartment’s entrance, followed by muffled voices and laughter. The moment shattered like glass.

“Oh my god, we’re going to be late,” came Sarah’s voice from the hallway.

Alex jerked back as if shocked, and Jamie’s heart sank. They watched as Alex grabbed a clean towel and began wiping their hands with unnecessary vigor, breaking the spell that had settled over them.

“The wine,” Alex said, voice slightly too loud. “We should probably open the wine before everyone gets here.”

Jamie nodded mutely, still reeling from how close they’d come to—well, to something. Whatever that had been hovering between them, threatening to explode. They turned back to the stove, focusing intently on the sauce as if it might burn if they looked away for too long.

But even as they stirred, Jamie could feel Alex beside them, could sense their nervous energy. The near-miss of that moment hung in the air between them, making everything feel charged and uncertain.

“I’m sorry,” Alex said quietly after a moment. “About—”

“No,” Jamie interrupted quickly. “Don’t apologize. It was—”

“What? Too much?”

“Perfect,” Jamie said honestly. “It was perfect.”

Alex’s laugh was soft, almost wistful. “Yeah. Perfect.”

They worked in comfortable silence for a few minutes after that, but the dynamic had shifted. The playful banter of earlier had deepened into something more meaningful, something that neither of them seemed quite ready to name. Every time their hands brushed while reaching for ingredients, both of them noticed. Every glance lingered a little longer.

Jamie found themselves stealing looks at Alex when they thought they weren’t looking—watching the way their hair fell across their forehead when they concentrated, the way their shoulders moved when they stirred or chopped, the small expressions that crossed their face when they tasted something particularly good.

“We should probably start setting the table,” Alex said eventually, glancing toward the small dining area where they’d set up a folding table earlier. “Unless you want everyone eating off the counters.”

Jamie chuckled, realizing they’d been lost in thought again. “Yeah, probably a good idea.”

As they moved to arrange plates and silverware, Jamie’s mind kept returning to that moment by the stove. They’d never felt that kind of connection with anyone before—not even close. It wasn’t just physical attraction, though that was definitely part of it. It was something deeper, something that made their chest tighten in a way that was both terrifying and exhilarating.

Alex appeared beside them again, holding two bottles of wine—one white, one red. “What’s the protocol for this kind of thing? Do we ask people what they prefer, or just pick one?”

“We could do both,” Jamie suggested, surprising themselves with the casual tone of their voice. “Give people options.”

Alex smiled, and Jamie’s breath caught. It was amazing how one person could make their heart race simply by smiling. “Good idea.”

They opened the bottles together, the corks popping releasing the first hints of what would become the evening’s centerpiece. The white wine— a crisp Sauvignon Blanc—filled the kitchen with bright, citrusy notes. The red—a medium-bodied Pinot Noir—added deeper, earthier aromas to the mix.

“Wine is like love,” Alex said suddenly, holding up the bottle of red. “You never really know what you’re going to get until you take that first sip.”

Jamie turned to raise an eyebrow. “Since when do you get philosophical about wine?”

“Since I started thinking about things that matter more than work for once.”

The directness of that statement made Jamie’s pulse skip. They wanted to ask what Alex meant, wanted to explore the implications of that sentence, but the sound of footsteps approaching grew louder, signaling the arrival of their friends.

“Perfect timing,” Alex muttered, and Jamie couldn’t tell if they meant the wine or the interruption.

As the first guests began filtering into the apartment, bringing with them the noise and energy of the outside world, Jamie felt a pang of something that might have been disappointment. They’d been standing on the edge of something important, something that felt like it could change everything, and now it was being pushed aside for the practicalities of hosting.

But as they moved through the evening—greeting friends, pouring wine, directing people to drinks and appetizers—Jamie caught Alex’s eye across the room. The connection was still there, unmistakable, and this time neither of them looked away.

Whatever was happening between them, Jamie realized, wasn’t going anywhere. It was simmering now, just like their sauce—building in intensity, developing deeper flavors, becoming something richer and more complex than either of them had anticipated.

And like the best dishes, Jamie suspected it would be worth the wait.
