
## Chapter 8: Burnt Offerings

The garlic bread had been in the oven for exactly seven minutes when the smoke began to curl from beneath the door, thin and insistent as a whisper. Jamie’s shoulders tensed, their hand hovering over the timer button. Seven minutes. They’d set it for ten, but something in the way the kitchen hummed—too quiet, too still—made them check early. A mistake. Always a mistake.

The oven door swung open with a hiss, and a wave of charred heat washed over them. The pan sat in the center of the rack, its contents blackened and blistered, edges curled like dying petals. Jamie’s stomach dropped. “No, no, no—” They lunged forward, but Alex was already moving, grabbing a towel to yank the pan out. The smell hit them both at once: acrid, bitter, unmistakably ruined.

“Oh,” Jamie breathed, staring at the charcoal briquettes that had once been bread. Their voice cracked. “Oh god. I—I completely forgot to check the temperature. It must’ve been too high, and I didn’t—” They bit their lip, hard, as if the pain could anchor them to the moment before everything had gone wrong.

Alex didn’t respond immediately. They set the pan on the counter, their fingers brushing Jamie’s wrist in the process. A spark of static, or maybe something else, but Jamie barely registered it. Their mind was already racing: the guests arriving in an hour, the menu scattered across the counter like a deck of cards after a storm, and now this—this inedible disaster.

“It’s just bread,” Alex said finally, but their tone was gentle, almost amused. They tilted their head, studying the blackened pan. “We’ll just say it’s charred to perfection.”

Jamie blinked, then laughed—a short, sharp sound that broke the tension like a snapped thread. “Charred to perfection?” They gestured at the pan with a theatrical flourish. “Alex, this is beyond charred. This is… this is a war crime against carbohydrates.”

“I dunno,” Alex said, leaning against the counter. Their eyes were bright, crinkled at the corners from smiling. “There’s something to be said for a bold flavor profile. Maybe we’re just ahead of the curve.” They plucked a piece of the bread from the pan, held it up to the light. “Look at that color. It’s practically artistic.”

“Artistic?” Jamie snorted, but they were smiling now too, the tightness in their chest loosening. “That’s not art. That’s a fire hazard.” They paused, then added, “I’m sorry. I wanted everything to be perfect, and instead—”

“And instead you’ve given us an excuse to order takeout and pretend we meant to do it all along,” Alex interrupted, tossing the burnt bread into the trash. “Relax. It’s one dish. We’ll make do.”

But Jamie wasn’t relaxing. Their hands were still shaking slightly, the phantom tremor of someone who’d spent too many nights second-guessing themselves. “What if they notice? What if they think we’re not ready for this? What if—”

“They’ll think we’re human.” Alex stepped closer, close enough that Jamie could see the flecks of gold in their green eyes. “Which, by the way, is way more interesting than being perfect.” They paused, then added, quieter, “You’re allowed to mess up, you know. It doesn’t make you any less brilliant.”

The words hit Jamie like a physical thing, warm and heavy. They opened their mouth to reply, but Alex was already turning away, rummaging through the fridge for butter and parsley to start a new batch of garlic bread. Professional, practical, moving forward. But Jamie’s heart was still racing, caught somewhere between gratitude and something that felt dangerously like hope.

As Alex worked, humming a tune Jamie didn’t recognize, they found themselves watching. The way Alex’s brow furrowed when they concentrated, the way their hair fell into their face when they leaned over the counter, the way they occasionally glanced back with a small smile, as if checking that Jamie was still there. It was absurd, how much comfort that glance provided. Absurd, and yet—necessary.

“You’re staring,” Alex said, without turning around.

“I know.” Jamie’s cheeks heated. “Sorry. Just… thinking.”

“About?”

“About how you always know what to say.”

Alex’s hands stilled for a moment before they shrugged, casual. “Trial and error. I’ve had a lot of practice saying the wrong thing.”

“Not tonight.” Jamie’s voice was softer now, almost to themselves. “Tonight you’re saying all the right things.”

The silence that followed was comfortable, unforced. Jamie watched as Alex slid the new pan of garlic bread into the oven, setting the timer with a decisive click. When they turned around, they leaned against the doorframe, arms crossed, and looked at Jamie with an expression that was hard to read.

“You okay?” Alex asked.

“Yeah.” Jamie nodded, then realized how unconvincing that sounded. “I mean, I was panicking a second ago, but… I’m okay now. Because of you.”

Alex’s lips quirked upward. “Good. I’d hate for you to have a meltdown over bread. We’ve got dessert disasters to save our energy for.”

“Speaking of which—” Jamie gestured toward the counter, where the tiramisu ingredients sat in neat rows, “—why are we making tiramisu if we’re already juggling three courses?”

“Because I’m ambitious and you’re a masochist?” Alex grinned. “Also, I may have promised our friends that we’d blow their minds with a five-course meal. No pressure.”

Jamie groaned, but they were laughing again. Laughing, and it felt good. It felt like the kind of laughter that came from a place of safety, of trust. They’d spent so much of their life in kitchens where mistakes were failures, where every dish had to be flawless or it wasn’t worth serving. But here, with Alex, even the burnt bread felt like part of the story, not the end of it.

As if sensing Jamie’s thoughts, Alex reached over and squeezed their shoulder. “Hey. We’re going to be fine. Better than fine. This is going to be amazing.”

And Jamie wanted to believe them. Wanted to believe that the dinner party would go smoothly, that the food would taste perfect, that the night would end with laughter instead of tears. But more than that, they wanted to believe that whatever happened between them—whether it stayed professional or spiraled into something more—would be worth it.

The timer buzzed, and Alex moved to check the garlic bread, pulling it out with a triumphant grin. “Look at that,” they said, holding it up. “Golden brown, just like it’s supposed to be.”

Jamie stepped closer, inhaling the buttery, garlicky scent. It smelled like redemption. Like second chances. Like maybe, just maybe, they were going to be okay.

“See?” Alex said, nudging Jamie’s hip with their own. “Told you we’d figure it out.”

And Jamie did see. They saw the way Alex’s confidence never wavered, even when things went wrong. They saw the way their laughter could dissolve tension like sugar in hot tea. They saw the way, even in the middle of chaos, Alex made everything feel possible.

“Yeah,” Jamie said softly. “We did.”
