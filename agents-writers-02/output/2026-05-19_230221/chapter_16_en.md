
## Chapter 16: Dessert Disaster

The kitchen hummed with the low rhythm of exhaustion and satisfaction. Plates clinked in the sink, their edges softened by soap and water, while the last of the guests lingered by the window, their laughter threading through the open door. Jamie wiped their hands on a dish towel, leaving streaks of flour and something darker—perhaps chocolate, perhaps desperation—across their knuckles. Alex leaned against the counter, arms crossed, watching the tiramisu wobble in its glass dish like a patient about to lose their balance.

“It’s too soft,” Jamie muttered, poking the dessert with a fork. The mascarpone mixture had slumped in the center, pooling around the ladyfingers in a way that defied both gravity and their combined effort. “I think I over-soaked them. Or under-whipped the cream. Or—”

“Or we’re just terrible at making tiramisu,” Alex said, but their tone lacked bite. They stepped closer, peering over Jamie’s shoulder. “Hey. It’s salvageable.”

Jamie snorted, but didn’t look up. Their lower lip jutted out in the way it always did when they were frustrated, a habit Alex had catalogued during their third week of working together. *Adorable*, they’d thought. *Frustrating. Adorable.* Now, the same expression made their chest tighten. “How? It looks like a mudslide.”

“Maybe it’s a mudslide with flair.” Alex reached past Jamie to grab a bowl of espresso-soaked ladyfingers, their fingers grazing Jamie’s wrist. “We could layer it again. Use the drier ones on the bottom, maybe dust the top with cocoa powder. Call it… deconstructed tiramisu.”

“Deconstructed.” Jamie repeated the word like it was a spell, then laughed—a short, surprised sound. “You’re insane. But maybe. Maybe it’s not a total loss.”

They set to work with renewed energy, tearing the ladyfingers into smaller pieces and layering them with what remained of the mascarpone. Alex cracked eggs with the precision of someone who’d done it a thousand times, while Jamie whipped cream until their arm ached. The kitchen filled with the scent of coffee and vanilla, a balm after the day’s chaos.

“Do you think they’ll notice?” Jamie asked, nodding at the guests.

“Notice what?” Alex arched a brow. “That we’re human? That we make mistakes? Please. Claire’s been drinking wine since she arrived and Mark’s too busy flirting with the hostess from the café down the street to care about dessert.”

Jamie rolled their eyes but smiled. “You’re too confident.”

“I’m realistic.” Alex’s smile was soft, almost shy. They paused, hands stilling on the bowl. “Besides, if this is a disaster, we’ll just say we’re avant-garde.”

The words hung in the air, and for a moment, the only sounds were the hum of the refrigerator and the distant clink of glasses. Jamie’s breath hitched. They wanted to say something—anything—but their brain had short-circuited somewhere between Alex’s smile and the way their hair had fallen across their forehead, damp with kitchen steam.

“Right,” they managed. “Avant-garde.”

When they finally presented the dessert, it looked less like a culinary masterpiece and more like a modern art piece. The ladyfingers had been arranged in jagged, uneven layers, the mascarpone oozing between them like a landslide. Alex had dusted the top with cocoa powder in a haphazard spiral, and Jamie had added a few chocolate shavings that resembled charred wood chips.

“Ta-da,” Alex said, placing the dish on the table with a flourish. The guests oohed and aahed, though Claire squinted at it like it might bite.

“It’s… rustic,” Mark offered.

“Rustic?” Jamie echoed, then laughed. “Yeah. Rustic.”

They served slices with trembling hands, bracing for criticism. Instead, Claire declared it “refreshingly unpretentious,” and Mark asked for the recipe. By the time the last bite was finished, Jamie was leaning against Alex’s shoulder, their shoulder, their *Alex*, and the world felt like it had narrowed to the warmth of their shared triumph.

Later, after the dishes were done and the apartment had settled into the hush of late evening, Jamie found Alex in the kitchen, staring into the fridge.

“You okay?” Jamie asked, leaning against the doorway.

“Yeah. Just… thinking.” Alex turned, and their eyes were bright, reflecting the dim light of the fridge. “That tiramisu was a mess, but it worked. We made it work.”

“We did.” Jamie stepped closer, drawn by the gravity they’d been fighting all day. “You’re good at that. Making things work.”

Alex’s laugh was quiet. “Not always. I burned garlic bread last week and cried for ten minutes.”

“You did *not* cry over garlic bread.”

“I did. It was a very emotional loaf.”

Jamie snorted, then sobered. “Thanks. For today. For… everything.”

Alex tilted their head, studying Jamie like they were a puzzle they couldn’t quite solve. “You’re welcome. Though I should probably apologize for the wine incident. I know it was unprofessional—”

“It wasn’t unprofessional.” Jamie’s voice was firmer than they’d intended. “It was… intimate. In a weird way.”

The space between them crackled, charged with every almost-kiss, every glance, every unspoken thing that had built up over weeks of cooking and conflict and quiet understanding. Alex’s hand lifted, hovering near Jamie’s arm, then retreated.

“Jamie.”

“Yeah?”

“Can I ask you something?”

“Anything.”

“Why do you always push people away when they get too close?”

The question hit like a slap. Jamie’s breath caught, their backpedaling instinct kicking in. “I don’t—”

“You do.” Alex’s voice was gentle but unyielding. “You test people, see if they’ll stick around after you’ve been… you know. Prickly. Difficult.”

“I’m not prickly.”

“You’re terrified of being ordinary.” Alex stepped closer, their voice dropping to a whisper. “But you’re not. You’re not ordinary at all.”

Jamie’s heart hammered against their ribs. They wanted to argue, to deflect, but Alex’s words had rooted them in place.

“I’m just…” They gestured vaguely at the kitchen, at the mess, at themselves. “I don’t know. I feel like I’m always one mistake away from falling apart.”

“Then let me help you hold it together.” Alex’s hand finally settled on Jamie’s arm, tentative but sure. “We’re a team, right?”

The simplicity of it—the sheer *rightness* of it—made Jamie’s throat tight. They nodded, then, because words weren’t enough, they leaned in and pressed their forehead to Alex’s.

“We’re a team,” they echoed.

Outside, the city buzzed on, indifferent to the quiet revolution happening in a cramped apartment kitchen. Inside, the tiramisu sat forgotten in the fridge, a testament to the beauty of imperfection and the things that could be salvaged with a little creativity and a lot of heart.
