The road to the citadel didnot so much lead as it bled into the horizon—a vein of grey silt and crushed shale cutting through a landscape of skeletal trees. Here the air tasted of wet copper and old smoke.  

Elara trudged forward, chin tucked into a frayed wool shawl, boots clicking a steady rhythm on the frozen earth. She avoided looking at the roadside. To look was to acknowledge the heaps of discarded clothing and the pale, waxen limbs that poked through the mud—the remnants of those who had lacked the strength to reach the gates.  

Her talent—what made her a pariah among the displaced—hummed beneath her skin like a fever. To Elara the world was not made of light and shadow, but of residue. The dead left stains—iridescent smears of psychic rot that clung to the air like smog. On this road the smog was thick enough to choke on.  

When the blood moon rose, casting a bruised violet light across the valley, Elara crested a ridge and saw the shrine.  

It was a stunted thing of leaning stone and salt-stained icons, huddled beneath the eaves of a black forest. A small group of pilgrims had gathered there, their frames gaunt, their eyes sunken pits of desperation. They huddled around a fire that produced more smoke than heat, their whispers frantic and thin.  

In the centre of the circle lay a child—no more than seven, his breathing a wet, rattling sound that echoed in the oppressive silence.  

“Please,” a woman sobbed, her voice a dry rasp, eyes widening with a mix of hope and instinctive revulsion as she caught sight of Elara. “The sickness… it took him an hour ago. He won’t wake.”  Elara stopped ten paces away. The coil of oily, translucent grey vapor winding around the boy’s throat was a carrion‑wraith, a scavenger of spirit that fed on the transition from life to death, savoring the agony it prolonged.  

“I can move it,” Elara said flatly, stripped of comfort. “But I cannot kill it.”  

The woman lunged, clutching Elara’s hem. “Save him. Please, just save him.”  

Elara sighed, a cold plume escaping her lips, and knelt beside the boy. The chill that washed over her as she reached out was predatory. She didn’t touch the boy’s skin; instead she dipped her fingers into the air and grasped the grey coil of the wraith.  

The connection was instantaneous and violent. Elara gasped, her back arching as the rot flooded inward. She felt the wraith’s hunger—a hollow, screaming void that demanded to be filled—tear into her consciousness, as if swallowing broken glass and freezing sludge. She pulled the parasitic filth out of the boy’s lungs and forced it into her own veins.  

The boy lurched forward, coughing up thick, black ichor, his eyes snapping open. He wailed—a thin, piercing sound—but he was breathing.  

Elara collapsed backward, breath coming in jagged shudders. She stared at her hands. From the fingertips to the wrists her skin had turned a bruised, necrotic black; veins beneath pulsed with a dark, rhythmic light, as if something new and hateful had taken residence in her blood.  

The woman gathered the child, weeping with a gratitude that felt abrasive to Elara’s ears. The other pilgrims backed away, faces twisted in fear. They had seen the boy saved, but they had also seen the price—the stain he left behind.  “Thank you,” the woman whispered, eyes averted. “God bless you.”  

*God has nothing to do with this,* Elara thought, a sudden, ravenous void gnawing at her stomach.  

The gratitude was a thin veil. As the woman hurried the child toward the distant silhouette of the citadel, Elara remained in the dirt. The hunger hit her then—not a craving for bread or water, but a visceral, aching need for the very decay she had just absorbed. She looked at the dead bodies lining the road and felt a sickening, instinctive pull toward them.  She rose slowly, black‑stained hands trembling. Above her, the blood moon hung like a ruptured organ, pulsing in time with the corruption in her veins.  

The citadel loomed ahead, its spires like jagged teeth biting into the crimson firmament. Elara began to walk again, each step heavier than the last, carrying a piece of the dead inside her, knowing that the only way to survive the road was to feed the thing she had become.
