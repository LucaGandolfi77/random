**Chapter 2 – The Desecrated Threshold**  

The citadel did not rise so much as erupt from the earth—a wound of black stone and broken towers thrusting up through the soil as if the land itself tried to expel something buried deep in its bones.  

Elara saw it across a *stretch* of churned mud and dead *furze*, the blood‑moon hanging low behind it like a swollen, infected eye. The walls were not merely old; they were violated. Ivy the colour of bruised flesh crept across them in thick, ropey veins, and from every crevice hung curtains of dark moss that wept a slow, viscous dew. The foliage sagged and trembled, though no wind stirred. It looked the way a body looks when it is still technically alive but has already begun to forget how.  

She walked toward it with the taste of rot still thick on her tongue, her boots squelching through puddles that reflected a sky that was no longer sky but a membrane stretched over something older and hungrier beneath. The carrion‑wraith moved inside her now—a cold knot of borrowed hunger lodged just below her sternum, pulsing in a rhythm that was not her own. With each step the black veins in her hands darkened further, branching like frost across glass, and the whispers that had always dogged her—those iridescent smears of psychic residue left by the dead—were no longer mere smudges on the air. They were screaming.  The road into the citadel was not so much a gate as a throat. A narrow, arching passage cut through walls thick enough to swallow sound, lined with the same weeping stone and black flora that covered the outer walls. The moss brushed against Elara’s shoulders as she passed beneath the arch, its fronds cold and damp against her skin, and she thought—briefly, and with a clarity that frightened her—that she could feel the pulse of something living inside the vegetation. Not growing. *Breathing*.  

Beyond the threshold the courtyard opened into a cavern of shadows and dying fire. What structures remained—a collapsed stable, a guttered watchtower, a lean‑to whose roof had caved in months, or years, ago—stood like the ribs of a carcass picked clean. And among them, huddled around a fire that spat and hissed with more spite than warmth, were the knights.  

There were perhaps a dozen of them. The word had once meant something—polished armour, oaths spoken with clean conviction, a hierarchy of duty and devotion—but time, plague, and the slow erosion of every edifice that had once propped up their world had reduced them to something closer to feral animals wrapped in rusting steel. Their tabards hung in tatters, their gauntlets were split and re‑bound with strips of leather and sinew, and the faces they presented—what could be seen above their dented visors—were gaunt, drawn, and threaded with a paranoia so thick it seemed to cling to the air like smoke.  

They should have stopped her. She was a stranger approaching a sealed sanctuary at the end of a road that had swallowed hundreds. But the knights barely looked at her. Their attention was fixed on the archway she had just passed through, on the darkness beyond it, and on the sounds that issued from that darkness—a low, rhythmic moaning that rose and fell like breath and seemed to come from everywhere at once.  

*“Echoes,”* one of the knights muttered without turning. His voice was scraped raw, his accent salted with the coastal provinces. He held a notched sword across his chest with shaking hands, and Elara noticed—though she did not stare, she could not help but see—that his gauntlets were not merely rusted. They were damp, as if whatever was sweating through the metal of his palms were not quite human heat.  

*“They come at dusk,”* another said, her voice higher but no less frayed. She sat with her back against a collapsed wall, a spear balanced across her knees, her eyes fixed on the shadows pooling beneath the arch like spilled ink. *“Not every night. But enough. They slip through the stones. They taste our memories and—”* She stopped. Swallowed. Adjusted her grip on the spear.  

Elara watched them. She said nothing. She had learned long ago that words spoken without invitation were weapons turned inward, and these people—survivors clinging to a fortress the world had already quietly abandoned—did not need another stranger explaining what they already knew.  

She settled against a half‑collapsed pillar at the edge of the firelight, close enough to feel its warmth but not so close as to be conspicuous. The wraith inside her stirred at the heat—a dull, pulling sensation, like fingers uncurling in her gut—and she forced herself to breathe slowly, to focus on the texture of the stone beneath her fingertips, the grit of it, the cold mineral bite of it, anything to anchor her to the exterior world rather than the one screaming inside her veins.  

She needed to understand the Echoes. Her talent—the terrible, uninvited clarity with which she perceived the residue of the dead—was already telling her things the knights could not see. The shadows pooling in the archway were not mere darkness. They were layered. She could see them the way she saw everything now—not as shapes but as smears of psychic residue, iridescent and heavy, each one carrying the imprint of a death that had not been properly mourned. There were hundreds of them. Thousands, perhaps. They clung to the citadel’s walls the way residue clings to everything that had passed through this place during the forgotten wars, and they had been growing restless.  

She had just decided to rise, to move deeper into the citadel before night worsened, when the Echoes attacked.  

There was no warning. One moment the shadows beneath the arch were merely shadows—dark shapes shifting in the pale moonlight filtering through the courtyard—and the next they surged forward, pouring through the passage like water breaking through a cracked dam. They came in a wave of cold so intense that the fire between the knights guttered and nearly died, and the air filled with a sound Elara could only describe as the opposite of music: a dissonant, keening vibration that resonated not in the ears but somewhere deeper, somewhere behind the sternum where buried grief and unresolved terror lived.  

The knights reacted with the desperate, rehearsed violence of men who had faced this before. Swords rose. The woman with the spear drove its iron point into the earth and shouted a word Elara did not recognize—a prayer, perhaps, or a ward—and the fire between them flared in a sudden, defiant burst of amber and gold. The Echoes recoiled—for a moment.  

But one of them broke free.  It came low and fast, a smear of darkness that moved against the geometry of the courtyard, slipping between the stones themselves—emerging from the flagstones, from the joints in the pillar against which Elara sat, from the air where there should have been nothing—and it struck a knight who had been standing apart from the others, half‑hidden in the shadow of the watchtower.  

Elara saw him clearly. He was young—not older than the boy on the pilgrim road, perhaps, though the years of war had aged him beyond his face. He wore a breastplate that bore the emblem of a sunburst, now so scratched and dented that the symbol was barely legible. His sword arm hung at his side, useless; his fingers curled around a blade too heavy for the strength he had left. And he was dying—not from the wound of any blade or plague, but from the Echo that had wrapped itself around his throat, its smoky tendrils sinking into the soft tissue beneath his jaw, feeding on the transition that was already underway, drawing out the light from his eyes with the patience of a creature that had learned to savour its meals.  

The knight's face went grey. Then white. Then a shade of colour Elara had no name for—a faint, luminous pallor that she recognized from the residue of the newly dead.  

She could have hidden. The pillar was behind her, the courtyard was chaos, and no one would have blamed her—no one would even have noticed—for pressing herself deeper into the stone and letting the knight become one more stain on the citadel’s already saturated ground. She was not a knight. She had no oath. The carrion‑wraith inside her, sensing the nearness of the dying man’s exit, surged with interest, and the hunger in her gut was so acute she could taste copper at the back of her throat.  

She did not hide.  

Instead, she reached out—with her hands, with the wretched talent that had already damned her more times than she could count—and she pulled. Not at the knight. At the Echo.  

Her fingers closed on something that was not quite solid and not quite cold—a sensation like gripping smoke made of frozen glass—and she wrenched it from the knight's throat with a violence that surprised even her. The Echo came free with a sound like tearing silk, a keening that rose in pitch until it was beyond hearing, and it recoiled from her hands as if they were made of fire. For a moment it hovered between them, a translucent smear of grey darker than the surrounding shadows, thrashing wordlessly, and Elara felt its hunger pass through her—felt the knight’s memories, his training, his terror, his quiet and abiding love for something he had never named—spill through her like water through a cracked vessel.  

She absorbed it. She had no choice. That was the nature of her talent. It was not a weapon; it was a corruption dressed as a tool, and every time she used it, it used her back.  

The Echo dissolved into her veins. The black lines on her hands pulsed once, twice, and then spread—crawling past her wrists to her forearms in thin, branching lines like the roots of a tree searching blindly for water. The knight crumpled to the flagstones, gasping, alive but with a look on his face that Elara recognized immediately from the pilgrims at the shrine: the look of someone who had died—or nearly died—and had been dragged back from the threshold by force, leaving behind something vital and irreplaceable.  

He survived. That was the cruelty.  

His eyes were open now, but they were no longer his eyes—they were the eyes of a man trapped inside his own dissolution, conscious of every stage of his unravelling, unable to scream or move or do anything but feel himself becoming something less than he had been. The Echo had been silenced, but it had been redirected—forced back into the knight’s body—and it would feed on him slowly, patiently, over hours or days, and he would remain alive for every moment of it because Elara had not been able to kill what she had moved.  

*“You—”* The knight’s voice was a wet whisper. His gauntleted hand pressed against his throat, feeling the thing squirming beneath his skin, and his eyes—wide, wet, full of a betrayal so absolute it had no word—found Elara’s across the flickering courtyard.  

She had nothing to say. She had learned that speech was a form of mercy she could not afford to give, because mercy required the illusion that things could be made right, and she had killed that illusion a long time ago on a road that bled into the horizon.  

The other knights drove the remaining Echoes back. The prayer—the ward, whatever it was—gained strength as the moon climbed and the shadows thinned, and by the time the courtyard was still again there were bodies to count: seven knights, five Echoes, and one man in gilded steel who knelt beside a pillar and stared at his hands as if seeing them for the first time.  

*“You saved my life,”* the knight on the ground said. His voice had steadied, but beneath the steadiness there was a tremor she knew better than her own heartbeat. He understood. He understood what she had done and what she had cost him, and the understanding was worse than the Echo because it would never fade.  

*“I moved it,”* Elara said. She kept her voice flat, clinical—the voice of a woman who had learned to strip comfort from language before it could be used as a weapon against her. *“That is what I do. I cannot kill it.”*  

*“Then what are you?”*  

She looked at him—really looked, past the blood and the grime and the dented sunburst on his breastplate—and for a moment the carrion‑wraith inside her stirred with something that was not hunger but its cousin: recognition. The knight was dying. Not today, not tomorrow, but in a way that could not be undone, and every hour he spent in her presence would remind him of how.  

*A pariah,* she said, and rose to her feet.  

She walked past him into the citadel, into the warren of dark corridors that yawned open before her like the interior of a throat. The walls here were damp, and the black moss pulsed faintly—almost imperceptibly—as if breathing in time with something vast and unconscious buried deep within the stone. The whispers were louder here. Not the scattered murmurs of the courtyard but a dense, layered murmur that seemed to seep from the walls themselves, and Elara knew—felt in the marrow of her corrupted bones—that the memories had found a new host.  Her.  

She pressed her back against the cold stone of a corridor wall and closed her eyes. The residue was everywhere in this place—thousands of smears, overlapping and bleeding into one another, a palimpsest of death and forgetting that stretched back centuries. The forgotten wars had not been forgotten by the citadel. They had been swallowed, absorbed, stored in its bones the way a body stores poison in its fat—patiently, silently, waiting for the moment when the immune system fails.  

The carrion‑wraith inside her pulsed. It tasted this place the way a starving man tastes food. And Elara, standing in the dark with the black veins spreading slowly beneath her skin and the voices of the dead whispering through walls that were not walls but wounds, understood with a sick and terrible certainty that she had not come to the citadel for sanctuary.  

She had come because the citadel had called to the thing inside her.  

And whatever it was that slept beneath these stones, it was no longer sleeping.  It was hungry—too.
