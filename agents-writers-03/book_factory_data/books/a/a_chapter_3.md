**Chapter 3 – Sanctum of the Hollowed (Edited)**  

The courtyard’s fire had dwindled to a handful of glowing embers when Elara rose, the carrion‑wraith’s pulse a counterpoint to the knights’ murmured fears. The citadel breathed around her—an inhalation of cold stone and ancient rot that seemed to measure the weight of every step she took.  

She followed the corridor’s curve away from the firelight, toward a section where the architecture grew softer, more organic, as though the stone itself had begun to digest its own foundations.  

The infirmary had once been a manor’s great hall. Its vaulted ceiling was now propped up by columns of amber sap that wept instead of bearing weight. The air hung thick with boiled roots and iron—a metallic tang that spoke of blood that had long since ceased to flow warm. Rough pallets lined the flagstones, each one cradling a figure swaddled in thread‑bare blankets, their breathing shallow as dying bellows.  

> “You’re not supposed to be here.”  

A thin voice slipped from behind a curtain of drying herbs. A man stepped into the dim light, his cassock patched with shards of leather that had once been belts. His skin was the pallor of someone who had not seen sunlight in months, but his eyes—still a sharp, icy blue—cut through the gloom.  

> “I could say the same,” Elara replied, feeling how strange the words sounded even to her own ears. The wraith that lived inside her seemed to recognize the timbre of this sorrow, to stir. “What miracle keeps them alive?”  

Kaelen—she would learn his name later, when he chose to offer it—studied her with the careful scrutiny of a man accustomed to measuring souls. His fingers traced the rim of a wooden bowl filled with steaming water.  

> “A different question entirely. These people don’t live because of miracles. They live because I take what wants to kill them and bear it myself.”  

He lifted the bowl, exposing a surface sheened with an unnatural slickness, as though oil had been worked into every molecule. From it rose a tendril of darkness—no thicker than spider silk—that coiled around his wrist and vanished into his sleeve.  

> “The Red Fever isn’t plague,” he said, as matter‑of‑fact as a weather report. “It’s a hunger. And hunger, as you well know, must be fed.”  

The wraith inside her twitched, a cold pressure building behind her ribs.  

> “And what happens when you can’t feed it?”  

Kaelen’s smile was the ghost of something that had once been warmth.  

> “Then it feeds on me.”  

He gestured toward the doorway at the far end of the hall, half‑hidden by a tapestry of saints whose faces had been scratched away. Beyond it lay a descent of stone steps, choking the air with dust and debris.  

As they moved forward, Elara noted the way the walls leaned inward, their stone ribs creaking ever so slightly with each footfall. The citadel remembered every drop of blood spilled within its bounds, every promise broken on its altars, every moment mercy failed. That memory pressed against her skin like damp wool.

### The Cellar  

In the cellar she found the well Kaelen had spoken of—no ordinary stone well but a living throat of blackened wood and pulsing root. Water rose from it not as a clear stream but as thick, vein‑like tendrils. When Elara brushed a fingertip across its surface, her reflection rippled; the black veins spreading from her wrists began to rise like frost across a cold pane.  

> “The citadel remembers,” Kaelen whispered from behind her. “Every death, every betrayal, every mercy denied. It keeps them here—in the walls, in the water, in the very air we breathe.”  

Elara recoiled, feeling the pull of countless voices—screams, prayers, final breaths—swirling together in the depths below. They were not angry; they were hungry.  

> “And you drink from this?”  

> “I sip,” he said, eyes never leaving the well. “Carefully. The alternative is… becoming like them.”  He gestured upward to the fever‑ridden patients. In the next moment, a young mother’s labored breaths steadied; colour returned to cheeks that had been the grey of tombstones. Yet as the fever broke, the price made itself known: Kaelen clutched his chest, his posture as though he were trying to hold his own heart together with bare hands.  > “The wellspring is connected to everything,” he whispered, more to himself than to Elara. “To the citadel, to the blood moon, to whatever lies beneath us all.”  

Elara felt the wraith within her coil tighter, recognizing kinship in that ancient, gnawing hunger. But where it would simply consume, she felt a different impulse—to preserve, to hold back the tide, however briefly.  

> “Show me everything,” she said.  The words hung between them, and Kaelen, whose faith had already died a dozen deaths, found himself nodding toward mysteries that would demand yet another resurrection.
