**Chapter 6 – TheVeins of the Earth (Edited)**  

The descent into the citadel’s underbelly was a burial of light. Behind them, the infirmary’s amber‑sap columns wept their last tears as Kaelen sealed the passage with a slab of stone that groaned like a dying animal. Ahead the corridors narrowed, their flagstones slick with a viscous film that tasted of iron and old earth. Elara’s hands, already webbed with black veins, tingled with an awareness that was not her own—the slow, pulsing thrum of the walls themselves.  

“Down here,” Kaelen whispered, his voice thin as a spider’s thread in the oppressive silence. The cassock that once marked his station now clung to him like a shroud, its leather patches stiffening as if soaking up the decay around them. His eyes—those icy‑blue remnants of faith—were fixed on the darkness ahead.  

“The Red Fever originated in the deep cisterns,” he said. “Whatever the citadel remembers, it remembers in its bones.”  

Elara nodded, though Kaelen could not see her. The carrion‑wraith coiled in her chest, a cold knot of borrowed hunger that seemed to recognize the descent. With each step downward the air grew heavier, thick with the scent of wet stone and something sweeter and rotter—flowers blooming in a grave.  

Above they had known arches and vaulted halls; below the architecture had mutated. The walls no longer merely wept; they secreted, their surfaces pulsing with a slow, organic rhythm that mirrored the wraith’s heartbeat inside her.  

The corridors curved like intestines, their floors sloping at unnatural angles. Where stone met stone, dark tendrils of fungus crept in thick, unhealthy veins, glowing faintly with an eerie light. The stone beneath Elara’s fingertips seemed to contract as she touched it, startled by her intrusion.  

“They call this place the Gut,” Kaelen murmured, his breath forming clouds in the frigid air. “The citadel eats, and what cannot be digested settles here.”  

A sudden tremor shook the passage, sending cascades of dust from the ceiling. Elara stumbled, catching herself against the wall. The stone was warm—almost alive. Through the wraith’s borrowed senses she felt the network of tunnels around them, not as stone but as something fleshy, something hungry.  

“The Echoes follow us,” Kaelen said, eyes darting into the black. “They taste the memory of descent, of purpose. They want to know where we’re going.”  

Elara felt them too—not as visual presences but as cold spots in the air, voids that seemed to absorb sound and warmth. They slipped through the stones like thoughts through a dying mind, sampling the memories of every soul that had ever passed this way.  

Ahead the passage choked on a collapse of rock and what looked like petrified vines. The way forward was completely blocked.  

“We go back,” Kaelen said, defeat tasting like ash. “There’s another path, but the fever patients won’t last.”  

Elara stared at the obstruction, the cold knot of hunger expanding, reaching for the stones, the vines, the darkness. It was a feast for something like her.  “No,” she whispered, and placed her hands on the obstruction. The black veins on her arms pulsed, spreading up to her elbows like creeping night.  

“What are you doing?” Kaelen asked, taking a hesitant step back.  

“Trust me.”  Closing her eyes, Elara focused not on the stones themselves but on the memory of their collapse—the force that had broken them, the vines that had grown through them. She commanded the decay, not to move it, but to remember its purpose and to part.  

The wraith inside her fed her understanding of the obstruction. She felt the centuries of pressure, the slow growth of the petrified vines, the give and strain of the ancient stones. Beneath it all pulsed the citadel’s own hunger—to consume what came below.  

With a guttural sound that seemed to rise from both her and the walls, Elara pushed. The black veins flared, spreading like roots across the stone. The obstruction shuddered, not violently, but with something like relief. The petrified vines retreated, the stones groaned and shifted aside, revealing a narrow passage beyond.  

Elara collapsed to her knees, gasping. The skin on her hands had taken on a rough, stony texture—a permanent corruption, a price for her power. The black veins ran up her arms like frost on winter glass.  

Kaelen stared at her, fear and something else—recognition—flashing across his face.  

“You commanded the stone,” he breathed. “That’s not just talent. That’s communion.”  

Elara looked at the stone‑textured skin of her hands, at the veins that now marked her. The wraith throbbed, satisfied. The hunger remained, but it was now directed, focused. They had a path forward, but she was irrevocably changed.  

As they slipped deeper into the new passage, Elara became aware of another sound—footsteps behind them. Not Kaelen’s careful, measured tread, but softer, slower, matching their own pace exactly.  

Kaelen froze, his face draining of the last of its color.  

“They’re following us,” he whispered. “The Echoes don’t just taste memory. They imitate it.”  

Elara did not look back. She kept walking, her stony hands clenched into fists. The footsteps echoed theirs perfectly, a perfect rhythm of pursuit. The citadel was watching. The citadel was learning. And somewhere below, the Red Fever waited—hungry for more than just blood.
