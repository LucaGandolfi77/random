**Chapter 5 – *The TrainRide***  

Rain had turned the city streets into rivers that slipped past glistening glass towers, but Violet Hartwell didn’t stop. Her heels clicked against the pavement as she chased Julian Reeves into the crowded train station, his silhouette already moving toward the platform for the 7:15 express. He hadn’t looked at her once—his focus buried in the leather‑bound thesis he clutched like a shield, the drafts he’d scribbled on buses, cafés, and park benches. *Too late*, she thought, watching his reflection blur in the train doors. *He’s gone.*  

She slipped aboard three stops too late.  

The cabin hummed with the sterile chill of air‑conditioning and the muffled thrum of distant announcements. Violet squeezed into a vacant seat by the window, her silk dress soaked at the hem from the downpour. A man in a rumpled suit eyed her, then glanced at Julian across the aisle—*his seat remains empty*—and sighed. The train lurched forward, plunging her into a blur of darkened suburbs until the landscape outside shifted: brick‑roofed houses, flickering streetlights, and a weather‑worn sign that read **Pine Hill Station**.  

*“We’re not scheduled to stop here until tomorrow,”* Julian muttered from two aisles over, already halfway toward the door to confront the conductor.  

But the conductor wasn’t waiting.  

A man in a worn flannel shirt and glasses materialized, clipboard in hand.  

*“We’ve got a research fellow named Dr. Reeves,”* he said, producing a laminated photo that matched Julian to a T. *“Pine Hill grants honorary fellowships to scholars who are actually working locally. Understand?”*  

Julian hesitated, brow knitting. *“Working *locally*?”*  

A train lurch jerked them both upright.  Violet watched the exchange from her seat, the pamphlet she’d pilfered from his thesis—a chapter on *Ethics on the Margins*—stained with coffee rings. His fingers tightened around the cover. *A job in the sticks?* Her lips curled. *A career in the shadows?*  

The train shuddered to a halt, doors hissing open to the same faded **Pine Hill Station** sign, now flickering in the rain. Julian turned, startled.  

*“I—I don’t need charity!”* he blurted, but the professor’s smile was already cold.  

*“This is a fellowship, Dr. Reeves. To stay, or to leave?”*  

*“Stay,”* Julian snapped, too sharply.  

*“You’ll thank me later,”* the man said, exchanging a glance with the train staff before walking away.  

Violet’s chest tightened. *Jealousy*, she labeled it—*resentment*. Why did he always sidestep opportunity? She rose to leave, but his hand shot out and seized hers.  

*“Wait.”*  

The ticket agent scrambled to correct the mistake. *“Wrong train. You’ve already passed Pine Hill thirty minutes ago.”*  Julian froze, his grip on her hand a vice. *“Liar.”*  

The words hung in the claustrophobic cabin as the train lurched again, pulling away from the phantom stop.  

They didn’t reach New York that night.  

*“Your sister’s letter,”* Julian pressed, jabbing a finger at the thesis case. *“Last semester’s draft. You filched it.”*  

Violet stiffened. *“My research isn’t—”*  

*“Ethics on the margins. You think it’s a ‘side project’?”* His voice cracked. *“For you, maybe. But this town—this quiet place—I don’t know how to leave it. It’s… sticky to me.”*  

Violet’s breath hitched. She’d expected bruises, Bollywood‑style melodrama. *This—the ache of his words—is what makes my heart a riot.*  

The conductor reappeared, sharp as a tack. *“Passengers lying about stops will be—”*  

*“Enough,”* Julian cut in, his pocketknife flashing in the dim light.  

The man blinked, defused, and retreated.  

The cabin dissolved into a world of their own—the clatter of lukewarm coffee, the smell of wet wool. Violet stared at his hand, still clasped in hers, and whispered, *“Why are you really here?”*  

Julian pulled her into an unoccupied doorway, the train’s roars muffled behind them. The booth behind the ticket counter held a single sheet of paper:  

**Attn: Dr. Reeves: Fellowship resigned. Effective tomorrow—effective today.**  

He fed it into a printer, and together they typed a brief reply:  

*“Violet Hartwell and Julian Reeves, Declining fellowship in exchange for a semester of radical listening.”*  

The conductor raised an eyebrow. *“Radical listening?”*  

Julian smiled—low, crookedly—and turned to her. *“You’re stuck here with me until I finish this. Not because you’re brilliant at screwing things up for me. But because…”*  

*“Because?”*  *“Because you’re not in it for the job.”*  

Violet’s throat tightened. *“I’m an idiot. You think I’d travel across the state to attach myself to your—”*  

*“Status. Influence. The illusion that you belong to something important?”*  The train screeched to a halt again, doors bashing open to a depot where a single flickering streetlight bathed them in gold. Julian kissed her—fast, desperate—and she let him, fingers splayed across his jacket, her own mind a storm of *“What‑ifs”* and *“Oh but what ifs.”*  

When they pulled apart, Violet’s bottom lip was pink as a bruise. *“I wanted you to choose me,”* she murmured. *“Not your ambitions. Me. The ugly, priceless fool I am.”*  Julian’s silver pulse lit her neck. *“You could’ve asked.”*  

They didn’t speak of the fellowship again.  

But the train ride home was silent, save for the rhythm of their breathing. The rain had turned to snow by nightfall, dusting the windows of Pine Hill Station in frost.  

*“Tomorrow, I’m moving to your office,”* Julian said on the platform.  

Violet stiffened. *“I’m not your keepsake,”* she snapped—then softened, *“but… how?”*  

He hesitated, then gestured to her notebook—still clutched in her hand, its pages freshly inked with the questions that had once made him doubt himself. *“You’re not my project,”* he breathed. *“But maybe… you’re mine.”*  

She smiled then, sharp and unapologetic. *“Don’t make me cry again, Reeves.”*  

The train roared onward, the small town shrinking into memory—and in the distance, a star blinked awake, steady and unflinching.
