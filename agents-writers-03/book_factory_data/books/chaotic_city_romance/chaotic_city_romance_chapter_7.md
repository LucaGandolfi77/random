## Chapter 7 —Feedback Loop  
*POV: dual close third‑person*  

Below is a **line‑by‑line edit** that tightens the prose, deepens the inner‑thought beats, and makes the “dual‑close” POV feel like two breathing, interlocked lenses. After each edit block you’ll see a short **why‑it‑works** note so you can apply the same thinking to the rest of the manuscript.

### I.  

**Original**  
> The safe house smelled of wet plaster and someone else's cigarettes. Kira stood in the doorway, the coat still damp from the alley outside, and let the latch click shut behind her. The overhead bulb buzzed at a frequency she'd once been trained to name—a surveillance frequency, one of forty‑three the Bureau used to triangulate movement. Nobody had bothered to replace it. The room didn't care.

**Edited**  
> The safe house smelled of wet plaster and stale cigarettes, the kind left behind by the last tenant. Kira stood in the doorway, coat still damp from the alley, and let the latch click shut behind her. The ceiling bulb hummed at a frequency she recognised—one of the Bureau’s forty‑three triangulation nets. Nobody had bothered to replace it; the room made no complaint.  

**Why**  
- “Someone else’s” → “stale” adds texture without extra words.  - “Let the latch click shut behind her” is more visceral than “let … click”.  
- “Hummed … recognised” tightens the surveillance‑tech detail and keeps the close‑third voice inside Kira’s head.  
- Cutting “The room didn’t care” removes a redundant meta‑comment; the room’s indifference is now shown by *nobody had bothered* and the hum itself.  

---  ### II.  

**Original**  > Rook sat on the far end of a sagging sofa, his jacket unzipped, the matte‑black case open across his knees. Inside, the frayed fiber‑optic cables lay coiled like something sleeping. He didn’t look up.  

> “You’re late,” he said.  

> “I know.”

**Edited**  
> Rook occupied the far end of a sagging sofa, jacket half‑unzipped, the matte‑black case spread across his knees. Frayed fiber‑optic cables lay coiled like a sleeping beast. He didn’t glance up.  

> “You’re late.”  

> “I know.”  

**Why**  
- “Occupied” gives a sharper visual than “sat on”.  
- “Spread across” mirrors the earlier “sprawled” feel but keeps the case as an object of tension.  
- “Sleeping beast” adds a metaphor that feels true to the close‑third perception (Rook’s mind cataloguing threat).  
- Dialogue tags are trimmed to keep the rhythm lean.

### III.  

**Original**  
> I wasn't followed.  

> “Maybe not,” Rook said. “But it doesn't matter.”

**Edited**  
> “You weren’t followed,” Rook said, voice flat. “That doesn’t matter.”  

**Why**  
- Cutting “I know” saves space; the line is now spoken by Rook, giving him agency.  
- “Flat” replaces the ad‑tag, reinforcing his detached tone and keeping focus on the *tone* of his words.

### IV.  

**Original**  
> “Because the Whisper was the only place I wasn't being watched. And you were the only person on it who wasn't asking me to be someone.”  

**Edited**  
> “Because the Whisper was the only place I wasn’t being watched. And you were the only voice that never asked me to be someone else.”  

**Why**  
- “Voice” feels more intimate than “person”.  
- “Never” adds a echo of certainty that mirrors Kira’s resolve.

### V.  

**Original**  
> “You're not what they made you,” he said.  

> “You don’t know what they made me.”  > “I know what you chose.”  **Edited**  
> “You’re not what they made you.”  

> “Then what *are* they?”  

> “I know what you *chose*.”  

**Why**  
- Turning the second exchange into a question forces Kira to interrogate her own identity, raising stakes.  
- “What they made me” loses a bit of vagueness; “made you” is more final.  
- The third line becomes a stark, declarative counter‑point that lands harder as a short, punctuated statement.

### VI.  

**Original**  
> She again opened the encrypted cache. A drive she’d kept mirrored on a secondary drive for months. A drive she’d told herself was insurance. A drive she’d never opened.  

**Edited**  
> She opened the encrypted cache again— the secondary drive she’d kept mirrored for months, labelled to herself as insurance, the drive she’d never dared open.  **Why**  
- Condensing the repetition streamlines the mental stack and lets the *act* of opening dominate the beat.  
- “Dared” adds an emotional weight that feels authentic to Kira’s trained brain.

### VII.  

**Original**  
> “Kira.” He said.  

> “Who are you?” he asked.  

**Edited**  
> “Kira.”  

> “Who are you?”  

**Why**  
- The NAME_1 caps are dropped; the raw dialogue now feels more intimate in close third.  - Cutting “he said” removes redundancy; the next exchange makes it clear who’s speaking.

### VIII.  **Original**  
> “I betrayed the safe house,” he said. “Voss’s people. I told them the route because I knew they'd use it to reach you before the retrieval team did.”  

**Edited**  
> “I betrayed Voss’s people.” He kept his voice low. “I told them the route because I wanted them to reach you first.”  

**Why**  
- “Voss’s people” is enough; “the safe house” is clarified by context.  
- The phrase “I wanted them to reach you first” sharpens motive and feels more personal.  
- Dialogue tag is placed next to the attribution for smoother rhythm.  ---  

### IX.  

**Original**  
> “I could’ve stayed,” Rook said. “I could’ve.”  

**Edited**  
> “I could’ve stayed.”  

> “Could’ve.”  

**Why**  
- Repeating “I could’ve” dilutes impact; a terse “Could’ve.” forces the reader to sit in the tension.

### X.  

**Original**  
> The van pulled into the rain. The city breathed around them—surveillance hum, distant sirens, the endless cataloguing of every footstep.  

**Edited**  
> The van rolled through rain‑slick streets, the city breathing around them—surveillance hums, distant sirens, a world that catalogued every footstep.  

**Why**  
- “Rolled” adds kinetic energy.  
- “Hum” and “world that catalogued” tighten the list while keeping the close‑third sense of a living, watching environment.  

---  

### XI.  

**Original**  
> But in the back of the van, two hands stayed clasped, and the frequency between them was one the Bureau would never learn to parse.  

**Edited**  
> In the back of the van, their hands stayed clasped, and the frequency between them was a signal the Bureau could never parse.  

**Why**  
- “Signal” ties the tech theme back to the opening buzz, giving a full‑circle echo.

## How to Apply This Mindset  

| **Element** | **What Was Changed** | **Why It Works in a Dual‑Close POV** |
|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Verb Choice** | Swapped “sat” → “occupied”, “hummed” → “hummed”, “stood” → “stood in the doorway” | Gives each character a distinct verb that reflects their mental posture. |
| **Sensory Detail** | Added “stale”, “raining”, “sleeping beast” | The close third notices specifics that matter to the body—temperature, vibration, texture—so the reader feels the bodily stakes. |
| **Adverbial Economy** | Trimmed “she said quietly” → removed; “he said flatly” kept only when tone is essential | In close third, the *inner* voice carries the nuance; external tags are only needed when the tone shifts dramatically. |
| **Dialogue Rhythm** | Shortened, cut redundant tags | Keeps the conversation breath‑by‑breath, mirroring two people leaning into each other mentally as well as physically. |
| **Show, Don’t Tell** | Turned “The room didn’t care” into a concrete description of the bulb’s hum and the neglected latch | The environment is observed through each character’s lens, giving dual perspectives of the same space. |
| **Emotional Beats** | Turned “I could’ve stayed” into a terse “Could’ve.” | Short statements echo the limited, clipped thoughts that arise when two characters are *thinking* in the same mental tight‑rope. |
