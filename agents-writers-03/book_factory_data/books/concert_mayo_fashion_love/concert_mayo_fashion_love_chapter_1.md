**CHAPTER 1 – “OpeningNight” (Edited & Polished)**  

*POV: Close third‑person (Lina’s perspective)*  

The lights in the old concert hall were low enough to make the velvet curtains look like bruised skin, yet bright enough that every ripple of silk on the stage caught a sliver of gold. Lina lingered at the back of the room, the echo of applause still humming through the rafters like a stray chord. She could still feel the phantom pressure of the runway lights on her shoulders, the way the audience’s eyes had flicked over the angular cut of her latest collection as she turned, as if they were trying to read a map in the stitchwork of her hem.  

She had come not to listen, but to speak.  A thin, handwritten sheet of paper lay folded in the pocket of her coat—a stanza she’d rehearsed all week, a line she’d never intended to share beyond the safety of her own notebook.  

*“Silk and static, a pulse beneath the thread, / I am the seam that holds the storm, the breath before the spread.”*  

The words felt too personal, too raw, to recite aloud in a room full of strangers. But the night was an invitation to risk something more than a garment.  She pressed the paper against her palm and stepped forward, the soft scuff of her heels against polished wood resonating like a metronome. The murmurs of the crowd died down as she drew a breath scented with cheap cologne and anticipation.  

“Excuse me,” she said, her voice edged with the practiced poise of a model who had walked a thousand catwalks, but now trembling somewhere beneath the surface. “I’m Lina—designer, writer, and—well—sometimes a little too eager to hear my own voice.” She offered a thin smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes and lifted the paper.  

The stanza slipped into the room like perfume—unexpected, layered. Each syllable was measured; each pause a breath held between the beats of a song only she could fully hear. The lines unfolded, and with them images flickered: a model strutting down a neon‑lit runway, the click of a camera, a hidden pocket of mayo tucked into the lining of a coat, the sticky sweet scent that lingered long after the party had ended.  

When she finished, the silence that settled wasn’t awkward—it was a thick, velvet hush that pressed the walls inward. She lowered the paper, her fingers lingering on the edge as if gauging the weight of what she’d just offered.  

Across the room, near the backstage door, a figure stood half‑turned, hands cradling a laptop and a tangle of cables. He was watching her with an intensity that made her pulse quicken. He wore a faded black T‑shirt with a small badge that read **Eli—Audio Engineer**. The soft glow of the stage lights caught the faint scar on his left cheek, a reminder that he was more than a silhouette.  

*He’s bored,* she thought, embarrassment blooming behind her ears. *He thinks I’m just another act, another flashy distraction in his technical world.*  

The misreading settled over her like a tide. She’d imagined him leaning forward, perhaps offering a polite smile, maybe even commenting on the beauty of her words. Instead, he simply nodded, his eyes flicking back to his laptop as if the performance were background noise to his work.  

But then Eli lifted his gaze again, catching the faint tremor in her voice that betrayed the confidence she tried to project. Something—curiosity, perhaps pity—crossed his features. He didn’t speak, but he placed a hand gently on the back of the chair he’d been leaning on, a silent invitation to stay.  

Her heart hammered against her ribs. The paper’s edge dug into her palm, a reminder of the vulnerability she’d just laid bare. She met his eyes, letting the flush in her cheeks be proof that she cared about his reaction.  

“I’m sorry if that was… too much,” she whispered, the words barely louder than the rustle of the curtains behind her.  

Eli’s mouth twitched into a half‑smile. “No,” he said, low and steady, “it was exactly what this place needed.” In the same breath he reached into the coat pocket and produced a small, crumpled envelope. He slipped it into her hand without another word.  

She opened it at once, heart thudding harder. Inside lay a sheet of lined paper—identical to the one she’d just recited from—filled with looping script that read:  

*“If you’re looking for silk and static, I’ll be the current that runs through it. Meet me where the lights dim, and we’ll find a new stanza together.”*  

In the corner, a tiny doodle of a jar of mayo stared up at her, rendered in a shaky hand.  

A strange, warm thrill cut through the earlier anxiety. She glanced at Eli, who had already turned back to his laptop, shoulders relaxed as if he’d just completed a routine task. The envelope was warm in her grip, the paper soft against her fingertips.  

She realized—in that instant—that the misreading was not a wall but a doorway; she could choose to walk through it or stay shut behind.  

The venue’s announcement crackled over the speakers: *structural renovations next month; final show scheduled for two weeks from now.* “We’ll be closing soon for structural upgrades,” the voice intoned, “so catch this final performance while you can.”  

Lina’s mind pivoted to the practical. The hall was her sanctuary, the place where she felt most at home in her own skin—whether that skin was painted with bold prints or lined with a secret stash of homemade mayo. If the space that held her most honest moments was about to be stripped down and rebuilt, she needed to decide whether to chase this fragile thread of connection or retreat before the lights went out for good.  

She slipped the envelope back into her pocket, feeling its weight like a promise. The hall’s ambience swirled as patrons began to file out. A final glimpse of Eli—now smoothing a stray piece of wire with deliberate care—met her eye. He gave a slow, deliberate nod; a silent acknowledgment of the risk she had taken, the poem she offered, the mayo joke that only they seemed to understand.  

Lina inhaled, cool hallway air filling her lungs. She realized that vulnerability, for all its terror, was also a kind of courage. She had spoken a truth that no runway could ever showcase and left a piece of herself—her poem, her mayo, her fear—out in the open. Whether Eli truly understood mattered less than the fact that she had taken the risk.  

She turned toward the exit, heels echoing against marble. The crowd’s murmur rose again outside. She caught one more look at Eli, who lifted his hand in a quiet, deliberate wave.  

She smiled, a small, hopeful curve. The renovation threatened to erase this chapter, but it also closed a door that could open another. She owed herself honesty, and honesty demanded she write him a poem tonight—*not* for the audience, *not* for the critics, but for the person who had the patience to hear her.  

Stepping into the rain‑slick street, city lights reflected off puddles like scattered verses waiting to be read. The first note of a new stanza had already been struck—fragile, yet unmistakable—and beneath the steady drip of rain, a melody she couldn’t yet name began to hum.
