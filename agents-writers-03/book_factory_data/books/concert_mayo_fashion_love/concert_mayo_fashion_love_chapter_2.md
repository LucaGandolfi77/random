**CHAPTER 2 – “The Mayonnaise Memoir”**  
*POV: Close third‑person (Eli’s perspective)*  The concert hall exhaled around him—slow, heavy, a breath of velvet and dust motes dancing in the single work‑light he’d left on. Eli stood at the cavernous mixing console, his fingers still resting on the faders as if they could still feel the last vibration of Lina’s voice.  

The air smelled of old wood, of electrical heat, and of the faintest trace of her perfume—something citrus‑sharp that cut through the room’s mustiness like a beam of sunlight.  

He’d watched her from the shadowed wings, a silhouette of architectural silk and nervous energy. When she stepped into the pool of light, her hand trembled. Not much—just a single quake as she folded the paper. He saw it because he was always listening for the minute tells that tell a room how it’s breathing: the hitch before a note, the shift of weight before a laugh. He catalogued every detail: the way her thumb smoothed the page’s edge, the defiant set of her jaw beneath the practiced smile, the fact that she’d mis‑buttoned the cuff of her left glove.  

He hadn’t been bored; he’d been *captivated* by the collision of her worlds—the glittering surface of the fashion persona and the raw, exposed nerve of her words. So when she finished and turned to leave, scanning the darkness as if searching for a verdict, he did something stupid. He wrote a reply on the back of a set list, a bad, heartfelt poem about echoes and seams, and drew a clumsy jar of mayonnaise in the corner—a private joke he didn’t even fully understand. A reference to the only thing he knew about her beyond the runway: a rumor that a designer always carried condiment packets in her clutch.  

Now, alone in the silence, he shrugged out of his faded black T‑shirt—the one with the tiny hole near the collar he kept meaning to mend. As he pulled it over his head, something crinkled in the pocket. Not a gum wrapper. A small, cool glass jar, no bigger than a thimble, its lid hand‑labeled in neat, feminine script:  

*“For Eli – Spread liberally. No judgment.”*  

His breath caught. Inside lay a pale, creamy substance—homemade mayo.  

The memory unspooled. Three weeks earlier, on a blustery Tuesday, he’d been wrestling a faulty cable beneath the front‑of‑house desk. A woman’s heels had clicked to a stop nearby. He’d emerged, grease on his cheek, to find *her*—Lina—crouched, holding out a paper towel. Flourished on it was a single teaspoon of mayonnaise.  

“For the engine,” she’d said, eyes glittering with a mischief that felt like a secret. “All great machines need lubrication.”  
She’d straightened, smoothed her skirt, and walked away, leaving him with the strange offering and a faint, childhood scar on his left cheek (a fishing‑hook accident he never told anyone about) buzzing like a live wire.  

He’d tossed the teaspoon aside, bewildered—just another fashion stunt.  

The jar in his hand was different. It was a relic. A message in a bottle from a world he had no map for.  

His engineer’s mind whirred: *Coincidence. She leaves these for everyone. A brand thing.* But the label was in her handwriting. The glass was the same faceted type his own mother used for her prized lemon curd. Warmth and terror flooded him—*she had noticed him. Specifically him. Remembered the teaspoon.*  

He turned the jar in his palm. The gesture was monumental. It said, *I see the man behind the console. The one who notices trembling hands.*  

Fear slammed cold and instant into his chest. *She’s a hurricane in haute couture. I’m a guy who calibrates subwoofers and eats microwave meals.* The chasm between them wasn’t just social; it was existential. He existed in decibels and deadlines; she *was* the art. What could he possibly offer that wouldn’t seem mundane, even disappointing?  

He couldn’t keep it. To keep it would be to admit he wanted to be seen, to be known. And that meant admitting he was already half in love with a ghost of a woman who loved mayonnaise and wrote poems about silk and static.  He had to give it back—not as a rejection, but as an answer. A clumsy, reciprocal offering.  

He found a fresh sheet of printer paper—a crisp white square. He placed the tiny jar in the center, its glass catching the work‑light with a dull glint. Then he folded the paper around it, the corners not quite meeting. It looked like a failed gift, which, in his mind, made it perfect.  

He walked back into the empty hall. The stage yawned like a dark mouth. He set the package on the very edge of the polished wood, precisely where her hand had rested when she’d read. A silent *I received your message. Here is mine.*  

Just as he turned to leave, his phone buzzed in his pocket. A text from his manager:  

*“Eli, you’re still on for the gala tech run tomorrow night. 6 PM sharp. Don’t be late.”*  

The gala. The charity event where he’d be crew, invisible in the rafters. Where *she* would be the star, reciting poetry to a room full of donors.  

A new, desperate hope flickered. Maybe she’d be there. Maybe she’d see *him*.  

He was at the stage door when he heard the familiar, rapid click of heels on marble. Not running, but with urgent purpose. He flattened himself against the wall in the shadows just as Lina burst through the backstage door, her coat askew, her face pale with a different kind of tension. She was on the phone, her voice tight.  “Mira, I can’t— Yes, I know the fitting is at noon, but the poem… No, it’s not a ‘bit.’ It’s… it’s important.” She paused, listening, her free hand clutching the strap of her bag. “Fine. Okay. I’ll be there. Just… send a car.”  

She hung up, slumped against the wall for a second, the fight draining from her shoulders, leaving a look of exhausted vulnerability. She looked… young. Lost.  

She pushed off and headed for the stage, probably to retrieve a forgotten poem or simply to stand where she’d been heard. Eli watched her go, the small package on the stage suddenly feeling like a pathetic pebble thrown at a mountain.  

He slipped out into the night, the image of her—not the poised designer but the tired woman on the phone—burned into his mind. The engineer in him calculated distance, timing, readiness. The man in him felt a pull so strong it terrified him.  

Back in his tiny apartment, he placed the jar of mayo on the kitchen counter. It sat absurdly beside his coffee maker and stack of manuals. He didn’t open it. He just stared at the creamy testament to a connection that felt both impossible and inevitable.  

He had no idea if she would find his reply. He had no idea if she’d even care. But for the first time in years, he had risked something that wasn’t about perfect sound or meeting a spec. He had risked being seen as the ordinary man he was, hoping it might be enough for the extraordinary woman who loved mayonnaise and wrote poems about silk and static.  

The fear was still there, a cold knot in his gut. But beneath it, warmer and more fragile, grew the first green shoot of hope.
