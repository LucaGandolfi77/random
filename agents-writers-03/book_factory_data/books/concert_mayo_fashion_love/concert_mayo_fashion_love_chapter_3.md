**CHAPTER 3 – “First Dress, Second Thought”**  
*Close third‑person (Lina’s perspective)*  

The coat hung in her dressing room like a promise—or a warning. Its hem was lined with thin LED strips that flickered faintly even in the dim light, each tiny bulb a reminder of how far she’d pushed the boundaries of fashion. The angular shoulder cut, the fabric that clung just enough to suggest without revealing, the narrow slash of crimson that turned her eyes almost black—everything shouted *look at me*.  

Exactly the reaction she’d tried to avoid.  

She’d chosen the coat for the charity gala because the invitation had asked for “bold elegance,” but now, as she fastened the last button, a cold knot settled in her stomach. *Bold elegance* had turned into a dare to be seen.  

The door cracked open.  

“Lina!” Mira’s voice cut through the silence, sharp as a whip. “The press is here. They want a sound bite before you go on.”  

Lina’s stomach dropped. A sound bite—no speech, no poetry—just thirty seconds that would be clipped, curated, and blasted to anyone who cared enough to click. She’d already given one yesterday; the clip had been reduced to a single line about “empowering women through fashion.” She’d wanted to vomit.  “Tell them I’m… uh… giving a private reading,” she said, hoping the words would sound smooth enough to buy her time.  Mira arched an eyebrow. “A private reading? Lina, it’s a charity gala. The whole point is visibility.”  

“I know that,” Lina snapped, sharper than she intended. She didn’t need Mira’s judgment now; she needed a moment to decide whether to fight or fold.  

The LEDs on the coat flickered again, and she caught her reflection in the dark window. Somewhere down the hall the auction was already humming—programs being handed out, donors murmuring about silk scarves and wine tastings.  

She’d come here to read a poem, to step away from the runway for a breath and let her words be the only thing people noticed. But the media had other plans, and she could feel the pressure building like a tightening corset.  

The door opened a second time, and she saw him.  

Eli stood in the doorway, lighting kit in one hand, clipboard in the other. He looked different here—less the anonymous tech from the concert hall, more a man who belonged in the background. His black T‑shirt was tucked into his jeans, his hair mussed from running cables, but his eyes were the same: observant, quiet, always watching.  

“Lina,” he said, and something in his voice made her heart skip—not a greeting, exactly, but a kind of recognition.  

She wasn’t sure how to read it.  “Mira said you might need help with the sound check,” he continued, stepping inside. His gaze swept over her—coat, makeup, the careful smoothing of her dress—then flicked to the hallway mic stand. “The mic’s out there. You’ll want to test it before the program starts.”  

She nodded, but her attention was already on the way he moved—how he adjusted the lighting rig without a second thought, how he glanced at the seating chart as if already mapping the room’s acoustics. He was all technical precision and unspoken confidence, and it made her skin prickle.  

“I’ll… test it now,” she said, forcing a smile.  

As she moved toward the door, he muttered under his breath, “…echoes in the balcony, need to dampen the high end for spoken word.”  

She paused, hand on the doorframe. “You know about the reading?”  

His head tilted just slightly. “The program lists it—ten minutes before the auction. I’m supposed to handle the sound for it.”  

“Oh. Right. The program.” She frowned, realizing she’d forgotten to mention the event. “I didn’t think anyone would actually be listening.”  

Eli’s expression softened, just a fraction. “People always listen when someone reads poetry. It’s just a question of whether they’re ready to hear it.”  

The words lingered between them, and Lina felt a flutter of something—like the opening line of a sentence she wasn’t sure how to finish.  

Mira appeared in the doorway, clapping her hands together. “Lina, the donors are getting restless. You need to go on stage in five.”  “Five minutes,” Lina said, more firmly than she felt.  

As Mira ushered her toward the hallway, Lina caught one last look at Eli. He was already turning back to his rig, but she saw him straighten his posture, as if preparing for something.  *Listening more than hearing*, he’d said. What had that even meant?  

She reached the end of the corridor and stopped in front of the microphone. The sound check had already been done, but she pressed the button anyway, just to feel the weight of the job in her hands.  

“Testing, testing,” she whispered.  

The voice that came back was clear, warm, intimate.  

“Lina,” she said aloud, and heard her own name echo off the walls.  

In that moment she made a decision—not about the coat, not about the press, not even about the poem. It was a decision about the story she was telling herself.  

She would not retreat behind her stage persona tonight. Not when the lights were on and the audience was waiting.  

Tonight, she would let her words be the only thing people noticed.  

Even if it cost her everything.
