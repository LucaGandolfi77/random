**Edited Version – Close Third‑Person (Eli’s Perspective)**  

*The rehearsal room sits two stories above the din of midtown, a loft stripped to exposed brick and ductwork, still echoing the building’s warehouse past. A single spotlight hangs above a makeshift stage, its circular beam a question‑mark on the scuffed hardwood. Lina stands at the edge of that circle, bare feet pressed into the wood as she runs a hand along the microphone stand. The scar on her left cheek tingles—an old childhood injury she’s never managed to hide under a stray stubble.*

*I watch her from the doorway, hands shoved deep in the pockets of my faded jeans, feeling the weight of the mixing board I hauled up the narrow stairs. She asked me to help with the sound, but as I step deeper into the room I sense she wants something else.*

> “Eli,” she says without turning, her voice catching on a syllable in a way that makes my stomach drop, “if you could just… make sure there’s no echo on the ‘verse about silk.’ I want every word to land like a needle on skin.”  *I nod—though she can’t see me—and cross to the console that dominates one wall. The board looks like a relic from another decade: rows of knobs and sliders that feel familiar even now, long after my days touring with indie bands. My fingers find their places automatically, smoothing the high frequencies so her voice won’t shatter against the brick walls.*

*She begins to move—not the practiced glide of a runway walk, but something looser, more exploratory. She paces the circle, each step a punctuation mark in the empty space. When she stops she lifts the mic as if it were a relic from an attic and begins to speak.*

> *“The seamstress holds the storm—”*  

*Her voice wavers at first, then steadies, each phrase falling into the room like a stone into still water. The reverberation catches my attention—not the unwanted echo she fears, but something subtler: the way her breath aligns with the rhythm of the words. I watch the rise and fall of her chest, unconsciously matching it with the fader movements under my palm. In that moment sound becomes tactile, something I can feel in my fingertips rather than just hear.*

*She is halfway through the second stanza when she glances at me. “How does it sound?”*  

*“It sounds like you’re trying to stitch the air together,”* I say, surprised by how intimate the metaphor feels. I turn the low‑midrange knob, letting a warm layer of bass creep in, as if wrapping her voice in a blanket I didn’t know I wanted to give.*

*She smiles—small, genuine—and the space between us seems to narrow. I catalogue the details: the way her hair catches the light, the faint tremor that betrays nerves, the way her shoulders relax once she realizes the sound is exactly what she wants. It feels as if she’s been waiting for someone to listen, to hear the silences between the words as much as the words themselves.*

> “I want to try something,” she says, lowering the mic. She walks over to where I stand, her bare feet silent on the wood. “There’s this bridge… about being seen.”  

*I set the headphones aside, the click echoing in the sudden quiet. “Okay.”*  

*She takes a breath and this time her voice doesn’t tremble.*  

> *“I have lived in glass cases, / my heart a mannequin dressed in borrowed skin. / But you— / you hear the seamstress in me.”*  

*Each line lands with deliberate grace, and the words settle in my chest like a hand pressing against my sternum. When she finishes she looks at me with an expression I can’t name—part curiosity, part something else that quickens my pulse. I want to tell her I’ve been hearing the seamstress for weeks, that every time I see her in the wings I notice how she folds paper like a secret, how she tucks a strand of hair behind her ear before any light catches her.*

*Instead I say,*  

> “You’re not borrowing skin anymore.”  

*She laughs—a sound of relief—and steps back toward the circle. “Maybe not. Maybe I’m finally wearing my own.”*  

*The loft door bursts open and a woman in a sharply tailored blazer strides in, tablet clutched like a weapon. Mira.*

> “Lina,” she calls, eyes scanning the room until they land on Lina’s profile, “we need you at the studio in fifteen. The fittings start at three, and the photographer wants test shots before the show.”  

*Lina’s shoulders tense, and for a heartbeat she looks at me as if apologizing. Then she forces a smile.*  

> “Give me a minute.”  

*Mira checks her watch. “You’ve got five. I’ve already cleared your afternoon.”*  

*I watch the exchange, feeling the familiar tug between my professional instinct to step back and my desire to stay exactly where I am. The console looks at me— I missed the deadline my firm set for the gala tech run. A text from my manager probably sits unread in my inbox, but leaving feels like abandoning something precious.*

> “What’s the rush?” I ask, sharper than I intended.  

*Mira’s eyebrows rise. “This is a major production, Eli. Lina’s one of our biggest draws tonight. We can’t have her disappearing for improv poetry sessions.”*  

*Lina turns fully now, her expression caught between gratitude and apology. “It won’t take long,” she says, her voice carrying a note of finality that makes me wonder if this will be the last time I hear her rehearse in such an intimate space.*  *I glance at the headphones still draped around my neck, then back at Lina. “Tell me what’s next,” I whisper, so only she can hear.*  

*She steps closer, close enough that I can see the gold flecks in her eyes, and whispers,*  

> “I’m thinking about letting the audience see the seamstress instead of the model.”  

*My breath catches. In that instant I understand what she’s asking—not just for sound, but for permission to be vulnerable in front of a crowd that will never truly see her.*  

*“But that means staying later,”* I say, the practical part of my brain protesting even as my heart agrees.*  

*She nods, and for the first time since we met she doesn’t retreat behind the practiced composure that usually shields her. “I know. And I don’t want to do it alone.”*  

*The choice settles between us like a chord held too long. I could leave, meet my deadline, and keep everything safe and compartmentalized. Or I could stay, risk my job, and help her step into a version of herself that might finally feel authentic.*  

*Mira clears her throat, impatient. “Lina.”*  

*Lina’s hand finds my wrist for a split second, grounding me. “I’ll figure something out,” she murmurs, more to herself than to anyone else.*  

*She moves toward the door but pauses at the threshold.*  

> “Eli?”  

> “Yeah?”  

> “Thank you for listening.”  

*She steps out, and her voice lingers in the empty space. I turn back to the console, fingers hovering over the faders as if deciding which notes of my own life I’m willing to amplify. The rehearsal room feels larger now—full of possibility and the weight of a decision I may never have to make again.*  

*Outside, the city hums with its usual indifference, but inside those brick walls something fragile and beautiful has shifted—and I’m determined to help it find its rhythm.*
