**CHAPTER 7 – “TheTrain‑Car Moment” (Re‑Edited)**  

*POV: Close third‑person (Lina’s perspective)*  

The 11:47 out of Midtown moved like a joke that no one ever laughs at: sluggish in the tunnels, a blur of sodium light and rain on the overpass. Lina kept her sketchpad open on her knees, her coat buttoned to the throat despite the winter draft that always whispered through the door seals each time the train braked. Overhead, one fluorescent tube flickered in perfect time with the rails, and she began to count the gaps—just as she used to count stitches in her grandmother’s kitchen, elbow‑deep in a jar of something that smelled like eggs and patience.  

She hadn’t meant to take this train. The 11:47 was the one Eli rode, the corridor he’d mentioned in his half‑Czech note, the one whose fold had softened from handling so many times. She’d told herself she was going home. Her apartment was twelve blocks south, well within walking distance, but walking would mean passing the café on 23rd where the mayo jar still sat on the windowsill like a patient confession, and she still wasn’t ready for that.  

The carriage was empty except for a man asleep in the far seat and, two rows back on the opposite side, Eli. He didn’t look up when she sat. A technical manual lay open across his thighs—something about acoustics, its cover dense with diagrams she couldn’t decipher. His thumb kept turning the same page corner back and forth, as if trying to loosen a knot of information. The jacket he wore was the same faded black as the shirt he’d worn at the rehearsal loft, and she noticed, with a specificity that embarrassed her, the tiny hole near the collar he kept meaning to mend. It was a clean, round edge, like a mouth that had closed around something and let go.  

She’d misread him so many times now it had taken on a texture of its own—a scar on the inside of her understanding. At the concert hall she’d taken his silence for boredom; at the set list she’d taken his stillness for indifference; each assumption had been wrong, and each time she’d walked away with a knot she couldn’t name.  

The train swayed, and the empty seat across from him seemed to exhale. She could sit there. It would look like coincidence.  

She sat.  

The distance between them was maybe three feet, maybe three inches—it shifted whenever the carriage lurched or the fluorescent tube buzzed. Eli turned a page. She pulled the sketchpad closer and wrote a line she’d been carrying for days, the one that kept slipping into her margins like a weed cracking through pavement:  

*Fleeting moments on moving rails—*  

She stopped. The line was true, but it was also a door left ajar, and she wasn’t sure she wanted him to see through it.  

The train rattled into a tunnel and the lights died. For a few seconds the only illumination was the blue glow of Eli’s phone screen, held just high enough for him to read. In that half‑dark she watched his profile—the sharp line of his jaw, the way his breathing slowed when the rhythm changed, as if the train’s lurch were a metronome he was learning to follow.  

“I know you don’t read those for fun,” she said.  

He looked up. The phone’s cold light caught his eyes, and the surprise that flickered there settled into something quieter, like a note that has finally been released.  “You don’t either,” he replied. “You just pretend the sketchpad is a diary.”  

“It is a diary. Just one that’s allergic to the word *feelings*.”  

He almost smiled. The corner of his mouth twitched, then the lights snapped back on and the moment collapsed back into ordinary reality: two people on a train, one reading, one writing, the silence between them shaped like a question neither had phrased aloud.  She kept her eyes on the page. “I wrote something about rails. Moving. Fleeting. The usual stuff.”  

“I’d like to hear it.”  

“I’d like to *not* hear it. I’d like to adjust the acoustics of this carriage so it sounds more like a studio.”  

He laughed—a short, real thing, not the polite exhale she’d catalogued at the gala. “That’s literally what I do for a living.”  “And I literally pretend I don’t care what you think.”  

The words landed heavier than she’d intended, as if a seam had split beneath them. Eli set the manual down, not leaning forward, but placing it on the seat beside him—a small, deliberate act of making space.  

She read the stanza. Her voice was lower than she intended, almost conversational, as if the poem were a secret she was sharing with a single listener rather than performing before a room.  

> *Fleeting moments on moving rails,*  
> *the window fogged with someone else's breath—*  
> *I press my forehead to the glass*  
> *and for a second I'm not the dress*  
> *not the brand, not the body*  
> *just a face watching another face*  
> *dissolve into the dark.*  

The train hummed. A beat passed. Two.  

“Mannequin,” she whispered, and immediately winced—she hadn’t meant to crack the metaphor open and expose the wire inside.  

“That’s what I told myself,” she continued, “when I started modeling at sixteen. I’m a mannequin. Something you dress up, stand in a window, and the window doesn’t care if I’m cold.”  

Eli was quiet. Not the kind of silence she had misread before—the kind that signalled an exit. This was a different quiet, the kind that listens the way a room does when you turn off the lights and let the darkness take shape.  

“I used to think silence meant someone was gone,” she said. “From the concert hall. From the set list. From the note under the jar. I thought if you weren’t talking, you were already leaving.”  

“And now?”  

She looked at him. The flickering light rendered his face in half‑tones; she thought of the sketchpad again—how she always drew people in loose pencil first, uncertain lines, before committing to ink. She hadn’t committed yet. She wasn’t sure she would.  

“Now I think you were just listening,” she said. “And I think that’s worse.”  

He reached into his jacket pocket and, absurdly, produced a tiny glass jar of homemade mayo. He set it on the narrow folding tray table between them as if it were a sacrament.  

“I made this this morning,” he said. “Too much, as usual.”  

The fluorescent light turned the pale yellow almost golden, and she could smell it—sharp, bright, the way her grandmother’s kitchen had smelled on the mornings she’d helped with breakfast, standing on a stool to reach the counter, being told *pomalu, pomalu* while the eggs were cracked one‑handed into the jar.  

“Thank you,” she said, meaning the jar, the train, the fact that he’d sat three feet away and listened.  

The automatic announcement crackled through the speakers: a service delay at the Queensboro switch. The carriage lurched. Eli rose to grip the overhead bar, and Lina rose too—physics didn’t negotiate, and suddenly the three feet of space between them widened into a chasm of standing room. The intimate bubble they’d built collapsed like a tent in wind.  

The delay lasted four minutes. They stood side by side in the aisle, close enough that she could feel the warmth of his sleeve against hers, and said nothing. The fluorescent tube flickered. The man in the far seat hadn’t moved. Rain against the window sounded like static, and for once she didn’t want to smooth it out.  

When the train lurched back into motion, Eli sat first. She hesitated.  

“I’ll get off at your stop,” she said.  

“You live past my stop.”  

“I know.”  

“Then why are you on this train?”  

She pulled the sketchpad from her bag and held it against her chest, shielding the half‑finished poem from the draft. A stray smudge at the edge revealed the word *mannequin* she had written moments before.  

“Because the 11:47 has a good rhythm,” she replied, “and because I wanted to know if you’d sit across from me again.”  

He stared at her for a long moment. Then he opened the technical manual to a dog‑eared page and let his eyes linger on her. She sat, and the train carried them forward through the dark. The mayo jar sat between them on the tray table, untouched, still shining.
