**CHAPTER 9 –“The Final Runway”**  
*POV: Close third‑person (Lina’s perspective)*  

The runway stretched before her like a spine of light, its edge blurred by the fog of stage‑smoke and the nervous energy of a hundred waiting breaths. Above, the tent’s silk ceiling shimmered with fiber‑optic stars that flickered in time with the bassline pulsing through the sound system—an ambient hum that blended Eli’s acoustic recordings with the distant wail of subway trains rolling somewhere beneath the city.

Each garment hung along the catwalk like a stanza waiting to be spoken:  

* a coat lined with circuitry that whispered poetry when touched,  
* a dress printed with lines about storms and seams,  
* a jumpsuit embroidered with the phrase **“I am the seam that holds the crisis.”**  

She started walking, heels clicking against the polished wood in rhythm with the music, each step a punctuation mark in a sentence she’d been drafting since childhood. The audience was a ripple of flashbulbs and whispered commentary, but she kept her eyes fixed ahead, where two figures stood apart from the sea of faces.

**Eli.**  

He was seated in the front row, cross‑legged on the floor, his black T‑shirt a shade more worn at the collar than she remembered. In his hand was a postcard‑sized card—cream‑colored, thick stock—the same paper that had carried the note he’d slipped into her kitchen drawer. Her chest tightened.

*Your words are my favorite fabric.*  

The message was scrawled in his looping hand, slightly smudged where his thumb had pressed too hard. She’d seen that script before—on the gala set‑list, on the envelope of his half‑Czech apology. Every mark was deliberate, every word a small rebellion against the silence he usually wore like a second skin.

She was halfway down the runway when doubt crashed over her. The lights were brighter than she expected, the silence between clips louder than any applause. Somewhere in the wings, Mira’s voice cut through the static of her thoughts:  

*“Lina, you’re next. The producers are waiting.”*  

But this wasn’t just another show. This was her capsule collection—a love letter to the city, to the seamstress who’d come before her, to the storm she carried in her bones. She could feel the weight of it pressing against her ribs, the same way the storm pressed against the seams of her dresses.

*Choose love over legacy,* she whispered to herself, the words barely audible above the hum of the lights.

A jagged vibration in her pocket made her stumble half a step. The screen flickered to life, and before she could pull the phone out, a text blazed across it:

**Priya:** *Lina, the board is asking for a statement. They want to know if you’re going to read the poem or stick to the script. Please advise.*  Her stomach dropped. The board. The investors. The people who had built her empire on the promise of bold elegance and nothing more. They didn’t want vulnerability; they wanted a product. A brand. A sound bite.

She lifted her gaze to Eli. He was watching her now, his expression a question. *Are you going to do this?*

The rest of the runway stretched empty, the garments waiting to be worn, the poetry waiting to be spoken. Behind her, the wings buzzed with last‑minute adjustments—handlers whispering about contracts, publicists rehearsing talking points, the inevitable machine of commerce grinding toward its next gear.

She could turn back. She could walk off the runway and let the evening dissolve into the kind of safe, polished performance they all expected. She could tuck the poem back into her notebook, let the board breathe easy.

Or she could step into the light and let the words tear through the silence like a seamstress’s needle through stubborn fabric.

Her heel struck the floor once, twice, then she began to move—not the practiced stride of a model, but the uneven, deliberate pace of someone choosing each step. The music faded as she reached the center, where the lights dimmed to a single spotlight.

“Excuse me,” she said, her voice carrying an edge that made the audience still. “I’m Lina.”

The tent fell silent.

She lifted the mic, fingers trembling only slightly, and began to speak.

> *“Silk and static, a pulse beneath the thread, / I am the seam that holds the storm, the breath before the spread.”*  

Each line landed like a hammer on an anvil, shaping something new from the raw material of her fear. She spoke of the seamstress who had come before her, of the storm she carried in her chest, of the moment when the choice between safety and truth became unbearable.

> *“Love is not a legacy, it is a seam / that holds the crisis, the breath, the spread.”*  

When she finished, silence stretched like a rubber band pulled to its limit. Then, from the front row, Eli stood and clapped—not the polite, obligatory applause of the crowd, but the genuine, thunderous praise of someone who had heard every word.

The board’s lawyers were already mobilizing, but she didn’t care. She was no longer the girl who had hidden her poems in coat pockets or her mayo obsession in secret drawers. She was the seamstress now, stitching herself into the fabric of her own story, one bold, unapologetic seam at a time.

As she walked off the runway, Eli’s card crinkled in her fist, and she knew that whatever came next—whatever contracts were signed or torn up, whatever brands were built or broken—this was the thread that would hold it all together.

The storm, she realized, had finally found its seam.
