## Chapter 10 – “Bleeding Horizons” (Edited)

### The sky over Oryntha  
*The sky over Oryntha was the colour of a bruise that had stopped hurting but hadn’t healed.*  

> Mara noticed this the way she noticed most things now—by the weight it pressed against her sternum, slow and cumulative, until she couldn’t separate the ache of the view from the ache of standing.

---

She stood on the eastern ridge with her hands empty.  
The medical kit was gone. She’d left it in the courtyard last night, beside the lantern, beside the map they’d never finished folding. The citadel sat below them like a jaw half‑open, its limestone ribs catching the bruised light and throwing it back in shades of ash.

*You can’t fold a map that’s been rained on*, her sister once said. Lila had been seven, Mara nine, and the map had been their father’s—one of the old ones, before the provinces were redrawn, before the “smiling provinces under the same sun.” Lila had tried to flatten it with her palms; it kept springing back, creased and soft, the ink bleeding into itself like watercolor left in the rain.

Mara pressed her tongue to the roof of her mouth and tasted copper.  

---

### EliEli had rehearsed this. He’d stood in the dark stairwell of the keep and run through every word, every posture, every possible answer to the questions he already knew would come:

*Why did you meet her? What did you promise? Where do you think you’re going?*

The answers were simple. They were also impossible.

He looked at the back of Mara’s neck—short dark hair, the sweat‑stain at her collar that she’d never bothered to clean—and thought: *I don’t know where I’m going. I only know I won’t go without you.*

The stone beneath his feet was cold. His left forearm ached, the shrapnel scar pulling tight when he flexed. He’d kept the rosemary sprig in his breast pocket for eleven days. It had gone brittle and faintly sweet, and he pressed his thumb against it now, the way you press a bruise to see if it still hurts.  

It did.

---

### Mara

“You don’t have to say anything.”

Eli’s voice was quiet—close enough that the words landed on the back of her neck, warm as breath.

She turned. His face was gaunt, hollowed by weeks of sleepless watches, but his eyes were steady—*the steadiness she’d first mistaken for calm and now understood was the opposite. He was terrified. He was standing here terrified, and he was choosing to.*

The leaders would speak first. Arlen would invoke duty. The Dominion captain would invoke order. Between them they would construct a cage of protocol and patriotism and ask Mara and Eli to step inside it voluntarily, to call it honour.

Mara had worn that cage before. She’d worn it to her graduation, to her first field deployment, to every morning she woke in the Federation barracks and says *yes* to a uniform that fits her body but never fits her.

She touched the empty space where the medical kit should have been.

---

### Eli

The meeting lasted less than an hour. Eli remembers it because he counted his breaths—the way he’d been taught to count breaths during sutures, during triage, during anything where his hands need to be more certain than his heart. Seventy‑two breaths. Mara had stood with her arms at her sides and her jaw set, not looking at either commander.

Then she spoke.

> “I kept the kit because it was his,” she’d said. “I kept it because if I gave it back, I’d have to say the work was over. And it isn’t.”

Arlen’s jaw tightened. The Dominion captain shifted his weight. Eli looked at Mara and saw something he’d never seen before—not defiance, not courage, but *arrival*: the look of someone who had finally stopped walking toward a door and turned around.

---

### Mara

Afterward, they walked.

Not together—not yet. Mara went east, toward the Dominion perimeter, because she’d made her choice and the direction of it no longer mattered. Eli went west, toward the Federation line, for the same reason. They moved parallel, the citadel between them, and the space was filled with the hum of engines and the low murmur of soldiers who didn’t know that two people had just made a decision the way you make a wound—cleanly, and with full knowledge of what it would cost.

Halfway to her line, Mara stopped.

She didn’t turn around. She listened.

Behind her, the scrape of a boot on stone.

---

### Eli

He took the long way. He went through the courtyard, past the broken bench, past the flagstones swept by desperate hands, past the lantern they’d left burning even though neither of them was supposed to leave fires unattended. The flame was dead now, but the wick still smelled of tallow and coffee and something underneath both—something that was just *them*.

He stopped beside her. Not touching. The citadel wall between them was low enough to see over.

Below, the courtyard. Above, the bruised sky.

“I thought you’d go east,” he said.

“So did I,” she said.

They stood there. The wind moved through the arches and stirred ash into a thin curtain that passed between them and the courtyard and the sky. Somewhere below, a radio crackled—a false alarm, or a trap, or a voice calling a name that didn’t belong to anyone alive.

Eli reached into his breast pocket. The rosemary was so dry it crumbled when he touched it. He let the fragments fall. They drifted between them, tiny and pale, and landed on the wall.

“Your hand,” he said.

She looked down. He was holding out his—open, empty, the scar along his forearm catching the light. An offering with nothing in it. Or everything.

She took it.

---

### Mara

His fingers were cold. Hers were colder. The contact was nothing—skin on skin, nothing clinical, nothing that could be logged or reported or used as evidence of fraternization. And yet her chest ached with a specificity that frightened her, the way a fracture aches, the way you know it’s broken because the pain is *located*, it has an address.

*Vulnerability as courage.* She’d written that in her head once, during a quiet moment in the keep, when she’d been pretending to read a field manual and actually watching Eli’s hands move over a wound—steady, sure, the way his father’s maps must have been steady.

She didn’t let go.

“I don’t know where we go,” she said.

“I know.”

“We could die.”

“I know that too.”

The wind shifted. Ash settled on their joined hands, light as snow, warm as nothing.

---

### Eli

He thought of his father’s maps. The ones he’d found after the fire—the provinces smiling under the same sun, borders drawn in ink that had bled into neighbouring colours until the whole thing was soft and indistinguishable, beautiful in the way that things are beautiful when they stop pretending to be anything other than what they are.

Mara’s thumb moved across his knuckle. Once. Twice.

*Timing and readiness.* He’d spent his whole life waiting for the right moment—waiting for the war to end, waiting for grief to loosen, waiting for a version of himself that deserved to stand beside someone without apologising for the standing. He’d been wrong. The right moment isn’t a door opening. It’s two people choosing, in the middle of ruin, to stay.

“I’m not going back to either line,” he said.

Mara’s grip tightened. Not in fear. In *recognition*.

“Neither am I.”

---

### Mara

They turned at the same time—toward the southern passage, the one that led out of the citadel and down into the valley where the roads had been cratered but not yet erased. The bruised sky stretched above them, vast and indifferent, the colour of something that had stopped hurting.

Behind them, faintly, the sound of engines. Orders being given. Maps being consulted. The machinery of two nations grinding forward, erasing each other, redrawing the borders with iron and ash.

Ahead: the passage. The valley. Whatever was there.

Eli’s hand was still in hers. The rosemary fragments lay on the wall behind them, pale as small promises, catching the last of the light.

“Say it,” Eli murmured.

She looked at him. The bruise‑sky. The ash. The ruin. The man whose father believed borders were arbitrary and whose hands could hold a wound closed long enough for it to begin healing.

“I’m not letting go,” she said.

It wasn’t the vow she’d imagined. It was smaller, and truer, and it sat in her mouth the way a stone sits in a pocket—not heavy, exactly, but *present*, constant, something she’d carry through whatever came next.

Eli’s breath caught. Something shifted behind his eyes—the mask, the steadiness, the performance of composure—all of it dropped, and what was underneath was just a man in love with a woman who had finally, after everything, chosen the same door he was walking through.

They stepped into the passage. The citadel groaned behind them, the way it always groans, the way old things groan when they let go.

The light ahead was thin and grey and real.

They walked into it.
