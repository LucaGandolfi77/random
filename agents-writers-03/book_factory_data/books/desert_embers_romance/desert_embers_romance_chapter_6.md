### **Mara’s View – Revised**

The night settled over the citadel like a curtain of ash; only the distant, low‑drone of artillery broke the silence. Mara slipped through the cracked archway to the abandoned watchtower, the same place she’d hidden a spare med‑kit when the rain turned the hallway into a river of mud. The stone stairwell groaned under her weight, each step a reminder that the ruins remember every footfall.

At the top she found a rusted metal door ajar. Inside, the space was a hollowed‑out alcove, its ceiling low enough that the air seemed to press against the skin. A thin wind slipped through broken shutters, scattering the faint scent of oil and old blood.

Eli stood by a flickering candle, shoulders hunched over a battered journal. The amber glow caught the edge of his silhouette, turning the faint lines on his face into something almost tender. He looked up as the door creaked; his eyes widened just enough for Mara to notice the tremor in his breath.

“Thought you’d vanished with the dawn,” he whispered, half‑joking, half‑serious. The words landed like a stone in still water; ripples of old quarrels—over supplies, accusations from command—swayed across Mara’s composure.

She set the lantern on a low table where a half‑unrolled map of the western sector lay smudged by rain. “I was waiting for the signal to be clear,” she replied, voice barely above the rustle of paper. “And for you to stop trying to outrun the sound of your own heartbeat.”

Eli managed a raw laugh. “You always know when I’m moving too fast,” he said, brushing the edge of the journal. “I’m not trying to outrun anything. I’m trying to keep it from moving… too fast for me to handle.”

Mara stepped closer. The candlelight caught a dark smudge of soot on his forearm—shrapnel from a skirmish weeks earlier. She remembered the scar, a silent testament to everything he never said. Now, hovering her hand an inch from his wrist, she felt the heat of his skin against the stone’s chill.

She reached out, not to touch his wound, but to press a single dried sprig of rosemary—something she carried for luck—against his palm. “Take it,” she said, thick with unsaid promises. “It’s the only thing I have that isn’t a weapon.”

Eli’s eyes widened; for a heartbeat he seemed ready to pull away, then relaxed as the rosemary stuck to his skin like a secret. “You really think this will keep us… safe?” he asked, disbelief threading his voice.

Mara smiled—a pause, not a smile—a breath held between two heartbeats. “I think it’s the only thing that can keep us from falling into the same mistake again.” She let the words linger; the candle flame danced, casting shadows that moved like whispered doubts across the walls.

In that suspended moment the world beyond the ruins fell away. The only reality was the fragile exchange: a sprig of rosemary, a hand on a scar, a breath held in common. The risk of discovery throbbed at the edge of her thoughts, yet for now, in this fissure of night, vulnerability felt like an act of bravery, not surrender.

### **Eli’s View – Revised**

The night outside was a low‑frequency hum of distant mortars, a rhythm that had become an unwanted lullaby for the soldiers who still dared to sleep. Eli crouched near a fire‑pit, his back pressed against a fallen column that still echoed an old battle. The cold had seeped into his bones, but the ember glow warmed his fingertips.

When the door panel creaked, his heart slammed against his ribs—a drumbeat of fear and anticipation. He watched Mara enter; the lantern’s light caught the glint of iron on her shoulder—her captain’s badge, the insignia she pretended not to wear. The sight of him—so raw, so exposed—made his skin prickle.

“Thought you’d vanished with the dawn,” she said again, softer now, as if testing a hypothesis. Eli swallowed; there was weight behind those words, the weight of every order that had tried to shackle him and every accusation that tried to silence his voice.

He opened his journal, pages filled with cramped medical notes and half‑drawn diagrams of field hospitals. “You always know when I’m moving too fast,” he repeated, voice a little hoarse. “I’m not trying to outrun anything. I’m trying to keep it from moving too fast for me to handle.” The words fell out before he could temper them—raw, unguarded.

When Mara’s hand hovered over his scarred forearm, the old wound flared—not the physical one, but the scar of years spent keeping others at a distance. He felt the heat of her palm against the chill of stone, like a sudden gust through a cracked window. “You really think this will keep us… safe?” he asked, a mix of cynicism and a yearning that felt absurd amid war.

Mara’s smile—more a pause than a smile—hung in the air. “I think it’s the only thing that can keep us from falling into the same mistake again,” she whispered. In that instant his mind jumped to his family: his mother tucking him into a thin blanket and whispering about a world beyond the capital’s walls; his sister’s fierce voice telling him that bravery isn’t the absence of fear but the willingness to move forward despite it. He remembered her words and felt a sudden, unbidden tenderness.

“Do you ever wonder why we do this?” he asked, the question slipping out as casually as a breath. “All this… hiding, pretending we’re just medics, just… survivors?”

Mara’s eyes flicked to the sprig of rosemary perched on her palm, and she let it fall lightly onto his skin. It brushed the scar that still pulsed faintly, as if reminding him that some things can never be erased. “All the time,” she answered, voice barely a murmur. “Maybe that’s why we keep doing it—because the alternative is giving up on the hope that there’s a place for us that isn’t built on ash.”

Eli felt the weight of her words settle on his shoulders—comforting and terrifying. He realized that the fear he carried—the fear that love was a liability—was the very thing that made him cling to the present. He brushed the rosemary with his fingertips, feeling the tiny leaves press into his skin, an intimate gesture that said more than any oath.

“Stay with me a little longer,” he whispered, not as a command but as an invitation. “Even if the night is thick with static and the world thinks we’re just shadows.”

The candle sputtered, casting a brief, trembling halo over their faces. In that flicker they understood the misreading that had haunted them: the retreat they thought was a withdrawal was, in fact, a silent offering—each willing to stay, to risk, to be seen. The static beyond the ruined walls crackled, not as a threat, but as a reminder that the world outside was still moving, still demanding—yet in this moment, for the first time in months, neither felt the need to run.

He closed his journal, the pages rustling like a promise, and tucked it away. “Then let’s make a lullaby of the night,” he said, his voice softer, steadier. “One we can hum when the guns finally fall silent.”

Mara nodded, the rosemary still pressed against his palm, a fragile token of a future they both feared and longed for. Together they stood in the hollow silence of the watchtower, the ember’s glow reflecting in their eyes—a shared resolve that, even in a world built on ruin, love could still find a place to breathe.

*The night held its breath, and for a heartbeat longer, the war outside seemed a story told by someone else—a whisper caught in the wind.*

## 3. OPTIONAL FURTHER TWEAKS

   *“Night fell on the citadel like a curtain of ash.”* (drops “settled over”).

3. **Trim the “misreading” meta‑comment** if you want the focus to stay purely on the characters:  
   *“In that flicker they understood the secret that had haunted them…”*  

4. **If you want a more visceral ending**, replace “love could still find a place to breathe” with something like “love could finally settle, a quiet pulse in the chaos.”
