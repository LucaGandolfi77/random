**Chapter 7 – “Crossfire”**  The citadel groaned under a bruised sky, artillery thudding in the distance. Somewhere below the shattered arches a shell slammed into stone, scattering limestone and ash like the breath of a wounded beast. In that instant Mara and Eli hovered between two worlds—the shattered certainty of their commands and the trembling promise that had taken root in the fissures between them.  

---

### Mara – close third  

The first blast ripped the thin veil of dust from the western corridor, sending a hot, copper‑tinged gust into Mara’s face. Her visor fogged; she blinked away sweat and ash, then lifted it with a practiced thumb. Ahead, rag‑tag stretchers hunched like wounded animals, surrounded by jagged shards of stone.  

“Red‑team, hold!” barked Captain Arlen from the far end. His thin voice cracked under the tremor in his jaw, and Mara’s chest tightened at the sound. The captain had warned her that any “friendship” with a Dominion medic was a war crime.  

She pushed forward, the stiff leather of her boots crunching over broken limestone. Each step echoed like a metronome counting down an unseen clock. The weight of her field pack pressed against her shoulder, but a sharper pressure pressed against the side of her ribcage—Eli’s hand, fingers laced around the strap of his own kit, steadying her as if she might stumble.  

*We’re both prisoners,* she thought, the mantra surfacing again, *and prisoners of each other’s ambitions.*  

She caught sight of a soldier on the floor, his leg mangled beyond repair. Blood pooled around his thigh, dark as the night sky that would soon swallow the citadel. Mara knelt, her gloved fingers finding the artery.  

“Hold on. I’m here.”  

Eli was already there, his gaze flicking between the wounded man and Mara’s face. He pressed a tourniquet with fluid precision, his knuckles trembling just enough for her to notice. Their eyes met for a fraction of a second—an unspoken exchange that said more than any report could.  

“Do you have the hemostatic powder?” she asked, voice low enough that only the stone walls could hear.  

He nodded, pulling a dented tin from his belt. “Got it. Let’s make this count.”  

Mara tipped the powder into the wound; the crimson spread like ink on paper. The soldier’s gasp softened to a ragged exhale. In that breathless pause she felt the heat of Eli’s breath ghost her ear.  

*If we get one more chance, I’ll make it count.*  

A sharp crack split the air—a shell landing a few meters away. Debris erupted in a cloud, and the makeshift triage site erupted into chaos.  

“Move! To the inner chamber—now!” Arlen shouted, panic edging his tone.  

Mara’s mind raced. The inner chamber was a half‑collapsed hall of vaulted arches, its ceiling riddled with gaps that let the angry gray sky pour in. It was the safest spot, but dragging the wounded deeper meant slipping farther from the command post that was already hunting for any sign of a Dominion presence.  

She glanced at Eli; his eyes were wide, his mask twitching as if it might slip off entirely.  

“Eli—”  

He shook his head, fierce and quick. “We can’t leave the man.”  

Mara’s heart hammered. She could feel the static from the radio crackling in the background, a thin white‑noise pulse that matched every heartbeat.  *We’re both prisoners.*  

She swallowed the surge of fear and made a decision.  

“Help me lift him,” she said, steadier than she felt.  

Together they hoisted the soldier onto a makeshift stretcher. His weight was a physical reminder of the weight they each carried—orders, duty, fear, longing. The metal scraped the stone, sending a thin, metallic sigh through the corridor.  

They shuffled toward the inner chamber as a puff of ash brushed Mara’s cheek. She turned, half‑expecting a Dominion patrol, but only the hollow echo of their footsteps returned.  

“Eli,” she whispered, “if this… if this is the end, I need you to know—”  

He stopped, his hand hovering over the wound, eyes flickering between the dying soldier and her. The world seemed to hold its breath.  

“I know,” he said, raw and low, as if confessing to the stone itself. “And I’m sorry I never said it earlier.”  

Mara’s throat tightened, a mix of relief and grief. She wanted to reach for him, to bridge the gap war had carved between their uniforms. Instead she placed a hand on his forearm, brief but electric.  

A distant rumble grew louder—the unmistakable thud of artillery rolling across the ruined city. A shell exploded nearby, sending a plume of fire and dust into the inner chamber like a sudden sunrise.  The harsh light cast stark shadows across Eli’s face. His mask slipped just enough for her to see the scar that ran down his left cheek—a jagged line of old shrapnel that had never quite healed. It reminded her of the faint, raised scar on her own wrist, a childhood burn she had hidden beneath a bandage for years.  

She brushed the scar lightly with a gloved finger, as if to reassure herself that the wound was real, that he was here, that she was still alive.  

*We’re both prisoners,* she repeated, and this time the phrase settled like a promise rather than a sentence.  

“Red‑team, fallback to the north tower! Dominion forces have breached the eastern wall!” a shout crackled through the din.  

Eli’s eyes darted eastward. The enemy was only a few hundred meters away, but the sound of their boots sent a fresh wave of dread through her.  

“Stay,” he whispered, barely audible over the radio’s static, “we can’t leave him.”  

Mara glanced at the wounded soldier, his breath shallow, his eyes fluttering. Every command she had ever taken pressed down on her—Captain Harlan’s letter in her pocket, the ironclad expectation that she prioritize the mission over everything else.  

And she felt the softer pull of Eli’s presence—burnt coffee and rosemary, the echo of his hand on her wrist earlier that night, the promise they both had been too afraid to name.  She made a choice in a heartbeat.  “Help me push the stretcher toward the north tower,” she said, voice steadier than before. “We’ll get him to safety, and then we can—”  

Eli’s gaze flickered with urgency, then softened. “Then we’ll figure out what we are, Mara.”  

Together they pivoted the stretcher, muscles straining, stone vibrating beneath each step. The corridor narrowed, the ceiling closing overhead like a throat. Sparks of falling debris illuminated brief moments of their faces—her fierce brow, his tense jaw, the raw wound on his cheek, the blood on his hands.  

Radio static crackled again: “All units—crossfire at grid twelve. Repeat, crossfire. All units—crossfire.”  

Mara’s breath hitched. *Crossfire* had always meant tactical danger, a place where bullets sang their deadly song. Now it echoed the tangled feelings between them—two forces aimed at each other, each pulling in opposite directions, yet somehow finding a point where they could exist together, however briefly.  They reached the north tower, its walls scarred by recent shelling. Inside, a small alcove offered a thin pocket of relative safety. Mara set the stretcher down; the injured man’s weight finally eased from her shoulders.  

Eli knelt, applying a pressure bandage with practiced hands. He glanced up at Mara, eyes dark with exhaustion and something else—hope, perhaps.  

“Do you think we’ll make it out of this whole thing?” he asked, voice a hoarse whisper.  

Mara exhaled, the sound catching in her throat. “I don’t know,” she admitted. “But I know that if I’m going to keep fighting… I need to keep fighting with you.”  

He looked away, mask catching the low light, and for a heartbeat she thought she saw a faint smile.  

“Then we’ll keep moving,” he said, finally meeting her gaze. “One step at a time. As long as we… stay together, we can cross any fire.”  

The artillery continued to roar, a relentless tide threatening to wash them away. Yet in the cramped darkness of the north tower, amid dust, blood, and the lingering scent of coffee, Mara felt something steady blossom—a fragile sanctuary built not of stone, but of whispered promises and shared breaths.  

She reached for Eli’s hand, their fingers interlacing with a tentative, deliberate squeeze. The contact sparked a current that ran through both of them, a silent oath that neither the mandates of their commanders nor the echoing blasts could easily erase.  

*We’re both prisoners,* she thought again, but now the words felt less like a sentence and more like a key.  

The radio’s static faded into the background, overtaken by the low thrum of the citadel’s heart—still beating, still broken, still hopeful.

*Crossfire was never just about bullets. It was about the moments when two wounded souls, caught in the same storm, chose to lean into each other instead of away.*
