**Chapter 10 – The Gallery of Judgment**  

The iron latch shivered behind me, exhaling a breath that seemed to die the moment it left the hinges. The air grew heavy, as though the very walls had drawn in my presence. Oil‑paint and the faint, sweet rot of wilted roses clung to the polished oak floor, weaving a scent that was both reverent and cloying.

A narrow slit in the ceiling poured amber light onto rows of portraits that lined the gallery like a tribunal of silent judges. Each canvas was a monument to the Vales, rendered in the sober chiaroscuro of a bygone age. Yet the longer I stared, the more the painted eyes narrowed, measuring the weight of my thoughts.

The first portrait—a stern patriarch in a black coat—had eyes that were once sharp, now softened to slits of unsettling, liquid darkness. As I stepped forward, a faint tremor ran through the canvas; the painted pupil contracted, then dilated, as if the man within were drawing a breath.

The house seemed to breathe with me. Its pulse was a low, resonant thrum that echoed through the timber of the beams above, and the floorboards creaked beneath my boots—each step a soft sigh that reverberated through the vaulted space. Candlelight flickered, casting elongated shadows that stretched and recoiled like living things.

In the periphery, the portraits began to shift—not with the garish swing of a jump‑scare, but in a slow, inexorable creep that made my skin prickle. The younger woman in a tattered white gown turned her head just enough to catch my eye, her gaze lingering on the crescent scar that marked my wrist. The scar, once merely a birthmark, now pulsed faintly with a phosphorescent hue.

My hand, still trembling from the chill that had seeped into my veins in the chapel, brushed the edge of a gilded frame. The wood was cold, but the metal warmed under my fingertips, as though recognizing a kinship with the scar that throbbed beneath my skin. I pressed harder, and the patriarch’s painted lips softened, parting as if to speak. A whisper, barely audible over the house’s sigh, brushed my ear:

*“The fire you hoard is not yours to keep.”*  

The words were not spoken; they were felt—a cold current that slid down my spine and settled in the hollow of my throat. I realized then that the house did not merely observe; it remembered, and it fed upon the memories I, a writer, had tried to imprison in ink.

I moved deeper into the gallery, each portrait a silent witness to the Vales’ sins. The next canvas depicted a lady of delicate poise, her hand resting upon a black‑inked book. The ink seemed to seep from my own veins. As I approached, the book’s cover cracked, and a thin ribbon of amber light spilled out, curling around my wrist and tracing the crescent scar as if it were a sigil being etched upon my flesh. With each pulse my skin grew paler, taking on a waxen sheen—as if my essence were being siphoned into the painted world.

A sudden, sharp intake of breath startled me; the patriarch’s eyes widened, their pupils blooming into voids that reflected not the room but an abyss. In that instant I saw—not my own reflection in the glass—but a gaunt version of myself, his eyes blackened, his mouth a thin line of ash. He stood before a mirror at the far end of the gallery, its surface dark as obsidian, and in that mirror my visage was distorted: cheekbones hollowed, skin turning parchment‑white, absorbing the candlelight rather than reflecting it.

The house is a repository of memory. Each portrait is a ledger of souls that dared to create, to love, to defy. The “thing that wears their faces,” whispered in servants’ hushed tones, is not a phantom but the cumulative weight of every suppressed desire, every repressed grief, every rot that has festered within these walls. It feeds upon the fire of creation, upon the very spark that once drove me to write, and it offers membership in its eternal tableau—if I yield.

My breath came in shallow, ragged bursts. The sweet rot grew stronger, coating my tongue with wilted roses and iron. The house’s gaze intensified, as though the portraits leaned forward, their eyes now fixed upon me with predatory focus. The floor beneath my boots seemed to pulse, the wood expanding and contracting like a living heart, and the shadows between the canvases deepened, swallowing the light and tightening the room into a claustrophobic embrace.

I reached for the brass key that had once warmed my wrist, hoping it might unlock a secret—a way out. The key lay upon a small oak table at the gallery’s center, dulled by dust, yet when my fingers brushed it the metal sang a high, clear note that resonated through the room, causing the portraits to shudder. The patriarch’s painted hand twitched; his fingers curled as if to grasp at something unseen. The lady’s gown fluttered though no breeze stirred the heavy drapes.

A sudden, oppressive silence fell, broken only by the faint ticking of an unseen clock. From the walls themselves came a whisper:

*“Feed us, writer. Let your fire burn, and we shall keep you.”*  

My heart hammered against my ribs, each beat echoing the house’s pulse. I felt blackening veins spreading up my forearm, my skin turning to pallor, the marrow humming with a cold fire. The urge to write, to create, to reclaim my voice, surged within me—yet it was a flame the house threatened to consume.

I stepped toward the mirror at the far end, my reflection a grotesque echo of the man I had been. As I drew nearer the glass rippled like water, and my image began to dissolve, edges blurring into the painted figures that surrounded me. The patriarch’s eyes widened further; the void within them expanded, swallowing the light, and in that void I saw—not my own face—but the ghostly silhouette of Elara, flickering between translucence and flesh, her hand reaching out as if to pull me into the painting.

The gallery seemed to close in. The walls breathed deeper, shadows thickened, the candlelight dimmed to a cold blue flame that burned without heat. My body grew lighter, as though the very atoms of my flesh were being coaxed into the painted world, to become part of the house’s memory.

And then, as the last candle guttered and the room fell into a darkness that is not merely the absence of light but a presence of its own, I saw—within the mirror—a final, horrifying image: my own reflection, now fully merged with the patriarch’s portrait, his eyes void, his mouth a blackened slit, his hand—my hand—clutching a quill that dripped ink as black as night. The ink seeped from the canvas, spreading across the polished wood, staining every inch of the floor. As it spread, the eyes of every portrait turned to the same void, their gazes locking onto mine, sealing a pact.

The last thing I heard before darkness claimed me was the house’s mournful sigh—a sound that felt like a lover’s goodbye and a curse all at once:

*“The fire is spent, and the archive is complete.”*  

In that instant, the gallery became a tomb of memory, the walls breathing, the portraits watching, and I—Alistair Vale—stood on the threshold between creation and oblivion. My body was a haunted archive; my fire, a sacrifice to the insatiable hunger of Blackwood Manor.
