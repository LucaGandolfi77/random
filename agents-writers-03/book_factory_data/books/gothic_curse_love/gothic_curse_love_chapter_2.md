**Edited Version**

The rain had softened to a persistent, whispering drizzle by the time Alistair could no longer endure the silence of his room and ventured out.  
The house was not asleep—it was ***waiting***.  

The corridor air was cooler, scented with the same sweet, almost floral rot he had noticed earlier, now mingled with the dust of ages and the faint, clean whiff of beeswax from some distant polishing.  

He followed the pull—not a sound, really, but a resonance in his own hollow chest—down a grand, curving staircase, past the judgmental gazes of the portraits (their eyes seemed to track his descent with a new, avid curiosity), and through twin doors he had never noticed before.  

The ballroom opened before him like a cavern of shadow and moonlight. The countless, half‑drawn windows are washed with the spectral glow of a full moon battling the rain clouds. Slanted shafts of light carve the air into dusty pillars, each holding a million motes aloft. The air is still, cold, tasting of neglect and forgotten celebration.  

And then he hears it: the faint, scratching sigh of a gramophone needle in a worn groove, followed by the first tremolo of **_The Blue Danube_**—thin, reedy, as if filtered through water or from the far end of a tunnel. The melody is there, but its joy has been leached away, leaving only a haunting, skeletal echo.  

At the centre of that vast floor, where the moonlight pools brightest, **she** dances.  She is a creature of ivory and shadow. Her dress, once silk, hangs in tattered, translucent layers that catch the moon and release it again as she turns. Her movements are not of this world; they are too fluid, too precise—every arc of an arm, every tilt of a head—delivered with a grace that is pure, unadulterated anguish. It is the dance of someone remembering a body they no longer possess.  

Alistair’s breath catches. He knows her—not by face, whose skin is as pale as the moon, whose features are sharp and perfect, whose eyes are closed in rapture or torment—but by the **essence** of her: the sigh in the hall, the breath in the silent rooms, the cold spot in a warm library, the scent of night‑blooming flowers in rot.  

His boots soundless on the polished parquet.  

Her eyes open.  

Winter‑sea, vast and depthless, they find his. The waltz falters, the gramophone’s sigh hitches.  **“You,”** she breathes. Her voice is not a sound but a vibration in the air, a melody woven into silence, resonating in the hollows of his bones. **“You have come.”**  

He tries to speak, but his throat is dry. The cold that emanates from her is palpable—a physical force that presses against his skin, yet it is not unpleasant. It is a cold that promises clarity, an end to the fever of his mind.  

**“I… I heard music,”** he manages.  

**“It is the house’s memory,”** she replies, drifting closer, her feet never touching the floor. **“It remembers every step, every turn. It plays them back on nights like this, when the veil is thin.”** She stops before him, shimmering at the edges. **“I am Elara.”**  

**“Alistair Vale.”**  

**“I know.”** A faint, sad smile touches her lips. **“You are the writer. The one who sees stories in the walls.”** Her gaze dissects him, unblinking. **“You are already haunted. That is why you can see me.”**  

Logic screams that she is a phantom, a trick of the lonely mind. Yet the paralysis that has knotted his pen for months dissolves, replaced by an electric awareness. Here, at last, is something *real*.  

**“What happened to you?”** he whispers.  

She smiles, the expression fading. **“I danced. I danced for them. For the house. For the legacy.”** She raises a translucent hand, and he sees faint, silvery scars tracing her palms, as if pricked by needles. **“They promised me eternity in the music. They lied. They only promised eternity *for* the music.”** Her eyes hold his. **“The curse does not grant life; it consumes it. It takes the fire of creation and leaves only the shell.”**  

She reaches for him. Her fingertips, when they brush his cheek, feel like a snowflake that burns—a searing cold that traces a path of fire down his skin. It is the most sensual, most *real* sensation he has ever known.  

**“You feel it too,”** she murmurs. **“The hollow. The need to make something that lasts. They feed on that. They always have.”**  

**“Who?”** he asks, leaning into her touch despite the chill that begins to seep into his marrow.  

**“The house,”** she breathes. **“The family. The thing that wears their faces.”** Her form flickers; moonlight passes through her in places. **“It is in the walls. In the portraits. In the blood. It needs new fire. It needs *you*.”**  The gramophone gives a final, dying wheeze and falls silent. The sudden absence of sound is a blow; the ballroom contracts around them.  

**“No,”** Alistair says, the word torn from his throat. **“Not just to feed it. To fight it.”**  

Elara’s winter‑sea eyes widen. A flicker of something—hope, or a cruel imitation of it—passes through her. **“To fight it? You would stay? With me?”**  From the tower the clock begins to strike midnight. As the first deep *bong* reverberates through the silent house, Elara dissolves. Not vanishing but unraveling, like smoke caught in a sudden draft. The scent of night‑blooming jasmine and that sweet, floral rot floods his nostrils, overwhelming the dust.  

She is gone.  

Alistair stands alone in the moon‑lit cavern. The chill remains on his cheek where she touched him. The silence is no longer empty; it is a held breath. The manor presses in, no longer merely the absence of sound but the presence of expectation.  

He lifts his gaze to the towering gilt‑framed mirrors that line the ballroom walls. In the nearest one his own reflection—pale, wide‑eyed—stares back. For a fraction of a second, however, behind him a faint, shimmering after‑image appears: a woman in a tattered dress, head bowed, shoulders shaking with silent sobs.  

Then it disappears.  

He is alone.  But the air still holds her perfume—cold, sweet, cloying. And as the profound, attentive silence of the house presses upon him, Alistair knows the game has only just begun.
