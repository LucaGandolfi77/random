**Edited Chapter III– The Locked West Wing**

---

The corridor beyond the ballroom was a throat of stone, each footfall echoing like a hammer upon a coffin lid. Alistair Vale moved beneath a ceiling of blackened beams, the air there heavy with the same sweet, almost floral rot that had first curled about his nostrils in the entry hall. Now it mingled with the dry perfume of oil‑cloth and the metallic bite of iron. He could feel the house breathing through the cracks in its walls—a shallow inhale that drew the heat from his skin and returned it as a damp, chill breath upon his neck.

In the few days since his arrival he had learned that the west wing was a place the servants whispered about only when the candlelight guttered low, when the portraits in the main gallery shifted their gazes just enough to betray a curiosity that was not wholly human. Mrs Throckmorton had spoken of it in a tone that might have been pity, had pity been a language the house understood.

*“The west wing is sealed,”* she had said, the words dry as rustling parchment, *“for reasons the family has long kept in the dark.”*  

The sentence settled in his mind like a stone dropped into still water, rippling outward ever since.

A narrow set of double doors, their oak darkened to the hue of dried blood, stood at the end of the passage. They were locked—not with a flimsy bolt that could be coaxed open with a trembling hand, but with a tarnished iron latch that had fused to the wood by time and neglect. The iron was cold to the touch, a chill that seemed to seep into his bones the moment his fingertips brushed it, as if the metal itself remembered the heat of bodies that had once begged for release behind those doors.

Alistair pressed his palm against the lock, feeling the faint pulse of rust beneath his skin, and thought of the ledger he had found hidden in a desk drawer of his own chambers—a ledger whose pages might hold the names of those whose creative fire had been siphoned away to feed some nameless hunger in the walls. He had turned those pages with the reverence due a reliquary, watching the ink fade as if the words themselves were being eaten by the very rot that scented the house. In that ledger the entries stopped abruptly in 1847, a year when a celebrated painter—his name later erased from the family tree—was reported to have *“taken his final breath upon the night of a new moon.”* The entry ended with a single line written in a trembling hand that dared not finish its thought:

*“The fire must be fed.”*

He drew a slow breath; the scent of beeswax and old pine rose to meet him. He pushed. The doors yielded with a sigh that seemed less like wood giving way than a throat opening to speak a long‑suppressed word. Beyond them lay the west wing, a labyrinth of corridors draped in shadows that pressed close as if eager to swallow him whole.

**The First Chamber**

The first room was a drawing‑room that had once glimmered with crystal chandeliers and the rustle of silk gowns; now its windows were shuttered, the panes thick with grime, the stained glass outside the moon a pallid gray. Dust hung in the air like ash from a funeral pyre, settling on a ruined settee whose cushions bore the imprint of bodies long gone. In one corner stood a massive portrait of a stern patriarch, his eyes painted so that they seemed to follow Alistair’s approach with an intensity that made the hair on his arms rise. When he passed, the painted stare lingered a heartbeat longer, as if the canvas itself were noting every breath he took.

Behind a tapestry of faded roses he discovered a door cracked ever so slightly, as though something on the other side had been pressing, patient and relentless, against its surface. The crack widened as he drew nearer, the wood groaning in protest, and within the gloom a faint amber luminescence pulsed—*a fire that burned not with heat but with a spectral light that painted the room in shades of violet and ash.*  

The hearth was scarred by soot and occasional sprays of water that had seeped through the cracked ceiling, leaving trails like fresh wounds on the ancient stone. At its base lay a pile of ash that smelled of burnt roses and iron—an aroma that tightened his throat, as though he were inhaling the very essence of a long‑dead ceremony.

On the far wall, half‑covered by cobwebs, hung a tapestry of a storm‑tossed sea, its waves frozen in a moment of violent splendor. As Alistair’s eyes adjusted to the dim, he thought he saw movement within the painted surf—shadows that rose and fell like the backs of men and women caught in some forgotten dance. Their faces, rendered in muted oils, were turned toward a single point on the horizon where a pale hand reached upward, fingers outstretched as if to grasp something just beyond the edge of the canvas.

The temperature in the wing seemed to drop with each step, a cold that settled into his marrow, making his joints ache as though they were the bones of a house built upon foundations of ice. He felt a pressure building behind his eyes, an unsettled perception that threatened to blur the line between what he saw and what the house wished him to believe. The portraits, once static, began to breathe— their eyes glistening with a wet sheen as though wept upon, their mouths forming silent words he could not decipher but felt reverberate in the pit of his stomach.

A sudden, almost imperceptible rustle made him turn. In the far corner of the room, beneath a tall bookcase whose shelves were crammed with leather‑bound tomes, lay an open ledger, its pages fluttering as though an unseen wind brushed them. The ink was dark, the script elegant yet trembling, as if written by a hand that could not altogether steady itself. The entry for 1849—just a year after the painter’s death—read:

*“The bargain is sealed. The fire feeds upon the soul that creates. Let the artist bleed, yet the house shall not die.”*

The words settled upon his tongue like ash, a bitter taste that rose from his very throat. He lifted his gaze to the painted faces in the hallway beyond, where the ancestors of this house stared down upon him with hollow, shifting eyes, their gazes now fixed, unblinking, as though they had been awaiting his arrival for a century and a half.

He stepped back; the floorboards groaned under his weight, and behind him a soft, melodic sigh drifted through the air—a breath that seemed to come from the walls themselves. It was not wind; it was a whisper laced with melody, a fragment of *The Blue Danube* that lingered like a promise of what had already been taken—and what would be taken anew.

---

**The Hearth**

The chapter ends upon the edge of an ancient fireplace, its iron grate twisted into the shape of a skeletal hand. As Alistair reaches out, a sudden flare of cold light erupts from the hearth, illuminating a figure standing just beyond the flames. She wears the same ivory‑threaded dress he saw in the moonlit ballroom, but now her form is more solid, more terrifying: her skin pallid as sheet linen, her eyes empty wells of darkness that swallow the light, and around her neck hangs a single, unbroken strand of black ribbon that pulses faintly with a rhythm that matches his own heartbeat.

Beyond her, the lock on the doors behind him gives way with a lamentable creak, sealing him within a space that is simultaneously a prison and a sanctuary. The walls close in, the portraits close their eyes, and a faint, lingering scent of floral rot fills the room—sweet, cloying, and undeniably final.

**The house has taken a new fire.**  

*And the flame that now burns within Alistair is not his own.*
