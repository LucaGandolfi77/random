**Chapter 6 – The Dancer’s Mirror**

The passage beyond the foyer grew tighter with every step, its walls pressing inward as though the house itself tried to swallow their intrusion. Alistair chased the spectral chill that trailed Elara—a cold that pricked his skin even as a fever burned in his veins from their first encounter. The floral rot he’d first sensed in the entry hall now mingled with the faint perfume of dried lavender and the metallic tinge of old silver.

“We shouldn’t be here,” Alistair whispered, though his feet carried him forward regardless.

Elara didn’t turn. “The house has many rooms, writer. Only this one remembers me truly.”

Her voice floated like the haunting waltz that still lingered in the ballroom—a melody that seeped into hollow places, stirring memories he could never have owned. The corridor ended at a weather‑worn oak door bound with tarnished iron that curled like sleeping serpents at its base.

Elara laid a hand—cold as grave earth yet impossibly delicate—against the wood. The lock sighed open, exhaling after a long slumber.

The chamber beyond defied the manor’s decay. A bed of black mahogany dominated the space, its canopy draped with crimson velvet that retained its deep hue despite the years. A single shaft of moonlight pierced the grimy window, throwing dust motes in dance and illuminating a vanity mirror that seemed to gulp the light instead of reflecting it.

On the vanity, a music box of mother‑of‑pearl inlay lay dulled by time. As they entered, it began to play—an impossible melody, half‑remembered, like a fragment of childhood dreams.

Elara gasped, her form flickering between substance and transparency. “That tune… I haven’t heard it since…”

She drifted toward the vanity, suddenly uncertain; the elegance that once defined her now frayed at the edges. When her fingers brushed the lid, the mirror’s surface showed a different motion—her reflection moved with certainty while her physical form hesitated.

“It plays for you?” Alistair asked, voice taut.

“It plays for the girl who once slept here,” she replied, her face a perfect mask of sorrow. “Before she became… this.”

She gestured at her ethereal self, moonlight catching in layers of silk that might once have been satin. “The house consumes our stories, Alistair. It takes our music, our memories, until all that remains is the echo of who we were.”

Drawn by a compulsion he could not name, Alistair reached for the music box. Its tune bypassed his ears, speaking directly to the emptiness where his creativity had died. As his hand touched the polished surface, his own reflection lagged a heartbeat behind—its motion a flicker of dissonance that made his breath catch.

“Look,” Elara whispered, her eyes fixed on the glass.

In the mirror, her reflection began to shift. The flawless features softened; the sharp edges of a ghost gave way to something more human. The youth faded, replaced by a woman marked by time and sorrow, eyes hollow with a grief that transcended death.

“The house shows us what we’ve lost,” she murmured, her voice now layered with countless others. “What it has taken.”

She moved toward him, but her reflection remained trapped in the glass, watching with eyes void of comfort. When she brushed his cheek, the cold seeped in as before, but beneath it lay an even more terrible emptiness—one that seemed to draw his warmth away, leaving a hollow ache.

“I never chose this,” she whispered, fingertips tracing the crescent scar on his wrist. “The house chose me. Now… it chooses you.”

In the mirror, her reflection smiled—a terrible, knowing smile that stretched her lips too thin, revealing teeth like polished pearls. The music box’s melody shifted, becoming a chorus of whispers in languages dead and unborn.

The room grew colder; shadows deepened as if the moonlight itself were being devoured. Alistair watched, mesmerised, as his own reflection in the glass began to change—features sharpening, eyes darkening with the feverish glow of his scar.

“The dance,” Elara whispered, her voice now emanating from both beside him and from the depths of the mirror, “never ends. We dance until the music claims us completely.”

Behind them, the door to the chamber slammed shut with a sigh that sounded like a final breath, sealing them within the room that remembered Elara too well. In the vanity mirror, their figures drew close, their edges blurring as the boundary between flesh and spirit, between past and present, dissolved into the terrible, beautiful music of the house.
