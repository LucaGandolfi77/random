Shadows pooled in the corners and coalesced into faint silhouettes that whispered his name in a chorus that was not quite human. A cold seeped into his fingertips—the same chill he’d felt when Elara touched him—an ice that seemed to pulse like a heart of its own.

He reached out, and the brushstrokes seemed to crawl under his skin, leaving a phosphorescent welt across his palm. The crescent scar on his wrist—long dismissed as a mere birthmark—thrummed, its edge lighting like a wick catching wind.  

Low murmurs rose from the walls, a chant older than the house:  

*“Fire feeds the void. The void consumes the fire. The fire must be spent, not hoarded.”*  

The attic itself seemed to breathe with those words; the rafters trembled, the beams leaned inward as if eager to listen.

**The Curse Takes Form:**  

The scar on his wrist began to dry, the skin hardening into a waxy sheen, as though ink were seeping out of his marrow. His writing hand, the instrument of his identity, went cold, fingertips turning ash‑gray.  

A gust swept the attic—no window open—scattering loose papers like startled birds. One sheet landed on his boot: **A contract, inked in blood, bearing his name.** His signature trembled upon it, though he could not recall ever signing such a thing.  

The contract whispered of a debt the Vale line owed to Blackwood: the *fire of creation* from any artist who entered the house, to be repaid in the *consumption* of his very soul.  

Alistair staggered back. Ceiling beams darkened, the rot scent intensified, and the house seemed to inhale his fear. The attic had become a crucible, the walls pressing against his ribs, urging him to surrender his creative flame to a hungry maw.  

In the far corner, a brass key—identical to the one he’d found on the massive desk in the library—lay atop a rusted iron lock affixed to an old trunk. The lock’s keyhole matched the crescent scar on his wrist; as the key brushed the skin, the scar erupted in amber light, blooming across his palm and illuminating every dust‑laden object like a ghostly halo.

**The Moment of Choice:**  

A small, tarnished mirror propped against a crate reflected his face. His eyes, once a clear searching brown, were now twin coals of sorrow; the crescent scar glowed like an ember. Behind him, Elara’s diary fluttered open on its own, turning to a single line written in a hand that was both hers and his:  

*“Only the willing may relinquish the flame.”*  A hollow, disembodied laugh echoed through the rafters—neither wholly human nor wholly spectral. The attic shivered; floorboards groaned as if the house were preparing to swallow him whole.  

Alistair sank to his knees amid stacks of forgotten canvases, the brass key clenched in his trembling hand, its light fading like a candle’s last breath. The attic’s dim glow slowed, dust motes froze in mid‑air, and the ceiling above pulsed once—a deliberate, final beat.  In the narrow space between darkness and the dying ember of his scar, the cold reached into his marrow, coaxing outward the fire that still burned within him—an ember waiting, perhaps, to be offered to the void.
