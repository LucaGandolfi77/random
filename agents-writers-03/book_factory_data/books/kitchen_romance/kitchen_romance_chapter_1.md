
**Chapter 1: Mise en Place**

The kitchen was a submarine. Every surface—the chipped porcelain sink, the avocado-green stove from the 70s, the single strip of counter beside the fridge—was in use. Steam fogged the one small window, trapping the smells of garlic, rosemary, and the faint, sweet perfume of the late-summer tomatoes Maya had insisted on buying from the market.

“You’re crowding the board,” Leo said, his voice flat. He didn’t look up from the perfect, even dice of a carrot he was producing. His knife moved with a quiet, rhythmic certainty that made Maya’s haphazard, enthusiastic chopping feel like a percussion solo next to a string quartet.

“There’s no space *not* to crowd,” Maya retorted, shifting her hip against the dishwasher to make room. Her own knife was a blur, producing irregular chunks. “It’s a tiny kitchen, not a culinary school lab. Loosen up.”

Leo finally glanced at her pile of carrots. “Those won’t cook evenly. You’ll have mush and raw centers in the same pan.”

“They’re *rustic*,” she said, air-quoting. “It’s a vibe.”

“It’s inefficient.”

He reached past her for the olive oil, his arm brushing hers. A jolt, small and electric, went through her. She jerked back, her elbow knocking a spoon off the counter. It clattered to the floor.

“See?” she said, a little too loudly, bending to pick it up. “Chaos. It’s my signature.”

When she straightened, he was watching her, his dark eyes unreadable. He’d been watching her all afternoon, she realized. Not with the easy camaraderie of an old family friend, but with a sharp, assessing focus that made her skin prickle. She told herself it was criticism. It had always been criticism. Since she was a teenager trailing after her older brother and his impossibly cool, slightly-older best friend, Leo had been the benchmark for everything she wasn’t: calm, precise, responsible.

“The playlist,” he said, nodding toward the portable speaker blaring a frenetic pop song. “It’s distracting.”

“It’s energy!”
“It’s noise.”

He turned it off. The sudden silence was louder, filled with the *thwack-thwack* of his knife and the frantic *chop-chop* of hers. The absence of the beat made the tension in the room palpable. Maya focused fiercely on her onion, her eyes stinging. It was just the onion, she told herself. Not the way he’d taken off his jacket an hour ago, revealing forearms corded with lean muscle as he’d rolled up his sleeves. Not the way he’d silently rearranged the utensils by the stove into a precise, logical order, his brow furrowed in concentration.

She was being ridiculous. He was just Leo. Her brother’s best friend. The guy who’d taught her to drive stick in his beat-up truck, who’d shown up at her high school graduation with a stiff, awkward hug and a check for $100. The guy who, for the last five years, had been dating Chloe—sweet, stable, effortlessly perfect Chloe—whom he’d probably marry. This was just a favor. A big, pre-wedding favor for her brother’s memory: cook a welcome-home dinner for their mutual childhood friend, Sam, who was in town for one night. A simple, one-night reunion.

So why did the air feel so thick? Why did the simple act of reaching for the same knife—a chef’s knife, the only one worth using—feel like a seismic event?

Their hands met on the handle. His was large, warm, calloused at the base of the thumb. Hers was smaller, nails short and practical. They both froze.

“You first,” he said, his voice lower than before.

“No, you. You’re in the middle of something.”

“It’s fine.”

A beat stretched, their hands still touching the cool metal. Maya could feel the warmth of his skin, the faint tremor of his pulse. She saw, with absurd clarity, a tiny scar on his knuckle—a burn, maybe from a pan. She wanted to trace it with her thumb.

He pulled back first, as if scalded. “Just… take it.”

She did, the handle feeling alien in her hand now. She attacked the onion with renewed, desperate vigor, the blade slipping. A piece went flying, landing near his foot.

Leo stared at it. Then he looked at her. And in his eyes, for just a second, she didn’t see criticism. She saw something else. A flicker of… amusement? Exasperation? It was gone too fast, replaced by that familiar, guarded neutrality.

But Maya was left with the echo of it. The misreading settled in her gut, hot and sour. *He thinks I’m a disaster. A careless kid who can’t even chop an onion.*

She was determined to prove him wrong. She would make the perfect soffritto. She would show him she wasn’t just his best friend’s little sister anymore.

Across the counter, Leo watched her struggle, a familiar, forbidden warmth spreading through his chest. *She’s not a girl anymore,* he thought, the realization hitting him with the force of a physical blow. She was a woman—all fierce, expressive eyes and stubborn chin, her hair escaping its tie in wisps around her face. And she was standing in a kitchen no bigger than a closet, trying so hard to impress him.

He remembered, with sudden, startling clarity, a rainy afternoon ten years ago. She’d been sixteen, furious at her parents, and had shown up at his apartment with two mugs of terrible, over-steeped tea. “I made this for you,” she’d said, thrusting one into his hands, her cheeks flushed. “Because you’re the only adult who doesn’t treat me like I’m made of glass.” The tea had been undrinkable, but the memory of her defiant, hopeful face was seared into him.

Now, she reached for the salt, and he saw the exact way she took her coffee—two sugars, a splash of cream, stirred three times counterclockwise. A detail he’d cataloged years ago and never let himself examine.

The onion fumes were getting to him. That had to be it.

“Pass the red pepper flakes?” she asked, not looking at him.

He handed them over, their fingers brushing again. This time, he didn’t pull away. He let the contact linger for a fraction of a second too long, feeling the static shock of it all the way to his toes.

Maya’s breath hitched. She snatched her hand back, her face flushing. *See?* the voice in her head whispered. *Disdain. He’s horrified by your incompetence.*

Leo, meanwhile, was thinking, *I remember you took your coffee like that when you were eighteen and heartbroken over your first boyfriend. I’ve remembered for nine years.*

The timer on the stove buzzed, a shrill, saving sound. They both jumped.

“Sauce is ready,” Leo said, his voice rough.

“Right,” Maya said, wiping her hands on her apron. “The… the sauce.”

They moved toward the stove, a clumsy dance in the narrow space. He stirred the simmering tomatoes, and she reached for the basil. For a moment, they stood shoulder-to-shoulder, looking down at the rich, red sauce. The kitchen was quiet but for the gentle *blup-blup* of the pot.

It was the most intimate moment Leo had experienced in years. And they weren’t even touching.

He cleared his throat. “It’s good. The soffritto is… rustic, but it works.”

Maya looked at him, searching his face. The criticism was still there, but layered under something else. Something that looked almost like pride.

She offered a small, tentative smile. “Thanks. For not throwing it out.”

He almost smiled back. Almost. Instead, he gave a curt nod and turned back to the stove, leaving the space between them cold and vast again.

But the simmer had begun. And neither of them had any idea how to turn the heat down.
