**Chapter 10: Taste of Tomorrow**

The cemetery was quiet in the way the kitchen was quiet after the last plate was washed—a held breath, a space between notes. Late afternoon sun slanted through the oaks, dappling the grass and the simple granite stone: *Miguel “Mico” Ruiz: 1985–2020*. Leo carried the container of leftovers—the ragu, the salvaged scallops, the focaccia that had finally, perfectly, baked. Maya held two forks and a paper towel, her movements precise, as if performing a ritual.

She knelt first, brushing dry leaves from the base of the stone. “We made too much,” she said, her voice soft, aimed at the inscription. “As usual.” A sad smile touched her lips. “But it was good. Really good.”

Leo knelt beside her, the container heavy in his hands. He looked at the name. *Mico*. His best friend. The brother who had made him promise, sweating and pale in a hospital bed, *“Look out for her, Leo. Don’t let her get lost.”*

He had interpreted that as a mandate to keep her at arm’s length, to be a steady, platonic anchor. He had failed in the most spectacular way possible—by falling in love with her.

“He’d have hated the fennel,” Leo said finally, his throat tight. “Called it a betrayal of the soffrito.”

Maya laughed, a short, bright burst that startled a bird from a nearby branch. “He would have stolen the last scallop,” she said. “And then said my sauce was ‘interesting’ with that tone that meant it was terrible.”

Leo opened the container, the smell of tomatoes, wine, and slow-cooked pork rising into the cool air. It was the smell of their disaster, their triumph, their *now*. He tore a piece of focaccia in two and handed half to Maya. “To Mico,” he said, tapping his half against hers.

“To Mico,” she echoed. They ate in silence, the simple bread the only offering.

When they were done, Maya wiped her hands. “I applied to the culinary program today,” she said, the words rushing out. “The one downtown. The night one. So I can still work at the bistro.”

Leo looked at her. This was the fear she’d carried—that she wasn’t serious, that she was just playing at cooking. This was her stepping out of being “Mico’s artistic sister” and into her own ambition. “That’s incredible,” he said, the word heavy with meaning. *You are incredible.*

She nodded, a flicker of vulnerability in her eyes. “I’m scared I’ll fail. Or that I’ll get in and realize I don’t belong.”

“You belong here,” Leo said, gesturing with his chin to the stone. “And anywhere you decide to cook.” He took a breath. “I told my dad I’m not taking over the restaurant.”

Maya’s eyes widened. “Leo…”

“I told him I have my own path. That I’m partnering with someone on a different kind of venture.” He brushed a phantom crumb from her knee. “It’s time I kept a different kind of promise. To myself.”

The promise to Mico hadn’t been to keep her at a distance. It had been to *look out for her*. And looking out for her meant believing in her, supporting her, standing beside her as an equal. Not as a guardian, but as a partner.

Maya’s chin trembled. “What if we’re terrible at this?” she whispered, the question not about cooking.

“Then we’ll be terrible together,” Leo said. The simplicity of it broke through the last of her fear. She leaned into him, and he wrapped an arm around her shoulders, pulling her close. They sat for a long time in the quiet of the dead, with the living scent of their meal between them.

Back in the tiny, scarred kitchen, the light was golden and low. The warped pan had been thrown out; the scorch mark on the wall was just a shadow. Maya washed the single container, and Leo dried it, their movements a silent, practiced dance.

“So,” Maya said, leaning against the counter. “*Al Dente*. Non-negotiable.”

Leo smiled, a real, easy smile that reached his eyes. “*Al Dente*. But we’re adding a section for ‘Mistakes.’ The soufflé pancake. The fire-roasted scallop.” He gestured to the space. “Our greatest hits.”

She laughed, the sound warm in the room. “Our story, on a menu.”

He finished drying the container and set it aside. Then he stepped into her space, his hands finding her hips. “Hey,” he said, his voice a low rumble. “We’re allowed to have a story.”

She looked up at him, her gaze clear, no misreading, no fear. Just a steady, hopeful light. “Yeah,” she whispered. “We are.”

He kissed her then, not with the frantic urgency of the near-miss by the stove, but with a deep, settling certainty. It was a promise, too. A promise that this—the ordinary, extraordinary mess of them—was worth the risk. When they parted, she rested her forehead against his.

“I should go,” she murmured, but made no move to step away.

“Stay,” he said. “Make the scrambled eggs. Burn them, even. I’ll eat them.”

A slow, brilliant smile spread across her face. It was the smile he’d carried in his heart for years. “Okay,” she said.

She turned to the fridge, pulling out eggs and milk. He watched her, the way she cracked the eggs with one hand, the way she hummed a off-key pop song. The kitchen was no longer a submarine, no longer a crime scene, no longer a pressure cooker. It was just a kitchen. Their kitchen.

The simmer had become a steady, rolling boil, and for the first time, he wasn’t afraid of the heat. He was just grateful to be in the kitchen with her.
