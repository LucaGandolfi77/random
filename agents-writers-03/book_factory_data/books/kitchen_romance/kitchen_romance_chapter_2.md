
**Chapter 2: The Onion Incident**

The onions were Maya’s idea. “For depth,” she’d declared, hefting a bag of yellow onions like a trophy. “French onion soup, or a rich base for the ragu. You can’t have a proper dinner without them.”

Leo had just nodded, already calculating the precise number of slices needed for even caramelization. He’d started on the soffrito—a fine dice of celery, carrot, and onion—his knife a metronome of control.

Maya, however, attacked her pile with gusto. “The best part is the cry,” she said, hacking off the top of an onion. “It’s like a release. All the pent-up… onion-ness.”

“It’s a chemical irritant,” Leo murmured, not looking up. “Syn-propanethial-S-oxide. Makes your eyes water as a defense mechanism.”

“See? Defense. My eyes are just defending themselves against your boring precision.” She began to chop, her knife a wild, effective thing. Within thirty seconds, her eyes were stinging. She blinked hard, her vision blurring.

“Told you,” Leo said, finally glancing over. His own station was a model of order, the diced vegetables uniform and ready for the pot. “Slow down. Use a sharper knife. Less cell damage, less gas.”

“I’m *fine*,” Maya insisted, her voice tight. She wiped her forearm across her eyes, smearing a little onion juice on her cheek. The sting intensified, a hot, prickling wave. A traitorous tear escaped, then another. She squeezed her eyes shut, furious at her own body’s betrayal.

A soft *shhh* sound came from beside her. She opened her eyes to see Leo sliding a small, damp kitchen towel across the stainless steel of her station, his movement economical and silent. He didn’t meet her gaze. “Press this to your eyes. The cold constricts the capillaries.”

Maya stared at the towel, then at his hands, which were already clean and dry on his apron. The gesture was so automatic, so devoid of pity, it undid her more than the onions. It was pure, unthinking care. She took the towel, her fingers brushing his. That same small jolt, but warmer now, threaded with something else—gratitude, sharp and unwelcome.

“Thanks,” she mumbled, pressing the cool fabric to her face. She breathed in the damp cotton smell, trying to clear the onion fumes.

From the pocket of his apron, Leo’s phone buzzed, a harsh, electronic intrusion. He ignored it, reaching instead for a pot to start the onions sweating.

It buzzed again. And again.

Maya’s curiosity, that persistent, needling thing, got the better of her. As Leo turned to adjust the flame, she caught a glimpse of the screen on the counter.

*Chloe: Miss you. Thinking about tomorrow. Can’t wait to see you. Xoxo.*

The words hung in the steamy air, as tangible as the garlic scent. *Tomorrow.* A date. An ordinary, loving text from a woman who was not her. The towel slipped from her numb fingers, landing with a soft plop on the floor.

Leo turned back. His eyes flicked from the fallen towel to Maya’s face, then to his phone. A shutter seemed to fall over his expression, closing off the moment of quiet tenderness. “Sorry,” he said, his voice back to its usual flat register. “Girlfriend. We’re having dinner tomorrow.”

*Girlfriend.* The word landed like a physical blow. Of course. There was a Chloe. There was always a Chloe. Someone who got the easy affection, the public hand-holding, the certainty. Not the messy, chaotic friend-of-the-family who cried over onions in a submarine kitchen.

“Right,” Maya said, forcing a bright, brittle tone. She bent to pick up the towel, her movements jerky. “Well, tell her I said hi. Or don’t. Probably don’t.”

“Maya…”

“What?” She straightened, clutching the towel. “It’s fine, Leo. Really. It’s not like I thought you were single and pining away for me in this kitchen of love.” She tried to laugh, but it came out as a wet sniffle. “Just… rustic onion vibes, you know?”

He watched her, his jaw tight. She saw the conflict in his eyes—the desire to explain, to apologize, to reach out and touch her arm again—warring with a deeper, more stubborn loyalty. To his brother’s memory. To Chloe. To the boundary lines he’d drawn so carefully around them both.

The moment stretched, thin and fragile. The only sounds were the gentle *hiss* of onions hitting hot oil and the frantic *thump-thump-thump* of Maya’s own heart.

Finally, Leo turned back to the stove, his shoulders straightening. “The onions are starting to stick,” he said, his voice leaving no room for argument. “You need to stir them.”

Maya looked at his back, at the set of his shoulders that suddenly seemed like a wall. The vulnerability she’d just shown—crying over a vegetable, accepting his quiet help—felt like an exposed nerve. She’d misread everything. That moment of connection wasn’t a crack in his armor; it was just him being… Leo. Competent. Helpful. A good guy who looked out for people, even chaotic women crying in his kitchen.

The real revelation wasn’t his tenderness. It was the crushing weight of the *girlfriend*. The ordinary, loving text. The tomorrow.

She picked up her knife again, her hands steady now for a new reason. “Stir them yourself,” she said, her voice flat. “I’m on carrot duty. Rustic, uneven carrots. They’re my signature.”

She hacked into a carrot with more force than necessary, the sound loud in the suddenly silent kitchen. She didn’t see the way Leo’s hands clenched on the wooden spoon, or the guilt that tightened his chest like a vise. She only saw the text, and the future it implied—a future that had no room for her, crying over onions in a tiny, aromatic kitchen.
