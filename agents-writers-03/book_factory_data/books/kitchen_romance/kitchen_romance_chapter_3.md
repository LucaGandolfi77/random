
**Chapter 3: Taste Test**

The ragu was ready for its final adjustment. Leo stood over the wide, shallow pot, a wooden spoon in hand, the rich, meaty aroma filling the small space between them. He’d just added a splash of red wine, and the alcohol burned off in a fragrant sigh.

“It needs something,” Maya said, appearing at his elbow, her own spoon poised. She’d been quietly watching him work for the last five minutes, the frantic energy of her earlier chopping replaced by a focused stillness. “It’s deep, but it’s… flat. Like it’s missing a top note.”

Leo frowned, tasting from his spoon. “It’s balanced. Acid from the tomatoes, fat from the pork, sweetness from the soffrito. What’s missing?”

“Soul,” she said, and before he could stop her, she dipped her spoon into the pot, scooping up a small portion. She blew on it softly, her eyes narrowing in thought.

“Hey—” Leo started. It was a professional kitchen sin, double-dipping, but the casual intimacy of the act short-circuited his objection. She was tasting *his* sauce, the one he’d meticulously built.

“See?” she said, not offering him her spoon. “It’s good. But it’s not *ours*.” She looked at him, her gaze direct. “Your grandmother’s recipe is perfect. But it’s a memory. We’re making dinner *now*. For our friends. It needs a little… now.”

Leo stared at her. She wasn’t just talking about the sauce. She was talking about them, about this forced collaboration, about the ghost of her brother and the life he was supposed to have led. She was saying their history was a foundation, not a prison.

“What do you suggest?” he asked, his voice quieter than intended.

She tilted her head, considering. “A pinch of fennel seed? Or… orange zest? Something bright to cut through the richness.” Her eyes flickered to his mouth, then away. “Something unexpected.”

He reached for the small bowl of zest he’d prepared for the salad, his fingers brushing the citrusy strands. “Too risky. It’ll overpower.”

“Since when are you afraid of risk?” she challenged, a familiar spark in her eyes.

“Since I learned that some risks aren’t worth the payoff,” he said, the words leaving his mouth before he could cage them. He meant it as a chef’s caution. But in the context of the last two days—the towel, the cramped space, the way her ponytail had come loose—it felt like a confession.

Maya’s spoon hovered over the pot. The playful challenge left her face, replaced by a soft, unreadable hurt. “Right. Some things are sacred.”

“That’s not what I meant.”

“Isn’t it?” She finally looked at him, and her eyes were too old, too knowing. “Some recipes you don’t mess with. Some people you don’t… taste from the same spoon.”

The air grew thick. Leo’s heart hammered. She was talking about Chloe. About boundaries. About the invisible line he’d drawn years ago when he’d promised his best friend he’d look out for his little sister.

He took the spoon from her limp fingers, his own brushing hers. That electric jolt, fainter this time but no less present, shot up his arm. He didn’t let go. He dipped *his* spoon into the ragu, then brought it to her lips. “Taste.”

Her breath hitched. She hesitated, then parted her lips. The sauce was rich, deep, familiar. But as she tasted it, his eyes locked on hers, the flavor changed. It tasted like the cramped kitchen, like the scent of her rosemary, like the unspoken words hanging in the steam. It tasted like *danger*.

“See?” he murmured, his voice rough. “It’s just sauce.”

She swallowed, the movement slow. “It’s not.”

He pulled the spoon back, his fingers lingering on the handle. The moment stretched, taut and fragile. He could lean in. He could close the three inches between them and find out if her lips were as soft as he’d imagined. He could break every promise he’d ever made.

A timer beeped sharply from the oven, making them both jump. The spell shattered.

“The focaccia,” Maya whispered, her voice unsteady. She turned away, busying herself with oven mitts, her back rigid.

Leo stood frozen, the spoon still in his hand, the taste of the ragu suddenly ash in his mouth. He’d come so close to the edge. The memory of her brother’s easy laugh, the weight of his promise, crashed over him. *She’s not a girl anymore.* The thought was a plea and a condemnation.

Maya pulled the golden focaccia from the oven, placing it on a rack to cool. Her hands shook slightly. She’d let him feed her. She’d tasted it, and it hadn’t been the sauce at all. It had been the look in his eyes, the heat of his skin, the terrifying, wonderful possibility that he might feel this too.

She heard him move behind her, the scrape of a chair as he sat at the small table, his back to her. The silent treatment. The wall. She’d just spent the last ten minutes chipping away at it with a spoon.

“The sauce is perfect,” she said to the focaccia, her voice too bright. “Really. You were right. It doesn’t need anything.”

Leo didn’t answer. He stared at the grain of the wooden table, his mind a battleground. *She’s Maya. She’s off-limits. She’s… the only thing I’ve ever truly wanted that I can’t have.* The conflict was a physical pain. He’d built a life of careful control, and she, with her chaotic chopping and her reckless tasting, was threatening to burn it all down.

The front door opened, and the sound of voices and laughter spilled into the kitchen—their friends, arriving early. The moment was gone, lost in the tide of the ordinary world.

Maya took a deep, steadying breath, the scent of rosemary and baking bread filling her lungs. She turned, a bright, brittle smile on her face. “They’re here. Showtime.”

Leo finally looked up, his expression smoothed into polite neutrality. “Right.” He stood, the mask falling perfectly into place. “Let’s feed them.”

But as he moved past her to greet their guests, his arm brushed hers, a fleeting contact that sent a final, silent promise—or a warning—between them. The simmer had reached a rolling boil, and neither of them knew how to turn the heat down.
