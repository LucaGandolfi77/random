
**Chapter 4: The Pressure Cooker**

The soufflé was Maya’s idea. “It’s dramatic,” she’d said, whisking egg whites into a glossy, hopeful cloud. “A grand finale. It says, ‘We pulled this off in a closet with a stove from the Carter administration.’”

Leo had frowned at the recipe. “It’s a chemical equation. One wrong move and it’s a sad, eggy pancake.”

“It’s *alive*,” she’d countered, pouring the batter into the ceramic dish with a flourish. “It has a heartbeat.”

Now, at 10:17 PM, the heartbeat was flatlining. They stood shoulder-to-shoulder at the oven window, watching the glorious, risen dome shudder and sigh, collapsing into a dense, sunken disc.

“Told you,” Leo said quietly, not with triumph, but with a weary sympathy.

“Shut up,” Maya whispered, but there was no heat in it. She slumped against the counter, the adrenaline of the near-successful dinner service draining away, leaving only a hollow ache in her bones. The kitchen was a disaster zone of floured surfaces, greasy pans, and the lingering ghost of the failed dessert.

“We have ice cream,” Leo offered, his voice soft. “And those berries you macerated.”

“That’s not the point,” she sighed, running a hand through her hair, leaving a streak of flour like war paint. “The point was the *poof*.”

He looked at her then, really looked. Not at the chef, not at the sister of his best friend, but at the woman who was so devastatingly disappointed by a deflated puff of egg and sugar. A woman who had spent the night orchestrating a symphony in a sardine can and was now mourning its one sour note. He felt a surge of feeling so potent it scared him—a fierce, protective urge to make it right, to erase the frown line between her brows.

“Okay,” he said, and his hand found its way to the small of her back, not guiding, just… present. “New plan. We start over. No recipe. Just us. What’s the move?”

Maya froze for a second at the contact, the warmth of his palm seeping through her thin t-shirt. The gesture was so automatic, so *them* in this space, it short-circuited her sadness. “Us?” she echoed, her voice barely a breath.

“Yeah,” he said, his gaze locked on the sad, sunken mess in the dish. “What do we do with a flop?”

She swallowed, the air suddenly thick. “We… we make trifle. Or bread pudding. We soak it in something boozy and call it a day.”

“Boozy sounds good.” He finally looked at her, and the heat in his eyes was unmistakable. It wasn’t about the soufflé anymore. It was about the two of them, alone in the steamy, flour-dusted dark, the rest of the world—their friends, their histories, Chloe—held at bay by a thin kitchen door.

They worked in silence then, a new, exhausted rhythm. Leo broke the fallen soufflé into rough chunks. Maya poured amaretto over them, the sweet, bitter smell filling the air. He whisked cream, his movements precise but slower now, less frantic. She hulled strawberries, the red juice staining her fingertips. They didn’t touch, but the space between them was electric, charged with the memory of almost-kissing by the ragu pot and the raw, shared vulnerability of the onion tears.

It was the most natural thing in the world when he reached for the bowl she’d just used, his fingers brushing hers as he took it to the sink. A jolt, milder this time but deeper, settled in her stomach. *This is how it happens,* she thought, a dizzy realization. *Not in a storm, but in the quiet cleanup. In the shared, mundane salvage of a disaster.*

The knock at the door was a gunshot.

They both jumped. Leo’s hand jerked, splashing soapy water onto his apron.

“Leo? You still here?” Chloe’s voice, bright and familiar, filtered through the door. “I got takeout from that new ramen place. Thought we could eat it while it’s hot.”

Maya felt the warmth in the room leach away, replaced by a cold, sinking dread. *We.* *Takeout.* The outside world, with its proper labels and uncomplicated paths, was at the threshold.

Leo went still, his back to the door. “Yeah,” he called, his voice carefully neutral. “Just… give me two seconds.”

He turned to Maya, his expression unreadable in the dim light. There was a question in his eyes, a silent *Can you…?* or *Should I…?* She didn’t know which. The fragile bubble they’d been in for the last twenty minutes shattered. The trifle, the amaretto, the shared silence—it all curdled into something tawdry and secret.

“It’s okay,” Maya said, forcing a bright, brittle smile that felt like a crack in her face. “Go. Your girlfriend brought ramen. That’s a higher calling than my boozy bread pudding.”

“She’s not—” he started, but the door was opening.

Chloe stood there, a paper bag in one hand, her smile wide and unsuspecting. She was pretty in a straightforward, sunny way, her presence filling the small kitchen with an immediate, easy energy. “Oh, hey Maya!” she said, as if she’d completely forgotten the last time they’d all been in a room together, when Maya had been the awkward little sister tagalong. “You’re still here? Leo said you guys were on a roll.”

“We were,” Maya said, wiping her hands on a towel, putting as much distance between herself and Leo as the cramped space allowed. “Just… cleaning up the evidence.”

Chloe bustled in, planting a quick, familiar kiss on Leo’s cheek. He let her, but his body was stiff, his eyes darting to Maya for a fraction of a second before looking away. “Smells incredible in here,” Chloe said, inhaling. “What is that, amaretto? Did you finally master the soufflé?”

“Collapsed,” Leo said shortly, turning back to the sink with a violent scrub of a pot.

“Oh, bummer.” Chloe didn’t seem too bothered. She started unpacking the ramen containers. “Well, this is way better than fancy French stuff anyway. Right, Maya?”

Maya nodded, unable to speak around the lump in her throat. *Right. Way better. Simple. Uncomplicated.*

Chloe chattered on about her day, the ramen, a movie they should watch. Leo responded in monosyllables, his shoulders tense. The easy, silent sync they’d just shared was a ghost in the room, a silent accusation. Every time Leo’s arm brushed Chloe’s as they moved around each other, Maya felt a phantom pain.

Finally, Chloe turned to Maya with a warm, open smile. “You know, I’ve always said you two should open a place together. You’re so in sync. It’s like you read each other’s minds in here.”

The words landed like physical blows. *In sync.* Yes. They were. Horrifyingly, beautifully, devastatingly in sync. And it meant nothing. It was a chef’s thing. A kitchen thing.

Leo went very still. He saw Maya’s smile freeze, saw the light in her eyes dim to something guarded and hurt. He’d thought her quietness was just fatigue. Now he understood. She was thinking he was *Chloe’s*. That this… whatever this was between them… was a dead end. A beautiful, impossible fantasy.

He had to fix this. He had to explain. But how could he, with Chloe standing right there, unpacking dumplings?

“Maya’s just a genius with flavor,” he said, his voice too loud, too forced. “I’m just the cleanup crew.”

It was a lie, and a bad one. He was the head chef. He was the one with the reputation. But saying it made him feel like he was putting her in her place, making her *safe* again. Friend. Colleague. Sister-figure.

Maya’s smile didn’t waver, but something in her eyes went far away. “He’s being modest,” she said, her voice light and airy. “He’s the one who makes it all look easy.”

She picked up her bag, the one with her wallet and keys, and slung it over her shoulder. “I should let you two eat. It’s late. Thanks for… everything tonight. Really. It was perfect.”

She didn’t look at Leo as she slipped past Chloe and out the kitchen door, her footsteps quiet on the stairs.

The silence that followed was deafening. Chloe finally looked from the door to Leo’s ashen face. “Wow. She’s really something, isn’t she?”

Leo didn’t answer. He was staring at the space where Maya had been, the smell of amaretto and failure hanging in the air. He’d pushed her away. Again. For what? For a quiet life with Chloe that suddenly felt like a life sentence?

“Yeah,” he said finally, the word hollow. “She really is.”

He turned back to the sink, the hot water now cold, and scrubbed at a pot that was already clean, the rhythmic motion the only thing keeping him from shattering the dishes.
