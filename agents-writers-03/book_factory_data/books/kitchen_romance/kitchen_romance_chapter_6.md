
**Chapter 6: The Guest List**

The kitchen was a crime scene. Flour dusted every surface like guilty snow, a greasy pan slumped in the sink, and the deflated soufflé dish sat on the counter like a shattered dream. The air still held the ghost of scorched egg and disappointment.

Maya wiped down the last counter, the damp cloth moving in slow, exhausted circles. Leo was at the sink, scrubbing the ragu pot with a ferocity that suggested it had personally insulted his ancestors.

“We should open a restaurant,” she said, the words slipping out before she could stop them. It was a joke, a fantasy spun from fatigue and the shared, secret language of their disaster.

Leo paused, sponge suspended in the suds. “A restaurant?”

“In this space. ‘The Closet.’ Our concept is ‘Intimacy and Slightly Underdone Protein.’” She finally looked at him, a tired smile on her face. “We’d be billionaires. People would pay for the experience of almost kissing while burning garlic bread.”

He chuckled, a low, warm sound that made her stomach flip. “The health department would shut us down in an hour.”

“But what a hour.” She leaned against the fridge, crossing her arms. The adrenaline was gone, replaced by a hollow, buzzing calm. Being near him felt less like standing next to a furnace and more like sitting by a banked fire—still hot, but manageable. “Seriously. Your classic technique, my… chaotic flair. We’d balance each other out.”

Leo dried his hands slowly, his gaze thoughtful. “We’d need a name that wasn’t an insult.”

“*Al Dente*. It’s perfect. It means ‘to the tooth.’ It’s about texture. It’s about… resistance before the give.” She watched him, her heart pounding a ridiculous rhythm. *Don’t say ‘like us.’ Don’t say ‘like us.’*

He nodded, a small, genuine smile touching his eyes. “I like it. But the menu would have to be… collaborative. No sacred cows.”

“Or sacred recipes,” she added softly, thinking of the ragu.

“Exactly.” He walked over, closing the distance she hadn’t realized he’d left. He was close enough that she could smell the clean scent of his soap over the kitchen smells. “So. Collaborative. What’s our signature pasta?”

They spent the next hour at the small, flour-dusted table, a bottle of cheap Pinot Noir between them. They sketched dishes on the back of an old takeout menu: a *cacio e pepe* with a hint of smoked paprika, a tiramisu with orange zest and a splash of amaro. It was effortless, a dance of “what if” and “yes, and.” Maya felt a terrifying, giddy hope swell in her chest. This was it. This was the thing she’d been missing—not just the cooking, but the *planning*. The building. With him.

“We’d need a great bread program,” Leo mused, swirling the wine in his glass. “Your focaccia is better than mine. We could do a sourdough starter named ‘Closie.’”

Maya laughed, then caught herself. “Closie?”

“For the closet. The kitchen closet. It’s where we’d keep the starter. It’s thematic.”

“It’s terrible. I love it.” She reached for the menu, her fingers brushing his. A jolt, smaller than before, but undeniable, shot up her arm. She didn’t pull away. “Okay, so the starter. And we’d need a signature cocktail. Something bitter, to cut the richness.”

“A *Bitter from the Closet*.”

“Leo.”

He grinned, and for a moment, the world was just the two of them, a bottle of wine, and a future that felt less like a ghost and more like a blueprint.

Her phone buzzed on the table. A text from her sister: *How’s the dinner? Did Leo behave?*

Maya typed back: *We’re opening a restaurant. It’s called ‘The Closet.’’*

She hit send and looked up. Leo was watching her, his expression soft. “What did she say?”

“She asked if you behaved.” Maya took a sip of wine, her throat dry. “I told her we’re opening a restaurant.”

“Good.” He reached for his own phone, which had been face-down by his elbow. “I should text Chloe. Let her know the dinner was… eventful. She’ll want to hear about the soufflé.”

The air shifted. Not dramatically, but enough. The temperature seemed to drop a degree. Maya’s playful hope curdled into a cold, heavy lump. *Chloe. Of course.*

“Right,” she said, her voice carefully neutral. “She’d be interested in the… structural failure.”

Leo’s thumbs moved over the screen. “She’s been helping me research locations. For the restaurant. She has a good eye for business.”

*Helping.* The word landed like a slap. Not a betrayal, but a confirmation. He was building this dream with Chloe. This was their shared future—*their* signature cocktails, *their* sourdough starter. She was just the chaotic friend who helped test recipes in a tiny kitchen.

She forced a smile, a brittle thing. “That’s… great. She’s so organized.”

“Yeah.” He sent the text, then looked up, his brow furrowed. “What?”

“Nothing. It’s a good plan. You and Chloe. The organized, put-together one.” The words were out before she could cage them. She tried to backtrack. “I just mean, she’s perfect for the front-of-house. All that… poise.”

Leo stared at her. He’d heard the edge, the ice under the words. “Maya, no. It’s not like that. She’s a friend. A business associate. The restaurant idea is just a fun… theoretical exercise.”

“Theoretical,” Maya echoed, nodding too fast. “Right. Of course.” She stood up, needing to move, to put the counter between them. “I’m just gonna… check on the ragu. Make sure it’s not judging us.”

She grabbed the container of sauce from the fridge and busied herself with a spoon, pretending to taste it. The rich, meaty aroma filled her nose, but all she could taste was the metallic tang of humiliation. *Stupid. So stupid.* She’d let herself believe, for one hour, in a fantasy where she was the chosen one. Where she was the one he planned futures with.

“Maya.” His voice was behind her, close. She could feel his presence, a heat at her back. “Hey. Talk to me.”

She wouldn’t turn. If she turned, she’d see the confusion in his eyes, the concern, and it would break her. “I am talking. I’m saying it’s a great idea. You should totally open a restaurant with Chloe. You can call it ‘Chloe’s Closet’ and have little monogrammed aprons.”

“Stop.” His hand came down on the counter, not touching her, but close enough to make her jump. “Just stop. What is this?”

She finally turned, her face carefully arranged in a mask of indifference. “This is me recognizing a good opportunity when I see one. You have a partner. You have a plan. I’m just the… the commis who stays late.”

“No.” He shook his head, his dark eyes flashing. “You are *not* just a commis. You are the reason the ragu had soul. You’re the one who argues about orange zest. You’re the one who makes me want to burn the recipe book and start over.” His voice dropped, intense and raw. “You’re the one I want to build this *with*, Maya. Not Chloe. *You*.”

The words hung in the steamy air, too big, too loud. They were a confession, an accusation, and a plea all at once. Maya’s mask shattered. The hope, the hurt, the terrifying longing all surged forward, leaving her breathless.

“But you’re with her,” she whispered, the truth of it finally spoken aloud. It felt like tearing off a scab.

“I’m *not*,” he said, frustration edging his voice. “I’m not with her. I’m… I’m figuring it out. With you. In this stupid, perfect, impossible kitchen.”

“Figuring it out?” The laugh that escaped her was hollow. “By mentioning her help on our dream restaurant? By texting her while we’re planning it? That’s how you figure it out?”

He opened his mouth, then closed it. The confusion on his face was real. He *had* thought it was innocent. He *had* been so caught up in the fantasy with *her* that he’d forgotten how it looked from the outside. How it felt.

The timer on the oven, set for the next day’s bread, chose that moment to beep—a shrill, electronic intrusion.

They both jumped. The spell, taut and fragile, snapped.

Maya grabbed the container of ragu and practically threw it back in the fridge. “I should go. It’s late.”

“Maya, wait.” He reached for her arm, but she sidestepped him, her movements jerky.

“Don’t. Just… don’t.” She grabbed her bag from the hook by the door, her hands shaking. “The ragu is perfect, by the way. Just like your grandmother’s. No soul needed.”

She was out the door and down the hall before he could say another word. She didn’t look back.

Leo stood alone in the wreckage of their kitchen, the phantom feel of her skin under his fingers, the taste of her hope and disappointment thick on his tongue. He’d blown it. Not with a grand gesture, but with a thoughtless text, a careless mention. He’d built a bridge with one hand and burned it with the other.

The timer beeped again, a persistent, mocking chirp. He slammed his hand on the oven button, silencing it. The silence that followed was worse. It was the sound of a future, so vividly imagined, now crumbling into dust.

He sank into one of the kitchen chairs, dropping his head into his hands. The scent of garlic and rosemary, once comforting, now felt like a taunt. He’d promised to protect her. To keep her safe. But he was the one hurting her. He was the danger.

And he had no idea how to fix it.
