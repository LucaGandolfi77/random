**Chapter 7: Heat of the Moment**

The final course was a pan-seared scallop with brown butter and lemon-thyme sauce. Maya’s last-minute addition—a gesture of elegance. Leo had called it an unnecessary risk. She’d worn him down with a look that said, *Just trust me.*

Now, as he heated oil in a heavy stainless-steel skillet, a prickle of unease crawled up his spine. The stove was ancient, its flame notoriously fickle. Maya stood beside him, zesting a lemon with quick, sure strokes.

“The oil’s shimmering,” she said, not looking up. “It’s ready.”

“It’s not,” Leo said, his voice tight. “Ten more seconds. You want a crisp sear, not a grease fire.”

“It’s fine,” she insisted, reaching for the bowl of patted-dry scallops. “I’ve done this a hundred times.”

“Not on this stove.”

But she was already sliding the first scallop into the pan. A perfect, satisfying *hiss*. Then another. Then a third.

For ten glorious seconds, the kitchen smelled like a promise: nutty, rich, bright with lemon. Then a microscopic betrayal—a stray droplet of water—hit the oil.

The pan erupted.

Not just a flare, but a *whoosh*—a dragon of orange flame shooting two feet into the air, licking the underside of the vent hood. The fire alarm, sensitive as a gossip, began its piercing shriek.

“Shit!” Maya stumbled back, hand flying to her face.

Leo didn’t think. He grabbed the skillet handle, his palm burning through the towel, and with his other arm, he shoved Maya behind him, turning his body into a shield. He slammed the pan onto an unlit burner, cutting the direct heat, then snatched the box of baking soda and dumped it over the fire. The powder burst into a choking white cloud, muffling the roar and the alarm.

Silence, save for the dying *hiss* of the fire and the relentless, beeping scream of the alarm.

They were pressed together, breathing in the acrid, soda-and-burnt-oil air. Leo’s back was against Maya’s front, his heart hammering. He could feel the frantic beat of her heart against his shoulder blade. Her hands were clenched in the fabric of his apron, just below his ribs.

The world had narrowed to the scent of her—clean sweat, lemon, the faint, sweet smell of her conditioner—and the solid, terrifying reality of her body against his.

The alarm finally stopped, leaving a ringing silence.

Slowly, Leo turned. She hadn’t moved. Her face was pale, her eyes wide. There were tiny freckles of soot on her cheek.

“You could have gotten hurt,” she whispered, her voice raw.

He looked at her. The fear in her eyes wasn’t for the fire. It was for him. For the stupid, reckless, protective thing he’d just done.

“Not what I’m afraid of,” he said, the words leaving his mouth before he could cage them.

The air between them changed. The charged, post-crisis adrenaline morphed into something thicker, heavier. The kitchen, still smelling of disaster, felt like the only safe place in the world. He was so close he could see the individual flecks of green in her hazel eyes. He could count the freckles scattered over her nose.

Her gaze dropped to his mouth. His did the same to hers.

This wasn’t a near-kiss built on simmering tension. This was *because* of it. The fire had burned away the last of their careful distance. The only thing left was the raw, blistering heat of *now*.

He leaned in. She tilted her head up.

The timer for the focaccia, set earlier for a bread that was now forgotten, chose that exact moment to beep.

*Beep. Beep. Beep.*

The sound was a gunshot in the silent kitchen. They jerked apart as if shocked, stumbling back. The spell shattered, leaving behind a painful, buzzing emptiness.

Maya’s hands trembled. She clasped them behind her back. “The… the bread,” she stammered, her voice too high. “It’s probably… charcoal.”

She turned and yanked open the oven door. A wave of heat and the smell of perfect, golden-brown focaccia hit them. She pulled the loaf out, placing it on the rack with a deliberate, clattering force that betrayed the shake in her hands.

Leo stood frozen, staring at the counter. His palms were still tingling from where he’d held the hot pan, and from where her hands had been. The ghost of her body heat lingered on his front.

“It’s… perfect,” she said, not turning. “See? Told you we could do it.”

“Maya,” he started, his voice rough.

“We should probably clean up the soda,” she interrupted, grabbing a towel and vigorously wiping the counter where the baking soda had fallen. “And the pan’s ruined. Totally warped. We’ll need a new one.”

She was chattering, a defense mechanism he recognized from years of knowing her. When she was scared or overwhelmed, she filled the silence with noise.

He wanted to grab her. To tell her to stop. To make her look at him and admit that the fire wasn’t the dangerous thing in the room—*they* were.

But the timer’s interruption had given his cautious nature a lifeline. It had given them both an excuse.

“Yeah,” he said, his voice flat, professional. “Ruined. I’ll write it off.” He bent to examine the pan, giving her his back, giving himself space to breathe. “We’ll use a different one for the scallops. Maybe poach them instead. Safer.”

*Poach them. Kill the heat. Remove the risk.*

He heard her swallow. “Safer,” she echoed, the word hollow.

They worked the rest of the night in a brittle, careful quiet. The intimacy of the near-disaster was now a chasm between them, filled with everything they weren’t saying. Every time their hands brushed reaching for a spoon, they both flinched as if from a burn.

The fire was out. But the heat in the kitchen had never been more dangerous. And for the first time, Leo was truly terrified of getting burned.
