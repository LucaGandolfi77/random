**Chapter 8: The Confession**

The kitchen was quiet, but it was a different quiet than before. The frantic, buzzing silence of the failed soufflé was gone, replaced by the low hum of the refrigerator and the soft, rhythmic *drip-drip* of the faucet Leo hadn’t quite managed to fix. The air smelled of lemon, burnt sugar, and the ghost of smoke.

Maya stood at the sink, scrubbing the last of the baking soda residue from the scallop pan. Her movements were automatic, but her mind was a riot. The adrenaline from the fire had faded, leaving a hollow, shaky feeling in its wake. And underneath it, a new, terrifying heat.

Leo was at the table, his back to her, methodically wiping down the already-clean surface with a damp cloth. He’d been quiet since the alarm stopped blaring, since he’d pulled her behind him and the world had narrowed to the smell of his skin and the solid wall of his back. He hadn’t yelled, hadn’t panicked. He’d just *acted*, and then retreated into this silent, methodical shell.

She watched the set of his shoulders, the way he concentrated on a single square foot of wood. It was his defense, she knew. When emotions got too big, he polished them away.

“You’re doing that thing,” she said, her voice too loud in the stillness.

He didn’t stop. “What thing?”

“The thing where you clean like you’re trying to scrub a memory out of the grain.”

He paused, cloth hovering over a water ring. “It’s just a table.”

“It’s *our* table. The one where you fed me that taste of ragu and I almost… and then the timer went off.” She said it flatly, a statement of fact, not a memory. She couldn’t afford for it to be a memory. “Now you’re cleaning it like it’s evidence.”

He finally looked at her over his shoulder. His eyes were shadowed. “Maybe it is.”

The word hung in the air, heavy and dangerous. *Evidence.* Of what? The attraction? The almost-kiss? The promise he’d made to a dead man?

“Evidence of what, Leo?” she asked, the question pulled from her by a string of pure frustration. “That we have chemistry? That we almost set the kitchen on fire? That you’re a human shield? Congratulations, it’s all been duly noted.”

He turned on the stool, facing her. The distance between them—the width of the small kitchen—felt like an ocean. “It’s evidence that I’m a hypocrite.”

That stopped her. She lowered the pan into the soapy water with a soft clunk. “What?”

“I told myself I was protecting you. From me. From this… this *thing*.” He gestured vaguely between them. “From the fact that I’m your brother’s best friend, and I’m supposed to look out for you, not… not look at you like this.” He ran a hand through his hair, a gesture of pure agitation she’d never seen from him. “And all this time, I was just protecting myself. From being happy. From moving on. From you.”

Maya’s breath caught. This wasn’t the script. This wasn’t the cautious, guilt-ridden man from the last six years. This was something raw and real, and it scared her more than the fire had.

“What are you talking about?” she whispered.

He stood up, his movements restless. He paced the three steps to the counter and back. “Chloe and I broke up this afternoon.”

The words landed like physical blows. *Broken up.* It was what she’d wanted, wasn’t it? What she’d secretly, shamefully hoped for every time she saw them together. But hearing it now, in this quiet, devastated kitchen, it felt like a betrayal. Of him. Of the life he’d had.

“Oh,” was all she could manage. Her hands, still in the soapy water, began to tremble.

“It wasn’t working,” he said, his voice rough. “It hadn’t been for a long time. I was just… going through the motions. Comfortable. Safe.” He looked at her, and his gaze was so full of a painful, dawning truth it made her ache. “I was waiting for a sign to be brave, and the sign was almost watching you burn.”

“Don’t,” she said, a tear finally escaping, hot and shameful. “Don’t make it poetic. Don’t make it about *me* saving *you* from a fire. That’s just another way to make it noble, and it’s not. It’s just… messy and human.”

“It *is* messy and human!” he shot back, the first true spark of passion she’d seen in him all night. “And I’m done trying to make it something else. I ended it because I realized I was in love with you. And being with Chloe felt like a lie, because every time I looked at her, I was comparing her laugh to yours, her way of chopping an onion to yours, the way you taste sauce off your finger…”

He stopped, horrified at his own admission. But it was out. The words hung between them, undeniable and huge.

Maya stared at him, the world narrowing to the sound of her own heartbeat and the dripping faucet. *In love.* Past tense? Present? The distinction felt like the only thing that mattered.

“So let me get this straight,” she said, her voice gaining strength, edged with a defensiveness she couldn’t quite suppress. “You were in a relationship. You ended it. And now you’re telling me you’re in love with *me*? Am I supposed to be flattered? Am I supposed to just… jump? Because you’re finally available?”

“No!” he said, stepping closer, but stopping just out of reach. “That’s the last thing I want. I don’t want you to jump because I’m available. I want you to consider me because I’m *me*. The guy who remembers you hate cilantro, who knows you take your coffee with too much sugar, who has spent six years being your brother’s friend and wanting to be something more but being too much of a coward to risk it.”

“Coward?” The word was a whip-crack. “You think I don’t know about the promise, Leo? The one you made to my brother. ‘I’ll look after her.’ That’s your sacred cow, isn’t it? That’s the boundary you’ve hidden behind while I’ve been… what? The girl you have to protect? The little sister?” Her voice broke on the last word. “I’m not a girl anymore. And I don’t need a protector. I need a partner. And if you’re only here because you feel guilty, or because you’re finally free of Chloe, then you’re right. This is a lie.”

She turned back to the sink, gripping the edge of the counter until her knuckles turned white, blinking back tears of anger and humiliation. She’d laid it all out—her fear, her pride, her desperate need for him to see her as an equal. And he was just standing there, probably realizing what a colossal mistake he’d made.

Then she felt his hand, warm and tentative, on her shoulder. Not pulling her, just… there. A point of contact.

“Look at me,” he murmured.

She shook her head, staring at the sudsy water. “I can’t.”

“Maya.” His other hand came up to gently turn her around. She kept her eyes down, fixed on the stain on his t-shirt. “The promise wasn’t about keeping my distance. It was about not breaking your heart. About making sure you had the space to become who you are without me in the way, clouding things.”

“And have you been in the way?” she whispered.

“Every second of every day for six years.” His thumb stroked a slow circle on her shoulder blade. “The night of the onion incident… when I gave you that towel… that wasn’t brotherly. That was me wanting to take care of you because I *like* taking care of you. When we argued over the ragu, I wasn’t just defending a recipe. I was defending the idea that I could still surprise you, that I could still matter to you in a new way.” He took a shaky breath. “The fire tonight… I didn’t shield you because I made a promise to your brother. I shielded you because the thought of you getting hurt is the only thing that still scares me more than the thought of losing you.”

She finally looked up. His eyes were bright, glistening. He was terrified. And he was here.

“So what happens now?” she asked, the fight draining out of her, replaced by a terrifying, fragile hope.

“Now,” he said, his voice thick, “we figure it out. Together. No more sacred cows. No more hiding behind the past. Just… us. If you want that.”

She looked at him—the flour smudge on his cheek from the soufflé disaster, the sincerity in his eyes, the way his hand was still trembling slightly on her shoulder. He wasn’t offering a fairy tale. He was offering a messy, human, *real* thing. And for the first time, it didn’t feel like a betrayal of anyone’s memory. It felt like a beginning.

Slowly, she lifted her hand and placed it over his, where it rested on her shoulder. His fingers curled around hers, a perfect, silent fit.

“Okay,” she said, the word barely a whisper. “Okay.”

It wasn’t a kiss. It wasn’t a grand declaration. It was a shaky, shared breath in a kitchen that had seen too much smoke and too many tears. But it was a start. The simmer had finally begun to bubble.
