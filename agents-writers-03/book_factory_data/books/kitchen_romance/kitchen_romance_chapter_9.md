
**Chapter 9: Simmer**

The kitchen was different in the morning. The submarine-like claustrophobia had lifted, replaced by soft, dust-moted light streaming through the small window. The air no longer held the ghosts of garlic and scorched sugar, but the bright, clean scents of lemon cleanser and… coffee. Real coffee, not the desperate, sludgy kind from a pot left on too long.

Maya stood at the counter, her back to the sink, watching Leo move. He was making scrambled eggs. Not the perfect, French-style curds he’d demonstrated for her once with a dozen eggs and a stopwatch, but simple, fluffy eggs in a non-stick pan, with a splash of milk and a grind of pepper. It was an ordinary thing, profoundly ordinary, and it made her chest ache.

He’d been quiet since they’d stumbled upstairs last night, through the dark living room, and into this kitchen for water. The confession had hung between them, a fragile, glowing thing. *I’ve been in love with you since you were 19.* She’d cried. He’d held her. There were no more grand gestures, no more interrupted moments. Just this: the quiet hum of the refrigerator, the soft *pat-pat* of his spatula, the way he’d automatically poured her coffee—black, one sugar—without asking.

“You’re staring,” he said, not turning from the stove. A faint, sleep-rough edge lingered in his voice.

“Observing,” she corrected, wrapping her hands around her mug. “Making sure you don’t turn them into rubber.”

He glanced over, a small smile touching his eyes. “Trust the process, Maya.”

It was their first date. This was their first date. Cooking breakfast in a kitchen still bearing the faint scars of their disaster dinner. The warped pan from the fire was in the trash. The memory of it—the heat, the press of his body shielding hers—was a fresh, electric imprint on her skin.

She pushed off the counter and came to stand beside him, their shoulders brushing. He didn’t pull away. He leaned into it, just a fraction.

“What’s the move for today?” she asked, nodding at the pan.

“Low and slow. Constant movement.” He demonstrated with the spatula, creating soft, creamy waves. “You want them moist, not dry.”

“Like us,” she said before she could stop herself. The metaphor slipped out, easy and dangerous.

He stilled, the spatula pausing mid-wave. He looked at her, really looked, and she saw the same terrified hope she felt reflected back at her. “Yeah,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “Like us.”

The word hung there, *us*, solid and terrifying. He went back to stirring, but his movements were less precise now, a little shaky.

“So,” Maya said, filling the silence with a sip of coffee. “The ‘Simmer’ chapter. Is that what this is?”

He huffed a laugh. “If by ‘Simmer’ you mean ‘barely contained panic disguised as domesticity,’ then yes.”

“I’m not panicked.”

“Liar.”

She smiled into her mug. He knew her. All of her, not just the version she showed the world. He knew the girl who cried over onions and the woman who wanted to add orange zest to a sacred ragu. He’d loved her through both.

“What are you afraid of?” she asked, the question that had haunted her for years finally voiced in the safety of this quiet kitchen.

He finished scraping the eggs onto two plates, added a piece of toast to each, and turned to face her, leaning against the counter. He looked exhausted and alive in a way she’d never seen.

“Not what you think,” he said, echoing his words from the fire. “I’m not afraid of you, or this…” He gestured between them, a tiny, encompassing motion. “I’m afraid of being the guy who finally gets you and then… breaks you. Or breaks the promise.”

“The promise to my brother.”

He nodded, his jaw tight. “He made me swear I’d look out for you. That I’d always have your back. I thought that meant keeping my distance. Being the steady, boring friend.” A wry, self-deprecating smile. “Turns out, ‘having your back’ might mean having *you*. All of you. And I’m terrified I’ll screw it up. That I’ll be the one who causes the mess in your life, not cleans it up.”

Maya set her mug down and took the plate he offered, their fingers brushing. The jolt was still there, but it was warmer now, less like a shock and more like a current they could both ride.

“Leo,” she said, her voice firm. “My brother loved you. He trusted you with *me*. That’s the biggest vote of confidence I can imagine. And you’ve been looking out for me my whole life. You just didn’t notice that I was growing up.” She stepped closer, into his space. “The mess is already here. We made it together. The fire, the soufflé, the ragu… that was *us*. And we’re still standing. We’re still cooking.”

He searched her face, his own a storm of emotion. “What if the next mess is us? What if we’re the ones who collapse?”

“Then we start over,” she said simply. “No recipe. Just us. Like you said.”

He let out a long, slow breath, the tension in his shoulders easing. He reached up, his hand coming to rest on her cheek, his thumb brushing the faint smudge of flour she’d missed by her ear—a ghost of last night’s cleanup. The touch was tender, certain.

“Okay,” he said.

It wasn’t a grand declaration. It was an agreement. A decision. *Okay. Let’s try. Let’s be the mess.*

She turned her head to kiss his palm, a gesture so intimate it made his breath catch. Then she took her plate and sat at the small, scarred kitchen table—*their* table—and began to eat. The eggs were perfect. Fluffy, moist, seasoned just right.

“See?” she said around a bite. “Low and slow.”

He sat across from her, watching her, his own food forgotten for a moment. “Yeah,” he said, a wonder in his voice. “I see.”

Outside, the city was waking up. Inside, in their tiny, aromatic kitchen, the simmer had become a steady, promising heat. It wasn’t boiling over. It wasn’t going cold. It was just… cooking. And for the first time, they were both brave enough to stay in the kitchen and see what would happen next.
