**Chapter2 – The Shared Project**  

The glass‑walled conference room buzzed with the low hum of fluorescent lights and the clatter of keyboards. Clara stared at the digital blueprint on her tablet. The historic building—half ruin, half relic—loomed like a puzzle waiting to be solved.  

“We need a phased approach,” she said, voice crisp and efficient. “Demolition first, then structural assessment. Delays aren’t an option.”  

Elias leaned against the table, fingers tracing the edge of the plan. *Phased* made him wince; too many projects had collapsed under the weight of haste. “The foundation’s compromised,” he pointed out. “You can’t rip out sections before we know whether the load‑bearing walls are even sound.”  

The word *standard* landed like a hammer. It was a phrase Clara had heard before, a shield against criticism. Her eyes flicked to her own hand—steady on the tablet, then a quick tuck of a loose strand of hair behind her ear. A tell she hadn’t meant to reveal.  

“Your method is… thorough,” Elias said, his tone neutral. *Thorough* sounded to her like a jab.  

The door slid open. Their supervisor, Marla, stepped in with a folder. “Gentlemen, the client wants the first phase done by Friday. No exceptions.” She glanced at Elias, then at Clara. “You’re lead architect. Clara’s your project manager.”  

The assignment hung there, a silent challenge.  

---  

That evening the office had emptied to shadows and the steady thrum of computers. Clara stayed late, cursor blinking over a spreadsheet. The door creaked open.  

Elias stood in the doorway, two coffees in hand. “You’re still here.”  

She looked up, surprised. “Just finalizing the timeline.”  He set the cups down—one steaming, the other already cooling. “Your sleep cycle’s been off since six,” he said, more observation than accusation.  

Clara’s breath hitched. *Concern* was a word she’d learned to distrust; *observation* felt safer. “I’m fine.”  

“Fine is a metric, not a state.” He placed the warm cup at her elbow. “You’ve been staring at the same line for an hour.”  

Her thumb hovered over the Enter key. The spreadsheet read **12:47 AM**—she’d been stuck on that cell for twenty minutes, thumb hovering like it might write itself. Unsaid words pressed against her ribs—*I’m not fine; I’m terrified of being wrong, terrified of being right*.  “I don’t need help.”  

“I’m not offering help.” He turned to leave, then paused. “But I’m in the adjacent office if you need… structural advice.”  

The door closed, leaving the aroma of coffee curling in the air.  

---  

Later, in his own workspace, Elias spread the blueprints across a second monitor. Clara’s timeline was efficient, but it skimmed over safety. He knew—*knew*—that without a firmer foundation, the building would not hold.  His phone buzzed. A text from his mentor:  
*“Perfection is the enemy of progress. Sometimes you have to trust the people you work with.”*  

He stared at the message, then at Clara’s name blinking in his contacts. *Not yet*, he typed, then deleted it.  

The screen went dark. Outside, the city pulsed with its endless rhythm, but inside the office two people sat in quiet, each carrying a weight they could not yet speak aloud.
