### Chapter 8: The Void of Success  

The champagne was a 1996 Dom Pérignon, and it tasted like nothing at all.  

Elias stood at the floor‑to‑ceiling windows of the corner office he’d just earned—the promotion arrived at 4:47 p.m., the exact moment the merger closed. Yet the bottle on his desk sat untouched; he poured one glass, let it breathe for twenty minutes, and then forgot it entirely.  

From thirty‑seven stories up the city unfolded like an abstraction: a grid of amber and white, the river a dark ribbon cutting through the glow. He’d chased this view for eleven years, and in the darker moments of his twenties he’d imagined standing here and finally *knowing* he’d made it.  

His phone buzzed, buzzed again, then buzzed a third time. Colleagues, clients, PR begging for a soundbite. He set it face‑down on the glass desktop; the vibration ceased, but the tiny seizure underneath persisted until he shoved it into his pocket.  

Silence settled—the kind of silence a room engineered for impressiveness never produces for living.  

He thought suddenly of Clara’s apartment—the cramped studio in the old industrial district she’d once mentioned in passing. “It’s falling apart,” she’d laughed, “but the light in the morning is extraordinary.” The light here was extraordinary, too—*art‑gallery* extraordinary. He could host dinners up here; prospective clients would be impressed; his mother would finally stop asking when he’d *settle*.  

He poured the champagne down the sink in the private bathroom, watched the golden liquid spiral away, and felt only a faint satisfaction that the crystal was lead‑filled, the fixtures Italian, that every surface had been chosen by someone who understood wealth.  

---

### Clara arrives at nine, as requested  

The elevator demanded a keycard; the hallway demanded a second; the door required a code that Elias had to spell out letter by letter, like a satellite transmission.  

“You look like you’ve just won something,” she said, stepping into the doorway.  

Her voice was careful, calibrated—as if she’d rehearsed it. She wore the black dress from his mother’s dinner, hair pulled back, and even in this palatial space she seemed to be attending a funeral.  

“I did win something,” he said, gesturing at the windows, the view, the glittering expanse of his achievement. “The merger closed. Northstar is ours.”  

She nodded slowly, a beat of something he couldn’t name passing between them, and then moved further in, eyes scanning the white leather sofa, the abstract sculpture on its pedestal, the dining table that could seat eight yet had never held more than two.  

“This is beautiful,” she said—*precise*, but the tone was *wrong*, too even, too practiced, like someone commenting on a museum piece behind glass.  

“I thought we could celebrate,” he said, reaching for another bottle. “I had catering send up something—”  

“Elias.” Her voice stopped him. He turned, champagne in hand, to find her standing in the middle of the room, sketchpad tucked under her arm. The sight of its worn corners, the pencil tucked into the spiral, tightened something in his chest.  

“I can’t stay long,” she said.  

The words landed like a closing door.  

“I thought—” He swallowed. “I thought we could talk. About everything. About what happens next.”  “What’s next?” Clara repeated, tilting her head so the sculpture’s light caught the sharp line of her jaw. “You won. The merger closed. Your father’s legacy is secure. Isn’t that what comes next?”  

Stressing *your father* made the possessive feel like a weapon. A flicker of heat—defensiveness—flared and then dissolved into cold.  

“It’s not just about my father,” he said.  

“No,” Clara whispered, her voice softening just enough to sting. “It’s about you. It’s always been about you. That’s the problem, isn’t it?”  

He couldn’t parse the argument; the sequence of cause and effect slipped through his fingers like water through a sieve.  

“I got you something,” he said, desperate. He pulled a navy‑blue velvet box from his pocket.  

Clara looked at the box, then at him.  

“You got me something.”  

He opened it: a platinum chain with a single diamond pendant. “I saw it and I thought of you. I thought—”  

“You thought you could fix it.” Her tone was quiet, almost gentle—*worse* than anger. “You thought if you found the right gift, said the right words, everything would go back to the way it was.”  

“That’s not—”  

“Elias.” She stepped closer, and for a heartbeat he imagined her hand might rise, might touch his chest. Instead it fell back to her side. “I don’t need you to buy me things. I don’t need you to win things. I need you to *be* somewhere. With me. In the mess and the uncertainty and the not‑knowing.”  

“I’m here,” he whispered, his voice hoarse. “I’m right here.”  

“Are you?” She looked at him the way she had at the gala, the way she had on the train—with that unsettling clarity that made him feel stripped down to his components. “Because I feel like I’ve been talking to a version of you that’s performing. That’s playing a role. And I don’t know how to reach the person underneath.”  The words struck somewhere below his ribs. He opened his mouth to defend, to explain, to *fix*—but nothing came out. The silence stretched, vast and terrible.  

Clara brushed the velvet box lightly, as one might touch something fragile.  

“It’s beautiful,” she said. “You should give it to someone who wants to be bought.”  

She turned toward the door. He didn’t follow. The city blazed behind him; the diamond winked on its velvet bed; the apartment—the beautiful, expensive, air‑conditioned emptiness—meant nothing to anyone.

### Afterward  

Later—much later—he sat on the white leather sofa and stared at the skyline, the word *presence* looping in his mind. *Presence* meant being there, but also being *present*—attentive, available, tuned to another’s frequency. He’d spent his whole life learning to be *there* (at meetings, at dinners, at galas) without ever learning to be *present*. They were two entirely different things.  

His phone buzzed again. He pulled it out—his mother, his assistant, his father, three clients, two colleagues, a journalist—then turned it off.  

The apartment was silent. The only sound was the hum of climate control.  He thought of Clara’s radiator, clanking every forty‑five minutes, and of the way she’d described the light in her studio—*extraordinary*. He thought of the fact that she’d come tonight at all, despite the distance he’d put between them, despite his inability to meet her where she was. She’d come. That had to mean something.  

He turned his phone back on, typed a message, deleted it, typed another.  

*I’m sorry. I don’t know how to be what you need. But I want to learn.*  

The words felt inadequate—too small, too simple, too much a problem to be solved rather than a wound to be tended. Yet they were true, and after eleven years of saying the right things for the wrong reasons, *true* had become the only thing that mattered.  

He pressed send.  

The message vanished into the digital void, and Elias sat in his beautiful, empty apartment, surrounded by everything he’d ever wanted, waiting for the only thing that might save him: a reply.
