**Chapter 9 – Rebuilding fromthe Ground Up (Edited)**  

The park bench groaned under their weight, its iron arms frosted by the night’s bite. Clara’s boots scraped the fallen leaves as she leaned toward Elias, the scar above his eyebrow catching the amber spill of a streetlamp. Her fingers hovered at his collarbone then slipped away, as if afraid to touch the heat that still smouldered between them.  

The air tasted of pine and unanswered breath.  

“My lease is up next month,” she said, her voice cracking the casual surface. “The apartment above the laundromat—walls that sweat in summer, rent half of what Northstar charges. But I’m scared to sign another lease.” She didn’t look at him; she stared at the rain pooling in the cuffs of his sleeve.  

Elias straightened his jacket, the movement stiff as a hinge. The dim yellow of the shelter canopy washed over his face, turning his features into a study of contradictions: sharp cheekbones softened by the city’s relentless hum, eyes flicking between the open manhole and the hollows of her cheeks.  

“You could own a whole block of this city,” he began, but she shook her head.  

“I’m not a block,” she replied.  

He exhaled slowly, the shift from her stillness to the city’s traffic sharp on his nerves. “You could. You’d need a design partner.”  

A beat stretched, hanging like a risk assessment—or a promise.  

Clara’s shoulders twitched. “What if I’m not the same person who drew those stupid wireframe sketches in your office?”  

“The person who drew those wireframes is the one who’s standing right in front of you now,” he whispered. His calloused thumb brushed a speck of dirt from her palm. “You’re more than the credit‑checking, brick‑and‑mortar Clara. You’re still the one who thinks about the kids who’ll walk through the doorways we designed for ghosts.”  

A crow landed on the bench’s edge, its wings twitching. Clara said nothing, but her knee pressed against his calf—a micro‑adjustment, tiny, deliberate, flawed.  

Across the park a lamppost buzzed to life, its light pooling around Elias’s shoes and tracing the scar like a crack in porcelain.  

“I leave Monday,” he admitted, the words disarmed by the crunch of leaves under his tread. “The merger’s live. They want a deposit in the Caribbean by Thursday.”  

Clara’s laugh was wet, fractured. She reached for the fraying collar of his coat, tugged it down over one shoulder until the cuff brushed her wrist. “You’re supposed to be a fixer,” she murmured. “Fixing the world, not fixing your ego.”  

Elias knelt, his fingers working the knot at her temple. “I wanted to fix this,” he said, the words landing on her shoulder. “But you won’t let me.”  

She shuddered. “And I won’t let me.”  The scorpion‑tailed wind carried the admission to a patch of dying grass. Between them, the space narrowed into something deliberate—neither fleeing nor conflating. Just repair‑tents pitched in the open.  

“You’re leaving?” he asked, really seeing her, as sweat beaded on the edge of her collarbone. Her pulse thrummed against his fingertip, a counterpoint to the hum of the streetlights.  

“I’m staying,” she said. Not a denial, but a referendum.  

The bench’s shadow swallowed them whole, a womb of creeping ivy and unspoken things. Elias’s hands slipped into her jacket pockets, the rhythm of his heart syncing with the metronome of her breath.  

Across the street a man laughed—brittle as crushed glass—while traffic drowned the scene. Clara hummed, a fractured melody from their college days, her chin resting on his shoulder. He didn’t flinch.  

Somewhere beneath the city streets a pipe clanked, protesting the weight of the world above. They limped home together, roots and sparks knotted in the marrow.
