## What to Keep in Mind While Revising  

| Issue | Why It Matters | Quick Fix |
|-------|----------------|-----------|
| **Redundant “I” statements** | Repetition can slow the pace. | Use active verbs or vary sentence openings. |
| **Long, breathless explanations** | The story is meant to feel claustrophobic; long exposition can break that feeling. | Keep explanations *in‑world*—state them as the protagonist’s inner monologue, not as an omniscient narrator. |
| **Inconsistent “corporate” branding** | Mixing “Mnemonica Corp” and “GriefLine” can confuse. | Decide on one naming style and stick with it (or clarify the corporate hierarchy early). |
| **Flashback clarity** | The twist hinges on a *recognition* that should feel sudden, not confusing. | Use sensory anchors (e.g., “tremor of the lower lip”) to cue the reader that something personal is happening. |
| **Stake escalation** | Readers need to know *what* is at risk. | Add a brief line about what will happen if the archive isn’t cleared (e.g., “If they trace it back, they’ll scrub my entire credit history”). |

- **Make the anomaly’s discovery feel abrupt** (short sentences, sensory spikes).

- **End on a punchy image** (door clicks from the hall, implying an unknown presence).
