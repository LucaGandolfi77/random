**CHAPTER 10 – PHANTOM LEDGER**  

The tower rose from the city’s gut like a needle from a dead man’s arm—chrome and black glass, forty‑seven stories of curated light. No signage. No logo. Just a name whispered in the lower rings: **Tower Seven**. You didn’t walk up to Tower Seven. You were summoned, or you were nothing.  

The fragment behind my left eye pulsed a single word: *summoned*.  

---

### The Arrival  

The security drone at the entrance didn’t scan me. It **recognized** me—iris, gait, the distinctive rhythm of a heartbeat flagged in three separate databases. The glass doors part‑ed like a held breath.  

“Ms. Voss,” a voice said. Female. Synthetic. The building itself speaking. “Director Senkova is expecting you on the forty‑fourth floor. East wing. You’ll find the conference room through the glass doors.”  

I stepped into an elevator that smelled of nothing. No ozone. No rust. No rain. Just clean, recycled air and the faint undertone of something floral—orchids, maybe, or the synthetic equivalent. The kind of smell that costs more than my apartment.  

The numbers climbed. My fragment pulsed.  

---

### The Forty‑Fourth Floor  

The forty‑fourth floor was a cathedral of surveillance. Floor‑to‑ceiling windows on three sides displayed the city below like a circuit board—neon veins, monorail arteries, the flickering pulse of a million lives being monitored, archived, traded. Desks lined the perimeter, each occupied by a technician in white, their ocular implants glowing soft blue as they processed data streams I couldn’t see.  At the center of the room a single table waited. Black glass. Two chairs.  

She was already sitting there.  

Drea Senkova looked older than her file photos—late fifties, maybe sixty—with silver threading through dark hair pulled back into a severe knot. Her face was a mask of professional calm, the composure you learn when you’ve built an empire on stolen childhoods. She wore a simple charcoal suit, no jewelry, no visible implants. Just eyes. Dark, unblinking eyes that matched mine.  

“Maren,” she said. Not a question. “Sit.”  

I sat.  

“You found the drive,” she said. “Cael was always sentimental. I thought he’d delete it himself, but he surprised me. He surprises me.” She paused. “He still thinks he’s making choices.”  

“What happened to him?”  

“He’s where he belongs—processing center in the lower grid. He’ll be reassigned in six months. New face. New name. New debt ledger.” She smiled, thin and cold. “He’ll forget he ever felt conflicted. That’s the benefit of working for us. You get to forget the things that hurt.”  

I didn’t answer.  

“Let me show you something.” She tapped the table. A holographic display flicked to life—files, images, data streams. Childhood photos. Hundreds of them. Faces I didn’t recognize, ages ranging from four to twelve, each tagged with a string of numbers.  

“Cell One through Cell Twelve,” Senkova said. “Twelve hundred children harvested between 2041 and 2058. Your cell—Cell Nine—was the most productive. Forty‑three subjects. You were the only one with **first‑witness fidelity**.”  

“What does that mean?”  

“It means your memories don’t decay like other people’s. Most children lose sensory clarity after a few years—emotions blur, details fade, the world becomes a vague impression. But you… you remembered *everything*. The texture of your mother’s coat. The exact pitch of her voice. The way light fell through a window at a specific angle on a specific afternoon. You recorded the world like a machine.”  “I was seven.”  

“You were seven,” she agreed. “And you were worth more than any adult we’ve ever harvested. Your first memories—the ones before we took you—sold for a premium on the Primer market. Buyers describe the experience as… transcendent. They feel what you felt. The terror of that room. The cold. The hands on your skull.” She leaned forward, and for a moment hunger flickered in her eyes. “Do you remember any of that?”  

A child’s sob. A laugh. *Mama, I want to go home.*  

“No,” I said. “You took them.”  

“We did.” She didn’t blink. “And now I’m offering to give them back.”  

---

### The Offer  

The offer was simple. A position in the Primer division—senior archivist, overseeing the refinement process. I’d have access to every stolen year, every lost morning. I’d get to *feel* what they took from me, packaged and processed, ready for consumption.  

In exchange, I’d help them improve the product.  

“You want me to help you steal from other kids,” I said.  

“I want you to help us *understand* what we’re selling.” She stood, walked to the window, and looked out at the city. “The Primer line generates three hundred million credits a quarter. It’s not just a product—it’s a paradigm. We’re teaching people to feel again. To experience empathy in its purest form. Your memories are the foundation of that.”  

“By taking them from children who don’t remember.”  

“By giving them to people who need them.” She turned. “You think I’m a monster, Maren. Maybe I am. But look at this city. Look at what we’ve become—drones scanning each other for profit, memories traded like currency, emotions edited out because they’re not *efficient*. Primer is the only thing that makes people *feel* anymore. And your childhood—your terror, your loss, your desperate need for home—it’s the most powerful emotional data we’ve ever collected.”  

She sat back down.  

“I’m not asking you to forgive me. I’m asking you to help me perfect it. And in return, you’ll get everything you lost.”  

The drive Cael had given me sat on the table, a small black rectangle that contained Senkova’s personal server access—the keys to her kingdom, or so he’d said.  

“You know I could take this and walk out,” I said. “Sell what you’ve built to your competitors. Burn it all down.”  

“You could try.” She smiled again, that thin cold curve. “But the fragment in your head is keyed to this building. Remove it, and the memory dies—permanently. Everything you ever were, everything you ever felt at seven years old—gone. Poof. Like it never existed.” She leaned forward. “You can walk out of here, Maren. But you’ll be empty. And you’ll know it.”  My fragment pulsed. My seven‑year‑old voice cried out, muffled, distant, reaching for something I couldn’t name.  

I picked up the drive.  

I stood.  

“I need time.”  

“You have until midnight,” Senkova said. “After that, the fragment degrades. It’s a failsafe. I built it myself.”  

The doors opened. I stepped into the elevator.  

“Maren,” she said just before the doors closed. “Cael didn’t give you the real drive. He gave you a copy. The real one is in his head—literally. It’s encoded in his neural architecture. If you want to burn this place down, you’ll have to go back for it.”  

The doors shut.  

---

### The Escape  

I walked out into a rain that felt like needles. The city pulsed around me—neon, surveillance, the constant hum of a million memories being traded, edited, erased. My fragment pulsed in time with the streetlights.  

I had a copy of a drive that didn’t work. A fragment that would die at midnight. A seven‑year‑old voice crying in a room with no windows, no way to reach her.  

And somewhere in the lower grid, Cael was being processed. Re‑wired. Made to forget he’d ever felt conflicted.  

I pulled my coat tighter and opened a channel I hadn’t used in years—a dead frequency, a name I’d sworn I’d never speak again.  

“Dekko,” I said. “I need a favor.”  

Silence. Then a crackle, and a voice I hadn’t heard since the sub‑levels—chewing on something, synthetic, bitter.  

“Voss. You’re late.”  

“I know. I have a job—memory‑scrubber, lower ring, tonight. Can you get me in?”  

Another crackle. A pause.  

“What’s the catch?”  

I looked up at the rain, at the towers, at the city that had taken everything from me and called it a transaction.  

“I need to erase someone,” I said. “Someone who doesn’t want to be erased.”  

“Name?”  

I said it. The frequency went silent.  

Then Dekko laughed, that hollow, synthetic laugh.  

“Voss,” he said. “You’re either the stupidest person I’ve ever met, or the best. Get to the sub‑levels in an hour. I’ll have a station ready.”  

He cut the link.  

I pocketed the useless drive and started walking toward the monorail. The fragment pulsed. The rain fell. Somewhere in this city, my seven‑year‑old self was still crying.  And tonight, I was going to find her.  

Even if it meant burning down everything Senkova had built.  

Even if it meant erasing myself in the process.  ---

The rain never stopped in this city. It just kept washing the neon into the gutters, carrying the memories of a million forgotten people into the deep.  

I didn’t look back at the tower.  

I knew exactly what I’d left behind.
