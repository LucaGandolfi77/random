**Chapter 2 –*The Broker***  

The flooded alley beneath Kessler Spine leaked that same sour perfume of ozone and rust that clung to the sub‑levels. Salvaged neon tubes—once bright рекла for defunct billboards—pulsed jaundiced orange over the stagnant water, turning every ripple into a flickering vein. Maren pressed the insulated case tighter against her ribs; inside the phantom‑memory fragment throbbed like a pulse‑laser, its edges smearing with a faint violet glow whenever a street‑lamp caught it.  

She ducked into the market proper, a maze of corrugated‑metal stalls and tarps bolted together to keep the tide at bay. Vendors hawked everything from synthetic opium to recompressed protein paste, their voices layered over the constant hiss of water pumps and the low‑frequency hum of hidden surveillance drones that buzzed like angry insects overhead. In one corner a rusted awning collapsed, exposing a narrow alcove where the light never reached.  

Sable sat there.  She was a wizened figure, half‑covered in flickering chrome‑plated dermal patches that scrolled tiny ads for memory‑auctions. A single strand of copper wire wound itself around a cracked ocular implant that glowed a sickly green. When Maren leaned in, Sable didn’t look up; she tapped a sequence on a rusted holo‑pad and a thin sheet of paper slipped out, inked in a fluid that seemed to move on its own.  

“**Child’s voice**,” Sable whispered, as if the words themselves might attract eyes. “You brought me something that doesn’t belong in any archive.”  

Maren set the case on the slick floor and opened it. The fragment spilled out—a shimmering slab of synthetic cortex—and for a heartbeat the entire alley seemed to hold its breath. The violet glow intensified, and an auditory snippet cut through the market’s clamor: a raw, ragged whine of a child sobbing, then a sudden, fierce laugh—*“Mama, I want to go home!”* The voice was unmistakably Maren’s, not the polished, edited versions she sold for a living but a trembling, unfiltered sound that cut straight to bone.  

Sable’s green implant flared, cycling through a cascade of data tags: **PHANTOM‑REC‑ETH**, **UNCATALOGED‑CHILD**, **SUSPENDED‑TAG**. She pressed a fingertip to the fragment; the surface rippled like liquid glass.  

“It’s real,” Sable said, her voice suddenly flat, stripped of the market’s static. “Not a glitch. Not a cut‑and‑paste. Someone *planted* this. Someone *wanted* you to hear it.”  

Maren’s breath hitched. The child’s voice was *hers*—seven‑year‑old Maren Voss, waving a small metallic toy that no longer existed in any catalog. The recall surged, a faint memory of a concrete hallway lit by flickering fluorescent strips, a slamming door, and a pair of gloved hands pressing a cold metal device to the back of her skull. Then the image dissolved, leaving only the wet echo of that child’s sob.  

“**Why am I hearing it now?**” she asked, voice barely louder than the drip of the leaky coolant pipe she’d left behind in the sub‑levels. “I… I thought I was the one who deleted it.”  

Sable’s implant cycled through a series of red warnings before she answered. “Because it was *locked* inside the archive of a dead executive. Someone smuggled it out, then *re‑embedded* it in a fragment you could find. It’s a beacon. It leads to the place where they kept you.”  

Maren’s stomach clenched. A place they kept her. A child’s memory of a windowless room—she’d lived it, but she’d never known the walls had been real.  

Before she could speak, a low metallic clang rang out from the far end of the alley. A patrol of Mnemonica enforcers flickered into view, their black uniforms patched with luminescent sigils that pulsed in time with the market’s neon. Their visors scanned the crowd, cataloguing every transaction, every whisper.  

Sable snapped her holo‑pad shut with a crack that sounded like a gunshot. “Leave,” she hissed, gesturing toward a narrow drainage grate that led to an even darker tunnel. “If they see you with that thing—”  

Maren didn’t need more. She slipped the fragment back into the case, shoved it into her jacket, and followed Sable through the grate. The tunnel swallowed them in a rush of cold air and the distant, metallic groan of water pumps. Above them the market’s neon faded, replaced by the dim, amber glow of emergency strips that flickered erratically, as if the whole world were trying to remember something that no longer existed.  When they reached the far end of the tunnel, Sable stopped. She pressed a hand to Maren’s shoulder and, for the first time, met her eyes. Behind that green glow—behind the implant—Maren saw something else flicker: a memory, not of a market or a sweep, but of a child’s hand reaching for a door that was never opened.  *“You think you’re looking for your past,”* Sable whispered, *“but you’re actually looking for the people who *stole* it. And they’re still watching. They’ll pay you for it, or they’ll take it back. Either way, you’re already on a ledger you can’t pay.”*  

Maren felt the weight of the case pressing against her ribs. The fragment pulsed—as if aware of the new debt she’d just accrued—against her chest. The alley’s cold water lapped at her boots, and in that moment the city’s neon glare seemed to stare back, indifferent and hungry.  

She stepped into the deeper darkness, the fragment’s glow dimming but never extinguishing, while the echo of her own seven‑year‑old voice lingered behind her—*a child crying in a windowless room, begging for a mother who never came back.* The chase had begun, and the alley behind her sealed shut without a sound.
