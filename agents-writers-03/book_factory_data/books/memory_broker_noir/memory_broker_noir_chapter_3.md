**CHAPTER 3 – DEBTSCHEDULES**

The rain sliced through the cracked skin of the upper‑grid apartment block the way a blade severs a fiber‑optic cable: clean, precise, leaving a slick sheet that mirrors the maze of street‑lights—a neon grid stitched into the night.

I ducked past the flickering sign **PENITENCE LABS**—more a warning than an invitation—and against the distant wail of sirens that curled through the streets like angry harps.

Flashes of financial data—brief, disjointed—ignited the corner of my left eye. **DEBT: 16 500 MEMCREDITS, DEFAULT**. The alert pulsed in cyan across the edge of my ocular implant, a neon ghost that seemed to echo my own pulse. I’d been hitting the office a little too hard for the last five hundred credits; the ledger’s ink kept over‑printing like an old VHS tape stuck on a loop.

I slid the hard‑wired contacts from my wristboard into the blackening socket at the base of the panel. It hissed—a sterile hiss that tasted of ozone and server dust—then cleared. The numbers on the screen faded into an amber world of bars and data points. The board above the table, a blinking list in binary, announced tonight’s **Penance Collected**.

Across the room a man in a data‑chip‑embedded trench coat leaned against the wall. His face was a matte mask beneath a hood, eyes hidden. He slipped a thin, encrypted scan—one of those puppies the street‑dealers sell out of cracked coats—under my gloved hand. He didn’t speak of negotiations; he was here to erase. Everyone called him **Mr. Lin**.

“The memory you’re removing isn’t… standard,” Mr. Lin murmured, his voice low enough to be swallowed by the hum of the building. He pressed his palm to a tablet. The screen peeled back an ancient dossier.

It was catalogued under **CASE: 41‑03‑Waterfall_74**, filed under the same protocols that govern the city’s elite executives—the brains behind the pilot channels that power the grid. On the surface the file was junk: a dining‑hall scene, a father’s embrace, an accusation of treachery, an “approved by” stamp. But when I fed it into the Reema‑Cod Fear protocol, the algorithm spit out an “utterance” and sealed the hunt.

The packet was larger than any human memory—six hundred terabytes of raw, half‑filmed blue noise, the auditory imprint of a heartbeat on the stomach of a dead man’s ex‑wife. Before she died she’d tried to siphon every human on the grid with her own unwilling body. The memory had been archived as a duplicate—canned as a “spectate” file, wrapped in pairs of old bulbs threaded around scapular data campaigns. The kind of memory showrooms would kill for.

A **? ? ?** flagged the metadata of one frame—the only mark of corruption in the entire capture—hinting that the file was more than a stakeholder copy.

Mr. Lin watched me. “We’ve had a leak,” he said, tapping the wristboard. “This is the only place that can fix it.”

He wasn’t a banker. He was a figure—a phantom with a metallic boot that had turned a badge into evidence.

I swallowed. The debt‑campaign on that cheap nightmare device I’d bought on the black market—one that held no ridicule, only raw fraud—pulsed like a siren crashing on a cracked sidewalk.

The entire ring of **memory‑dealers** seemed to hover on the edge of a ghostly threshold, each transaction balanced by a ledger of numbers. Ten jobs later I’d decoded the code that could fix it.

> **“What do you want this memory used for?”** I asked.

Most of the people above me trembled like thorn‑plants. The truth was simple: Mr. Lin wouldn’t pay me, and I wouldn’t take revenge. Instead he offered a deal—if the memory were deleted, its chain would collapse, and the **moni‑tions** administrator would pause collections, giving me time to settle the debt.

“I’ll hold the debt until you break the law,” he signed, the words hollow as cracked glass.

I glanced at the software line hovering in the memory. Hidden behind the calamity was a **fonds**—a black‑market depot, a data alley at the lower ring, the telephone cove I used to pick up orders from a data trader. I hovered my thumb over the menu, wondering whether to **re‑architecte** the file. His face went glassy; the whole scene trembled, a vibration seething under the surface.

Her diary left only one line: **“I have to pay money. I won’t.”**

A heavy disturbance rippled through the web of work details, and I realized no one was doing this for a ledger payment.

> **“We didn’t pay for that file. We’re going to sell it.”**

The interface highlighted the line in blood‑red. I stared at the third job on a coin.

Mr. Lin noted the groove of my pause, the hollow of his own breath. Even then I couldn’t sneak away; the moment had taken hold. I wondered if she would ever be comfortable with the debt. I read the topics I was leaving behind, the favors unpaid.

In the end, he whispered, “I can use it—this is a win for us.”

We were in an hour that felt like a bullet balanced in my palm. Outside, the rain kept falling, the neon city pulsing like a heart unwilling to stop. In that moment the storm seemed less a weather pattern and more a warning: the next strike could clear my debts—or bury them forever.
