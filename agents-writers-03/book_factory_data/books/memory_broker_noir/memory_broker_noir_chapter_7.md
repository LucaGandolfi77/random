**CHAPTER 7 – EDITED TRUTH**

The ochre ocular implant on Sable’s wrist flickered, cutting through the market’s haze like a lighthouse in a storm. Rain slicked the flooded alley; corrugated‑metal stalls dripped water from over‑worked pumps while vendors shouted above the gurgle of sewage. Maren slid the stolen chip into Sable’s reception port and heard the tell‑tale crackle of data loading.

“Late,” Sable muttered, her voice rasping through a layer of mechanical distortion. She didn’t look up; the implant’s screen sputtered red text, then cleared.

“Redaction protocol active,” Sable said, her implant glowing green as it probed deeper. “Multiple layers. Someone’s actively erasing evidence.”

Maren leaned in. Sable’s fingers danced over the glowing pads, dermal patches flashing red warnings. “They’re not just hiding this from auditors,” Sable said, finally meeting Maren’s eyes. “They’re hiding it from *themselves*.”

A week before the fragment was planted, a senior Mnemonica executive scrubbed every reference to “under‑age harvesting” from internal logs. This wasn’t a leak. It was a cover‑up.

“Who’s protecting it?” Maren asked, voice sharp.

Sable hesitated—a human hesitation in a world that tries to eliminate them. “Drea Senkova. The Primer director. The one who took your memories. She’s not just selling them anymore. She’s weaponising them.”

Maren frowned. “Weaponising?”

“She’s using your childhood—those stolen moments—to build a new Primer line. One that bends empathy itself. The fragment wasn’t a trap; it was a *summons*.”

Sable slid a data strip onto the counter. It was a fragment of a deleted Mnemonica report, heavy with redactions. “She knows your cluster of memories is unique—first‑witness fidelity. She needs you to find the others.”

Maren stared at the strip. “Why tell me this?”

Sable’s implant pulsed coldly. “Because you’re the only one who can follow the breadcrumbs. And because,” she added quietly, “you’re already on a ledger they’d kill to silence.”

A Mnemonica drone buzzed overhead, its red sigil flashing. Sable flicked a plasma‑edged blade from her sleeve and snapped it on. “Don’t waste my time, kid.”

The drone descended. Too late. With a flash of blue heat, Sable sliced it open; the machine erupted in sparks and fell silent.

Maren didn’t flinch. She slipped the data strip into her pocket and followed Sable through a rusted grate down into the tunnels. The air was thick with ozone and the sour scent of desperation. Water pumped somewhere far below, echoing in the darkness.

“You should’ve gone to Lam,” Sable said, loading Maren’s satchel with spare tools. “He’d have a gun. Fewer questions.”

“But he’s not that person anymore,” Maren replied. “He’s *him*.”

Sable’s jaw tightened. “Does that matter to you?”

“It matters to *him*,” Maren said. “It matters to *me*.”

Sable muttered a curse and gestured toward the tunnel’s mouth. “Move. Before the rest of them find you.”

They emerged into the neon‑green glow of the Lapse, the city’s perpetual rain casting long shadows on the cracked pavement. The data strip pulsed faintly against Maren’s thigh—still warm from Sable’s chip. An old memory flickered at the edge of her mind, a seven‑year‑old voice that wasn’t hers:

> *“Mama, I want to go home.”*

Sable glanced at the sky, where a Mnemonica alert pinged from an unseen pager. *“Asset detected.”* The fragment of memory screamed in response.

Sable didn’t answer when Maren asked, “Who’s watching?” Instead, she turned and said, “They already have a plan.”  

The fragment’s glow bent around Maren like a compass, not just a memory but a map. The real architects of her stolen past were closer than she thought—and they were far from done with her.

*The tunnel swallowed them, the neon‑green light from the waste‑water below turning their shadows into stark silhouettes. Somewhere above, a drone’s rotors stuttered to a halt, and the market fell silent. The hunt had only just begun.*
