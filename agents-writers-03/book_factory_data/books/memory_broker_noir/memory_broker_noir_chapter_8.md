**CHAPTER 8: SURVEILLANCE STATEMENT**  

Rain hit the window at the same rhythm as my own heartbeat. I wasn’t sleeping—I was mapping exits.  

My apartment was a storage unit that someone had bolted a desk onto—a single room beneath a recycling hub in the mid‑ring. Monorails screamed past overhead every eleven minutes, and the landlord changed the locks quarterly. I added my own lock on top of his. The fragment rested in a shielded case beneath my mattress, pulsing faintly, like a second heartbeat I hadn’t consented to.  

Three Mnemonica drones hung in the alley when I came home. They drifted in a pattern that wasn’t random—triangulation. Someone on the ground was feeding them coordinates.  

I pulled on my coat and slipped out through the service hatch. No lobby. No cameras I hadn’t already accounted for.  

---

Kess’s rooftop was on the forty‑second floor of a gutted hotel in Thornwall—a building the city had stopped pretending to maintain. The wind tasted of copper and engine coolant. Kess had a folding table, three laptops, and a parabolic antenna she’d pilfered from a Mnemonica listening post in Kessler Spine.  

She didn’t look up when I climbed through the access panel. She was already mid‑sweep.  

“Third drone, lower left, two‑hundred meters. Mnemonica Corpsig series. New model—IR pulse instead of laser. Harder to spoof.” She tapped a key. “Your implant leaks. Old firmware.”  

“I know.”  

“Do you?” She finally met my gaze. One eye was real. The other was a matte‑black implant with a cracked housing. “You have three watchlists on you—Mnemonica, GriefLine, and someone else. No division, just a clearance code: **FRAGMENT‑7‑RETRIEVAL**.”  

I said nothing.  “They consider you property.” Kess swiveled her chair. “Your fragment is classified as retrieval stock. Not evidence. Not a memory. *Inventory.*”  

The wind pressed against the building. Somewhere below, a monorail hissed through a curve.  

“GriefLine,” I said. “What about GriefLine?”  

Kess turned her chair. “You owe them sixteen thousand five hundred MEMCREDITS. Sixteen months of memory‑rent unpaid. They don’t want your money, Maren. They want the case under your mattress. The debt—that’s the leash. You can’t pay it. You won’t negotiate. The only way to clear it is to hand over what you’re carrying. So they’re *paying* themselves to take it back.”  

I felt the fragment pulse behind my ribs, answered by my own stupid, borrowed heartbeat.  

“I’m not their asset.”  

“You are.” Kess closed the laptops. “The question is what you do with that.”  

---

I didn’t go back to the apartment.  

I went to the Lapse.  

The rooftop bar was half‑flooded from a pipe burst three weeks earlier. Water pooled ankle‑deep around bar stools. Regulars stood on crates, perched on overturned tables, drinking with their feet in it. Neon from the district below bled through a broken skylight in thin washes of magenta and sick green.  

Vesper was in the back corner. She saw me before I saw her. That was the thing about her—she watches like she’s already reading the outcome.  

“You’re being followed.” I slid into a stool. It wobbled in the water.  

“Yes.”  “Lam?”  

“Lam is one of them. Not all of them.” She turned her glass. The liquid caught the neon and held a bruise of color. “Sit. You look like you’ve been running calculations instead of sleeping.”  

I told her about the watchlists. About GriefLine. About the fragment being inventory. I kept my voice flat, my hands still.  

Vesper listened. When I finished, the bar noise filled the silence—shouting, a glass clinked, a vendor two tables over hawking compressed memories from a battered case.  

“Someone inside Mnemonica wanted you activated.” She was quiet for a long moment. “You didn’t answer my question.” She leaned forward. “Who told you to come here?”  

I didn’t answer.  

Vesper reached into her coat, pulled out a thumb‑sized data strip, and slid it across the wet bar top. It projected a single image onto the table: a seven‑year‑old girl on a metal cot in a concrete room, fluorescent light above, a stenciled number on the wall behind her.  

**Nine.**  

My hands went cold—not from the temperature, but from recognition.  

“It’s me,” I said, flat as a report.  

“Taken by who?”  

“Ludo Ash’s device.” The name landed like a stone. “Chief of Mnemonica’s internal review board—the man who signs off on the harvest authorizations.”  

I stared at the image. Seven years old, sitting on a cot, hands on knees, eyes open but empty, as if taught never to look for an escape.  

“How did you get this?”  

“I didn’t. It was slipped into my dead‑drop by someone who wanted it found. Along with a note.” She tapped the strip again. Text bloomed beneath the photograph:  

**Use Cael. Not against you. For you.**  

She didn’t need to say it. “Lam is Cael.”  

“And you knew.”  

“I suspected.” She tapped the strip twice. “Someone inside Mnemonica activated the fragment. They planted it in that dead man’s archive three weeks ago and let it surface through you. Every step since—Sable, the exchange, Cael—has been engineered to bring you here.”  

The neon pulsed. The water lapped. A vendor two tables over laughed, high and sharp, then fell silent.  

“Why?” I asked.  

Vesper’s expression didn’t change, but something behind it did—a flicker, quick and human. “Because Drea Senkova is tired of managing you from a distance. She wants you in the building. She wants you close enough to choose.”  

I slipped the strip into my pocket. It was warm—impossible, but true.  

“Find Lam,” Vesper said. “Not to run from him. To use him. Because Cael is the only person in this city who knows the layout of Senkova’s server farm, and he’s been trying to warn you in the only way he knows how.”  

I stood. The stool scraped the wet floor.  

“The fragment,” I said. “If someone inside Mnemonica planted it, why does it feel like mine?”  

Vesper held my gaze for a long moment. “Because they made it from you. And then they gave it back. That’s the cruelest part, Maren. It *is* yours. They just decided when you get to remember.”  

I left the Lapse with the strip in my pocket and Vesper’s words settling into the base of my skull like a second implant. The rooftop door screamed on its hinges behind me. The rain had stopped, but the air still dripped from every surface—neon, condensation, a chemical sting that clung to the back of my throat.  

Three drones still hovered above, still triangulating.  

I pulled my collar tight and walked into the grid, the city watching every step.
