**Chapter 9 – Personal Reconciliation (Revised)**  

The maintenance tunnel reeked of ozone and rust, neon bleeding through cracked windows overhead. Maren moved fast, boots echoing on the metal grating. She’d traced Cael’s signal to this abandoned artery beneath the monorail lines—somewhere the city’s surveillance grid thinned to static.

He waited in the shadows, hands open, so he wasn’t packing heat.

“So you found me,” Cael said, his voice still carrying that hollow edge from the first time he’d called her *the child from Cell Nine*. Up close he looked younger—too young. The kind of face that hadn’t learned to lie yet.  

Maren stepped into the pool of light spilling from her implant. “You planted that fragment.”  

“I opened a door.” His fingers twitched, as if he wanted to touch something but couldn’t decide what. “Mnemonica shut down the holding cells three months ago, standard procedure when audit flags get too loud. But they didn’t destroy the memories—they transferred them.”  

“Transferred them how?”  

“Through the Primer network. Your memories—every first‑witness, every raw impression—went into the system. They’re the backbone of the new Primer line now.” He pulled the data drive from his jacket pocket and tossed it onto the grating. “Mnemosyne’s selling *Primer Echo* memories. It lets buyers experience childhood trauma like a luxury commodity.”  

Maren’s ocular implant flickered red: **DEBT: 16,500 MEM‑CREDITS**. She hadn’t paid it down; she’d only survived.  

“Why?” she asked.  

“Because you were perfect.” Cael’s eyes flicked to the corridor behind her. “Your memories recorded the world with zero emotional filtering. No smoothing, no rationalization. Most people’s childhoods degrade into vague impressions. Yours stayed sharp.”  

He hesitated. “Senkova’s waiting for you. She’s got a server farm dedicated to your memories now. Wants to use you as a template for mass production.”  

Maren took the drive, feeling a jolt as his fingers brushed hers—a flash of memory, raw and unfiltered.  

“Where is she?”  

“Tower Seven. High grid. Access codes are on this drive.” He pulled a tiny device from the edge of his coat. “They’re encrypted, but—”  

“...but what?”  

“But you’re still Mnemonica.”  

The admission hit like a stun rod. “I’m not—” He looked away, swallowing hard. “They gave me a choice. Keep the fragment, keep my job, keep my life. Return it, and they’d scrub my memories clean. Start me over as a blank asset.”  

“So you played both sides.”  

“I tried to minimize casualties.”  

A monorail train whooshed overhead, screeching against the tracks. Through the cracked windows, neon arteries pulsed with data traffic. Somewhere in that maze, her childhood was being sold for entertainment.  

“The access codes,” Maren said.  

“This first.” Cael slid the device across the metal floor. Inside was a fragment smaller than the one in her pocket, unregistered, raw.  

Maren recognized the timestamp: **Age 7, Day 42, Cell Nine**.  

“Why give me this?”  

“Because you’re not the only one they took.” His voice cracked. “There are others. Thirty‑seven of us in the original harvest. You’re the only one who’s been active. The rest are scattered across the grid, living normal lives, completely unaware.”  

He pressed his palm to the wall. A panel slid open, revealing a bank of monitors. On each screen a face—all children, all variations of Maren.  

“They’re not clones,” he whispered. “They’re *reflections*. Memory echoes. Each one carries a piece of what you were. What you lost.”  

Maren stared at the faces. “And you want me to find them.”  

“I want you to remember.”  

The monitors flickered and died. Cael turned, his silhouette dissolving into darkness.  

Maren looked down at the drive in her hand. The encryption was standard Mnemonica issue—three layers, biometric locks, temporal decay protocols.  

She had **seventy‑two hours** before the codes would expire.  

Seventy‑two hours to locate thirty‑seven missing children.  

Seventy‑two hours before Drea Senkova turned her childhood into a product.  

The fragment in her pocket pulsed once, warm and insistent, like a heartbeat she’d forgotten she’d lost.  

She thought about calling Sable. About meeting Vesper for that drink they’d promised themselves. About the debt she still owed and the life she’d built in the spaces between memory fragments.  

She thought about the faces on the monitors, blinking in the dark, waiting to be remembered.  

Maren closed her fingers around the drive and stepped into the neon‑lit tunnel.  

The monorail’s shadow passed over her, and for a heartbeat she could swear she heard a child’s voice call her name.  

She pressed onward, the weight of a thousand stolen memories humming in her palm, and the city’s data‑drenched veins pulsed around her, promising both oblivion and redemption in equal measure.
