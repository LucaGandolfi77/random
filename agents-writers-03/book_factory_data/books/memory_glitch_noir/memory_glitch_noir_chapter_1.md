Theneedle was a hairline fracture of chrome that slipped into the soft meat of my wrist. I didn’t flinch; flinching ruins the stitch.

I was deep in a client’s head—Aris, a mid‑level corporate defector who’d scrubbed his own memory of a heist to dodge the Aethelgard interrogation suites. My job was to hunt the jagged edges of the deletion, the ghost‑data left in the wake of the erase, and re‑knot them into something a buyer could use.

The feed was a sludge of gray static and fragmented screams. I was threading a digital hallway of blurred faces and flashing sirens when the glitch hit.

It wasn’t a flicker. It was a rupture.

The gray vanished. Suddenly I was standing in a field of real, buzzing green under a sky that wasn’t the bruised purple of the city’s smog‑shield. A breeze landed on my skin, a sensation so alien it felt like a physical blow. Then came the smell—metallic, fresh, cold.

*Rain.*

Rain hadn’t touched this hemisphere’s soil in fifty years. The atmospheric processors kept the city in a permanent, humid decay, but this was raw. Ancient.

“What the hell is this?” I whispered, my voice echoing in a space that shouldn’t exist.

I reached out to touch a blade of grass. The moment my digital hand made contact, a string of crimson code flared across my vision—a high‑fidelity, unencrypted ghost‑file, embedded in Aris’s mind like a parasite. Its timestamp glowed in my HUD: **Pre‑Desiccation**.

Impossible. A recording from a dead world.

When I tried to isolate the fragment, the feed screamed. A spike of feedback ripped through my neural link, slamming me backward. I ripped the needle from my wrist, my arm jerking violently. I crashed onto the stained floor of my apartment, gasping, copper flooding my mouth.

The “Meadow Fragment” burned into my retinas—an emerald scar against the dim light of my room.

I lay there, listening to the rhythmic *thrum‑thrum* of the monorail pounding overhead, shaking the thin walls of my unit. I should have deleted the file. Any sane broker would have purged it the second it flagged a lethal signature. But the scent of that rain still clung to my senses, a ghost‑limb that felt more real than the peeling synth‑wallpaper of my life.

Then came the knock.

It wasn’t a polite request for entry. It was a heavy, rhythmic thud that made the door frame groan.

I didn’t move. I didn’t breathe. My cracked tablet flickered with a perimeter feed. A single man stood in the corridor—a bulkhead of a man in matte‑black tactical gear, a neural scanner pulsing a hungry blue in his hand.

He wasn’t here for the four thousand credits I owed the Low‑Sector lenders. He was tracking a ping.

The scanner turned a violent, screaming red.

“Voss,” the enforcer growled, his voice modulated through a throat‑synth. “We know you’ve got the leak. Open the door, and we might leave you with enough fingers to keep stitching.”

I stared at the data‑port on my desk, where the Meadow Fragment still glowed softly in the dark. I had just found the only beautiful thing in a city of plastic and neon, and it had already put a target on my spine.
