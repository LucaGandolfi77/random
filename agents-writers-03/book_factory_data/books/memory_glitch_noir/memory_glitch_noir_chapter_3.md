**Edited Version**

The alley spatme out into neon—wet, crawling neon that pooled in gutters and turned every surface into something oozing. I had three blocks before the drones locked onto my heat signature again. Three blocks and a door I’d bribed open six months ago.

The Memory Den didn’t have a sign. It had a curtain—real fabric, not a projection—that breathed exhaust from a noodle stand six doors down. I pushed through it and the world turned amber.

Inside was a coffin of stained wood and mismatched chairs, lit by strings of node‑lamps that flickered like dying synapses. Every surface was coated in the grime of a place where people pay to feel things. The air tasted of someone else’s cigarette, old sweat, and the chemical sweetness of shared sensory residue.

I slipped into the back corner—always the back corner.

The bartender, a woman with subcutaneous ink that swam like fish in dirty water, set a glass of something brown in front of me without being asked. I set it down. I needed the port.

I pulled the fragment from the port at my temple and let it unspool across the table. *The Meadow*: a green so violent it hurt, a sky so blue it was obscene, and rain that fell like it meant it. The sensory stamp still burned on my retinas—*Pre‑Desiccation, unencrypted, L1 integrity*. I’d cross‑referenced it with the black‑market archives Jax had sold me two weeks ago, before he stopped selling to me. The timestamp didn’t match anything in the public geological record—not even a whisper.

I ran the archival file alongside it.

Stitching was instinct. You feel the seam where two data‑sets are torn apart, then thread them back together. But when I lined the archive up with the Meadow fragment, something happened I’d never seen in eleven years of brokerage.

They *reacted*.

Not merged. Not combined. Reacted—like two compounds meeting in a sealed vial. The Meadow’s color bled outward, saturating the archive, and deep in the subconscious layer—a zone most people don’t even know exists, the part of a memory that carries meaning rather than image—I saw a coordinate. Not GPS. Not mesh‑tag. A coordinate shaped like a poem: six numbers arranged in a pattern that made my teeth ache.

I wrote them down on a napkin before the bartender could see.

That’s when the drones hit the windows.

Three searchlights swept the curtain in sequence—left, right, center—each slicing the amber light into harsh white slices. The patrons didn’t move. They never do. You learn not to react to searchlights the way you learn not to react to rain. The woman next to me kept her eyes closed, cycling through someone else’s beach vacation in her mind. I pressed my back into the wall and let my hand drift to the glitch‑grenade in my jacket pocket.

Not drawn. Not thrown. Just *there*—a blunt cylinder of misfired code that could scramble every HUD in the room for two seconds. I wasn’t ready to use it. Not yet.

The searchlights pulled back. The curtain settled. Nobody had found me.

But someone had been waiting for me to look up.

“You’ve seen it too,” said a voice from the bar’s mouth.

She was small, maybe sixty, with a face that had been rearranged by too many surgeries—features crowded together, slightly off‑kilter, like a painting hung a quarter‑inch crooked. Her eyes were natural. That was the giveaway. Nobody left their eyes natural in a city that sold you better ones.

“I’ve seen a lot of things,” I said. “Rain’s not one of them.”

She slid onto the stool beside me, close enough that I could smell her—old paper and something faintly electrical, like static before a storm.

“I dream in meadows,” she said. “Every night for six years. The same field. The same rain. The same sky that doesn’t exist.” She turned her natural eyes toward mine. “You stitched it. I could feel it when you walked in. Something unlocked.”

The napkin trembled between us.

Outside, a drone’s searchlight lingered a beat too long on the curtain. Then it moved on, but the curtain didn’t settle. It hung open, and through the gap I could see the noodle stand empty, the street bare, and a man standing in the alley with no forehead—just a shelf of bone and moleskin padding the dark plates of his headgear.

His oculars glowed the pale, flat amber of a city block after curfew.

He wasn’t looking for me.

He was looking at Maren.

“I don’t know what you’re talking about,” I said.

“You’re a terrible liar. That’s why they fired you.” She smiled—a sad, resigned thing. “My name is Maren. I used to work in archival at Aethelgard. Before they decided some files were better buried than indexed.”

My pulse hit the walls. The monorail throbbed above us, a slow mechanical heartbeat keeping time with the dread.

“Aethelgard,” I said.

“They deleted my memories of the project. Not all of them—just enough. Left me with the dreams and nothing to explain them.” She leaned closer. “The coordinate you pulled from the fragment. It’s not a location. It’s a *key*. And the door it opens isn’t on any map.”

The napkin quivered in my hand.

The man in the alley took a step forward, his headgear clicking softly. Maren’s eyes never left mine.

“Ready?” she whispered.

I glanced at the glitch‑grenade in my pocket, then back at the napkin, at the six numbers that felt like a pulse in my palm.

*Yes,* I thought, and let the words spill out before I could stop them: “Let’s open it.”
