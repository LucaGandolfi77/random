**Chapter 5: Data Bleed – Revised**

The data market seeped through the cracked walls of the abandoned noodle stand—humming servers, the ozone bite of over‑heated wiring, the sweet rot of fried circuits. I slumped in a corner booth, the stolen drive from the slain engineer cooling in my palm. Three hours after the sniper that ended him, his neural scream still clung to the back of my throat.

Below me, the canyon of glowing racks breathed data like polluted air. Vendors hawked stolen memories and neural patches that promised to taste like “first love” or “sunrise on a beach you never saw.” I watched a woman pay credits for a fragment of a first kiss; her eyes welled as a foreign romance blossomed on her senses. This city trades in nostalgia for lives that never happened. I should know.

My port ached for a splice, but the market’s mesh was thick with hunters. Aethelgard wasn’t just looking for me; it was hunting the Meadow Fragment’s signature. One slip, one stray packet, and this canyon would become my tomb.

The drive pulsed against my skin—not pure data, but a resonance that matched my pulse. I slipped out my stitcher, a modified neural needle that could read encrypted drives without leaving a trace. The tip glowed as I pressed it to the drive’s port.

The data hit like a physical blow. Not just files—memories. Thousands of them. Corporate espionage logs, black‑market deals, childhood snippets that felt both foreign and familiar. Then the gaps. Not empty voids but polished cuts, as if someone had scrubbed away whole sections and smoothed the edges.

I pulled up my own archive—the collection I’d been building since I began brokering. Overlaying it with the engineer’s memories revealed a pattern that turned my blood cold: the blank spots in his recollection aligned perfectly with gaps in mine. Like two halves of a shattered whole.

A childhood flash surged forward—a park, a girl whose face I could not recall, then a clean static. Five years of missing time, the same “missing years” the engineer had spoken of in connection with the Seed File project.

I wasn’t just a broker. I was a product.

The stitcher burned in my hand. The market’s neon flickered, the air shifted from ozone to something metallic and fresh. Cold drops hit my forehead—not sweat, but rain. Impossible rain.

I looked up. The neon signs above the stalls bled, their colors running down like watercolor. The crowd dissolved, replaced by a field of impossible green. The air I breathed changed, tasting of wet earth and something pure. I was standing in the Meadow—finally, not just seeing it, but feeling it. The grass under bare feet, the chill of water soaking through my clothes, the endless blue of a sky that stretched forever.

The sensory bleed was overwhelming. My port screamed with phantom data, flashing sterile rooms, needle‑like instruments, children crying behind glass. This wasn’t a memory fragment. It was my own, buried, edited, sold back to me in pieces.

“What’s a pretty thing like you doing in a place like this?” a voice called from the edge of the meadow.

I spun. A woman stood where the grass met a wall of impossible green. She wore the robes of a tech‑priest, but her skin shimmered with synthetic flesh, and her eyes glowed amber, the same hue as Voss’s. Her presence distorted the light, bending the meadow’s edges.

“The market’s dying,” she said, her voice layered like a neural echo. “It can’t hold the weight of the truth you carry.”

I clutched the stitcher, sweat slick on my fingers. “Who are you?”

“I’m the one who re‑stitched the memories they gave you… and the ones they took.” She took a step forward. “The Meadow isn’t just a memory, Kai. It’s a key. And you’re the lock.”

Behind her, the market unraveled completely. The crowd vanished, replaced by rows of children in white suits, shaved heads, blank eyes. My own reflection stared back from a glass pane—shaved, blank.

“You were their perfect subject,” she whispered, closing the distance. “Until you started remembering what you weren’t supposed to.”

I raised the stitcher, but my hand trembled. The meadow flickered, then faded, replaced by the dying neon of the market, but the terror remained. The gaps in my memory weren’t just holes—they were doors, and someone had been walking through them for years.

“Your turn to wake up,” she breathed, her fingers brushing my temple. The world dissolved into static, and for a heartbeat I was nothing but data in the machine.
