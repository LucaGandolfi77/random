**Chapter 7:The Cascading Error**  

The first scream came from a noodle stand three streets away.  
I heard it over the hiss of my breath mask—raw, animal, cutting through the perpetual hum of the Low Sector like a knife through synth‑flesh. Then another, then another. Within minutes the whole district was drown‑ing in a cacophony of panic.

The broadcast had done exactly what I’d hoped—and exactly what I’d feared.  

I pressed into the shadow of a corroded service hatch as the first wave of rioters spilled around the corner. They weren’t fleeing anything; they were charging toward it, arms outstretched, faces twisted in expressions I’d only seen in corrupted memory files—pure, undiluted longing.  

“**My daughter**,” a woman sobbed, clawing at the air. “She’s playing in the rain.”  

*Rain.* The word hit me like a punch. Through the chaos I saw it again—the emerald grass, the impossible blue sky that had haunted my dreams for weeks. The Seed File wasn’t just a memory anymore; it was contagious.  

My port throbbed against my temple, picking up the psychic static of a thousand half‑remembered lives crashing against each other. The mesh was bleeding—memory data spilling into the physical world like oil from a ruptured pipeline.  

***All units, Code Crimson,*** a synthesized voice crackled from somewhere above. ***Eraser squads deploying. Suspect is armed and networked.***  

I peered around the hatch’s edge. Black drones descended like mechanical wasps, amber under‑glows pulsing as Aethelgard’s signature. Erasers—designed not just to kill, but to erase, to make sure no one ever remembered what they’d seen.  The first drone spotted me.  

I dove back into the alley, boots splashing through knee‑deep water that shouldn’t have been there. The Low Sector’s flood systems had been offline for months, yet somehow it was raining—real rain, mixing with the grime and neon runoff in the gutters.  

The drones’ targeting lasers painted the walls with deadly red dots. I could feel their thermal signatures on my skin, could taste the ozone of their charging capacitors. They moved with the precision of hunting predators, their algorithms predicting my next move before I made it.  

A dumpster exploded behind me, sending shrapnel across the alley. I rolled with the impact, coming up against the rusted pharmacy wall. Through the broken window, I saw my reflection in a shard of mirrored glass—and something else.  

The Meadow flickered at the edge of my vision like a corrupted overlay, vivid greens and blues that hurt to look at directly. My implant was struggling to keep the artificial memory separate from reality. The Seed File was rewriting my perception, using my own neural architecture as a broadcast tower.  

***"Found you, little ghost,"** a voice whispered from the shadows.  

Jax stepped from behind an overturned hovercar, his usual smirk replaced by something almost like worry. He held a sleek black pistol—the kind Aethelgard’s clean‑up crew used.  

“Always knew you’d be trouble, Kai. Should’ve listened when they said you were too valuable to keep loose.”  My fingers closed around the glitch‑grenade in my jacket pocket. It felt heavier now, almost alive, hungry.  

***“Valueable to who?”** I asked, voice low.  

“The people who made you, obviously. But more importantly, the people who want to unmake you.” He gestured toward the riot. “You really thought your little stunt would go unnoticed? That broadcast reached every node in the system. Every artificial memory in this city just got a taste of something real.”  

The second Eraser drone rounded the corner, its targeting systems locking onto Jax instead of me. For a heartbeat I considered letting it take him.  

Then I remembered the child’s laugh from the fragment—the same one echoing through the rioters’ fevered hallucinations. My daughter, the woman had said. What if that was real too?  

I pulled the pin on the glitch‑grenade and hurled it at the drone. The explosion was beautiful and terrible—a burst of pure digital chaos that turned the machine’s systems inside out. Its optical sensors melted into fractal patterns, and it crashed into scrap metal, spitting sparks.  

Jax cursed and raised his weapon, but before he could fire, the world tilted sideways.  

A figure dropped from the fire escape above us—a woman in a patchwork coat made of decommissioned smart‑fabric. Her face was half‑hidden behind a respirator, but her eyes—her eyes glowed the same amber as Voss’s ocular implants.  

***"You’re bleeding reality into the mesh,"** she said, voice muffled by the mask. “**That’s going to attract attention.**”  

Jax demanded, “***Who the hell are you?***”  

***"The silence between heartbeats.***" She pressed something into my hand—a data crystal pulsing at the same frequency as the Meadow fragment. ***“They’re calling themselves Identity Purists. They don’t want your truth or your lies. They want to burn it all down.***”  

As if summoned by her words, the street erupted in a new wave of violence. Men and women armed with nothing more sophisticated than pipes surged forward, attacking anything that looked corporate—including Jax, whose expensive clothes marked him as a target.  

I grabbed his arm as he went down, pulling him into the doorway’s cover.  

***"What do they want?"*** I shouted over the din.  

***"To return us to the void!"*** the woman yelled back. ***"To strip away every lie we’ve been sold and see what’s left underneath!"***  

Through the chaos I saw them—figures moving with purpose amid the riot, uniforms marked with the Purist sigil: a stylized eye crossed out. They weren’t stopping the violence; they were orchestrating it.  

The crystal in my hand grew warm, almost hot. I knew what I had to do. I had to get to the Dead Man’s Switch before either side found it—and before I lost myself completely in the cascade of borrowed memories flooding my system.  

But first, I had to survive long enough to choose: sell the truth, or set it free.  

And right now, survival meant trusting a stranger in a coat of dead dreams while the city burned around us with the fire of remembered lies.
