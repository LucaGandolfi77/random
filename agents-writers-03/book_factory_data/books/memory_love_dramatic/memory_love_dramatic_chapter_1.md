## Edited Text  

**Chapter 1: The Letter in the Vault**

The neon signs above Blackwater Market flickered like dying stars, their electric blues and pinks bleeding into rain‑slicked pavement. Holographic stalls hummed with static, each screen flashing fragmented memories for sale—*a child’s first step, a soldier’s last breath, a lover’s whispered confession*. The air thrummed with the low buzz of neural links and the sharp tang of ozone from overloaded circuits.

Miri Kade crouched in the shadow of an abandoned transit hub, her gloved fingers dancing across the biometric scanner of a vault door. The job paid enough to keep her crew fed and her hideout stocked with black‑market memory cores, but tonight’s payload was different.

*Ink on paper*.

A relic from a time before memory chips and neural implants—a piece of analog history that the client had demanded. *An analog artifact*.

She cracked the vault in under three minutes.

Inside, the air was cool and still, lit only by the pale glow of emergency lighting. Rows of memory banks lined the walls, their crystalline cores pulsing with stolen moments. Miri’s scanner pinged on a sealed envelope wedged between two synthetic memories of a dead man’s dreams.

The letter was older than the city’s memory trade, older than the corporations that now controlled it. Yellowed paper, faded ink, and a looping hand that could only belong to one person: *Elena Voss to Marcus Chen, 1994*. A love letter, folded tight and sealed with a lipstick kiss.

Miri slipped it into her neural sheath, feeling its weight settle against her skull like a secret.

*Don’t trust the buyer*, she whispered to herself. *They always want it back once they know what it is.*

A hiss echoed behind her. The vault door swung open, and a silhouette stepped into the dim light.

Evelyn Thorne.

The heiress of ThorneTech, the woman who owned half the city’s memory infrastructure—and the reason Miri had been running jobs for the past six months.

“You’re late,” Evelyn said, voice cool even in the damp air.

Miri didn’t answer. She wasn’t here for small talk.

“The letter,” Evelyn continued, closing the distance. “Give it back.”

Miri’s hand drifted to the pocket of her coat, fingers curling around the envelope. “This isn’t yours.”

“It *is* mine.” Evelyn’s gaze hardened. “I’m here on family business.”

*Family business*—the phrase hung in the air like a threat. Miri had heard it from memory guards, corporate raiders, and the ghosts of anyone who’d crossed the wrong people.

“You want it destroyed,” Miri said, more certain now. “You *always* want it destroyed.”

Evelyn’s jaw tightened. “I want it *returned*.”

The misreading was obvious. Miri had assumed the worst, as always. But Evelyn wasn’t here to erase the letter—she was here to reclaim it.

“I don’t think so,” Miri whispered, backing toward the exit. “I’m not handing it over.”

Behind her, the vault door hissed open wider. A flicker of red light spilled onto the floor—Miri’s neural implant had just triggered a silent alarm. She lunged for the stairwell, the envelope burning against her temple.

Three blocks away, in a penthouse overlooking the market, Evelyn Thorne stared at the recovered letter through a magnifying lens. The same faded ink, the same trembling script. Her throat worked as she fought the weight of memory—*her* memory.

Marcus Chen. Her father. The man who had vanished the day the letter was written, leaving behind only a trail of questions and a company that would stop at nothing to keep its secrets buried.

She slipped the letter into a secure data drive, her neural implant already scanning for hidden codes, encrypted fragments, anything that could lead her to the truth.

The letter wasn’t just a keepsake.

It was a key.

And someone had just stolen it back.
