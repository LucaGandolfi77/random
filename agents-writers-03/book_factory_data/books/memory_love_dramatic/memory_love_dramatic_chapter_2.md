## **Chapter 2: Collateral Memory**  
*Edited for pacing, clarity, and lyrical consistency*

---

Evelyn Thorne pressed the elevator button for the forty‑second floor with deliberate slowness. The mirrored walls reflected her silhouette—sharp, tailored, a line against the city’s neon haze—yet inside, her pulse thrummed against her ribs like a caged bird. The boardroom waited, its polished table ready to dissect her proposal for the **Memory Tax**, but her thoughts kept drifting back to a narrow café tucked behind the market stalls, where a ghost of ink and paper had slipped through her fingers.

*“Don’t trust the buyer,”* the stranger had whispered, a phrase that had replayed in Evelyn’s mind since childhood. It was a warning her mother uttered whenever she tried to pry open the lockbox of family secrets. Still, something in the way **Miri Kade** had held the letter—*as if it were a living thing*—made Evelyn’s skin prickle.

The doors slid open. She stepped onto the marble corridor, heels clicking a staccato rhythm that echoed off glass walls lined with data streams scrolling in green code. Her assistant, **Lian**, materialized beside her, tablet in hand.

> **Lian:** “ThorneTech’s quarterly reports are ready for the Senate hearing. The Memory Tax rollout is slated for next month.”  
> 
> **Evelyn:** *[nods, eyes scanning the projections]* Each figure represents a citizen’s extracted experience—moments of joy, grief, love—monetised and catalogued. I’ve watched my father build an empire on those memories, watched the city become addicted to the rush of reliving someone else’s life. And now I will add to it.  
> 
> “Did you find anything on the… artifact?” she asked, lowering her voice.

Lian’s brow flicked. “Just static. The biometric seal on the vault is still intact. Whoever stole it knows how to move through our systems without leaving a trace.”

Evelyn’s fingers tightened around the tablet. *She knows*, she thought, recalling how Miri’s eyes had lingered on the letter before slipping it into a neural sheath. *There’s more here than simple theft.*

---

### The Café

The café was a relic beneath an overpass, the hum of hover‑cars fading into background static. Miri slid onto a cracked vinyl stool, the letter now nestled safely behind her left ear, its paper edge cool against her skin. The Archivists occupied the far corner, hunched over tablets and coffee that had gone cold hours ago.

> **Dex**, neon dreads swaying: “Yo, Kade, you look like you’re carrying the whole market on your shoulders.”  
> > **Ren**, hushed: “Did the client respond?”  
> 
> Miri gave a thin smile. “Not yet. But I think they will.” She glanced around the cramped space, noting how the fluorescent lights flickered in sync with the soft whir of memory dealers outside, hawking stolen moments on cracked screens.

Her neural implant buzzed—a silent chime only she could hear. A secure line opened.

> **Evelyn (voice, filtered):** “Ms. Kade, this is Evelyn Thorne. I believe we need to talk.”  

Miri’s stomach dropped. She had hoped to avoid this particular buyer.

> **Evelyn:** “You have something that belongs to my family. Meet me at the café on 9th and Willow in thirty minutes. Come alone.”  

The line went dead. Miri stared at the table— the letter seemed to burn a hole in her thoughts.

> **Dex:** “I’m not going.”  
> 
> **Ren:** “If she knows about the letter… maybe we can use this.”  

Miri slipped her sleeve over a faint scar on her wrist, a nervous habit that flared whenever danger loomed. “No. I’m going. But I’m not giving her anything.”

---

### The Meeting

The bell above the café door chimed as Evelyn entered, heels clicking against worn tiles. She paused at the threshold, scanning the room until her gaze landed on Miri. For a heartbeat something flickered across Evelyn’s face—not anger, but something softer, almost wistful.

Miri watched her approach, noting how Evelyn’s hand absent‑mindedly traced an old scar on her left wrist—a nervous habit she’d never seen in a boardroom. The gesture was intimate, vulnerable.

> **Evelyn:** “You came.”  

Miri’s voice was low enough that only Evelyn could hear. “I don’t usually hand over what I steal. But I’m curious—what makes a ThorneTech heiress care about old paper?”

Evelyn’s eyes flicked to the letter, then back to Miri’s face. “My father built this company on memories. Those memories have consequences—both for us and for the people whose lives we’ve… catalogued.” She swallowed, throat working. “The letter isn’t just a love note. It’s a key.”

Miri felt something shift in her chest, a mixture of wariness and intrigue. “A key to what?”

> **Evelyn:** “The original architecture of memory extraction. My father tried to erase it, to keep it from destroying what we’ve built. But someone… someone wanted it found.”

The words hung between them, heavy with implication. Miri’s implant buzzed again—a warning signal: a ThorneTech drone was circling the block, its scanner sweeping the streets for unauthorized neural signatures.

> **Evelyn:** “We don’t have much time.” She leaned forward. “I need to borrow the letter for a week. Let me run some diagnostics, see what hidden protocols are embedded in the ink. After that… you can have it back.”

Miri’s jaw clenched. Every instinct screamed to refuse, to protect the crew, to keep moving. But there was something in Evelyn’s eyes—loss, expectation, a mirror of her own—that made her pause.

> **Miri:** “What guarantee do I have that you won’t just take it and disappear?”  

Evelyn pressed a button on her earpiece. “Then we’ll both be answering to my brother. He doesn’t take kindly to… missing property.”

The threat was clear, but the desperation underneath it cut deeper. Miri catalogued every micro‑expression, every shift in posture. In the end, she reached slowly into her jacket, fingers closing around the letter’s edge.

> **Miri:** “Fine. But I’m coming with you. Whatever’s in that ink, we figure it out together.”  Evelyn’s lips quirked into something that almost resembled a smile. “Together,” she echoed, testing the word’s weight.

Outside, the drone’s searchlight swept past the café window, casting stark shadows across their table. Neither woman moved; they both knew that the next seven days would either stitch them closer together or tear them irrevocably apart. The letter lay between them, its secrets poised to rewrite not just their lives, but the very fabric of the city’s memory.
