**Chapter 4– Echoes of the Past**  

Rain fell in silver sheets against Evelyn Thorne’s penthouse window, each drop racing down the glass before merging into a single, cold river. Below, the city glittered like a circuit board, but Evelyn saw only the static of her own mind—the echo of Miri’s fingers brushing against hers when she’d handed over the letter. It had been a fleeting contact, a connection she wasn’t sure even existed.

She traced the scar on her left wrist, a raised road‑map of her childhood. When she was six, her mother had yanked her too close to a memory‑extraction device. “Don’t trust the buyer,” the woman had whispered, the warning reverberating ever since—the same warning that still hummed in her thoughts following the Blackwater Market incident. And now she was about to ask the buyer for another favor, one that could endanger them both.

The office door slid open without a sound. Lian stepped in, tablet in hand, her expression carefully neutral.

> “Director Kade is here for the quarterly review,” Lian announced. “He’s brought security protocols for the Memory Purge law.”  
> “Give us ten minutes,” Evelyn replied, her fingers tightening around the desk’s edge.

---

Miri watched the rain from the cramped window of her safe house. Droplets raced down the glass like memories fleeing a neural implant. The letter lay on her desk, its yellowed surface catching the dim light. She’d read it a dozen times, memorizing every loop and flourish, though she knew that wasn’t what Evelyn wanted.

A creak announced the front door. Ren slipped inside, hood dripping onto the floorboards.  
> “They’re coming,” Ren whispered. “Kade’s Memory Purge task force. They’ve got warrants.”  

Dex emerged from the kitchen, neon dreadlocks stuck to his forehead.  > “We’ve got ten minutes to move what we can. The rest gets wiped.”  Miri closed her eyes, inhaling the scent of ozone and rain.  
> “Evelyn’s behind this,” she said, the words tasting like ash. “She warned me about her brother.”  

> “She’s playing both sides,” Ren replied. “Memory Purge wipes anyone with unregistered memories. That includes us.”  

> “Then we’ll show them what happens when you mess with history,” Dex snapped, slamming a fist on the table.  

---

The boardroom of ThorneTech was a cathedral of glass and steel. Rows of scrolling data streamed behind Evelyn, representing every memory the corporation had catalogued. She sat at the head of the polished table, her reflection merging with the green cascade of code.

Director Kade stood at the far end, his tailored suit a stark contrast to the board members’ casual tech‑wear.  
> “The Memory Purge is not optional,” he intoned. “It’s necessary to protect the integrity of our extraction systems.”  

A silver‑haired board member cleared his throat.  
> “Public backlash against the Memory Tax has been significant. The Purge could restore confidence.”  

Evelyn kept her expression neutral, though her fingers trembled on the tablet before her.  > “The Purge targets illegal memory traders,” she said, deliberately omitting that many of those memories were the only record of lives before extraction became mandatory.  

Kade’s eyes narrowed.  
> “It also targets those who hoard unregistered memories. Like the one you’ve been keeping in your desk drawer.”  

The comment hung in the air, a silent accusation. Evelyn met his gaze, refusing to flinch.  
> “The letter is under investigation. It contains information relevant to our security protocols.”  

> “You’ve had it for weeks,” Kade countered. “What have you found?”  

Evelyn’s pulse quickened.  
> “It’s encoded with a memory seed—a prototype from before extraction was commercialised.”  

Kade’s lip curled into a thin smile.  
> “Then why haven’t you handed it over? Unless you’re developing unauthorized extraction methods yourself.”  

Board members shifted in their seats, their gazes alternating between the siblings. Evelyn felt the weight of their judgment, the suspicion that had always simmered between her and her brother.  
> “I’m following protocol,” she said, the words hollow.  

---

Miri was packing what they could into waterproof cases—synthetic memories wrapped in protective sleeves. The apartment had been their sanctuary for three years, a place where the city’s forgotten memories could breathe without being commodified.

Ren slipped a small data chip into her hand.  
> “Backups of everything we couldn’t save. The letter’s still on you, right?”  

Dex wheeled the last case onto a hovering cart.  
> “We’ve got a place in Sector 7. Closer to the archives, further from Kade’s scanners.”  

Miri nodded, though her thoughts were elsewhere. The archives—ThorneTech’s central repository of every extracted memory. Evelyn had mentioned it during their rooftop meeting, spoken of a fragment from the letter’s originator, a memory that might explain why Marcus Chen vanished in 1994.

Ren placed a hand on her shoulder.  
> “You’re thinking about her again.”  

Miri’s fingers brushed the neural sheath behind her ear where the letter rested.  
> “She wants something from the archives,” she confessed. “Something the letter can’t tell us.”  

> “And if you get caught?”  

Miri pulled her sleeve down over the scar on her wrist.  
> “Then I’ll have a story worth telling.”  

---

Evelyn pressed a thumb against the biometric scanner of her private study. The door sealed with a soft hiss, creating a sound‑proof sanctuary from the corporate world outside. She activated a holographic projector; the image of Miri flickered before her, rainwater glistening on her lashes.

> “I need you to do something for me,” she whispered into the emptiness. “Something dangerous.”  

She paced, the echo of her heels on marble punctuating a thought that had become a mantra. Kade’s suspicion was a warning—a reminder that her position was precarious. The letter was both her protection and her vulnerability. The memory seed it contained threatened ThorneTech’s monopoly, but it also held the key to understanding her father’s disappearance.

She stopped before the window, her reflection overlaid on the city’s neon glow.  
> “The archives have a fragment of Elena Voss’s memory,” she murmured. “The day the letter was written—the day Marcus Chen vanished.”  She tapped the secure communicator, waiting for the link to establish. When Miri’s face appeared, Evelyn felt a jolt that had nothing to do with technology.

> “I need you to retrieve something from the archives,” Evelyn said, voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. “A memory fragment from Elena Voss.”  

Miri’s expression remained neutral, but her fingers clenched at her sides.  
> “What’s in it for me?”  

Evelyn allowed herself a small smile.  
> “The truth. About your brother.”  

The name hung between them, unspoken but understood. Miri’s brother had been one of the first casualties of ThorneTech’s early extraction experiments—a fact Evelyn had uncovered while digging into her father’s past.

Miri’s gaze hardened.  > “Why should I trust you?”  > “Because we both know Kade is coming for you,” Evelyn countered. “And if you’re going to fight back, you’ll need information he doesn’t want you to have.”  Miri studied Evelyn’s face, searching for deception.  
> “What exactly am I looking for?”  

> “A memory from Elena Voss on the day she wrote the letter. There’s something in it that explains why Marcus Chen disappeared.”  

> “And if I find it?”  

> “Then you’ll have leverage to make Kade back off. To make all of them back off.”  

Silence stretched between them, thick with the unspoken tension of a dangerous bargain. Miri brushed the letter hidden beneath her sleeve.  

> “Where do I start?”  

Evelyn smiled, the expression finally reaching her eyes.  > “Sector 3, sub‑level 7. Container 1994‑B.”  

The connection terminated. Evelyn pressed her hand against the window, watching the rain blur the city lights below. She had just sent the woman she was falling for into the heart of her family’s empire—a place designed to protect their secrets at any cost.

The risk was enormous, but the potential reward was something neither of them could ignore. In a world where memories were currency, they were about to make the most dangerous trade of all—trading their hearts for the truth.
