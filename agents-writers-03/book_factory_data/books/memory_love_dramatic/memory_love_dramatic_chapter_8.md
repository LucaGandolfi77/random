### Chapter 8: Crossed Wires  

---

The rain had stopped, but the city still wept—water still dripping from fire‑escapes and awnings, still pooling in the cracks of old pavement. Evelyn stood at the entrance of the hearing chamber, her heels clicking against marble that echoed like a heartbeat in an empty room. The cameras were gone. The committee had dispersed. Only the ghost of her testimony clung to her skin, a memory‑seed she’d hidden in her neural sheath.  

She could still feel Senator Pryce’s stare—those unblinking eyes that had watched her stumble over the letter, the theft, the “unauthorized extraction” that had rocked ThorneTech’s quarterly projections. She’d held her ground. She’d lied with the precision of someone trained since birth to protect the family name.  

But the lie had left a hollow behind her ribs.  

*Where is she?*  

She lifted the neural map on her wrist. Miri Kade’s biometrics had vanished thirty minutes ago—no ping, no trace—yet Evelyn knew better. Miri moved through the world like water through cracks, slipping past security protocols with the ease of someone who’d been running her whole life.  

She also knew where Miri would go.  

The train station was a cathedral of rust and glass, its vaulted ceiling stained with decades of exhaust and neon. Commuters streamed through its corridors, faces lit by the blue glow of their implants, eyes fixed on destinations that mattered more than the present moment.  

Miri sat on a bench near Platform 9, hood pulled low, fingers tracing the edge of an envelope in her coat pocket. The letter. Still there. Still warm from the heat of her body.  

She shouldn’t have come here. After the hearing—after watching Evelyn’s face flicker on those massive screens—composed, regal, lying with every calculated pause—Miri had wanted to disappear. To melt into the city and forget the way her chest had tightened when Evelyn’s voice cracked, just slightly, on the word *integrity*.  

*Integrity*. The word tasted like ash in Miri’s mouth.  

She’d thought—foolishly, maybe—that the hearing would change something. That Evelyn would finally step out from behind her corporate armor and admit the truth: that the letter mattered, that the memory seed was dangerous, that ThorneTech had been selling stolen lives for decades. Instead Evelyn had performed. She’d given the committee exactly what they wanted—a polished, rehearsed defense of corporate ethics—and Miri had sat in the audience, jaw clenched, watching a stranger wear the face she almost trusted.  

The platform announcement crackled overhead. *Train to Sector 7 departing in two minutes.*  

Miri stood, tucking the envelope deeper into her pocket. She needed to move. Needed to get the letter to Ren and Dex before the Memory Purge task force could trace her signal. She needed to forget the way Evelyn’s eyes had found her—in the crowd, just once—that brief flicker of recognition that had made Miri’s heart stutter like a glitched program.  

She didn’t see the woman watching her from across the platform.  

---

Evelyn’s heels echoed on the concrete steps, each click a small defiance against the exhaustion that weighed her down. She’d changed out of her hearing attire—no longer the sharp tailored suit but dark jeans and a jacket that blended into the crowd. She wasn’t ThorneTech’s heiress right now. She was just a woman looking for something she’d let slip through her fingers.  

The platform was crowded, faces blurring together in a wash of neural implants and tired eyes. Evelyn’s implant filtered through biometric signatures until she found the one she was looking for.  

*There.* Platform 9. Sitting on a bench, hood up, fingers moving restlessly against her pocket.  

*Miri.*  The woman looked up. Those dark eyes—still guarded, still wary—met Evelyn’s, and for a moment neither of them spoke. The noise of the station faded to static. Only the two of them, standing on opposite sides of a widening chasm.  

“Evelyn.” Miri’s voice was flat, controlled. “Didn’t expect to see you out of your element.”  

“My element.” Evelyn let out a breath that was almost a laugh. “Is that what I am to you? An element?”  

“You know what I mean.” Miri stood, her movements slow, deliberate. “Corporate. Boardrooms. Testifying before committees while your company’s security drones scan the streets for people like me.”  

“I wasn’t—” Evelyn stopped, her jaw tightening. “I came here to find you. Not to—”  

“To what? Remind me that I’m a criminal? That the letter is property?” Miri’s voice rose, sharp enough to cut. “You made yourself pretty clear in there. ‘Unauthorized extraction.’ ‘Corporate malfeasance.’ You threw me to the wolves, Evelyn. You stood up there and—”  

“I was trying to protect you!”  

The words hung in the air, raw and unguarded. Evelyn’s chest heaved; the walls she’d built cracked under the weight of something she didn’t yet have a name for.  

Miri stared at her, something flickering behind her eyes—surprise, maybe, or the ghost of hope. “Protect me,” she repeated, softer now. “By lying. By pretending the letter doesn’t matter. By—”  

“By keeping you alive!” Evelyn stepped closer, close enough to smell the rain on Miri’s jacket, the faint trace of ozone that clung to her skin. “If I told them the truth—if I admitted that the letter is a key to the original memory architecture—they would have come for you. Not just Kade. Not just the task force. The committee. The Memory Guard. Everyone who has a stake in keeping the past buried.”  

“And what about you?” Miri’s voice cracked, just slightly. “Are you going to keep the past buried too? Are you going to stand there and tell me that everything we found—the memory seed, the poem, the story of Elena and Marcus—don’t matter?”  Evelyn’s throat tightened. She thought of the rooftop three nights ago, when she’d read the letter aloud and felt something unlock in her chest—a memory she’d never known she was carrying, a connection to a past that had been erased from the official record. She thought of the way Miri had looked at her then, eyes that saw too much, and how it had felt like being seen for the first time in years.  

“I can’t,” Evelyn whispered. “You know I can’t.”  

“Then we’re done.” Miri’s voice steadied, but her hands trembled as she pressed a paper back into Evelyn’s palm. Her fingers brushed Evelyn’s palm—a flash of static, of electricity, of the last beat of a song fading.  

“Keep it,” Miri said. “Do whatever you want with it. Sell it to your board. Burn it. I don’t care anymore.”  

She turned and walked toward the train, her figure disappearing into the crowd boarding the Sector 7 line.  

Evelyn stood frozen on the platform, the envelope warm in her hand, the weight of everything unsaid pressing down on her chest like a physical thing.  

---

The train was crowded, bodies pressed together in the dim cabin light. Miri found a seat near the back, her back against the cold metal window, eyes fixed on the blur of stations passing by.  

*Sector 3. Sector 4. Sector 5.*  

She shouldn’t have given the letter back. It was leverage—her crew’s lifeline, the only bargaining chip against ThorneTech’s endless reach. But looking at Evelyn’s face, at the raw desperation in those carefully composed features, Miri had felt something crack inside her.  

*Vulnerability as courage*, she thought bitterly. *What a joke.*  

The train lurched to a stop at Sector 6. Passengers shuffled on and off, a constant flow of faces that blurred together. Miri didn’t look up. She was too busy replaying the conversation in her head, cataloguing every word, every flicker of emotion she’d tried so hard to ignore.  

*I’m trying to protect you.*  

The words echoed, a counterpoint to the rational voice that told her it was a lie. Evelyn Thorne was a corporate heiress, a representative of everything Miri’d spent her life fighting against. She couldn’t be trusted. She wouldn’t choose Miri over her family’s empire—when the stakes were this high.  

And yet…  

Miri’s hand moved to her pocket, reaching for the letter that was no longer there. Her fingers closed around empty air, and a spike of panic stole her breath.  

*When—*  

She looked down. The pocket was empty. Her eyes scanned the floor, the seat, the space around her feet. Nothing. The letter was gone.  

*When—*  

The platform doors slid open at Sector 7. Miri stood, heart hammering, hands shaking as she searched the cabin one more time. A woman in a gray coat was moving toward the exit, hand tucked into a pocket, a yellow, crumpled shape visible between her fingers.  

*No.*  

Miri pushed through the crowd, but the woman was faster—slipping through the doors, disappearing onto the platform before Miri could reach her. By the time Miri burst out of the train, the woman was already gone, swallowed by the neon glow of market stalls below.  

The letter was gone.  

---

Evelyn stood on the platform long after the train had departed, fingers tracing the envelope’s edge, mind still reeling from what had just happened.  *We’re done.*  

The words kept looping, a loop she couldn’t break. She thought of Miri’s face—the way those dark eyes had hardened, the way her voice had gone flat and controlled, the way she’d handed over the letter as if it were nothing more than a scrap of paper.  

But it wasn’t nothing. It was everything.  

Evelyn looked down at the envelope, at the yellowed paper and the looping hand that had written a love story decades before she was born. She thought of the memory seed hidden in its fibers, the dormant consciousness that could rewrite everything ThorneTech had built. She thought of Elena Voss and Marcus Chen, of a love that had been erased from history, and of the choice she now faced.  

*Keep it. Bury it. Let the past stay buried.*  

Or—  

Her neural interface buzzed. A message from Lian flashed red in her peripheral vision.  

*Director Kade has issued a city‑wide alert. Memory Purge task force is targeting all unregistered artifacts. The letter is now classified as a Level 1 threat.*  

Evelyn’s blood ran cold.  

*They know.*  

She looked up, eyes scanning the station, searching for the familiar figure in the crowd. But Miri was gone—vanished into the city’s veins, just as she’d promised.  

And now the letter, the one thing that could have changed everything, was loose in the world.  

---

Three blocks away, in a cramped office above a noodle shop, a woman with neon‑dyed hair slid the letter across a cluttered desk of circuits and stolen data. Across from her, a scarred man counted credits, eyes gleaming in the glow of a holographic display.  

“Good find,” he said, tucking the paper into a case lined with static‑resistant fabric. “This one’s worth more than both of you combined.”  

The woman smiled, sharp and predatory. “Told you. The heiress always leads us to the good stuff.”  

In the case, the letter lay dormant, its secret still hidden, its memory seed still waiting.  

But not for long.
