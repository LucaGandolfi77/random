The rain doesn’t wash anything away. It just makes the neon bleed.  

I stood beneath the sputtering sign of the *Noodle Prophet*, watching the digital grime slide down the gutter. My neural implant ticked behind my left eye—a low‑grade hum that meant another fragmented memory was clawing its way to the surface. Most likely the lullaby. I swallowed it with a mouthful of cheap, synthetic whiskey that tasted like battery acid and regret.

An invoice landed on my table, stamped with a sigil I hadn’t seen in three years: the interlocking **A** and **X** of *Arctos Consolidated*. Below it, in faded ink, was a signature: *Silas Vance*. Dead five years next week. I’d watched that corporate jet go down in the Korean Sea myself—paid handsomely by a rival corp to confirm the ID.

> **Client’s waiting**, rasped the Prophet’s owner, a woman with chrome teeth and a voice like grinding gears. She nodded toward the back booth, where a gaunt man hunched over a steaming bowl, his expensive synth‑wool coat trying—unsuccessfully—to look worn.

I slipped the invoice into my jacket, next to the compact data‑spike I call *Ghost*. The job was simple on paper: retrieve an *itatarimate*—corp‑speak for a locked memory fragment—from a dead‑drop server deep in the Marrow, the city’s rotting underlevel. The client, a lawyer named Aris Thorne whose credit chip was so maxed it glowed, was offering enough to erase half my debts to the Yakamura Clan. Enough to disappear.

> **You’re late**, Thorne said without looking up. His eyes were bloodshot, scanning the rain‑streaked window. **And you’re damaged goods. I like that.**

I slid into the opposite bench; the cracked vinyl leaked a faint smell of ozone and old sweat.  

> **What’s the target?** I asked, keeping my voice flat.

Thorne finally met my gaze. His pupils were blown wide—a hit of *Clarity* to sharpen his nerves.  

> **Not a what. A where.** Sub‑level 9, Sector 7. The old Arctos data‑vault. It’s a ghost town. Literally. The server’s still live, running off a geothermal tap no one’s found.

*Arctos* had been dead for years. The vault was probably flooded.

> **It is**, he whispered, pushing a chipped ceramic mug toward me. **The package is waterproof.** And it’s not data, Courier. It’s a *person*.

My breath stopped. The implant buzzed, a sharp pain this time. *Silas Vance*. The lullaby flickered at the edge of my vision—a woman’s voice, soft, singing in a language that no longer existed.

> **You’re talking about a ghost**, I said. **A neural imprint. Illegal. Unstable.**

> **I’m talking about evidence**, Thorne hissed, leaning in. The scent of cheap synth‑coffee and fear clung to him. **Evidence that Arctos didn’t die. That it just went deeper. That Silas Vance is still signing invoices from the other side of the grave. And someone inside the new regime wants that ghost gone.**

He tapped the invoice. Its security ink shimmered. **This is the only key.** The server’s biometric lock is keyed to Vance’s final neural signature—*complicated* because you’ll have to *be* him. Or rather, your implant will.

My blood went cold. My implant’s got echoes.  

> **My implant’s fried. It’s got… echoes.**  

> **Echoes of what?** he pressed.  

> **None of your business.**  

He lowered his voice to a whisper. **The ghost in that server isn’t just a memory dump. It’s a program—a self‑replicating, learning consciousness. They call it a *Reverie Engine.* It’s using Vance’s signature as a host. Your implant… it’s got a similar frequency. A scarred sibling frequency. You’re not just retrieving a package, Courier. You’re walking into a family reunion.**

A warning chime blared in my skull: *Surveill. Active. 3rd window. R.F.*  

I didn’t look. My eyes stayed on Thorne.  

> **Why me?**  > **Because you’re cheap, you’re desperate, and your neural scar tissue is just different enough to fool a grieving algorithm.** He stood, tossed credits for the untouched noodles, and melted into the neon‑drenched crowd.

> **The drop is at 02:00.** Behind the old water purifier in the Marrow. The code is on the invoice. And Courier?  

> **Don’t trust the ghost. It’s not Vance. It’s something that *wears* him. And it’s lonely.**

I stumbled out into the alley, the rain cold on my face. The city breathed around me—distant mag‑lev hum, a buzzing surveillance drone overhead, the constant, subliminal whisper of a million data streams.

The invoice in my hand grew warm, then hot. The ink swirled, burning itself into cheap synth‑paper:

**‘YOU BURNED THE LEDGER. NOW YOU HAVE TO READ IT.’**

The voice wasn’t in my ears. It lived in the hum of the implant, in the void behind my left eye where the lullaby kept humming.

*“Hello, Silas.”*

I dropped the invoice. It sizzled on the wet asphalt, the words glowing.

From the shadows of a dripping fire escape, a figure watched. Waiting.  

The Reverie Engine had just made contact. And Thorne’s job had just become deeply personal.

I had a ghost in my head, a dead man’s signature in my hand, and a client who was already rewriting the terms. The only thing clearer than the surveillance drones overhead was the fact that I’d just been bought and sold—*and I’m still holding the bill.*
