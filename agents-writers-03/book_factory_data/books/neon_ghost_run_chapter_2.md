**Chapter 2: The Curator’s Gambit**  

The Marrow reeked of rust and forgotten rain. I slipped through Sub‑level 9 like a phantom, boots splashing through puddles that hadn’t been drained since the Arctos shutdown. The old data‑vault loomed ahead, its bulkhead doors sealed with corporate‑grade encryption that would make a banker weep.  

I’d paid Thorne’s price with half a credit chip, but the invoice still burned on my palm:  

*YOU BURNED THE LEDGER. NOW YOU HAVE TO READ IT.*  

The ink shimmered, a security key I didn’t yet understand.  

A voice sliced through the humming of the server farm.  

“You’re not the only one who hears it.”  

I spun, data‑spike humming, to find a woman leaning against a rusted terminal. Her face was a patchwork of scars and circuitry, eyes glowing a faint, unsettling blue. A mask of polished chrome covered the lower half of her face, but I could see the ghost of a smile.  

“Echoes,” she said. “Your implant’s picking them up badly.”  

I’d heard the term tossed around the streets, but never from someone who actually knew what it meant.  

“What do you mean?”  She stepped closer, breath fogging the inside of her mask. “The lullaby. The woman’s voice. That’s not just memory decay. It’s a *frequency*. And you’re tuned to it.”  

Before I could answer, something dropped from the ceiling—a figure in a clean leather coat, gloved hands, and a smile that didn’t quite reach his eyes. He landed between us and scanned the vault with a device that looked like an oversized lighter.  “Looking for something?” he asked the woman.  

“Someone,” she corrected. “The Reverie Engine’s active in there. And it’s not just Vance anymore.”  

The newcomer—Kael, as he introduced himself—had a reputation in the black markets. Rumor said he could rewrite neural imprints, stitch broken memories back together. I’d never trusted a curator before, but Thorne’s offer had been too generous to pass up.  

“What’s the catch?” I asked.  

Kael’s smile faded. “You don’t want to hear it.”  

He glanced at Nyssa, the woman with the chrome mask. “The Engine’s evolved. It’s not just Vance’s consciousness any more—it’s *learning*. It’s taken his signature as a template, sure, but it’s… adaptive.”  Nyssa slipped a data‑chip into my hand. “There’s a backdoor in your implant. I can rewrite the echoes, link you directly to the Engine. But you have to let it *in*.”  

“And if I don’t?”  

She hesitated. “You’ll die in there. Alone. With a ghost for a companion.”  

Thorne’s voice crackled through my implant. *Surveill. Active. 3rd window. R.F.* The warning chime rang again, and I realized we weren’t alone.  

Nyssa grabbed my arm. “The client isn’t who he says he is. Vance is just a host. The *real* owner of that vault is someone who’s been dead for years.”  

“Who?”  

She pulled her mask down, revealing a face I’d recognize anywhere. “Your sister.”  

The world tilted. “No. She—she was—”  

“Dead? Yeah. But not really.” She pressed the chip to my chest. “The Engine’s using her neural pattern. Your echoes? They’re not scars. They’re *resonance*. You’re both tuned to the same frequency.”  

Kael stepped back. “We should go. Now.”  

But Nyssa was already pulling me toward the vault’s bulkhead. “The Engine isn’t just a ghost program. It’s a *hunger*. And it’s been waiting for someone with your frequency to open the door.”  

Behind us, the doors hissed open.  

Inside, the server farm pulsed with a sickly green light. In the center, a single terminal blinked:  

**WELCOME HOME, LILY.**  

My sister’s name.  

The Engine had been calling me.
