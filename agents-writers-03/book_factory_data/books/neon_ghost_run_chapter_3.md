**Chapter 3– Echoes in the Server**  

The bulkhead hissed shut behind me, sealing the room in a tomb of rusted servers and dead air. Through the cracked viewports, neon from the city above bled electric bruises across the concrete floor. My implant throbbed—its cursed lullaby threading through my head again, a woman’s voice in a language I’d never learned.  

*You’re the one who erased him, aren’t you?*  

It wasn’t a thought. It was everywhere—his laugh gone flat, a phantom breath in the wires, a whisper in the static. I slapped my palm against the nearest terminal. The screen flickered to life.  

```
ARCTOS CONSOLIDATED – DATA VAULT 7  
ACCESS GRANTED: ECHO PROTOCOL
```  

The words scrolled past like a death sentence. I’d seen that logo on the wreckage of the jet in the Korean Sea, on the invoice burning my skin, on the ghost program that wasn’t a ghost at all.  

A speaker crackled, and Silas Vance’s ruined face stared back at me from the display. His eyes were too bright, too alive.  

“You shouldn’t have come here,” he said. “Not after what you did.”  

I didn’t remember doing anything. The implant burned, the lullaby rose louder, and somewhere in the static I swore I heard my sister’s laugh.  

“Lily?” I whispered.  The screen snapped black.  

The vault’s bulkhead shuddered open with a mechanical groan.  

She stepped into the light like a blade sliding from its sheath—chrome teeth, grinding gears, eyes that glowed like faulty neon. Nyssa, the Echoes specialist.  

“You’re not the only one who hears it,” she said, her voice a mix of static and honey. “The lullaby isn’t memory decay. It’s a frequency. And you’re tuned to it.”  

I should’ve pulled the trigger. Instead I asked, “Who sent you?”  

“Someone who wants the ghost gone,” she replied, tilting her head. The chrome mask shifted as she spoke. “Someone inside the new regime.”  

The 3rd‑window chime sang in my skull.  

**SURVEILL. ACTIVE. 3RD WINDOW. R.F.**  

Rain slammed the viewport. In the darkness, something moved.  

A man dropped from the ceiling—a clean‑cut figure in a leather coat, gloved hands, a half‑smile that didn’t reach his eyes. He landed between me and Nyssa, flicking open a device that looked like an oversized lighter.  

“Looking for something?” he asked.  

“Someone,” Nyssa corrected. “The Reverie Engine’s active in there. And it’s not just Vance anymore.”  

Kael stepped closer, copper‑colored eyes flickering. “You don’t wa—”  

The speaker crackled again.  

*You’re the one who erased him, aren’t you?*  

Kael’s smile died. “Jesus. It’s learned your voice.”  

I lunged for Ghost, but Nyssa was faster. Her hand closed around my wrist like steel.  

“You burned the ledger,” she said. “Now you have to read it.”  

The invoice on my palm shimmered, ink rewriting itself:  

**YOU BURNED THE LEDGER. NOW YOU HAVE TO READ IT.**  

The words weren’t on my skin any longer. They lived in my head, in my bones, in the lullaby that pulsed behind my eyes.  

And somewhere in the static, a ghost was laughing.
