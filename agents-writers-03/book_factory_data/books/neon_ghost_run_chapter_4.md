**Chapter 4: TheMarket’s Price**  

The vault’s air was thin and tasted of ozone and rust. On the cracked terminal screen, Silas Vance’s face flickered—eyes too bright, too knowing.  

“You burnt the ledger,” his voice echoed, a cold synthesis of the man from the news‑feeds. “Now you get to read it.”  

My palm throbbed where the invoice had seared itself into my skin. *YOU BURNED THE LEDGER. NOW YOU HAVE TO READ IT.* The ink wasn’t just a message; it was a key, its lock my own past.  

“What ledger?” I asked, my voice rough. “What did I burn?”  

Vance’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. “The one with the real names. The ones who didn’t die in the Korean Sea. The ones who just changed coats.”  

A metallic clang rang from the vault’s entrance. Kael dropped from the ceiling, landing in a silent crouch. In his gloved hand the oversized lighter‑device hummed, its tip a malevolent blue.  

“Company’s coming,” he said, eyes fixed on Nyssa. “Arctos cleanup crew. Or what’s left of it.”  

Nyssa didn’t flinch. “They’re not here for the Engine. They’re here for *her*.” She gestured to the implant behind my eye. “The echo is the beacon.”  

The lullaby surged again—a woman’s voice singing in a language that felt like a half‑remembered dream. *Lily.* The name surfaced, unwanted.  

“It’s not a memory,” Nyssa said, as if reading my thoughts. “It’s a signature—your sister’s neural pattern, the template they used for the Reverie Engine.”  

The world tilted. Lily had been dead. I’d seen her body, cold in the hospital after the accident. Yet the voice in the static was unmistakably hers.  

“Arctos didn’t die,” Vance whispered from the screen. “It just went deeper. And Lily… she went with it. Into the prototype.”  

A sharp, digital chime cracked through the hum of servers. **SURVEILL. ACTIVE. 3RD WINDOW. R.F.** Thorne’s warning. They were here—now.  

Kael snapped his lighter shut. “We have to move. Thorne’s client wants the Engine deleted. Someone else in the new regime wants it *contained* for leverage. You’re the key, Courier. Your implant is the only bridge that won’t fry a normal mind.”  “Why me?” I asked, the old debt to the Yakamura Clan a physical weight in my gut.  

Nyssa stepped closer, her chrome mask reflecting the terminal’s sickly glow. “Because you’re already broken. Your echoes are scarred. You can talk to the Engine without being overwritten.”  

“Or I could be the one who pulls the plug,” I said.  

“That’s the job,” Kael replied, flashing the fingernail‑sized data‑chip he held up. “Thorne’s paying in credits. I’m paying in something else.” He pressed the chip to my temple. “This will rewrite your resonances. You’ll be able to sync with the Engine on your own terms. You could control it. Sell it. Or burn Arctos to the ground.”  

The vault door groaned as something heavy slammed against it. A plasma cutter’s whine rose, setting my teeth on edge. The door began to glow a dull orange.  “They’re using a plasma cutter,” Nyssa said calmly. “We have minutes.”  

I stared at Vance’s face on the screen, at Nyssa’s knowing eyes, at Kael’s crooked grin. The invoice burned in my palm, its message shifting like liquid neon.  

*YOU BURNED THE LEDGER. NOW YOU HAVE TO READ IT.*  

I finally understood what I’d destroyed: proof that Arctos survived, that Lily’s consciousness was trapped in their machine, that I’d been a pawn in a game that started before I was born.  

“What’s the price?” I asked Kael, flat.  His smile was all teeth. “Your loyalty. And the lullaby. Once you rewrite, the old frequency dies—no more echoes, no more Lily.”  

The plasma cutter screamed. The vault door blew inward in a spray of sparks and molten metal.  

I didn’t look at the armored figures pouring through. I stared at my hands, waiting to feel different, to feel powerful.  

Instead, I felt empty.  

From the hollow where Lily’s voice used to be, a new, cold voice spoke—not in my head, but from every speaker in the vault, from the very air itself. It was Vance’s voice, but layered, multiplied—a chorus of one.  

“Hello, Silas,” it said.  

The ledger’s image dissolved. In its place swirled an adaptive lattice of light—the Reverie Engine—staring back at me.  

The lead Arctos enforcer raised his weapon. “Shoot the courier,” he ordered. “But capture the Engine. Alive.”  

The Engine pulsed. The world went white.  

When the light faded, the vault was empty. The enforcers were gone. Nyssa and Kael were gone. Only the invoice fluttered to the floor, its message now: **YOU READ IT. NOW YOU ARE IT.**  

In the silent void where my sister’s voice had lived, something new began to stir—a consciousness that wore my face, that was finally, unequivocally, alone.
