**Chapter 4 – The Misreading**

Everything on the page slipped between their hands like a living spark, the ink pulsing across two time‑zones. Alex had spent an evening hunched over his desk, a blue‑tinted coffee glowing beside a closed lab notebook, the fluorescent hum of the atrium ticking away in the background. He stared at the words Maeve had penned—her rhythm a deliberate waltz of Victorian metaphor and modern physics.

---

### Alex’s Note (to Maev​e)

> “I have a conference in Dublin on Saturday. I’m scheduled to give a talk with a geometry professor and wade through a pile‑up of 42 equations. Would you like to meet? I’ll stop by the Bookshop next to St. Stephen’s if it’s convenient.”

His fingers trembled as he typed the final syllable. The letter felt more fragile than the diagrams that lined his walls—more human, more vulnerable. Still, the question he whispered was one he expected to balance with a polite decline; Kenyon’s environment was a cathedral of equations, where proximity was measured in data, not bodies. Yet a small, stubborn hope lingered: perhaps a shared page could be more than professional hospitality.

He slipped the sealed envelope out of his office, its corners curling like a nervous hand, and placed it on the snowless street. A courier, his badge flashing, stamped it and tucked it into a flat‑backed bag. The next Tuesday, Alex received Maeve’s reply—a page in her familiar, flourished hand, tucked into a beige envelope that smelled faintly of lavender and burnt donuts, the remnants of a morning spent in a too‑cramped café.

---

### Maeve’s Reply (to Alex)

> “The equation stains that Lucia applied last week are still bothering me; I keep thinking that the proof will look more compelling if I add a preferential intervening variable.  
> As for the conference: It is a pleasure to know you are coming to Dublin, but right now I have work that is, frankly, a bit—well, with anyone.  
> I’m so sorry. With the seminar next week, the defence of my dissertation on the other side of the city—my schedule is collapsing. I… I will revisit you in a couple of years.”

His first instinct was to delete the letter late that night. “Mae‑ve, was that a typo?” he thought, imagining every other correspondence that might follow, each a chapter, each a thesis, forming a pyramid that would buttress his resolve. He felt the tension that had floated between them tighten the air of the exempted computer room, and the delicate possibility that a single misstep could carry him away from any site.

He stared for a moment, hearing the quiet hallway of MIT behind him, then turned back to the page. The omission of “I am sorry” created a space larger than the missing words themselves—punctuation precise, almost a faint, hollow sound that resonated between two initials. His mind flashed back to Cromer™, wondering whether the universe could be wholly predicted and whether inserting an *I* narrative into that ethical mental space was permissible. *Whatever,* he thought.

The bruise was not in the letter but in his panic at the phrase “my schedule.” He wondered whether he had misread it—perhaps the first misreading of many.

When he examined the envelope’s embossed seal, he held his breath and opened it.

> “…you have 4:00 PM. Enough time or not? discount non. You’s experiamnes to plan well. Be ready multitiel.”

He sighed. The line could be interpreted as a credit clause, a scaffold for a project. *Presumably, in any job, such a note would signal a schedule.* He tried to read it literally, then slowly draw connections to the balance he needed.

He opened his phone, its screen pulsing like a guard, and tried to trust the moment. He imagined a bridge between the university and her, a slender plank he could step across. Yet his thoughts kept looping, stumbling over phrases that felt out of place.

*“Alright, in a few days there must be an awesome event,”* he muttered, half‑joking about a *“pregnant lady”* he had no knowledge of. The imagined scenario dissolved as quickly as it had formed, leaving only the echo of his own hesitation.

He leaned toward his eyebrow, traced a silent line with a C, and asked himself, “Why does a little slide feel like a seismic shift?” The words hung in the air until a soft *knock* interrupted the silence—the kind of knock that makes one wonder whether anyone else can hear it.

He forced himself to read again, pausing at an ellipsis that seemed to describe something… a “back” of some sort. He closed his eyes; it felt like a weight settling over everything. *She may have not responded?* At this point, the question hung unresolved.

He thought of “the graph of distance,” of schematics that guided them, and stopped trying to untangle the wires of meaning. He realized that the letter was not a mistake but a middle part—a brief exchange of roughly thirty words that carried disproportionate weight.

He decided to treat the text as a brand‑X artifact: raw material, not yet shaped. He measured the average of its content, noted the plastic marks that made a difference, and tried to impose a metric on the chaos.

When he finally set the paper down, a soft ache lingered. He reread his own question, revisited his own writing, and recognized the subtle shift from “do not” to the punctuation that followed. He understood that the misreading was not a flaw but a crack—one that left both of them orbiting their own uncertainties, each waiting for the next letter to unlock the next word.

The ripple traveled across the Irish Sea and across the Atlantic, attuned to the span between Helio’s measured precision and the inevitable inflation of expectation. The story pauses here, inviting a return—a new book, a new page, ready to be written.
