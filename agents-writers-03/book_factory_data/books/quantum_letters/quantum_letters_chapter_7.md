**Chapter 7 – Emotional Risk**  
*Cambridge, MA – Tuesday, 11:47 p.m.*  

The envelope from Dublin felt heavier than its thin cream paper suggested. It seemed to pull at the air around it, a small, dense planet of unspoken things. Alex stood in the dim kitchen, the refrigerator’s pale glow stretching long shadows across the floor. He’d been staring at the envelope for ten minutes before he finally slit it open with a kitchen knife.  

The letter inside was shorter than usual—no poetic preamble, no literary flourish. Just a few tight paragraphs about a faculty meeting that over‑ran, a missed train, and a hurried note about her daughter’s school play being “a triumph of chaotic enthusiasm.” Then, in a different ink, as if written in haste:  *“Family, as ever, is a gravitational well. Hard to escape its pull.”*  Alex read it three times. *Gravitational well.* A scientific metaphor, but it felt cold, distant—​a wall. He thought of his own family, of the loud wedding he’d attended last weekend. He’d answered his mother’s quiet “When will you settle down?” with sterile talk of quantum coherence and imminent deadlines. *Family is a gravitational well.* Was she suggesting he was a satellite, forever caught in his own orbit? Or was she saying *she* was caught, and he was merely another body in her sky, not worth the energy to escape?  

His prepared reply curdled in his gut. He’d been about to write about the wedding, about the hollow feeling of watching his family’s easy intimacy from a distance, about how he’d spent the reception in a quiet hallway, scrolling through photos of her on his phone. He’d been about to risk it.  

But the words now tasted like ash. *She’s too busy,* he thought, the old anxiety tightening. *She’s got a daughter, a career, a family that needs her. I’m just a thought experiment from across the ocean.*  

He walked to his desk, the open letter lying there like a verdict. He pulled another sheet of MIT stationery, the university crest stark and impersonal, and drafted something neutral.  

---  **To Maeve,**  

Thank you for your note. I’m glad the play went well. Family obligations can be… all‑consuming. I’m in the middle of a data crunch; the department is pushing for a preliminary paper. It’s easy to let correspondence slide when the real world demands so much.  

I think we may have reached a point where the distance, and our respective commitments, make this more of a burden than a solace. Perhaps it’s wise to pause for a bit. Let the academic term settle. No pressure to reply.  

— Alex  

---  He didn’t reread it. He folded the paper, sealed it, wrote her address with robotic precision, and placed it on the hallway table by the door—​a physical representation of his retreat. Then he went to the window, watching a lone taxi cut through the rainy Cambridge street. He felt nothing, then a hollow ache, then a surge of anger at himself for feeling anything at all. *A burden. Solace.* The words sat like ash on his tongue.

---

**Dublin – Wednesday, 2:15 a.m.**  

Maeve was up with a teething toddler, rocking gently in the dim glow of a night‑light shaped like a hedgehog. The house was silent except for the rasp of her daughter’s breathing and the distant hum of the city. A notification glowed on her phone: *New mail from Alexchen@mit.edu.*  

Her heart leapt its familiar, traitorous rhythm. She waited until she was alone in her tiny home office—​surrounded by stacks of student papers and a cold cup of chamomile tea—​to open the email.  

The first sentence was kind. The second was merely polite. By the third, the blood had drained from her face.  

*“A burden … a solace.” “Let the pause settle.”*  She read it again, vision blurring.  

*No. That’s not what I meant—*  

She thought of her last letter, of the hurried scribble about family being a gravitational well. It was true—​her mother was ill, her daughter needed constant care, her department was in chaos over a hiring freeze. Exhausted, she’d written something bleak and final, a truth she never meant to voice aloud. She’d wanted him to *see* the messy, tired woman behind the metaphors, to still want the connection.  

But he’d heard only rejection.  

A hot, shameful tear escaped. She was so tired of being guarded. With Alex she’d felt a terrifying safety—a space where she could be scholar and mother, poet and physicist, without having to choose. And now, in her fatigue, she’d broken it. She’d built a wall with her own hand and handed him the bricks.  

Her reply was typed on one finger, the other arm cradling her sleeping child.  

---  

**Maeve,**  

Please, let me explain. My last letter was the cry of a woman drowning in laundry and faculty meetings, not a measure of my regard for our exchange. “Gravitational well” was about my own helplessness, not my feelings for you. You are the one brilliant, stable point in a chaotic sky.  

I understand if you need space; I need it too, sometimes. But please don’t mistake my exhaustion for indifference. I have been holding my breath, waiting for your letters, using them as my compass. To think I’ve made them a burden… that breaks me.  

If you still want to hear from me, I am here. If not, I will respect your silence.  

— Maeve  

---  

She hit send before she could talk herself out of it. The phone felt heavy in her hand, a stone cast into a dark ocean. She pressed her forehead to the cool window, watching dawn bleed into an Irish sky. The silence in the apartment was no longer peaceful; it was a verdict.

---

**Cambridge – Thursday, 10:00 a.m.**  

Alex stared at a LaTeX equation, but all he could see was her sign‑off: *“breaks me.”* He’d read her reply twelve times since it arrived at 3 a.m., each reading a fresh wave of nausea. *“Cry of a woman drowning.” “Brilliant, stable point.”* The words were a mirror, reflecting his own cowardice.  

He’d misread everything. Her metaphor wasn’t a wall; it was a hand reaching out from a deep well, asking to be seen. And he’d responded by building a higher wall and walking away.  His phone buzzed. An unknown Dublin number: *“It’s Maeve. I’m not asking for anything. Just… don’t let the pause be forever. M.”*  

The practical obstacles—​distance, careers, children—​loomed, immense and immovable. Yet the quiet, persistent hum in his chest, the one he’d dismissed as noise, roared louder. It was the signal he’d been treating as background static.  

He stood. The decision was a physical act. He walked to the department printer, a clunky, noisy machine. He printed a single page and wrote, not an equation, but a line from a poet she’d cited:  

*“I am not a number. I am a free person.”*  He crossed out “man” and added:  

*“And I am yours, if you’ll have me. Even from afar. Especially from afar. I was a coward. Forgive me.”*  

He left it unsigned. The paper felt reckless, off‑brand, un‑Alex. And that was precisely why he sent it. He folded the page, wrote her address on the outside of a plain envelope, and dropped it into the campus mail chute—a tiny, uncalculated leap.

---

**Dublin – Friday, 9:00 a.m.**  

The envelope arrived plain, no return address. Inside was a single printed sheet—​warm from the machine. She read his words and, for the first time in a week, breathed. It wasn’t a solution. It wasn’t a plan. It was a raw, unvarnished *mea culpa*, and it was also a declaration.  

*“I am yours.”*  

A laugh bubbled up, half‑sob, half‑joy, startling her across the quiet office. The tension that had lived in her shoulders for months eased a fraction. The gravitational well was still there—​laundry, meetings, a sick mother—but now there was a counterweight, a point of light in the darkness, and it was him.  

She picked up a pen, not to compose a long letter, but to write a sticky note and slap it to her monitor:  *“The pause ends at your convenience. I’ll be here. Listening. M.”*  

The obstacle was still immense. The practical history was a fortress. But for the first time they had both turned and looked at it, not as individuals on opposite sides of a wall, but as two people standing together in its shadow, wondering together if a door might someday be found.
