## Chapter 9: The Decision

### Cambridge, MA – Tuesday, 3:14 p.m.

The email notification blinked on Alex’s screen like a heartbeat: *Dr. Alexander Chen – Visiting Faculty Position, ETH Zurich*. The salary figure made his stomach drop—not greed, but the sharp pang of possibility. Three years. A lab of his own. Funding without the constant hum of grant deadlines.  

He closed the attachment and opened his notebook instead, fingers tracing the equation Maeve had sent last week—the one that finally made his decoherence model click. *ε as signal, not noise*. She was right, of course. Always had been.  

His phone buzzed. Another email. This one from Dublin: *Maeve O’Connell – Congratulations on your Zurich offer. I’ve just been offered the Boston visiting professorship. Small world*.  

Alex stared at the screen. *Small world*. As if their lives were folding inward like a quantum state until they collided.  

---

### Dublin – 8:47 p.m.

Maeve’s laptop battery died mid-sentence, forcing her to prop it against a stack of books to type one-handed while Lucy stirred in the next room. The offer letter from Boston felt weightless in her palm—closer, warmer, *possible*—but her mother’s voice crackled through the phone: *“You’ll be alone again, * aois *.”*  

She’d been following Alex’s work since the Zurich announcement. Not stalking—*scholarship*. His latest paper on narrative coherence in quantum systems had arrived that morning. She read it twice, then a third time, searching for the metaphor he hadn’t written but somehow meant: *“The observer’s presence alters the observed, not by force, but by becoming part of the system.”*  Her reply to his congratulatory email hovered over the keyboard. *I’ve been thinking about your paper. The ‘observer’—what if we’re both observing each other without knowing it?* She deleted it. Typed: *The Boston position is confirmed. I’m accepting. Perhaps we’ll finally have somewhere to meet between the lines.*  

Sent. Then stared at the ceiling, wondering if she’d just handed him the keys to a door neither of them knew was locked.  

---

### Cambridge, MA – Wednesday, 12:07 a.m.

Alex read her letter three times. The first, he focused on the words. The second, on the spaces between them. The third, on the way her handwriting steadied toward the end—as if she’d found something solid to anchor against.  *Boston*. Not Dublin. Not Zurich. But closer.  
*Perhaps we’ll finally have somewhere to meet between the lines*.  

His throat tightened. He pulled up his calendar and circled the dates: *October 15–18*. Dublin. A conference. She’d be there.  

Typed: *I’m coming to Dublin. I’ll be at the Shelbourne on the 15th. If you’re free for coffee, I’d like to—*  
Deleted.  
Typed: *I’d like to see you.*  
Deleted.  

Final version: *I’m coming to Dublin. I’ll be at the Shelbourne on the 15th. If you’re free for coffee, I’d like to talk about the paper. About what comes next.*  

Sent. Then spent an hour wondering if he’d just risked everything on a sentence that might mean nothing.  

---

### Dublin – Thursday, 9:03 a.m.

Maeve read his reply on the LUAS, clutching her tablet as the tram rattled toward Sandymount. *Coffee*. Not dinner. Not a walk on the beach. Coffee—safe, public, temporary.  

But it was something.  

She remembered their first letter exchange, the way his equations had made her laugh despite herself. *“Uncertainty isn’t a flaw,” he’d written. “It’s the space where possibility lives.”* She’d taped that page inside her desk drawer, next to her mother’s wedding photo.  

Her phone buzzed. A text from Alex: *Flight details attached. I’m staying at the Shelbourne. October 15th, 10:00 a.m. Sharp. Don’t be late.*  

Maeve smiled. Then paused.  
*Was he serious?*  ---
