**Chapter 1 – “The Weight of Roots”**  

The greenhouse loomed like a hollow eye in the cracked earth, its glass panels fogged with frost and the sour tang of mold. Kael crouched, his fingertips tracing the graffiti on the rusted door—*Built to last. Built to fail*. Mira leaned against the opposite wall, the callouses on her palms still raw from a trowel she’d set down only minutes before. This was their third cache of the week; the others had been smaller, more precarious. Scarcity never showed itself in vaults—it showed up in the arithmetic of *enough*.  

They’d agreed to split the harvest evenly: twenty kilos each. Mira counted the amber seeds in her palm, the way she always did. “For the fields south of Redoubt,” she said, tapping the handful. Kael didn’t answer. He wanted to keep them all. *Just one more day of control*. Mira always anchored reality, but today the weight of the seeds felt heavier than the paper they were written on.  

A squirrel burst from the freshly patched fence, copper fur flashing, its tiny paws scattering gravel. Both froze. It turned, ears twitching, and bolted toward the open doorway.  

“It’s heading straight for the cache,” Kael hissed, already stepping into the breach.  

Mira raised a hand. “We inventory first.”  

The vault was a ruin of its own making—dust motes drifting in the faint light that leaked through the broken panes. Shelves sagged under grain sacks and rusted canisters. Kael began sorting the seeds by tribe: heirlooms, expired, promising shoots. Mira followed, their motions falling into a familiar rhythm—until Kael stopped.  

“Pause.” He held up a cluster of tiny white seeds, the kind that could sprout a whole season’s worth of wheat. “Two kilos for you. The rest…” His fingers clutching the pile trembled.  

“You want to hoard them?” Mira’s voice was quiet, but edged with a tenderness she didn’t try to hide. She reached out, fingers brushing his wrist. “We’ve already lost so much. Let’s not lose this, too.”  

Kael’s eyes flicked to the squirrel’s empty perch, then back to the seeds. “Store them until—” He couldn’t finish. The squirrel’s sudden appearance had shaken something loose in him—a flicker of the freedom they’d both been chasing.  

Mira’s breath steadied. “They’re food. Then *use* them. The fields can’t wait.”  A beat of silence settled over the vault, broken only by the drip of condensation on the far wall. Kael exhaled, the air tasting of rust and possibility, and finally let the white seeds slip into a half‑measured bin. He took 18 kg, Mira took 20 kg, and the tally settled into an uneasy balance.  

Later, at the perimeter camp, the rations became a silent ledger. Kael weighed his portion on a scarred scale, eyes locked on the numbers. “You left me with barely enough for one day.” He didn’t name *what* the shortfall meant.  

Mira met his stare, the weight of their shared hunger humming between them. “You felt entitled.”  

He didn’t deny it. Instead, he traced the grains with a fingertip, marking each meal in meticulous lines: 500 g per day, 428, 430, 443. Every bite began with a countdown that felt less like survival and more like a promise he wasn’t sure he could keep.  

The squirrel returned at night, a single nut clutched in its jaws. It scurried toward Mira’s sleeping bag, paused, then vanished into the darkness. Kael woke before dawn, the faint rustle still echoing in his ears.  

He stared at the empty space where the creature had been, then at the half‑filled bins. The vault’s door creaked open as the first pale light bled into the ruins.  

“Come on,” he said, voice low but decisive. “The fields aren’t going to wait for us.”  

Mira hesitated only a heartbeat, then gathered the remaining seeds, tucking them into the satchels they’d carried through every cracked highway and abandoned warehouse. Together they stepped out of the ruined vault, the weight of the seeds now balanced by the lighter, steadier rhythm of their footsteps.  

The earth beyond the greenhouse was cracked but open, and for the first time in weeks, the horizon felt reachable.
