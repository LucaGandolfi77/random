**CHAPTER 2 – “The Broadcast”**  
*Edited for clarity, rhythm, and emotional punch*  

---

The ridge rose like a broken spine against a bruised‑purple sky, its jagged shoulders clutching the last sliver of daylight. Below, the greenhouse’s glass‑eyed sigh still murmured, *“Built to last. Built to fail.”*  Kael slipped the strap of his satchel tighter. The amber and white seeds shifted against his ribs, a tangible weight of tomorrow. Mira kept pace, her coat’s hem snagging on the broken fence‑post that had become their compass.

They reached the old radio shack at the ridge’s edge just as the sun bled into a thin line of violet. The metal box was half‑buried in sand, its antenna twisted into a jagged curl. Inside, a cracked speaker sputtered a half‑gone lullaby – the same melody Mira once sang to a child in the orphanage before the walls fell. The tune hung in the air, a fragile thread that threatened to snap.

Kael knelt, palms pressed to the cold metal. A faint glow pulsed from a solar panel strapped to his back – chipped cells still humming after weeks of rain‑soaked neglect. He’d scavenged the panel from a collapsed power station two valleys east; it was a relic of the *Branded Method* of power generation that the old world had patented and then abandoned.

Mira’s eyes narrowed. “We can’t use that,” she said, voice as flat as the cracked concrete. “If anyone sees it, they’ll come looking for the source. They’ll think we’ve got a supply line.”

Kael lifted his chin, the panel catching the dying light. “It’s just a few watts. Enough to pull the static off the band, maybe catch a voice. We’ve been hoarding food, but we’re starving for signal.”

A flicker of tension passed between them – a tremor in the chest that settled in the throat. The radio crackled, a hiss that seemed to rise from the earth itself. In the distance a siren wailed, then fell silent.

“Branded Method,” Mira repeated, tasting the phrase like old law. “They used it to tag everything that mattered. If we tag ourselves with that panel, we’re advertising we still have power. That makes us a target.”

Kael’s fingers tightened around the strap. “Or we’re advertising we still have hope.” He opened the weathered casing, exposing thin copper coils. “One more day of this, and we might actually hear something that isn’t our own thoughts echoing back at us.”

Mira breathed in short, measured bursts. She glanced toward the horizon, where the dead suburb sprawled like a graveyard of broken windows. Somewhere beyond, a convoy of makeshift carts rattled over shattered pavement, their drivers counting coins of salvaged copper. The thought of another trade lingered – a micro‑exchange of energy credits for a few moments of clear communication.

Kael set the panel on the ground, angling it toward the weak sun. The cells flickered, then steadied into a thin green pulse. He nudged the radio’s power switch; a soft pop announced a surge. The speaker sputtered, then, against all odds, a voice emerged – a woman’s tone, trembling, reading a list of names.

*…Mira H., Kael J.,…*  

The names hit them like stones. Their own names, spoken aloud by a voice that seemed to belong to a world they’d thought dead. Mira’s hand froze over the panel. Kael’s ledger – the one he’d kept since the first ration log – suddenly felt heavier in his mind.

> **Log Entry 47 – Solar output 0.7 kW; ration allocation 500 g per day, 60 % shelter, 40 % signal.**  

He had written the split in black ink just days before, the numbers a promise to himself: survive, then maybe, maybe rebuild. The radio’s crackle rose; the woman’s voice layered the lullaby’s refrain over her list.

Mira swallowed, throat dry. “If we keep this thing running, we’ll need more power than we have bread.” She gestured toward the half‑measured bin of white seeds still perched on the ridge’s lip. “Every gram of seed is a gram of tomorrow. Every watt of power is a gram of that tomorrow taken away.”

Kael stared at the panel, then at the bruised sky. “We can’t keep the radio on all night.” He adjusted his voice, steadier now. “We’ll ration the signal like we ration the water – a burst, maybe once a week, when the wind is calm enough to carry it far enough.”

Mira’s eyes softened, a smile flickering at the corners. “A broadcast that costs us half the day’s food… a trade we can’t afford, but maybe we can barter.” She lifted a rusted canister of water. “Two days of water for a half‑hour of voice.”

Kael tapped the canister, feeling its cold metal. “Trade with whoever’s listening on the other side?” He laughed, short and raw. “We don’t even know if anyone’s out there.”

The radio hissed, swallowing their words. Then, as if on cue, a small rustle erupted from the ridge’s edge. The copper‑furred squirrel from the greenhouse reappeared, clutching a single nut. It scuttled across the cracked concrete, paused at the edge of the solar panel, and—deliberately—dropped the nut onto the metal before vanishing into the gloom.

Mira stared at the nut, then at Kael. “It’s still here,” she whispered. “Even when we think we’re alone.”

Kael lifted the panel, feeling its steady hum against his shoulders. He tightened the strap and nodded toward the dead suburbs, the cracked highways stretching ahead like veins of an old body. Rain began to patter on the metal roof of the shack, each drop a reminder that scarcity was measured—water, food, power, hope.

They turned, and Kael’s ledger ticked off another entry in his mind:

> **Log Entry 48 – Solar panel secured. Ration split: 300 g seed, 200 g water, 0 g power. Decision: broadcast weekly, sacrifice 10 % of daily seed ration to maintain signal.**  

The trade was made. The balance tipped, but they moved forward, the weight of roots still heavy in their hands, the future still unplanted, yet no longer completely out of reach.
