**Chapter 4 – “The Harvest”**  

The first light bled through the cracked canopy of dead trees, turning the field into a mosaic of ruin. Kael led Mira through a tangle of vines—*their makeshift map of the plots where crops once thrived*. Now the plants clawed at each other, roots wrapped in a language of desperation. He’d promised to check this field, a relic of the old agrists’ faith. *They built shelter here*, he’d told Mira before dawn. *They trusted the soil*.

She said nothing. Her boots sank into mud that clung to soybean pods knee‑high, the scent of rot and alcohol sharper than the usual musk of earth. Kael’s canteen hissed as he tilted it, water sloshing into the leather of his satchel. *Leaked again. Since yesterday.* He’d tried to fix it all night, but the seal failed when a squirrel—*can’t blame the damn animal*—did what it did.

Mira paused at a patch of withered corn. “We can’t eat this,” she said, eyeing ears that cracked open to reveal blackened kernels. “Unless you *want* to gnaw on pesticide.”

He reached for the two seed pods he’d saved from the last cache. “These might sprout in weeks. If we plant them—”

“We don’t have soil,” she snapped. The words came out sharper than usual. Her voice was a fraying thread. Kael noticed.

The vines snagged his ankle as he stumbled. Mira caught him, her fingers brushing his wrist—*not the faint scent of lavender, not the orphanage lullaby that had once been her comfort, but the raw grease of survival*. The touch was a reprimand.

“Eat the seeds,” she muttered, tossing them into his hand. “You’re dragging yourself around like a dead weight.”

He chewed them anyway, the starch bitter. Mira held his canteen, refusing to let another drop be lost. Every movement drained their reserves—*350 g of water, 200 g of seeds*. No waste. No excess.
