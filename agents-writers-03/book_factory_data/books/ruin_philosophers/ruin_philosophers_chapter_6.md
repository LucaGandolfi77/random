**Chapter 6 –“The Arbitrium of Stone” (Edited)**  

---

Dawn cracked the horizon in a thin line of ochre, barely enough to see by. Kael led them down the ridge’s eastern slope, the satchel thumping against his hip with each step. Inside, the seeds settled into a quiet weight—3 kg of amber, 2 kg of white—still accounted for.  

Mira kept three paces behind, boots skidding on loose scree. She hadn’t spoken since they’d packed the radio panel. The half‑hour of radio chatter had cost them a cold night and a kilogram of white seed traded for copper contact, but the woman’s words—*supplies moving east to old mill, copper‑trade convoy passes ridge at noon*—were still sharp in Kael’s skull.  

He checked the sun. They had maybe four hours before the convoy reached the ridge, maybe five if the carts were heavy. They needed to be down in the valley, hidden, before anyone saw movement.  

The tunnel mouth appeared out of the grey rock like a wound—a dark gash ringed by rusted rebar and crumbling concrete. The road had once run through it, a two‑lane cut straight into the mountain. Now the ceiling sagged in the middle, a slab of stone held up by prayer and a few twisted beams.  

Kael stopped. Mira drew level with him, breath fogging the cold air.  

*“We can make it through in twenty minutes,”* he said, measuring the distance with his eyes. *“On the other side, the mill is half a day’s walk east. We’d be there before nightfall.”*  

Mira knelt, fingers brushing the crack in the asphalt. A trickle of dust fell from the ceiling. *“That beam is bowing. If it goes while we’re inside—”*  

*“Then we dig out.”* His answer was flat, without conviction.  

*“With what? Our hands? We have one shovel, a leaking canteen, and eight‑hundred grams of dried rations between us.”* She stood, wiping grit from her palms. *“Even if we make it through, we’ll lose half the day’s water sweating in the dark. The longer route adds three hours but keeps us in the open. We can see what’s coming.”*  

Kael stared at the tunnel. Its darkness had a texture—thick, damp, heavy with rust and wet stone. He thought about the ledger entry he would write tonight: *‑20 min tunnel vs. +3 h detour. Risk of collapse vs. risk of exposure.* Neither choice felt clean.  

*“The convoy,”* he said slowly. *“If they’re moving east at noon, they’ll see us on the open ridge.”*  

*“If we’re in the tunnel when it collapses, they won’t see anything. Neither will we.”*  

He wanted to argue. The tunnel was direct, efficient, the kind of calculus he trusted. But Mira watched him with that steady, tired patience she always wore when she was right.  *“The alternate route?”* he asked.  

She pointed southeast, where ground fell into a ravine choked with vegetation. *“That gully runs parallel to the ridge. We follow it for two klicks, then cut back through a saddle. The growth is thick—we’ll have to push through, but it’s cover.”*  

*“Thick with what?”*  

*“Hard to tell from here. Something wiry. Maybe blackberry, maybe sumac. Could be wild rose, could be poison ivy.”*  

He touched the white seeds in his satchel. Three kilos of possibility. *Don’t gamble them on a tunnel that might hold.*  

*“We go around,”* he said.  

Mira nodded once. No triumph, no relief—just the quiet acknowledgment of a trade made.  

---

The ravine was worse than she’d described. The vegetation was a wall of tangled stems, thick as a man’s wrist, studded with thorns the length of a fingernail. Kael pushed first, using the shovel to hack a path. The vines whipped back, catching his coat, scoring his forearms. He didn’t stop.  

Behind him, Mira followed in the gap he made, her pack scraping against the brambles. She said nothing, but he heard her breath quicken with each step.  

An hour later they’d covered maybe half a kilometre. The sun had climbed, but the ravine was deep enough that only thin shafts of light broke through the canopy. The air was still, heavy with the smell of crushed leaves and something else—a sweet, cloying odor that clung to the back of the throat.  *“Stop.”*  

Kael lowered the shovel. Mira was crouched, examining a stem he’d just cut. The sap oozed clear, then turned milky white, then yellow.  

*“Don’t touch it,”* she warned. *“That’s white oleander. The sap’s toxic. If it gets in a cut—”*  

He looked at his arms. Dozens of small scratches, already reddening. He wiped the shovel blade on his pant leg.  

*“We can’t go back,”* he said. *“We’re too deep.”*  

*“We keep moving. Just don’t touch your face. And don’t let it get in your eyes.”*  

They pressed on. The path narrowed until they were walking single file, shoulders brushing the thorn walls. The sweet smell grew stronger, mingling with the copper tang of blood from Kael’s arms. He kept his head down, watching his boots find purchase in the soft earth.  

A flicker of movement ahead. He froze, raising a hand. A copper‑furred squirrel sat on a fallen branch, head cocked, nose twitching the air. Then it darted deeper into the ravine, disappearing around a bend.  

*“It’s leading us,”* Mira whispered.  

*“Or it’s fleeing something.”*  

*“Same thing.”*  

They followed. The squirrel stayed just ahead, visible for a few seconds before vanishing. After twenty more minutes the ravine widened into a clearing. The ground here was different—less compact, dotted with pale mushrooms that glowed faintly in the dim light. Among them: strange, translucent stalks that grew in spiral patterns, leaves like jelly.  Mira stopped at the edge of the clearing. *“We don’t know what that is.”*  

*“We don’t know what anything is anymore,”* Kael replied. He stepped forward. The ground squelched under his boots—not mud, but something spongy, yielding like decayed flesh.  

The squirrel sat in the centre of the clearing on a flat stone that seemed out of place. It chittered once, then bounded away through a gap in the far wall of vegetation.  

Kael looked at Mira. She was pale, lips pressed thin. *“We go through the clearing, or we climb out of the ravine and risk the open ridge.”*  

She was silent for a long moment. Then she pulled the ledger from his satchel—he hadn’t even noticed she’d taken it—and read the last entry he’d written: *Source C‑13. 350 g water. 1,200 g dry rations. 3 kg amber seed. 2 kg white seed. 8 h to mill.*  

*“If we detour over the ridge, we lose another hour,”* she said. *“That puts us in the open during the convoy’s window.”*  

*“So we go through the clearing.”*  

*“We don’t know if the ground will hold. Or what’s under it.”*  

Kael looked at the stone where the squirrel had sat. It was smooth, almost polished, half‑buried in the spongy earth. Carved into it, letters worn but legible:  

**ARBITRIUM**  

“Judgment,” he said. *“This is a boundary stone.”*  

Mira traced the letters with her finger. *“Someone marked this place. Marked it as something.”*  *“Maybe a cemetery. Or a sanctuary.”*  

*“Or a warning.”*  Kael stood. The clearing stretched ahead, maybe a hundred metres of pale, glowing flora. On the far side, the ravine resumed—steeper but open. He could see sky again.  

*“We go through,”* he said. *“Quickly. Stepping only on the stones.”*  

Mira hesitated, then nodded. She pulled the blanket from her pack and wrapped it around her exposed hands; Kael did the same with his coat sleeves.  

They moved in a tight line, stepping from one embedded stone to the next. The ground pulsed slightly underfoot, as though the earth itself were breathing. The sweet oleander scent faded, replaced by a sterile, chemical odor—like the inside of a sealed vault.  

Halfway across, Mira stumbled. Her foot slipped off a stone into the pale soil. The ground opened like a mouth, sucking her boot down to the ankle. She gasped, jerking back, and came free with a wet tearing sound. The soil closed again, seamless.  

Kael caught her arm. *“Keep moving. Don’t stop.”*  They ran the last twenty metres, boots pounding on the stones, and burst out of the clearing into the narrow throat of the ravine beyond. The air here was cool, normal, smelling of dirt and water.  

Kael doubled over, breathing hard. Mira leaned against a rock, inspecting her boot. The leather was stained with a viscous, clear liquid that beaded like sweat. She scraped it off with a stick.  

*“New rule,”* she said, voice thin. *“No more shortcuts through glowing clearings.”*  

*“Noted.”*  

Kael uncapped his stub of pencil, opened the ledger, and wrote:  

*Ravine cleared. Lost shoe, gained route. Ground unstable at clearing. Arbritium marker—origin unknown. Travelled by stone path. No contact. No injury. 5 h to mill.*  

He lifted his eyes to the sky. The sun was past noon now, sliding toward evening. The convoy would be cresting the ridge behind them, maybe already past. They hadn’t been seen.  *“We keep moving,”* he said. *“Another two hours, maybe three, we’ll hit the saddle. From there we can see the mill.”*  

Mira pushed off the rock. Her coat was torn at the shoulder, a thin line of blood showing through. She didn’t mention it.  

They walked in silence, the squirrel nowhere in sight. The ravine walls grew steeper, the vegetation thinning to scrub and occasional patches of brittle grass. The mill was close; Kael could feel it in the way the air changed—drier, warmer, carrying the faint scent of burnt wood and old ash.  

But the sterile taste of the glowing clearing lingered in his mouth. *Arbritium.* Judgment. He wondered what the stone marked, and whether they had passed a test—or simply failed one by choosing to cross.  Mira’s voice cut the quiet. *“When we get to the mill—what do we trade the seeds for?”*  

*“Water. New canteen. Maybe a tarp if we can afford it.”*  

*“And the broadcast? Do we try to find the woman who named us?”*  

Kael had no answer. The ledger entry for today was already full—numbers, routes, a name carved in stone. Tomorrow’s page was blank.  

*“We find shelter first,”* he said. *“Then we decide what to grow.”*  They walked on, the ravine opening slowly into grey light, the weight of the seeds pressing against Kael’s ribs like a second heartbeat.
