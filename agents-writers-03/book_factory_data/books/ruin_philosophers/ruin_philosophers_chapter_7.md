**Chapter 7 – “The Weight of Stones” (Edited)**  

The tremor that rolled across the ridge was not a distant rumble but a shudder that ran up their boots. Dust fell from the cracked ceiling of the old radio shack, and the solar panel’s amber glow flickered as if the earth itself were exhaling.  

Kael crouched by the ledger, thumb tracing the line for today’s balance:  *‑500 g water, ‑200 g food, +1 h solar.*  

He glanced at the satchel.  

- 3 kg amber seed  
- 2 kg white seed  
- 350 g water leaking from the canteen  
- 400 g dried rations  Numbers were a thin line of hope in a world that measured life in grams.  

Mira stood a few paces away, her coat clinging to the thin blanket she’d salvaged from an abandoned shelter. She pressed the cracked speaker of the radio to her ear. Static hissed, then a faint voice—her old orphanage lullaby—drifted through, broken by the wind.  

“Do we stay?” she asked, voice flat, the same tone she used when weighing the cost of a single seed.  

Kael’s eyes flicked to the ridge’s eastern slope. The collapsed tunnel mouth yawned like a wound. The path through it was the fastest route to the old mill, where a copper convoy might bring fresh water and a trade for the white seeds. The alternative—venturing into the forgotten tomb at the base of the ridge—was slower, exposed, and carried the risk of a collapse that could bury them both.  He weighed the options in his mind, the ledger’s cold arithmetic louder than any shouted warning.  

**Option A – Split the group**  
- Kael would go down into the tomb alone, using the remaining water and a handful of amber seeds as a makeshift beacon.  
- Mira would stay in the shack, conserve water, keep the radio powered, and watch for any sign of the tremor’s source.  

**Option B – Stay together**  
- Descend the ridge as a pair, navigate the tunnel while keeping the ledger balanced.  
- This would cost precious daylight and risk attracting scavenger patrols.  

Kael’s fingers tightened around the satchel strap. He glanced at the cracked concrete of the tomb’s entrance, half‑buried under a slab of stone shifted by the tremor. A gust brushed the opening, revealing a thin line of dust and the faint outline of a doorway framed by rusted rebar.  

“Fine,” he said, voice low. “We split. I’ll go in. You keep the radio alive. If the stone gives, we both die. If it holds, we both get something.”  

Mira’s jaw tightened. She reached for the ledger, her pen scratching a new entry:  *‑200 g water, ‑100 g food, +1 h solar, +1 person (Kael).*  

A second line, a counter‑balance:  

*+1 person (Mira) – stay, conserve.*  

She looked up, eyes meeting his. “If you find the seed, we’ll have a chance. If you don’t… we’ll both be dead.”  

Kael nodded. He slipped the satchel off his shoulder, feeling the weight of the amber seeds shift against his ribs. He took one white seed, tucked it into his pocket, and turned toward the tomb.  ---

### The Descent  

The descent into darkness was a slow, deliberate crawl. The air grew colder; the smell of damp earth thick with rust. Kael’s boots scraped broken concrete, each step sending a thin plume of dust into the stale air. The ledger pressed against his chest—a reminder that every gram counted.  

He reached the doorway and pushed aside a slab of broken stone. Inside, the tomb was a vaulted chamber, its basalt tiles once bearing the insignia of a long‑forgotten agrarian council. In the center a shallow depression held a basin of sand, half‑filled with dry, cracked earth.  

Kael knelt, breath fogging the air, and lifted the sand. Beneath it lay a bundle of woven cloth, its fibers frayed but intact. He peeled it back to reveal a small iron‑capped chest, its surface pitted by time. A rusted latch resisted his attempts to pry it open.  

He set the ledger on a nearby slab and recorded:  

*‑50 g water (to keep the latch from rusting), ‑100 g food (to keep his strength), +1 h solar (to power a small torch).*  

An oil‑lamp salvaged from a wrecked vehicle flickered, casting trembling amber light over the chest. With a trembling hand he forced the latch. The lid creaked and swung open.  

Inside lay a single, perfectly formed seed pod, larger than any he’d seen in the greenhouse. Its shell was a deep violet, speckled with faint luminescent veins that pulsed faintly in the torchlight.  

Kael’s mind raced. The mythic seed—rumored to sprout a tree that could feed an entire valley—was said to be buried in the “heart of the old world.” If this was it, the seed could indeed save them. The chamber, however, was unstable; the tremor had already shown that the stone could shift at any moment.  

He checked his watch—a salvaged digital timer that now read **02:13**. Roughly ninety minutes of daylight remained before night fell completely, and the temperature was dropping fast. The ledger demanded a decision: keep the seed and risk a collapse, or leave it and preserve his life.  

He slipped the seed into his satchel, feeling the weight of hope settle against his ribs. Then, with practiced motion, he began to backtrack, counting each step, noting the distance to the entrance. He knew that every extra second spent inside increased the chance of a cave‑in.  When he emerged, the ridge was bathed in the last amber glow of the setting sun. Mira was already there, silhouette framed against the dying light, the radio antenna still pointing skyward.  

“Did you find anything?” she asked, voice barely above the wind.  

Kael held out the satchel; the seed’s violet shell caught the last rays. “Found a seed. It’s… it’s alive.”  Mira’s eyes widened, but she didn’t smile. “What’s the cost?”  He opened the ledger, pen hovering over the page. He wrote:  

*‑1 kg amber seed (used to mark path), ‑200 g water (leak), ‑100 g food (consumed), +1 h solar (torch), +1 person (survival).*  

He looked at Mira, the numbers stark against the bleak horizon. “If we stay together, we’ll need to ration water and food for the next two days. If we split, you keep the radio, I keep the seed. We both risk the collapse.”  

Mira stared at the seed, then at the ledger, then at the dark mouth of the tomb behind them. She inhaled sharply, the sound too loud in the stillness.  

“Let’s stay,” she said. “We’ll go down together. If the stone gives, we die together. If it holds, we get the seed and the water we need for the mill.”  

Kael felt reluctant agreement surge through him. Staying meant sharing the limited water and food, risking that the seed would be lost if the chamber collapsed, but it also meant they would not be alone when the tomb shifted.  

He added a final line to the ledger:  

*‑200 g water, ‑150 g food, +1 h solar (shared torch), +1 person (both).*  

The ink dried, the numbers a cold contract between them. They turned toward the collapsed tunnel, the ridge’s jagged spine looming above, the wind howling like a warning.  

The descent this time was slower. Mira led, finding purchase on loose scree; Kael followed, the satchel thumping against his hip. The tunnel’s ceiling sagged, a slab of stone propped up by a rusted steel beam that groaned with each step. Every creak sounded like a promise of doom.  

Halfway down, a sudden shudder rattled the walls. Dust fell from the ceiling, and a low rumble reverberated through stone. Mira stopped, hand gripping the beam, eyes narrowed.  

“We’re not alone,” she whispered, though no one else was there. “The tremor… it’s not just the ridge.”  

Kael glanced at the ledger. Water was down 200 g, food down 150 g. They had barely enough to last the night if they stayed in the tunnel.  He made a decision. “We’ll set up a temporary camp here.” He said. “We’ll use the torch for light, ration the water, and keep the seed safe. If the stone gives, we’ll have a chance to crawl out before it’s too late.”  

Mira nodded, face set. She spread a thin piece of canvas over a flat stone, creating a makeshift shelter. Kael placed the seed in the center, covering it with a thin layer of sand to protect it from the cold.  

They ate the remaining 400 g of dried rations in silence, each bite a reminder of how thin their margin was. The ledger recorded the consumption:  

*‑150 g food, ‑100 g water (sipped sparingly), +1 h solar (torch), +0 person (still together).*  

Night deepened; the wind wailed through the tunnel’s mouth like a wounded animal. The torch’s flame flickered, casting long shadows that danced across cracked walls. The seed’s violet shell glowed faintly, as if reacting to the darkness.  

At first light, a thin ribbon of pale sun filtered through the tunnel’s opening, illuminating a narrow passage that led deeper into the earth. Mira stood, stretching her stiff limbs, and looked at Kael.  

“We have two choices now,” she said. “We can follow this passage and hope it leads to the old mill, or we can backtrack and try to reach the ridge before the sun is high enough to expose us.”  

Kael checked his watch. **06:45**. Roughly three hours of daylight remained before the ridge would be fully exposed.  

He weighed his options in his head, the ledger’s numbers flashing before his eyes.  

**Option C – Follow the passage**  
- Could lead to the mill, but the tunnel is uncharted; collapse risk is high and they may lose the seed.  

**Option D – Return to the ridge**  
- Use remaining daylight to reach the old mill directly, conserving the seed and water, but risk never testing the seed’s promise.  

He looked at Mira, already pulling the satchel tighter around her shoulders.  “We’ve already split the water and food,” he said. “If we go deeper, we’ll need to ration even more. If we turn back, we’ll have enough to make it to the mill, but we’ll lose the chance to test the seed.”  

Mira’s eyes softened for a moment, then hardened.  

“We’ve already lost so much. Let’s see if this seed can give us something more than just a few extra days.”  

Kael nodded. He added a final entry to the ledger:  

*‑100 g water, ‑50 g food, +1 h solar (torch), +1 person (both). Decision: proceed deeper into the tunnel.*  

They gathered the remaining supplies, secured the seed in the satchel, and began moving deeper into the tunnel. Each step was a gamble, each breath a reminder that the world outside cared little for their hopes or fears.  

The tunnel widened into a larger chamber, its walls lined with ancient glyphs that glowed faintly in the torchlight. In the center stood a stone pedestal upon which rested a rusted iron box. Kael approached, heart pounding, lifted the lid, and found a small, weathered map—a parchment depicting the old mill, the ridge, and a marked location labeled **“Seed Vault.”** The ink was faded but legible.  

Mira traced a line with her finger.  

“If we follow this, we’ll reach the mill in two days. The seed could be used to start a new crop there. It’s worth the risk.”  Kael looked at the seed, then at the map, then at the ledger. He wrote a final note:  

*‑50 g water, ‑50 g food, +1 h solar (torch), +1 person (both). Path chosen: follow map to seed vault, aim for mill.*  

The decision was made. They turned back toward the tunnel’s entrance, the weight of the seed pressing against Kael’s side, the ledger’s numbers a cold reminder that every gram counted. The ridge loomed ahead, the sun climbing higher, promising both danger and a chance at a future that might finally be more than survival.  

As they emerged from the tunnel onto the dusty slope of the ridge, the wind picked up, scattering loose stones across the path. In the distance, the silhouette of the old mill rose against the pale sky, its broken walls a promise of water and perhaps trade.  

Kael glanced at Mira, the ledger clenched in his hand, the seed’s violet shell catching the morning light.  

“We’re moving,” he said, voice steady despite the tremor still echoing in his bones. “One step at a time.”  

Mira gave a small, determined nod.  

“And we’ll keep the ledger. If we survive, the numbers will tell the story.”  

Together they began the trek toward the old mill, the weight of stones and hope pressing on their shoulders, each step a concrete tradeoff between scarcity and the stubborn, humane desire to rebuild.
