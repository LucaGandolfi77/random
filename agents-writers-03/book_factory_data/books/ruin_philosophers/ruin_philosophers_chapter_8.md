**Chapter 8 – “The Messenger’s Throat”**

The wind had changed. It carried the smell of wet stone and something else—iron, perhaps, or old blood.  

Kael was checking the ledger by the last of the solar glow when a rustle rose from the dead ferns below the ridge. Not the skitter of a squirrel, but a heavier drag.

Mira was already at the radio, ear pressed to the speaker, when she froze. “Something’s moving.”

Kael cut the panel’s output. Darkness swallowed the shack. He pressed a finger to his lips, then pointed to the south slope. A shadow detached itself from the grey rock.

It was a boy—maybe ten—wrapped in a coat of greasy pelts, one arm clutched to his side. He stumbled into the clearing, his breath ragged. His eyes, wide and fever‑bright, found them.

“Water,” he croaked. “Please.”

Mira started forward. Kael’s hand shot out, gripping her wrist. “Stop.”

The boy froze, his gaze darting to the pistol at Kael’s hip, then to the solar panel, the radio. He understood. “I’m alone,” he said, the words cracking. “The creek bed… it’s poisoned. My… my sister drank.”

Mira tugged against Kael’s hold. “He’s just a kid.”

“They send kids,” Kael said, his voice flat. “To find the soft spots. To see who shares.” He calculated: their water was down to 280 g in the canteen, plus 150 g in a salvaged bottle—two days’ worth, if they stretched it. The wound on the boy’s side—a deep gash stained black with old blood—was a siphon.

The boy took a shuddering step. “I’m not a scout. I ran. From the convoy. They… they take the wounded.”

Mira’s eyes met Kael’s. She didn’t plead. She just waited, her hand open at her side, as if already offering.

Kael’s jaw tightened. He glanced at the ledger in his other hand, at the columns of debits and credits: *‑500 g water, ‑200 g food*. He thought of the violet seed pod in his satchel, pulsing like a tiny, desperate heart. *What civilization is worth rebuilding?* The question wasn’t philosophical; it was in the weight of the canteen.

“The water’s tainted,” he lied, the words like stones. “We have nothing clean to spare.”

The boy’s face crumpled, but he didn’t beg. He nodded once, turned, and shuffled back into the ferns, a broken thing returning to the poisoned earth.

Mira fell silent. She stared at the spot where he vanished, her expression unreadable. The lie settled coldly in Kael’s gut.

“He’ll die out there,” she said finally.

“Or he’ll lead them back here,” Kael countered. He checked the pistol, the action smooth and familiar. “We survive the next forty‑eight hours; we reach the mill. That’s the only story that matters.”

She didn’t argue. She went to the radio, turned the dial, and let the static hiss fill the silence. The lullaby that sometimes emerged was gone—only the empty, crackling void remained.

At dawn, Kael found her at the eastern edge of the ridge, staring down. The boy’s tracks were already blurred by the wind, but a single, dark feather lay on a stone. Not a bird’s—too fine, too black.

Mira picked it up. “What if we’re the convoy?” she asked softly. “What if we’re the ones who take the wounded?”

Kael took the feather from her. It was light, insubstantial. “Then we’re dead. And the seed dies with us.”

He dropped it. It spun on the wind, a tiny, useless messenger, and was gone.

They packed the solar panel, the ledger, the last of the water. The path to the tomb was clear, exposed. The tremor had left new cracks in the stone. As they descended, Kael’s boot dislodged a rock. It clattered down into the dark throat of the tunnel, and for a moment they both listened to the echo, waiting for an answer that never came.
