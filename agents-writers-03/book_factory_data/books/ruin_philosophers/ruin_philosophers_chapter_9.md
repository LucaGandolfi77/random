**Chapter 9 – “The Poisoned Stream” (Edited)**  

The narrow mouth of the creek they’d found at dusk held only damp earth and the occasional drip of water from a hidden spring. Kael slung his satchel over his shoulder and brushed the limp handful of white seeds he’d reluctantly given Mira earlier. The numbers on his ledger stared back at him:  

- **280 g water** left in the canteen  
- **350 g dried rations** remaining  
- **0 kw** of stored solar power – only a gamble on the night ahead  

Mira was hunched over the cracked speaker of their salvaged radio, the static‑filled microphone silent. The boy had slipped to the edge of the forest, his hand drumming an erratic rhythm against the crevice wall – a half‑remembered lullaby that Mira once sang to soothe crying children. His skin was slick with fever, the black ooze from his wounds seeping into the mud.  > “You don’t have to—” Kael began, but Mira snapped her hand up, her voice colder than the night air.  

> “He’s not asking. He’s *demanding*.”  

The boy flinched, his eyes locking onto the half‑filled vial of water glinting in Mira’s other hand — a salvaged bottle from the greenhouse cache.  

---  

### The Trade‑Off  

Mira knelt, feeling the poisoned soil sink under her boot. The creek bed nearby shimmered with a sickly green sheen. A memory surged: the orphanage children she’d once cradled, their chuckles turning to coughs after a sudden flood. *Was that why the orphanage had been closed?*  

> “Tell him we can’t.” Kael’s tone was final, a wall of resolve.  

Mira’s fingers trembled as she uncapped the bottle and forced the water down the boy’s throat. The liquid surged down his chest, his cries cutting the silence like glass.  

> “It’s bad,” Mira whispered, but the words felt hollow even to her.  

Kael moved instinctively to snatch the vial away, but Mira pulled it back, her grip bruising his palm. “You think they’re coming for him?” she muttered. “The convoy? The *brandeds*?”  

---  

### The Revelation  

The boy’s wound began to fester mid‑swallow. Pressure built in his abdomen; he doubled over, a hot spray of blood spilling from his mouth. Kael froze, pistol half‑raised. Mira didn’t flinch.  

> “He’s lying,” Kael growled. “No one drinks poisoned water unless they’re desperate—and *scared*.”  

The boy’s face went ashen. “They said it was safe. My mother drank it too…”  

Mira’s hand found his arm, squeezing hard enough to draw a gasp. “You’re not their target,” she said. “They want the *water*, not you.”  The boy could answer only with a wet, choking sigh. His body betrayed him—a slow, merciless death. Kael’s chest tightened. For the first time he wondered whether his own terror of scarcity had turned him cold.  

---  

### The Cost  

By the time the boy’s breath finally stilled, Kael had emptied the last of the dry rations from his ledger into the boy’s empty mouth. The food tasted like ash.  

Mira added up the losses:  - **280 g water** → **0 g** (spent)  
- **350 g rations** → **150 g** (consumed)  
- **1 kw** of solar credit (unsold) – unchanged  

> “We’re walking,” Mira said.  

---  

### The Movement  

The ridge loomed ahead, but now a new threat hung in the air: the boy’s fresh blood. A low hum rose from the forest—scavenger drones, or worse, the copper convoy.  

Kael weighed the ledger again. *Move fast. Hide the body. Forget the boy.*  

Mira stared back at the crevice. The boy’s cot, now a makeshift grave, seemed to watch them.  

> “We leave him,” she said.  

Kael didn’t bother replying. He dumped the satchel, slipped the remaining white seeds into a rusted cart he’d scavenged from the radio shack, and they trudged away. The boy’s name never entered the ledger.  

---  

### The Echo  

As they crested the hill, the copper‑scaled squirrel re‑appeared, darting between them like a living shadow. It led them to a patch of bitter but edible wild greens. Mira’s tongue was slick with soda‑pops as she hesitated.  

> “We’re not monsters,” she whispered.  

Kael offered no reply. The squirrel slipped into the trees, leaving only a single blackened root in its wake.  

The root pulsed faintly, as if it held a direction of its own.  

By dusk they found a hidden aquifer beneath a fissure in the hill. Its water was clear and cold, a rarity in these wastes. Kael logged it as **+500 g clean water**.  

The trade‑off was clear: one life taken, one column erased from the ledger. The choice still pricked at Mira’s conscience, but the seeds and water were theirs again.  

---  

**End of Chapter 9**  *The crew’s path now leads toward the old mill, while the memory of the boy’s blood stains the creek and the promise of a new marker—a pulsing root—waits beneath the hill.*
