## 2️⃣ DEEPENING THE DUQUET POV SWITCH

You’re aiming for **dual POV** without head‑hopping. The trick is to **anchor each shift with a concrete sensory cue** that belongs only to that character. That way the reader knows instantly whose head they’re in.

**Example of a clean POV hand‑off:**

1. **Elias POV (first half of the scene)** – focus on sound, vibration, the weight of the hull, the metallic taste of exhaust. Because he’s a former tech‑manager, let him filter everything through a *system‑log* lens: “*The pressure gauge spiked to 3.2 psi—anomaly flagged.*”

2. **Transition cue** – a flash of light on the water, a sudden ripple, or an in‑world signal (e.g., a dive‑crew radio ping). It can be as simple as Elias *tensing* his shoulders, then the narrative *cuts* to Lyra’s inner monologue, where the same ripple translates to *“the sea breathed against my skin, cold as a lover’s sigh.”*

3. **Lyra POV (second half)** – keep the language **more tactile** and **organic**. She notices the *texture* of the rope, the *smell* of seabrine, the way the light catches Elias’s scar. Because she’s been in the low‑tide trenches for years, let her mind *catalogue* surroundings as a survival map: “*The current nudged the hull; the coral arch was a perfect choke point.*”

When you hand‑off, end a paragraph on a **physical sensation** that both characters are aware of, then launch into the other POV with a line that starts with their immediate bodily reaction. This maintains continuity while giving each voice its own rhythm.

### How to keep the **slow‑burn** credible

1. **Delay the “big” emotional confession** until at least Chapter 4–5. Readers love the anticipation; the tension never feels forced if it’s earned by a series of small, believable moments.
2. **Use “shared resource” moments** – air, a rope, a limited power cell – as metaphors for *dependence*. Every time they depend on each other, their bond deepens *organically*.
3. **Inject “private journals” or written logs** that the other character discovers. This is a classic way to expose inner thoughts without overt dialogue.
4. **External pressure** (the crew’s skepticism, Khazad’s threats) should always **mirror** the internal pressure. When a leak threatens the hull, it also threatens the fragile trust they’re building.
