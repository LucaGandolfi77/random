**Chapter 2– Oxygen and Salt**  

Elias crouched in the wreck’s belly, the slim beam of his dive‑light slicing through a cloud of silt that tasted of rust and ancient rain. The metal ribs of the cargo hold stretched like the bleached bones of a long‑dead leviathan, each one humming with the pressure of the deep. He adjusted the regulator on his wrist, feeling the familiar sting of cold metal against his skin, and watched the breath‑meter on his visor flash amber‑green—halfway between safety and surrender.  

A soft scrape echoed from behind a tangle of corroded girders. Instinct snapped his head toward the sound, and there, peeking from a fissure in the stone, was Lyra. Her tail flicked in slow, deliberate arcs, the iridescent scales catching the beam and splintering it into shards of teal and amber. She was no monster; she was a woman whose eyes held the depth of a trench he had never dared explore.  

His gloved hand hovered over the handheld scanner at his thigh. He could have released a puff of gas to mark her position, could have tagged her with a data pulse and retreated to the safety of his ship. Instead, he let the scanner idle, the red‑orange LEDs blinking in rhythm with his quickening pulse. He lifted his hand—not to command, but to signal, the same deliberate motion he used when teaching his sister the language of underwater hand signals.  

Lyra tilted her head, the movement fluid as water slipping over stone. Her fingers brushed the edge of a broken plate, sending a ripple of phosphorescent algae across the sand. The light flared, casting a fleeting halo around her form. For a heartbeat Elias thought he saw the outline of a scar on her forearm—a scar that matched the one in the photograph hanging in his sister’s old dorm room, the photograph that still haunted his dreams of drowning.  

He took a step forward; the sand shifted under his boots with a muted crunch louder than the hum of the ship’s engines above. His scanner pinged, a soft chime that vibrated through the water and into his chest. Lyra’s eyes widened, and she recoiled, her tail tightening as if ready to bolt. In those seconds a misreading crystallized: Elias’s gentle gesture was read by her as an attempt to bind her, to trap the last free creature of this drowned world.  Lyra’s mouth opened, a thin line of water spilling out, and she whispered in a language that felt more like pressure than sound. The words coiled around his thoughts like kelp, wrapping his fear in a spiral of understandable suspicion. “You come with tools,” she seemed to say, “but the sea does not forgive those who try to own it.”  

Elias froze. The scanner slipped from his grip, clattering against a metal rib and sending a cascade of bubbles toward the ceiling. He felt the weight of the moment settle on his shoulders—the unspoken decision to retreat or to trust the fragile bridge he was building with a being who had learned to keep everyone at arm’s length out of necessity. He let the scanner sink, its faint echo the only sound louder than his own breathing.  

Lyra watched him, pupils dilating as she took in the raw vulnerability of his stance. She could have fled, let the currents carry her into the endless dark, but something in the way his shoulders hunched—despite the armor of his gear—drew her attention. She pressed a hand against the cold stone, the texture familiar as a map she had memorized for years, and let the sensation ground her.  

“You will not take me,” she said—not in words, but in the tremor of a breath that carried a thousand tides of warning.  

Elias felt heat rise in his cheeks under the pressure of the water, a sensation he hadn’t known since his sister’s funeral. He had always measured his worth in metrics—bytes transferred, numbers on a screen—yet here, against the crushing weight of the abyss, he measured himself in breaths, in the unsteady tremor of his hands. He lifted his palm, open, not to command but to offer.  

“I’m not here to take,” he said, his voice muffled by the regulator, “just to understand.”  

The word hung between them, fragile as a glass bubble, and for a moment the ocean seemed to hold its breath. In that suspended instant Lyra’s tail slowed, the motion becoming almost lyrical. She reached out with a hand that was more fin than finger; the webbed digits brushed the back of his wrist in a fleeting touch that sent a shiver tracing his spine—not fear, not triumph, just aching hope wrapped in uncertainty.  His scanner, now silent on the seabed, recorded nothing but the faint pulse of his own heart. The data core in his pocket remained untouched, its dormant glow a reminder that the cargo they had salvaged might hold answers they were not yet ready to hear. Yet in that quiet exchange, Elias realized he was no longer just a technician hunting for scrap; he was a man standing at the edge of a new kind of risk—one that required him to lower his guard, to let someone else see the scar he kept hidden beneath his suit.  

The water around them seemed to close in, the pressure pressing gently against their chests, as if the ocean itself was listening to the unspoken promise that hung between two strangers: they would keep looking, keep reaching, even when every instinct screamed to retreat.  

And beneath that promise, a new thread began to unwind—a thread that would bind them tighter as the ruins whispered of curses, of love, and of the impossible choice that lay ahead.
