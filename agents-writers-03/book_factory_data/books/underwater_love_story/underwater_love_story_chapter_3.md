**Chapter 3 – The Language of Currents**  
*Elias’s Viewpoint*

The *Marauder*’s dive‑computer glowed a steady, mocking green: **02:17**. I’d been down for over an hour, the decompression ceiling ticking away like a metronome of my own mortality. But I wasn’t after salvage—not today.

I had returned to the fissure where I’d first seen her, a narrow cut in the obsidian rock still threaded with veins of fool’s gold. I carried nothing but a sealed vial of surface air—a luxury, a folly—and a single, smooth stone I’d pocketed from the beach outside my apartment, its edges worn by millennia of tide and surf.

I settled on a slab of coral‑encrusted concrete, the universal signal for *I am not hunting*. I waited.

Minutes stretched. The pressure hummed against my ears, a constant reminder of the weight above. Then, a flicker of movement—not from the fissure but from the shadowed overhang to my left. She was there, half‑hidden, her tail coiled beneath her like a sleeping serpent. Her eyes, wide and luminous in the gloom, locked onto the stone in my hand.

Holding it up, I let the soft glow of my dive‑light—diffused with a scrap of cloth—catch its curves. I placed the stone on the sand between us, a careful arm’s length away, and nudged it gently toward her with a slow, deliberate finger.

Lyra didn’t move. She scanned the stone, then my face, then my hands, building a map of intent. Finally she extended a hand, fingers delicate, webbed, tipped with pearlescent nails, hovering just above it. She didn’t touch. Instead she pointed to the stone, then to the bioluminescent algae clinging to the ruin’s wall, which pulsed in a slow, rhythmic rhythm.

*You bring a piece of the dry world. I show you a piece of mine.*

A silent understanding passed between us.

She pointed to the algae and to herself, then whispered the human word that felt foreign on her tongue: **“Lyra.”** The algae brightened for an instant. She repeated the name, linking herself to the light.

*Light‑bringer… or light‑keeper.*

She nodded. I pointed to the algae, then to her eyes. *You see through this?* Her answer was a quick, sharp nod.

She pointed to my dive‑light, curiosity flickering in her expression. I switched it off. The darkness fell for a heartbeat, then softened as the algae’s ethereal glow and the starlike sheen of plankton came into view. I pointed to my own eyes, then to the darkness, and gave a thumbs‑up.

*You navigate the dark. So do I.*

A slow, graceful movement. She was pleased.

I risked a small, practiced gesture—one I’d learned from my sister’s sunlit pool: the sign for *friend*. I touched my chest, then extended my empty palm, open.

Lyra’s reaction was instant and violent. A shudder ran through her; she pressed herself against the stone. Her gills flared wide, and a low, pained trill vibrated through the water. It wasn’t anger—it was terror. The scar on her forearm, a lattice of white lines, seemed to pulse with an inner light.

*Pain,* she signed, fingers moving in sharp, jerky motions I didn’t understand. *Binding. Curse.*

The curse. It had to be the ancient ward the *Chronicle*’s files spoke of, the one that equated any gesture of union with agony and violation. I had just proved its existence was more than myth.

Holding my hands up, empty, I pointed to the stone, to the algae, to the water itself. Then, deliberately, I made the friend sign again—this time directing it toward the space between us, then shaking my head.

*Not binding. Not taking. Just… here.*

She trembled. The pain in her eyes was sharper than any wound I’d seen. I understood then: the curse wasn’t only a barrier between our peoples; it was a barrier inside her, a biological law that turned any claim of connection into suffering.

From my belt I drew the sealed vial of surface air. I uncapped it and let a single, perfect bubble rise—small, fragile, a sphere of sky. I pointed at the bubble, then to myself, then to her, and finally made the friend sign again, not toward her but toward the bubble as it vanished into the ruins above.

*See?* *I release it. I keep nothing. I do not bind.*

Lyra stared at the spot where the bubble had disappeared. Her ragged breathing slowed; the light from her scar dimmed. She uncurled just enough to speak again.

She pointed to the vial in my hand, then to her own chest. With delicate, cradling fingers she mimicked holding a breath, then pointed to the algae, which pulsed gently. *My world is a vial,* she seemed to say. *To try to take me would be like trying to crush that bubble in your fist—it would shatter, and so would I.*

That thought broke through my scientific detachment. She wasn’t a specimen; she was a person, imprisoned by a legacy of fear, communicating entire souls through movement, light, and water.

She had to answer my offering of peace—not with logic, but with equal vulnerability.

I detached the dive‑computer from my wrist—the *Marauder*’s lifeline, the last tether to my remaining air. The screen still glowed **01:45**. I placed it on the sand, mirroring the stone I’d offered earlier. I pointed to the countdown, then upward toward the distant hull of my ship, and sliced my throat with a finger.

*My time is limited. My world is up there. I choose to spend it here, with you. Not to take. To stay.*

I pushed the computer toward her.

She froze, as if it might explode. Her body locked, eyes wide with the terror we both knew her ancestors felt at any surface touch. Yet nothing erupted. No surge of pain, no flare of her scar. She brushed the casing with a trembling fingertip—a gesture that said, *I can touch without dying.*

A shuddering breath escaped her. She looked from the computer to my face, eyes filled with wonder so pure it stole my breath. She had spent a lifetime believing any contact with the other side was a wound; I had just handed her my lifeline, and she had held it without breaking.

She pushed the computer back to me, then placed a flat palm on the sand, palm up, between us—a silent invitation.

I mirrored her, glove to glove, not touching. Our hands hovered, separated by a universe of history, curse, and fear. In that space something new flickered: choice.

I inched forward. Water resisted, then yielded. My gloved hand brushed her scales.

A jolt—pure *recognition*—shot through me, a static shock of awe and belonging so intense it stole my breath. She gasped, eyes fluttering shut. The scar on her forearm flared brilliantly, then faded.

The curse was awake. It pressed gently against my chest, a warning, a boundary.

Lyra’s eyes snapped open, brimming with iridescent bubbles that clung to her lashes. She ripped her hand away as if burned by the curse itself, curling inward to face away.

*You are a bridge made of tinder,* her expression screamed. *I am a match.*

**Lyra’s Viewpoint**

*Pain. Always the pain.*

When his skin—synthetic, dry, *other*—touched mine, the Tide sang in my blood. It was a pressure building behind my eyes, a pressure that warned *SEPARATE*. It was the ancient voice of my ancestors, of the Deep itself, telling me that this warmth, this *Elias*, was a fissure in the world’s shell, and pressing against it invited collapse.

I had built walls of silence and suspicion for a lifetime. He had spent an hour dismantling them with stones and bubbles and a crippled computer. And now… this. The ultimate proof that the curse was not a story but a living thing inside me, designed to keep me alone.

He looked at me with sorrow, not accusation. *I am sorry,* he signed.

The realization struck me like a pang: he was apologizing for the curse itself, for the uncrossable sea that made my body scream when touched by any surface soul.

I faced the stone, shame burning hotter than the Tide’s pressure. I had reached for him. I had *wanted* the contact, even knowing the cost. Was I so starved for a voice that wasn’t my own echo that I invited agony?

A shadow fell over me—not from above, but from him. He moved nearer, each step telegraphing intent. He wasn’t pitying me; he was seeing the terror in my eyes.

He pointed to his chest, then to mine, and signed a simple universal question: *How?*

I didn’t know how to answer that. The curse’s origin—a tale of betrayal and war, of a king who loved a surface dweller and doomed them both—was a story of an ending already written. To speak of its beginning was to speak of its end.

Instead, I pointed to the computer blinking **01:20** on the sand. I pointed upward to the surface, to his ship, to his *life*. Then I pointed to myself, to the crushing dark, to the ruins, to my *death*. I brought a hand to my heart and extended it toward him, palm open, empty.

*My world ends where yours begins. To cross is to break. To stay is to drown.*

I was telling him to leave. To save himself from a choice that would destroy one of us.

He stared at my hands, his face a mask of conflict. The air in his tank ticked like a metronome of his dwindling time. He could surface now, return to light and air, forget the scar, forget the silent language of currents, forget a love that was a death sentence.

He looked from me to the computer, to the scar on my arm, to the one he recognized on his own soul—the one mirrored in the old photograph of his sister. His expression hardened with a question that needed no sound: *How can we break it?*

He didn’t point to the surface. He didn’t gesture for *go*.

Instead, he held out the sealed vial of surface air still clutched in his other hand. He uncapped it—not to release a bubble, but to offer the breath itself. He pointed toward my lips.

A gift—not of a stone, but of the very essence of his world. A single breath, offered freely.

The curse stirred—not with pain, but with a fragile curiosity. For the first time in a thousand years, the Tide was silent, waiting to see what I would do.

*I… I can take it,* I thought, feeling the Tide pause. *Maybe this is how we begin.*
