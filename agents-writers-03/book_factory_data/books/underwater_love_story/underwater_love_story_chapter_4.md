**Chapter 4:Shallow Breath**

The *Marauder*’s dive‑computer glowed a steady, mocking green: **02:17**. I’d been down for over an hour, the decompression ceiling ticking away like a metronome counting down my own mortality. But I wasn’t after salvage—not today.

I’d returned to the fissure where I’d first seen her, a narrow slit in the obsidian rock still threaded with veins of fool’s gold. I carried nothing but a sealed vial of surface air—a luxury, a folly—and a single, smooth stone I’d pocketed from the beach outside my apartment, its edges worn by millennia of tide and surf.

I settled on a slab of coral‑encrusted concrete, the universal signal for *I am not hunting*. The pressure hummed against my ears, a constant reminder of the weight above. Minutes stretched. Then, a flicker of movement—not from the fissure but from the shadowed overhang to my left. She was there, half‑hidden, her tail coiled beneath her like a sleeping serpent. Her eyes, wide and luminous in the gloom, locked onto the stone in my hand.

Holding it up, I let the soft glow of my dive‑light—diffused with a scrap of cloth—catch its curves. I placed the stone on the sand between us, a careful arm’s length away, and nudged it gently toward her with a slow, deliberate finger.

Lyra didn’t move. She scanned the stone, then my face, then my hands, building a map of intent. Finally she extended a hand, fingers delicate, webbed, tipped with pearlescent nails, hovering just above it. She didn’t touch. Instead she pointed to the algae clinging to the ruin’s wall, which pulsed in a slow, rhythmic rhythm.

*You bring a piece of the dry world. I show you a piece of mine.*

A silent understanding passed between us.

She pointed to the algae, then to herself, and whispered the human word that felt foreign on her tongue: **“Lyra.”** The algae brightened for an instant. She repeated the name, linking herself to the light.

*Light‑bringer… or liar?*

I pointed to myself. “Elias.”

Her gaze held mine. The name settled in the water between us, a fragile bubble of sound.

Then, from the inner pocket of my dry‑suit, I pulled the vial. The glass was cool, the surface air inside a captured piece of another universe. I held it up beside the stone, offering both.

Her pupils dilated. She reached out, not for the vial, but for the stone. Her fingertip brushed its surface—a whisper of contact—and a jolt, like static shock mixed with a sudden drop in pressure, shot up my spine. I flinched.

She snatched her hand back as if burned, her expression one of startled confusion. The algae’s pulse stuttered, dimmed.

The curse wasn’t just a story; it was a physical wall humming in the water between us.

I forced a slow breath through my regulator, the sound loud in my own ears. I pointed to the vial, then to my chest, and made a gesture of drinking. *Air—for me.*

Then I pointed to the stone, then to her, and mimed placing it gently on the ground. *A gift—for you.*

Her suspicion didn’t vanish, but it receded, replaced by a dawning, wary curiosity. She took the stone and clutched it to her chest. The simple act of possession seemed to steady her.

I unsnapped the vial’s cap. The seal broke with a soft *pfft* of escaping pressure. I didn’t drink; instead, I tilted it, letting a single, perfect bubble of my world’s air rise from the rim. It wobbled, a tiny, fragile planet, before popping against the water’s surface far above.

A gesture of sharing—a piece of my breath, given to the deep.

She watched the bubble’s ascent, her head tilted. Then she looked back at me, and in her eyes I saw a reflection of my own loneliness—the quiet, hollow space that had driven me down here in the first place.

I pointed to the algae, vibrant and alive on the wall. “Your world,” I said, the words muffled by my mask.

She pointed to the empty vial, now in my hand. “Your world,” she echoed, the human syllables clumsy but clear.

The silence that followed was different. It wasn’t the silence of predator and prey, or of two wary strangers. It was the silence of two people standing on opposite sides of a vast, dark room, suddenly aware they were humming the same tune.

Then, a new sound—a low, rhythmic *thrum* that vibrated up through the concrete slab and into my bones. Not the pressure hum. This was mechanical. Distant, but closing.

Lyra’s body went rigid. Her eyes, fixed on mine, widened with a terror that had nothing to do with me. She pointed urgently toward the fissure’s exit, then to herself, and made a sharp, cutting motion across her throat.

*Khazad.*

The salvage crew. They were scanning. They were near.

The fragile bubble of our understanding shattered. She slipped back into the shadows of the overhang, vanishing like a wisp of silt. The stone she’d taken was gone with her.

Alone in the belly of the wreck, the taste of rust and ancient rain in my mouth, the mechanical *thrum* growing louder, my air was low and my secret exposed. For the first time, the crushing darkness above felt less like a refuge and more like a prison.

I had shared a breath. And in doing so, I had handed them a map to her door.
