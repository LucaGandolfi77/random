**Chapter 5: The Crushing Depth**  

The comm crackled—a low, steady buzz that meant the surface was trying to talk to him through three hundred feet of ocean. Elias ignored it, his gloved fingers tracing the edge of the smooth stone he’d left in the sand on his last visit. It sat where he’d placed it, a dull whisper against the silt, and for a heartbeat he almost believed the ruins were holding their breath with him.  

His dive‑light cut a thin, trembling swath through the dark as his pulse quickened. The water felt thicker here, as if the wreck itself were exhaling, pushing currents past him that smelled faintly of rust and something sweeter—kelp, maybe, or the memory of open sky.  

He had been coming back to this place for three weeks now, each descent a little longer than the last, each return to the *Marauder* a little more unsteady. His tanks registered an amber warning that he’d learned to read like a prayer: **02:17**. Time enough to feel, not enough to stay forever.  

A flicker of movement caught his eye—a ripple in the sand, a flash of iridescent scales. Lyra emerged from the same fissure in the obsidian rock where she’d first appeared, her tail moving with a grace that made the water around her pulse and shimmer. She stopped a few meters away, perched on a broken column that jutted out like a gravestone.  

He could see the scar on her forearm, pale against the darker skin of her wrist, and his chest tightened. It was the same mark his sister had borne, the one he’d stared at in photographs until the edges of the paper began to curl. A coincidence, he told himself, though the word felt flimsy underwater.  

“Lyra,” he said, his voice garbled by the regulator but carrying across the short distance. The name seemed too small for the space between them, too human for the depth they inhabited.  

She tilted her head, eyes reflecting the thin beam of his light. In the weeks since their first meeting they had developed a choreography—each gesture a sentence, each pause a paragraph. Today, her hand moved to the scar, pressing it as if to remind herself it was real, then extended the same hand toward him, palm up.  

The invitation was unmistakable.  

His heart thudded against his ribs, a rhythm that matched the soft percussion of the hull creaking above them. He took a step forward, then another, feeling the sand shift beneath his boots in a muted crunch that sounded, absurdly, like applause. The scanner at his thigh blinked its idle warning, but he didn’t look at it. He didn’t need its confirmation—he could feel the pull, magnetic and electric, drawing him closer to something he wasn’t sure he understood.  

She was within arm’s reach now. The water around them seemed to pulse, and he realized she was touching the bioluminescent algae that clung to the stone walls, coaxing it into a slow, rhythmic glow. The light painted her face in shades of blue and green, making her look more creature than human, more memory than person.  

Elias lifted his free hand, fingers splayed the way he’d seen her do countless times when she wanted to show him something without words. She mirrored the motion, and for an instant their palms hovered a breath apart, separated only by the thin film of water and something heavier—an unseen weight that seemed to press from all sides.  

The moment stretched, taut as a wire.  

Then, just as he was about to close the final gap, her eyes flicked past him toward the shadowed opening of the main cargo bay. A low rumble vibrated through the metal ribs of the wreck, a sound that didn’t belong to the ocean at all. It was mechanical, deliberate—the kind of noise that meant someone else was down here.  

Lyra’s expression hardened. She pressed a hand to the scar again, then brought it to her lips—a gesture that might have been a kiss or a warning. Her movements were swift, urgent. She tapped the stone he’d offered her, then pointed to the darkness beyond the fissure, then finally to herself.  

*I can’t,* she seemed to say without words. *It’s not safe. Not for you. Not for them.*  

He wanted to argue—to tell her that the danger was precisely why he came back, why he lingered in this crushing depth long after his tanks warned him to surface. Instead, he watched as she turned, her tail flicking once, twice, and disappeared into the tunnel that led deeper into the ruins.  

The rumble grew louder, a grinding that set his teeth on edge. He caught a glimpse of metal limbs, the unmistakable silhouette of a salvage subtractor moving through the water with purpose—Khazad’s crew.  

Elias clenched the stone in his fist until the edges bit into his palm. He could follow Lyra, could try to bridge whatever chasm the curse had carved between them, but the proximity of the other divers made the water feel like it was being squeezed from all sides. The curse—whatever it was—felt less like a mystical barrier now and more like an old, familiar pressure in his ears.  

He turned back toward the exit, toward the *Marauder*, toward the surface and the life he’d left behind. Each kick of his flippers sent him upward, away from the fissure, away from the promise he could not quite reach. The stone dangled from his glove, a dull weight that reminded him of promises kept and broken.  

When he finally broke the surface, gasping the thin, salty air of the world above, the sun was already slipping behind a bank of clouds. The *Marauder* bobbed gently beside him, its crew shouting instructions in voices that seemed impossibly far away.  

Elias stripped off his helmet and let the metal clank against the deck. For a moment he closed his eyes, trying to hold onto the feeling of her hand hovering just inches from his own. The memory was a bruise—sharp, tender, and impossible to ignore.  

He didn’t notice the scanner at his belt flicker once, twice, as if trying to speak. He didn’t notice the faint pulse of light that began to travel along the bioluminescent trails deep within the wreck, moving faster now, racing toward the surface like a heartbeat.  

All he felt was the weight of the stone in his hand and the echo of a name whispered in the crushing dark.
