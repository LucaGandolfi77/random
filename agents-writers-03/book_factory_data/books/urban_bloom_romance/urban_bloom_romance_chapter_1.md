**Chapter1 – Neon Shadows and Shared Umbrellas**  

The city swallowed the sky that night, suffocating it beneath a low, breathing cloud of traffic. Sirens hissed like wounded animals, and neon‑lit billboards dangled from the invisible scaffolding between steel giants. Across the street, a concrete playground buckled under a sudden flash of rain, turning the asphalt into slick, black glass.  

Inside a forgotten rooftop garden—one that only existed when the city’s lights dimmed—a thin silver glow slipped through pitted windows, turning pale leaves into dim emeralds.  

Lena pushed the door shut behind her, breath steaming between her lips. The rusted latch clicked, mocking her. She had to stand against the flood of water on the street, cursing herself for being late—again. Her coat was damp, a thin smear of the storm’s skin, and the few pre‑planned steps of the week had turned into a long, meandering walk. The garden was a simple architecture of hanging pots, cracked concrete, and a lone elm that stood like a roaring stone.  

Kai was already there, arms wrapped around a small bundle of seedlings. He was a wiry shadow in the violet flicker of the city lights, the kind of person who never seemed surprised by “what if.” The day’s work had left an invisible bruise across his chest; his fingers were stubbornly twisted around the green shoots of life.  

Lena lifted her phone to her ear, her voice jittering with the city’s hum.  

> **“I won’t be back until… No, I need to—”**  

She was shouting at the absurdity of her own disorientation. The ringtone burst through the air like a broken record, echoing from the rooftop signal tower that had missed its cue.  

**“Lena?”**  

She turned, visibly flustered. He stood in the little privacy of a broken fountain; his eyes, startlingly glossy, seemed to read the tremor in her nerves. For an instant he was a calloused shelter, a hand that could hold.  

He stepped forward, dropped a folded piece of paper from his pocket, and handed it to her. The digits bled through the edges of the printed script, a variant that made her eyes widen. She remembered the helplessness of a neon spark leaping from her skull—the city, whether used or not.  

> **“Did you…?”** she began, breath catching.  
> **“I… you—”**  Kai shook his head, amused.  She stared at him. Then she heard the soft, trapped exhalation of herself and decided… he was a stranger.  

The rain forced him to tuck his umbrella into his jacket. He saw himself taking a long walk in the night, the fluorescent lights humming. She cared about direct connections; she preferred something eerier.  The voice on his phone shivered as he ducked behind the break, but it remained smooth.  

> **“Already. I am no longer latched.”**  

He gently placed a lively breath on her shoulder, and she took a vestibular confidence. The details made the ghost of their last encounter feel like a titless bottle held between them.  

The garden faded to a taste of a future that would bring the scent of comfort while still opening the current of their careful dance.  

> **“…and the glucose’s…”**  

Lena looked out at the night, half‑laughing. He held an acrostic that waited for a bad edge, and it swirled— they walked for a while.  

She lowered her eyes.  

She was *wrong*… or maybe just *different*.  

***

The glow of those neon lights flickered, and the city would not let her be. Yes, it was enough; they deserved the magnitude of this moment. It was a pass—a slant—she studied this new, grown league.  

Lena left those few hours later. The funeral of a city.
