Elisa's lips curved in a mask of defiance. "You think you can toy with our pasts? This orchard is a memory, not a fire. And I will not let it burn for your coin," she said, voice steady but thin.

Mara's throat tightened. "Then lose whatever memory you hold dear. Without the knowledge of your mother, you cannot control the silences that gnaw at your throne. Turn the page," she urged.

The ledger's edge whispered a painful threat as Elisa groped for the knife at her belt. She thought of the orchard's roots, of the delicate, glowing strands that once reached for the sky. The thought of that fire—once kindled by her own hand—burned hotter than the summer sun.

In a sudden, desperate motion, Elisa lifted the knife and set it on the ledger. Her hand trembled, every shiver a chord of grief. She stared into the glass fruit, and for a heartbeat, she saw the orchard—how fire had once surged through rings of wood, how her mother had stood beneath falling leaves, how the night of the first blaze had ended in a silent loam of burnt earth.

With a breath that felt like a bark in wind, Elisa plunged the blade into the glass.

The fruit shattered. Each shard snowed down, humming with sorrow. One crystal fell into Mara's palm and cracked open, scattering a shard of memory into the air—images of a woman who had spoken, a laugh that once touched the stone. The shards glowed like a constellation of broken hopes.

"Lara," Mara whispered, as the shard sang the name. It was too late—the shard's light grew into a blazing flare that licked the roots of the orchard.

Elisa, with eyes muzzle a cracked, watched her orchard's roots ignite in a living memory—a blue flame that sang of lost dreams, not of physical combustion. Her own memory of her mother, a fragile, half‑lost lullaby, began to seep into the fire and vanish.
