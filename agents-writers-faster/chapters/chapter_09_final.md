In the dim chamber, the ledger lay open, its parchment cracked like old bone beneath a silver night. Ink seeped across the fibers, thickening until the letters seemed to pulse with a liquid light, as if the words themselves were rivers of memory.

Elisa stood, candle fire trembling in her palm. The glass that had once burned was now a shard, a shard that, when struck, sang a low, iron chorus. Above, the orchard’s roots—stretching through the bones of the earth—glowed with a new, unsettling light, a memory fire that pulsed with a color no eye had ever seen.

"Every promise is a price," Mara whispered, her voice a crackling ember. The ink on the ledger glowed brighter, as if listening. She fixed her gaze on the shadowed corner where a hand had pressed against the paper. The ledger’s final line blinked into existence, a sliver of silver script that wrote itself: _"The forgotten god once spoke; he asks not for what we lose but for what we cherish."_

Elisa’s throat tightened. The air grew thick; the scent of burnt parchment mingled with the sweet decay of the orchard’s memory‑fire. She remembered the first blaze—the impulse that had set the sky aflame. The flame had consumed more than wood; it had burned a part of her own heart. The ledger’s words made that memory brittle, like glass.

"You can turn it away," Mara urged, eyes bright as shattered moonstone. "We can stop it. The shard slipped from her fingers like a sigh. A cold wind around the desk whispered of the orchard’s roots; the light in them flickered, as if breathing. Elisa rose, heart unclenching as a stone guttering in grief. She poured ink into a glass cup, watched it rise, and watched it sink.

She pressed her palm to the ledger. The ink sang. The light in the orchard flared, casting a prismatic fear across the room.

"If I erase my mother’s face from the page, will the orchard keep living?” she asked, her voice a candle flame at the edge of a storm.

Mara moved forward, a steady hand, offering a balm of drinks and acceptance. She heard the ledger’s words in her ears, the name of the forgotten god humming. 

She looked out. The orchard’s light smelled like wet soil, yet smelled like stars and fire, the impossible.

Elisa looked back to her own shadow, her mother’s eyes behind it—a face that no ink could shatter.

"If I do it, I am a child who taunts the very thing that made me,” she whispered. 

The shards shattered and cracked. The orchard quivered. The ledger’s final line glowed brighter still, a promise: _"Burn the fire within or feel the memory bleed the rest of the orchard forever"_.
