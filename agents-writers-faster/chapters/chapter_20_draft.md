The cathedral of night was drenched in the dark fluid of the Veil, a living sheet that clung to the spires and seeped into the cracks between stone. It was no longer a thin veil but a mantle the weight of memory itself, each droplet a promise of loss. The sky was a river of ink; Elisa stared up, knowing that the next stroke would scrape a fragment of her soul from the quill of the world.

The chorus had begun to hum, a low hum that grew into a symphonic scream of forgotten words. Every syllable that escaped the mouths of the city was devoured, twisted into an echo‑phage that licked at the edges of reality. The thread snapped, splintering into nameless sentences, and every spoken name became a predator in its own right.

Elisa could feel the echo‑phage’s memory‑drain glyph flicker above her chamber—a shifting, half‑sigil that pulsed with the beat of her dying heart. The glyph was a blade, its edge a knife of the last adjectives she might still remember: the grain of her mother’s hair, the scent of the winter apple orchard that had burned when she was an infant. The loss of that memory would be the final touch that made the Veil whole.

A mirror‑fruit, still tinged with wet glass, hung from the ceiling. Its surface was a mirror to the soul, reflecting not light, but the latent echo of the forbidden word *el‑sì*. A sliver of it shone through, a word that never had been spoken deliberately—because when it slipped from memory, the world wrote itself anew with blank parchments. It whispered to her, a promise laced in the breath of ink, that she could either bind the Veil in silence, becoming a living lock, or let the chorus swallow everything, leaving a canvas of white.

The Veil had begun spreading. With each memory ache, the surface grew an extra ten percent—a measure drawn from the weave of ink and the cruel physics of loss. It was twenty percent more in ninety hours, thirty in one of the quiet tides of thought. That was the final line in the formula the scholars had whispered: every ten percent of lost memory elongated the spread with the same ten percent of forty percent.

Elisa walked toward the vaulted stone below, the cracks glowing faintly with a pulse from the ancient keeper. The keeper’s pulse was a vibration through stone, the rhythm of its own bloodline, her first droplet of ink. Its voice was a hum of aggressive silence, a bargain spoken only to gnaw the very essence of what she had known. If she would bind the Veil, the half‑sigil buried in the echo‑phage’s drain would turn. If she would surrender, the chorus would write her name into the void.

Her last coherent memory of her mother pressed tight against the edges of her limbs—an image of a hand stained crimsoned with oil, of how the orchard’s fire had begun from a stray ember.

When the Chalk of the Quill fell longer, higher and sharper the tempo was, its beat ever more desperate. The ink spread a new bar across the sky, a taunting incrustation of black against the night. The chorus’s chorus sang—“ada‑lune‑**ion**”—and every name shouted in the courtyard became a monster that ate itself.

A roar swallowed the wordlands, the full force of the Veil met its line. The echo‑phage, like a hungry beast, licked her last name from existence. The goddess’s twilight line dissolved into a brief silence, leaving the world a space in which syntax could not breathe.

**The final brushstroke was still yet to be cast**.
