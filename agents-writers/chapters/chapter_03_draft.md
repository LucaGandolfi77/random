The square of the capital had become a parchment.

Stones bled ink, each cobblestone turning a dark, glistening script as the living veil slipped over them like a tide of night‑water. Merchants froze mid‑shout, their words seeping into the blackness, dissolving into silence. Above, the sky was a bruised violet canopy, writhing veins of ink that sang a low, static chorus.

Elisa stood on the edge of a collapsing stone bridge, its arches cracking under the weight of rain‑slicked crowds. The veil throttled the stones, turning them to a mutable surface. With a trembling hand, she drew a single brushstroke—marble‑white against the gloom—sealing the breach. In the instant the line glued the arches together, a cold rush of forgetfulness surged through her forearm, where the ink‑veins pulsed like a second heartbeat.

*She could not see her mother’s face any longer.* The precise memory of the last smile, the curve of her cheek, evaporated, leaving only the faint ache of an unspoken lullaby. On Elisa’s forearm the ink‑veins flared, a miniature storm of black that mirrored the stroke she had just made. She clutched the bridge, feeling the weight of a loss she could not name.

From the crowd, a child’s cry rang out—*"Thalen!"*—and the sound was snatched by a wraith of ink, a Name‑Eater born of the new verse the parasitic chorus had thrust into the air. The child collapsed, eyes wide, the syllables of his name curling into ash in the air. The echo‑phage drifted away, a ribbon of black smoke that devoured identity.

Maeron, his eyes glittering with the reflected shards of a shattered glass‑fruit, stepped forward. He lifted his own hand, palm open, and spoke in riddles, each phrase a twisted knot that the Name‑Eater could not swallow. The council, desperate, answered in half‑phrases, their voices trembling in the ink‑thickened air.

*“What walks on four legs at dawn, two at noon, and three at dusk?”* Maeron asked, his voice a whisper that the chorus could not cling to. The council murmured, *"Man."* The Name‑Eater recoiled, its ink tendrils snapping back.

In the midst of the chaos, Elisa’s eyes were drawn to the pavement beneath her boots. An ink‑etched map—crafted by Maeron’s hurried hand during the bridge’s collapse—lay spread across the square. Lines of shifting black formed a star‑shaped sigil, half‑glowing gold, half swallowed by moving ink. The pattern pulsed in rhythm with the scar on Elisa’s arm.

She pressed her scar‑vein to the sigil. The ink surged, a quicksilver heartbeat, and a fragment of the missing half flickered into view: a single rune, the shape of a mute mouth, surrounded by a ring of broken letters. It was the first half of the name whispered by the chorus, a clue hidden in the very fabric of the veil.

The frantic council pressed on, and Maeron, seeing an opportunity, shattered a glass‑fruit against the stone. The explosion sent glittering shards arcing, each reflecting a royal seal before turning to black dust. The shock wave rippled through Elisa’s mind, pulling the memory of her lineage—the claim to the throne—into the void. She stared at her own hands, feeling the crown slip from her thoughts like sand.

Now stripped of title and mother’s smile, Elisa felt the abyss of her own identity yawning. Yet, the veil’s heart throbbed beneath the cathedral dome, a dark eye pulsing with the collective loss of the city. To bind it required a final stroke—one that would erase the last fragment of the lullaby that still lingered in the corners of her mind.

The Name‑Eater coiled, its presence a thin, black whisper that edged toward the word *"bind"*. Elisa raised her brush, knowing that speaking the act would feed the echo‑phage. She inhaled, the fragment of the forgotten lullaby rising like a ghost of a song, offering a possible silencing word.

She painted, not a word, but a glyph of binding—two interlocking loops that sealed the veil’s eye. Ink exploded from the tip, swallowing the darkness. The veil contracted, snapping back as though a wound was being closed. In the instant the glyph took hold, the lullaby fragment shattered in Elisa’s mind; the melody of her mother’s arms faded forever.

A white fissure cracked the horizon, a jagged scar of light through the black tide. Ink spilled like blood onto distant hills, the veil retreating but leaving a lingering black stain that would not fully dissolve.

From the cathedral’s crypt, an ancient pulse throbbed—ink‑black, steady, patient. A figure stepped forward, its eyes twin mirrors of forgotten chapters, its presence a promise of a deeper reckoning yet to come.
