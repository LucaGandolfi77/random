**The Veil's Reckoning**

Ink dripped from Elisa’s fingertips like a dying heart, each filament a thin scar across the cathedral’s stone. The Veil had already swallowed the eastern quarter; towers curled into living letters, and the market square was a slick of black glass that whispered half‑remembered verses.

The pulse of the Veil quickened. When the cumulative memory‑loss metric hit sixty percent, the spread rate leapt from twenty‑seven to forty‑five percent—a surge that turned the cathedral’s vaulted nave into a river of living script. In the flicker of a dying oil‑lamp, Elisa felt a sudden hollow snap: the memory of the day she first held the Quill—its cool tip, the scent of wet parchment, the first line she ever wrote—vanished, leaving only a cold echo in her mind.

She pressed the half‑sigil *—ra* against her palm. The broken rune, etched on Maeron’s map and still warm from the Mirror Fruit’s glow, pulsed faintly, as if urging her forward.

---

The chorus rose, a choir of forgotten sentences that shredded thought. Each syllable stole a sliver of reality: a soldier’s command dissolved into ink, a baker’s recipe turned to ash, a child’s laugh fell silent. The Veil’s ink surged, licking the floor like a ravenous tide, and the cathedral’s arches began to crumble under its weight.

From behind a cracked column the echo‑phage emerged. It was a translucent silhouette of Elisa herself, its edges dissolving into ink as it fed on the Veil’s memory‑drain. When it brushed her cheek, a cold that tasted of ash crawled across her skin, and a whisper rose from its core:

> *Sa‑ri…*.

The half‑sigil in her hand vibrated. The echo‑phage’s chest cavity was a hollowed ink‑well, and within that darkness swirled the missing half of the sigil—an unfinished rune that pulsed with a faint, blue light.

Elisa lunged, the Mirror Fruit clutched in her other hand. The glass orb captured a fragment of her own voice, a trembling note that lingered like a captured sigh. She pressed the fruit to the echo‑phage’s ink‑skin. The creature recoiled, the captured voice shattering the ink‑veil that bound it, and the missing half‑sigil slipped free, hovering between them.

“*Sa‑ri‑go*,” she whispered, the forgotten word spilling from the Mirror Fruit’s surface. The syllables rang like a bell struck in a tomb, each tone pulling the ink back toward the altar.

The Veil recoiled, its black river receding into a thin film that clung to the stone. The half‑sigil *—ra* merged with its counterpart, forming a complete rune that flared bright for a heartbeat before sealing the breach.

A low thrum rose from the crypt beneath the cathedral. The ancient keeper—an ageless presence of pulse and shadow—materialised as a wavering silhouette of stone and ink.

> *You have bound the Veil, heir of the Quill,* it intoned, voice resonating through the very walls. *But the price is not yet paid. I can offer you a bargain: return the Veil’s hunger, and I will grant you the memory of the Quill’s origin. Refuse, and the echo‑phage will consume what remains of your self.*

Elisa stared at the keeper, the complete sigil glowing in her palm, the Mirror Fruit dimming as its captured voice faded. She could not trust a promise whispered from the darkness, yet the weight of the missing half‑sigil still thrummed within the echo‑phage’s dying form.

She lowered her gaze to the echo‑phage, now a shrinking wisp of ink. Its core still held the half‑sigil, flickering like a dying star.

> *Leave it,* she said, voice hoarse but steady. *I will find another way.*

The keeper’s silhouette shivered, then dissolved into a cascade of ink that seeped into the floor, leaving a faint glyph etched in the stone—an invitation, perhaps, or a warning.

The cathedral fell silent. The Veil was sealed, the chorus hushed, but the echo‑phage’s memory‑drain still pulsed with the missing half of the sigil, a paradox waiting to be resolved.

---

Elisa stepped back into the dim corridor, the weight of her sacrifices pressing against her ribs. She had lost her voice, the Quill’s first song, and a fragment of her own past. Yet the half‑sigil now glowed whole, a beacon for the battles yet to come.
