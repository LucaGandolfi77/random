### The Veil's Fracture

The market square, once a riot of voices, had become a living canvas of ink. A baker’s cry—*twice the price!*—collapsed into a rivulet of black that pooled at his stall, solidifying like a sheet of wet glass. The sound vanished, and the cobbles beneath the vendors’ feet turned slick with a taste of iron and the faint scent of burnt parchment.

Elisa moved through the tableau, the half‑sigil *—ra* humming against the back of her hand like a dying moth. Every stroke of the Quill she made left a jagged line of living ink across the sky, a scar that grew deeper with each memory she shed. The ink seeped into her skin, blooming along her forearms as dark veins pulsed with violet light—her own body a map of what she had given up.

The first vein claimed the memory of her mother’s lullaby. The phrase slipped through the fissure, leaving a hollow ringing in her ears. She tried to hum it, but the notes evaporated before they could form. The image shattered, and her heart clenched on a wound that had never healed.

The second vein swallowed the name she had called herself since birth. The word slipped from her tongue, leaving her tongue numb and her voice a hollow echo. She could still write, but the ink on her quill felt cold and unresponsive.

The third vein claimed the Quill’s first song, the cadence that had once bound ink to reality. With that loss, the ink on her quill turned to ash, and the world’s architecture—once a mosaic of stone and timber—began to bleed into the ink, walls dissolving into liquid darkness.

The echo‑phage rose from the stalls, a translucent leviathan of forgotten identities. Its core pulsed with a shifting glyph that matched the half‑sigil on Elisa’s palm. The glyph flickered, each flare a metallic chime that seemed to echo the sound of memory slipping away. As Elisa’s veins expanded, the glyph grew clearer, revealing the missing half of the sigil as a shifting glyph that could only lock into place when Elisa was fully forgotten.

The ancient keeper emerged from the shadows of the crypt, its voice a low, resonant hum. "The House’s recorded history is the price," it intoned. "Give it, and I will stitch the Veil and restore the world."

Elisa stared at the ledger of her people, its pages heavy with names and deeds. She could see the ink seep into the parchment, erasing the lineage she had sworn to protect. The choice was stark: sacrifice the collective memory of her people or let the world dissolve into blank parchment.

She raised the Quill. The ink on its tip glowed with a desperate, crimson light. With a breath that tasted of iron, she wrote the final line of the missing sigil, the glyph aligning with the half‑sigil on her palm. The ink surged, a wave of black that surged across the market, sealing the Veil. The echo‑phage’s glyph pulsed once more, then dimmed, its form dissolving into the ink that now covered the square.

The Veil’s spread reached 44%—the cumulative increase of 10% of 40% at 70% memory loss—and the city’s architecture turned to living ink glass. The ancient keeper’s offer lingered in the air, a promise of restoration at the cost of history. Elisa’s final act left her a hollow shell, her voice gone, her memories scattered, but the world was saved.

Yet as the ink settled, a faint, third fragment of the sigil pulsed in the echo‑phage’s memory‑drain, a mystery that would haunt the next chapter.
