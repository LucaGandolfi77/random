Cemetery Chorus

Mercy Bell

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane, Compressed

I am Mercy Bell.
I set the winter lamp and counted pulses after the room emptied, bandaging the town while my own skin went unwarmed; usefulness bloomed like frost, and I called it love. Thomas Quill held a basin without spectacle, and that quiet held me longer than my own confessions. In autumn, work and worry and weather signed their treaty against my body, yet I was carried into town memory—where mercy rings like a borrowed bell. Gideon kept the face they expected; I kept the doubt that Silas Vale lent me mine, and hated how the doubt felt like a door I was never meant to enter, yet longed to knock upon. Let crisis find me calm, but do not ask how I sleep: I have learned to answer only in bandages, and to mistake tending for being tended to, until the lamp gutters and the pulse answers for me at last, soft as a secret tucked into a coat pocket against the gossip of snow. Tenderness, yes; resolve, yes; the rest is the hush between wanting and being wanted, and the careful arithmetic of self-neglect that almost, but never quite, paid for belonging."

Ruth Vale

Melancholic Irony With Restrained Fury

A bell of office, rung from beneath old water,
I measured love in ledgers, charity in coins,
while Silas drew rivers from the mill’s throat
and the Harrows pawned blankets for bread.

They’ll sing of my discipline, my frost-kindled grace,
but the flood ledger whispers: *he stole the current.*

I turned Jonah’s grudge into a town meeting,
let pride sculpt my face like a statue of stone,
while the autumn gnawed my ribs to dust.

Silas told me truths that unraveled us both—
before supper, before the ledger’s ink dried,
before the town forgot how to breathe.

They’ll call me mayor. I’ll be the ledger’s ghost,
counting affection in arrears, as the mill grinds on.

—Ruth Vale, 1902

Cross references: Silas Vale, Jonah Harrow

Gideon Bell

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane, Compressed

Here lies the cracked hymn book that sang order into a spring too willing; I preached severity to drown the ink of my own longing, and Mercy was carried into town memory while I carried on, composed, with manners ordinary as a locked gate. Let doctrine keep the ledger: eloquence disciplined the tongue, and hypocrisy paid for the silence between verses where desire learned my handwriting and almost walked away with me. Now work, worry, and weather have agreed, and I am redeemed by the very ridicule I feared—small, middling, and finally stilled in Morrowfield, 1904, where the bell of my name tolls for the appetite I offended and the redemption I could not out-sermon. Let the town remember: Gideon Bell was carried into town memory, and Mercy Bell with him, each step a perfect ordinary manner guarding something costly and unspoken until spring agreed to carry us both home.

Frank Harrow

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane, Compressed

I am Frank Harrow.
Here lies the merchant-child who balanced Caleb’s restraint with Miriam’s appetite for feeling—patient, witty, undecided, restless. Born and the old loyalties of Morrowfield bent around his cradle; he carried several families at once while Arthur kept vigil for kin. Autumn came and work, worry, and weather agreed against the body. He wanted only belonging and warmth, feared hunger and abandonment, and left the town’s arithmetic one sum short of choice. The future voice still shapes itself in the family weather, too young to perform, yet already holding us like rain we did not ask for and could not refuse. Rest, small merchant: you counted us in, and we are learning to count without you."

Cross references: None

Jonah Harrow

Resentful Introspection With Undercurrents Of Grim Resolve

I am Jonah Harrow.
I carved my name in the river’s throat, / where the Vales’ ledgers once whispered my debt. / Sawdust choked my lungs, but I breathed / the arithmetic of justice— / until the sluice markers lied, / and I became the ghost who counted / the cost of a lie in barley and blood.

Cross references: Samuel Harrow, Arthur Harrow, Bennett Harrow

Silas Vale

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane, Compressed

I am Silas Vale.
I turned when spring came calling and let age cash the note I kept renewing; the wheel still hums, though it has ground a hand too many. I joked when fright knocked, bargained shame into flour, and let gratitude wear the coin of wages so the mill—and I—might stay upright. June Harrow carried several families at once into our door, and I stepped beside Caleb with practical kindness to make the hard hour look almost ordinary; then I told him the secret I had banked like soot, and we were changed before the kettle sang. Continuity begged, forgiveness lingered, and the room, out of habit, arranged itself around a tired patriarch who finally said aloud what the town had only guessed—then folded into the hush of a broken wheel that feared exposure more than rust, and feared the body’s slow unmaking most of all. Let the stone keep spinning; let it be lighter than I was."

Cross references: None

Ada Quill

Warm Yet Wry, Like A Glass Half-Filled With Laughter

I am Ada Quill.
I kept Gideon Bell's letters to the singer from Galena behind the brandy shelf because holiness deserves at least one witness who can laugh.
