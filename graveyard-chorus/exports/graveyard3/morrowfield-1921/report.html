Cemetery Chorus

Mercy Bell

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane

I am Mercy Bell.
In autumn, I became a face, not a name, and the town held me close while I wondered if I belonged anywhere.

Cross references: None

Ruth Vale

Reflective And Slightly Wistful

I am Ruth Vale.
I am the epitaph I wrote for myself—careful, precise, yet quietly worn. My voice, measured in the hush of office, still echoes the way a landlord counts rent. I count affection like a ledger, but it costs more than rent.

Cross references: None

Gideon Bell

Reflective With A Wry Edge

I am Gideon Bell.
I am the echo in a quiet pew, the voice that speaks in measured tones while my heart races beneath the surface. In the year of our Lord two thousand and four, I stood among the townsfolk, preaching with a calm that could soothe or unsettle, depending on the hour. My words were order, my discipline unshakable, yet my hands trembled with a hunger I dared not name.

Cross references: None

Frank Harrow

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane

I am Frank Harrow.
I was born in Morrowfield, a year the town still counted with a pencil on a ledger. My voice was too young to sing, too young to perform, yet I learned to listen in the hush between Caleb’s restraint and Miriam’s hunger. I carried more than names—carried the weight of several families at once, as the Harrow family remembers. Arthur kept watch for kin, a silent vow in the autumn wind. The town added me to its arithmetic, but none confessed what it meant.

Cross references: None

Jonah Harrow

Wry Resignation

I am Jonah Harrow, milled by river‑sawdust and stubborn endurance,
who shifted the sluice one wet spring to make the Vales look greedy,
and hated them harder when the trick still felt righteous.
Samuel and Arthur kept vigil for my kin, loyal as the mill’s wheel,
while Bennett Harrow bore whole families through our streets like an ark.
Spring took me when work, worry, and weather finally agreed against my body,
leaving behind a ledger where tenderness sounded like bad accounting
and justice, dignity remained unpaid.

Cross references: Samuel Harrow, Arthur Harrow, Bennett Harrow

Silas Vale

Wry Resignation

: I, Silas Vale, miller of Morrowfield, lie where the wheel finally stalled—my industry ground grain, my wit kept the jest alive when fear crept in, yet I bargained in shame and expected the world to bend to my tired patriarch’s will. I drew relief into the mill, letting gratitude stand for wages, for power felt easier than fairness. June Harrow arrived bearing many families; beside Caleb I showed practical kindness, making hard hours seem ordinary. Before supper I spoke a truth long concealed, and it changed us both. In spring, age collected the debt it had extended, and the town carried me into its memory. I sought continuity, forgiveness; I feared exposure, the frailty of flesh. Here rests a man who joked when frightened, bargained when ashamed, and hoped the room would arrange itself around him.

Cross references: None

Ada Quill

Warm Yet Wry, Like A Candle In A Drafty Room

I was Ada Quill, bartender in Morrowfield, 1911. I served drinks with a smile that could outlast the town’s winter and a heart that beat for laughter more than for duty. I wore my courage like a coat, my humor like a lantern in the dark.

Cross references: None

Thomas Quill

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane, Compressed

I am Thomas Quill.
Black wool and cedar kept me courteous; spring, worry, and weather kept me still. I closed lids and counted on being necessary—applause for the working hand—while jealousy and fatalism stitched my patience to fear. The spare room I built for Mercy Bell learned my tenderness before my hand did; now the town carries me into memory, steady as a rumor I never earned and gentle as the proof I left behind: that to be reliable is to risk being loved, and to be gentle is to outlast ridicule without ever silencing it."

Cross references: None

Eliza Harrow

Lyrical, Intimate, Ironic, Humane, Compressed

I am Eliza Harrow.
I stitched mercy into linen by lamplight and let the hours outrun the hurry; summer, work, and weather finally agreed against me. I kept the ledger soft so the living could survive their names and the dead keep her quiet, and at last I said aloud what Morrowfield only guessed—then asked for nothing louder than peace and a door that closes on shame and needless suffering. Bennett and June kept their vigil; I kept mine in craft and secrecy, and the town bled where it lied about the wound, but the cloth held, and the crying stopped before the light did."

Cross references: None
